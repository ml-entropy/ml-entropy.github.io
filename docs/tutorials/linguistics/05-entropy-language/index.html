Entropy of Natural Language

How much information does English carry per character? Per word? Claude Shannon himself tackled this question in 1951, and the answer has profound implications for everything from data compression to the evaluation of modern language models. In this tutorial, we trace Shannon's original experiment, define the entropy rate of language, quantify English's remarkable redundancy, and connect these ideas to the metrics used to evaluate every language model built today.

1. Shannon's Guessing Game

In his landmark 1951 paper "Prediction and Entropy of Printed English," Shannon devised an elegant experiment. He showed human subjects a passage of text one character at a time and asked them to guess the next character. By recording how many guesses were needed, he could bound the entropy of English.

Shannon's Upper Bound $F_N$

Let $q_i$ be the probability that a human subject needs exactly $i$ guesses to identify the next character (out of 27 possibilities: 26 letters + space). Shannon defined:

$$F_N = -\sum_{i=1}^{27} q_i \log_2 q_i$$

This provides an upper bound on the true per-character entropy $H$, because a human predictor cannot be better than the optimal predictor, but can be worse.

Shannon also derived a lower bound $F_L$ using the observation that if the subject guesses correctly on the first try with probability $p_1$, then:

$$F_L = -p_1 \log_2 p_1 - (1 - p_1) \log_2 \frac{1 - p_1}{26}$$

Example: Guessing After "THE QU"

After seeing "THE QU", virtually every English speaker guesses "E" on the first try. The probability $p_1 \approx 1.0$, giving:

  • Surprisal: $-\log_2(1.0) = 0$ bits
  • This character carries almost no information — it is entirely predictable from context

Compare with the first character of a sentence, where many letters are plausible. Here $p_1$ might be only 0.15, yielding much higher entropy.

Shannon's Results

Shannon found that his bounds converged as the context length $N$ increased:

The true entropy of English lies somewhere in the range $H \approx 1.0$ to $1.3$ bits per character. This is far below the maximum possible entropy of $\log_2 27 \approx 4.76$ bits/char, revealing that English is highly structured and predictable.

2. Entropy Rate

Language is a stochastic process — a sequence of random variables $X_1, X_2, X_3, \ldots$ where each variable depends on those before it. The appropriate measure of information content is the entropy rate.

Entropy Rate

For a stochastic process $\{X_i\}$, the entropy rate is defined as:

$$h = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} H(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n)$$

For a stationary process, this equals the conditional entropy:

$$h = \lim_{n \to \infty} H(X_n \mid X_{n-1}, X_{n-2}, \ldots, X_1)$$

The entropy rate measures the average information per symbol when all preceding context is known.

The distinction between entropy and entropy rate is crucial:

  • $H(X)$: entropy of a single symbol in isolation (~4.07 bits for English characters by frequency)
  • $H(X_n \mid X_{n-1})$: entropy given one character of context (~3.32 bits)
  • $H(X_n \mid X_{n-1}, \ldots, X_1)$: entropy given all history (~1.0–1.3 bits)

Each additional character of context reduces uncertainty. The entropy rate is the limit of this process — the irreducible uncertainty that remains even with infinite context.

Stationarity and Ergodicity

The entropy rate is well-defined only for stationary processes (statistical properties don't change over time) and is estimable from a single long sequence only if the process is ergodic (time averages equal ensemble averages). Natural language is not truly stationary — topic, style, and vocabulary shift — but over long texts the stationarity assumption is a reasonable approximation.

3. Estimating English Entropy

We can build up to Shannon's estimate by computing entropy at progressively higher orders of approximation.

Zero-Order (Uniform)

If all 27 characters (letters + space) were equally likely:

$$H_0 = \log_2 27 \approx 4.76 \text{ bits/char}$$

First-Order (Unigram Frequencies)

Using the actual frequency distribution of English characters (where 'e' has probability ~0.127, 't' ~0.091, space ~0.183, etc.):

$$H_1 = -\sum_{c} P(c) \log_2 P(c) \approx 4.07 \text{ bits/char}$$

The non-uniform distribution saves about 0.7 bits per character.

Second-Order (Bigram)

Conditioning on the previous character:

$$H_2 = -\sum_{c_1} P(c_1) \sum_{c_2} P(c_2 \mid c_1) \log_2 P(c_2 \mid c_1) \approx 3.32 \text{ bits/char}$$

Why Bigrams Help

After 'q', the next character is almost certainly 'u' — very low entropy. After 'e', many characters are plausible — higher entropy. Averaging over all bigrams gives 3.32 bits, a significant reduction from 4.07.

  • $H(\text{char} \mid \text{q}) \approx 0.1$ bits (almost always 'u')
  • $H(\text{char} \mid \text{e}) \approx 4.0$ bits (many options)
  • $H(\text{char} \mid \text{space}) \approx 3.8$ bits (word-initial position)

Word-Level Entropy

We can also measure entropy at the word level. With a vocabulary of ~50,000 common words:

$$H_0^{\text{word}} = \log_2 50{,}000 \approx 15.6 \text{ bits/word}$$

But the actual word unigram entropy is ~11.8 bits/word (Zipf's law makes common words very frequent), and the entropy rate conditioned on context drops to approximately 7–10 bits/word for well-modeled text.

4. Redundancy of English

Redundancy

The redundancy $D$ of a source with entropy rate $h$ and maximum possible entropy $H_{\max}$ is:

$$D = 1 - \frac{h}{H_{\max}}$$

It measures the fraction of each symbol's capacity that is "wasted" on predictable structure rather than carrying new information.

For English with $h \approx 1.2$ bits/char and $H_{\max} = \log_2 27 \approx 4.76$ bits/char:

$$D = 1 - \frac{1.2}{4.76} \approx 0.75 = 75\%$$

English is approximately 75% redundant. Three-quarters of what we write is predictable from the remaining quarter.

Consequences of High Redundancy

Reading Degraded Text

You can easily read this:

"Th_ qu_ck br_wn f_x j_mps _ver th_ l_zy d_g"

With 30% of characters removed, the text remains perfectly intelligible. This is possible only because English is highly redundant — the missing characters are predictable from context.

Even more dramatically:

"Aoccdrnig to rscheearch, it deons't mttaer in waht oredr the ltteers in a wrod are."

Scrambled internal letters are readable because word recognition relies on just the first/last letters plus word shape — the rest is redundant.

Redundancy serves several critical functions:

  • Error correction: In noisy environments (crowded rooms, bad phone connections), redundancy allows listeners to reconstruct the message despite lost portions
  • Predictive processing: The brain exploits redundancy for fast, incremental comprehension — predicting upcoming words before they arrive
  • Compression opportunity: Compression algorithms like gzip exploit redundancy. English text compresses to roughly 1.5–2 bytes per character of the original

Redundancy Across Languages

Different languages have different redundancy levels, but all are substantially redundant. Languages with larger alphabets (like Chinese characters) have higher $H_{\max}$ but also higher per-character information, maintaining a roughly similar redundancy ratio. The redundancy appears to be tuned by evolutionary and cultural pressures to match the noise characteristics of the speech channel.

5. Cross-Entropy and Perplexity

Shannon's entropy tells us the fundamental limit of how well language can be modeled. Cross-entropy tells us how well a specific model actually does.

Cross-Entropy of a Language Model

Given a true distribution $P$ (the language) and a model distribution $Q$, the cross-entropy on a test sequence $w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_N$ is:

$$H(P, Q) = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \log_2 Q(w_i \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1})$$

This measures how many bits the model needs per word to encode the test data. Lower is better.

The fundamental inequality connects cross-entropy to entropy:

$$H(P) \leq H(P, Q)$$

with equality if and only if $Q = P$ — the model perfectly captures the language. The gap $H(P,Q) - H(P)$ is the KL divergence $D_{\text{KL}}(P \| Q)$, measuring how much worse the model is than optimal.

Perplexity

Perplexity is the exponential of cross-entropy:

$$\text{PP}(Q) = 2^{H(P, Q)} = 2^{-\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \log_2 Q(w_i \mid w_{1:i-1})}$$

Intuitively, perplexity is the effective vocabulary size — the number of equally likely words the model considers at each position. A perplexity of 100 means the model is, on average, as uncertain as if it were choosing uniformly among 100 words.

The Gap That Remains

Even the best language models have perplexity above 1 (cross-entropy above 0). Shannon's estimate suggests the entropy of English is ~7–10 bits/word (depending on measurement). Modern LLMs approach but haven't reached this limit. The remaining gap represents aspects of language that models still don't fully capture: world knowledge, common sense, pragmatic inference, and the full depth of human context.

6. The 39 Bits/Second Universal

One of the most striking findings in quantitative linguistics comes from Coupé et al. (2019), who measured the information rate of 17 typologically diverse languages.

The Trade-Off

Languages differ enormously in two dimensions:

  • Speech rate: Japanese averages ~7.8 syllables/sec, while Vietnamese averages ~5.2 syllables/sec
  • Information density: Vietnamese packs ~8.0 bits/syllable, while Japanese carries ~5.0 bits/syllable

But the product — the information rate — is remarkably constant:

$$R = \text{syllable rate} \times \text{bits per syllable} \approx 39 \text{ bits/sec}$$
LanguageSyllables/secBits/syllableBits/sec
Japanese7.845.0139.3
Spanish7.825.3641.9
French7.185.6440.5

Mandarin5.187.1837.2
Vietnamese5.228.0041.8
Thai4.987.3636.7

A Cognitive Bottleneck, Not an Articulatory One

The universality of ~39 bits/sec suggests that the bottleneck is cognitive, not articulatory. Our mouths could potentially produce syllables faster, and our languages could encode more per syllable, but the brain's capacity to formulate and comprehend messages is the limiting factor. This is consistent with the ~40 bits/sec limit observed across all conscious information processing modalities (see Tutorial 06).

Why Languages Compensate

This compensation mechanism works through multiple channels:

  • Phonological inventory: Languages with fewer distinct sounds (like Japanese with ~15 consonants) need more syllables per word, leading to faster speech rates
  • Morphological complexity: Agglutinative languages pack more information per syllable through complex morphology, allowing slower rates
  • Tonal distinctions: Tonal languages like Vietnamese and Mandarin multiply the information per syllable, enabling slower speaking rates

7. Entropy and Language Change

Languages evolve over centuries, and information-theoretic pressures play a role in shaping this evolution.

Phonological Mergers

When two phonemes merge (become indistinguishable), the language loses a distinction. This is tolerable only if the lost distinction was highly redundant — if context almost always disambiguates.

The cot-caught Merger

In many American English dialects, the vowels in "cot" and "caught" have merged. This is possible because:

  • The two vowels appeared in largely non-overlapping word sets
  • Context almost always disambiguates: "I caught a fish" vs "a baby cot"
  • The information loss is minimal: $\Delta H \approx 0$ because the distinction was already predictable from context

Mergers that would create too many ambiguities (high information loss) are resisted by the language.

Grammaticalization

High-frequency content words often become function words over time ("going to" → "gonna" → future tense marker). Information theory explains this: as a word becomes more predictable in context, it carries less information and can be phonetically reduced without information loss.

Regularization of Irregulars

Irregular forms (e.g., "holp" as past tense of "help") tend to regularize over time ("helped"), unless they are frequent enough that speakers memorize them. The information-theoretic explanation: infrequent irregular forms have high surprisal (the listener may not even recognize them), while regular forms are low-surprisal because the pattern is predictable. The few irregulars that survive (was, went, been) are among the most frequent words in the language.

Zipf's Principle of Least Effort

George Zipf proposed that language structure reflects a trade-off between the speaker's effort (who wants to minimize production effort) and the listener's effort (who wants maximum clarity). Information theory formalizes this: the optimal code minimizes the expected message length subject to maintaining decodability. Zipf's law for word frequencies — $f(r) \propto 1/r$ where $r$ is rank — is approximately what an entropy-maximizing code would produce.

The entropy of natural language sits at the intersection of information theory, cognitive science, and linguistics. Shannon's estimate of ~1.0–1.3 bits per character reveals English as a highly redundant, deeply structured code. This redundancy is not waste — it enables robust communication in noisy environments and efficient predictive processing in the brain. The cross-entropy framework gives us a principled way to evaluate language models, and the universal 39 bits/second finding suggests that human cognition, not language structure, is the ultimate bottleneck in communication.
