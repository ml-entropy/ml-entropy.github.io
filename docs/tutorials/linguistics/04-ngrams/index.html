N-gram Language Models

Language models are the foundation of modern NLP. Before neural networks dominated the field, n-gram models were the workhorses of speech recognition, machine translation, and text generation. They remain essential for understanding how statistical models capture linguistic structure — and they connect directly to information theory through perplexity and cross-entropy.

1. What is a Language Model?

A language model assigns probabilities to sequences of words. Given a sequence $w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_n$, the model computes:

$$P(w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_n)$$

Using the chain rule of probability, we can decompose this joint probability into a product of conditional probabilities:

Chain Rule Decomposition
$$P(w_1, \ldots, w_n) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(w_i \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1})$$

Each word is predicted given the entire preceding context. This is exact — no approximation has been made yet. The challenge is estimating each conditional probability.

This formulation is powerful but impractical: to compute $P(w_i \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1})$ for arbitrary-length histories, we would need to observe every possible prefix in our training data. For a vocabulary of size $|V|$, the number of possible histories of length $k$ is $|V|^k$ — an astronomical number even for modest $k$.

2. The Markov Assumption

Computing $P(w_i \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1})$ exactly requires too much data. The key insight of n-gram models is to truncate the history using the Markov assumption: approximate the full history with only the last $n-1$ words.

The N-gram Approximation
$$P(w_i \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1}) \approx P(w_i \mid w_{i-n+1}, \ldots, w_{i-1})$$

An n-gram model conditions on the previous $n-1$ words only.

This gives us a family of models with increasing context:

  • Unigram ($n=1$): $P(w_i)$ — no context at all. Each word is independent.
  • Bigram ($n=2$): $P(w_i \mid w_{i-1})$ — one word of context.
  • Trigram ($n=3$): $P(w_i \mid w_{i-2}, w_{i-1})$ — two words of context.
  • 4-gram, 5-gram, ...: $P(w_i \mid w_{i-n+1}, \ldots, w_{i-1})$ — diminishing returns beyond trigrams for most corpora.
Why "Markov"?

This is called the Markov assumption because the future (next word) depends only on a fixed window of the recent past, not the entire history. A bigram model is a first-order Markov chain over words; a trigram is second-order.

3. Estimating N-gram Probabilities (MLE)

The simplest way to estimate n-gram probabilities is maximum likelihood estimation (MLE): count occurrences in a training corpus and normalize.

Bigram MLE
$$P_{\text{MLE}}(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) = \frac{C(w_{i-1}, w_i)}{C(w_{i-1})}$$

where $C(w_{i-1}, w_i)$ is the count of the bigram $(w_{i-1}, w_i)$ in the training corpus, and $C(w_{i-1})$ is the count of the unigram $w_{i-1}$.

More generally, for an n-gram:

$$P_{\text{MLE}}(w_i \mid w_{i-n+1}, \ldots, w_{i-1}) = \frac{C(w_{i-n+1}, \ldots, w_i)}{C(w_{i-n+1}, \ldots, w_{i-1})}$$
Worked Example: Bigram Counts

Consider the tiny corpus (with sentence boundaries <s> and </s>):

<s> the cat sat </s>
<s> the cat ate </s>
<s> the dog sat </s>

Bigram counts:

Bigram Count Bigram Count
<s> the 3 cat sat 1
the cat 2 cat ate 1
the dog 1 dog sat 1
sat </s> 2 ate </s> 1

Computing probabilities:

$P(\text{cat} \mid \text{the}) = \frac{C(\text{the, cat})}{C(\text{the})} = \frac{2}{3} \approx 0.667$

$P(\text{dog} \mid \text{the}) = \frac{C(\text{the, dog})}{C(\text{the})} = \frac{1}{3} \approx 0.333$

$P(\text{sat} \mid \text{cat}) = \frac{C(\text{cat, sat})}{C(\text{cat})} = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5$

4. The Zero-Frequency Problem

MLE has a fatal flaw: many valid n-grams will never appear in the training data. If an n-gram has count 0, MLE assigns it probability 0:

$$C(w_{i-1}, w_i) = 0 \implies P_{\text{MLE}}(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) = 0$$

This is catastrophic. Because language model probabilities multiply together, a single zero-probability n-gram makes the probability of the entire sentence zero:

$$P(w_1, \ldots, w_n) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) = 0$$
How Severe is the Problem?

Consider a vocabulary of $|V| = 50{,}000$ words. The number of possible bigrams is $|V|^2 = 2.5 \times 10^9$. Even a corpus of 1 billion words will only contain a tiny fraction of these. For trigrams ($|V|^3 = 1.25 \times 10^{14}$), the situation is far worse. Most valid n-grams will have count zero regardless of corpus size.

5. Smoothing Techniques

Smoothing (also called discounting) addresses the zero-frequency problem by redistributing probability mass from seen n-grams to unseen ones.

Laplace (Add-1) Smoothing

The simplest approach: add 1 to every count. This ensures no n-gram has probability zero.

Laplace Smoothing
$$P_{\text{Laplace}}(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) = \frac{C(w_{i-1}, w_i) + 1}{C(w_{i-1}) + |V|}$$

where $|V|$ is the vocabulary size. The denominator adds $|V|$ to keep the distribution normalized: each of the $|V|$ possible next words gets +1, so the denominator increases by $|V|$.

Problem with Add-1

Laplace smoothing is too aggressive. It shifts far too much probability mass to unseen events. For a vocabulary of 50,000 words, adding 1 to every bigram count dramatically distorts the distribution. In practice, add-1 smoothing is almost never used for n-grams (though it works reasonably for text classification).

Add-k Smoothing

A natural refinement: add a fractional count $k$ instead of 1:

Add-k Smoothing
$$P_{\text{add-}k}(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) = \frac{C(w_{i-1}, w_i) + k}{C(w_{i-1}) + k|V|}$$

Typical values are $k = 0.5$, $k = 0.1$, or $k = 0.01$. The optimal $k$ can be tuned on a held-out development set.

Good-Turing Smoothing

Good-Turing smoothing uses a clever insight: re-estimate counts using the frequency of frequencies. Let $N_c$ be the number of n-grams that appear exactly $c$ times in the corpus.

Good-Turing Adjusted Count
$$c^* = (c+1) \frac{N_{c+1}}{N_c}$$

The adjusted count $c^*$ replaces the raw count $c$. Crucially, n-grams with count 0 get the adjusted count $c^* = N_1 / N_0$, where $N_1$ is the number of n-grams seen once (hapax legomena) and $N_0$ is the number of unseen n-grams.

Intuition Behind Good-Turing

Imagine fishing in a lake. You've caught 10 species of fish. The number of species you caught exactly once tells you something about how many species you haven't caught at all. If many species appear only once, there are likely many more unseen species. Good-Turing formalizes this intuition.

Kneser-Ney Smoothing

Kneser-Ney smoothing is widely considered the gold standard for n-gram models. It combines two ideas: absolute discounting and a clever backoff distribution based on continuation probability.

Kneser-Ney Smoothing
$$P_{\text{KN}}(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) = \frac{\max(C(w_{i-1}, w_i) - d, 0)}{C(w_{i-1})} + \lambda(w_{i-1})\, P_{\text{continuation}}(w_i)$$

where:

  • $d$ is the discount (typically $d \approx 0.75$), subtracted from each non-zero count
  • $\lambda(w_{i-1})$ is a normalizing factor that distributes the discounted mass
  • $P_{\text{continuation}}(w_i)$ is the continuation probability: the proportion of unique contexts in which $w_i$ appears
Why Continuation Probability?

Standard backoff uses the unigram probability $P(w_i)$ as the lower-order distribution. But this is wrong. Consider: "Francisco" has a high unigram count (because of "San Francisco"), but it should have low probability as a novel continuation — it almost always follows "San".

Continuation probability fixes this by asking: "In how many different contexts does $w_i$ appear?" A word like "the" appears after thousands of different words, so it gets high continuation probability. "Francisco" appears after essentially one word, so it gets low continuation probability.

6. Backoff vs Interpolation

When an n-gram has zero count (even after discounting), we need a strategy for falling back to lower-order models. There are two main approaches.

Backoff (Katz)

Backoff uses the highest-order n-gram that has a non-zero count. We only "back off" to a lower-order model when the higher-order count is zero:

$$P_{\text{backoff}}(w_i \mid w_{i-2}, w_{i-1}) = \begin{cases} P^*(w_i \mid w_{i-2}, w_{i-1}) & \text{if } C(w_{i-2}, w_{i-1}, w_i) > 0 \\ \alpha(w_{i-2}, w_{i-1}) \cdot P_{\text{backoff}}(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

where $\alpha$ is a backoff weight ensuring the distribution sums to 1.

Interpolation

Interpolation always combines all orders of n-gram, even when higher-order counts are available:

Linear Interpolation
$$P(w_i \mid w_{i-2}, w_{i-1}) = \lambda_3\, P_3(w_i \mid w_{i-2}, w_{i-1}) + \lambda_2\, P_2(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) + \lambda_1\, P_1(w_i)$$

where $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \lambda_3 = 1$ and all $\lambda_i \geq 0$. The weights are typically learned on a held-out set using the EM algorithm.

Backoff vs Interpolation in Practice

Interpolation generally outperforms backoff because it always benefits from lower-order statistics, even when higher-order counts are non-zero. Modified Kneser-Ney with interpolation is the standard choice and consistently achieves the best perplexity among n-gram methods.

7. Perplexity: Evaluating Language Models

How do we measure how good a language model is? The standard metric is perplexity — a direct connection to information theory.

Perplexity
$$\text{PP}(W) = P(w_1, \ldots, w_N)^{-1/N} = 2^{H(W)}$$

where $H(W)$ is the cross-entropy of the model on the test data. Lower perplexity means the model assigns higher probability to the test data — it is less "surprised" by what it sees.

The connection to entropy is direct. The cross-entropy is:

$$H(W) = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \log_2 P(w_i \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1})$$

Perplexity can be interpreted as the weighted average branching factor: if a model has perplexity 100, it is as uncertain as if it had to choose uniformly among 100 words at each step.

Example: Computing Perplexity

Suppose our bigram model assigns these probabilities to a test sentence "<s> the cat sat </s>":

$P(\text{the} \mid \text{<s>}) = 0.5$, $P(\text{cat} \mid \text{the}) = 0.4$, $P(\text{sat} \mid \text{cat}) = 0.3$, $P(\text{</s>} \mid \text{sat}) = 0.6$

Cross-entropy: $H = -\frac{1}{4}[\log_2(0.5) + \log_2(0.4) + \log_2(0.3) + \log_2(0.6)]$

$H = -\frac{1}{4}[-1.0 + (-1.322) + (-1.737) + (-0.737)] = -\frac{1}{4}(-4.796) = 1.199$

Perplexity: $\text{PP} = 2^{1.199} \approx 2.30$

Connection to Entropy

For a perfect model that matches the true distribution of language, the cross-entropy equals the true entropy of the language. Shannon estimated the entropy of English at roughly $1.0$–$1.5$ bits per character, or about $7$–$10$ bits per word. This means the best possible word-level perplexity for English is around $2^{7}$–$2^{10}$, i.e., roughly 128–1024. See Tutorial 05 for a deep dive.

8. Practical Considerations

Start and End Tokens

Every sentence is padded with special tokens: <s> (start) and </s> (end). This lets the model learn which words begin and end sentences. For a trigram model, we pad with two start tokens: <s> <s>.

Unknown Words

Words not in the vocabulary are mapped to a special <UNK> token. Common strategies: replace all words appearing fewer than $k$ times in training with <UNK>, or fix the vocabulary in advance and map out-of-vocabulary words.

Log Probabilities

In practice, we always work in log space to avoid numerical underflow. Multiplying many small probabilities produces numbers too small for floating-point representation. Instead:

$$\log P(w_1, \ldots, w_n) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log P(w_i \mid w_{i-n+1}, \ldots, w_{i-1})$$

9. From N-grams to Neural Language Models

N-gram models were the dominant approach to language modeling for decades (1980s–2010s). Their limitations motivated the development of neural language models:

Property N-gram Models Neural LMs
Representations Sparse (exact counts) Dense (learned embeddings)
Context Fixed window ($n-1$ words) Arbitrary length (RNNs, Transformers)
Generalization No sharing between similar words Similar words share representations
Memory Grows exponentially with $n$ Fixed model size
Training Simple counting Gradient descent (expensive)
N-grams Are Not Dead

Despite the dominance of neural models, n-grams remain widely used in practice: speech recognition (as a decoding-time language model), keyboard prediction (on-device, low latency), spelling correction, and as baselines for evaluating new models. Their simplicity, speed, and interpretability are genuine advantages.

10. Summary

N-gram language models are the simplest approach to the fundamental problem of assigning probabilities to word sequences. They make language modeling tractable through the Markov assumption, but this creates the zero-frequency problem that demands smoothing.

Key Takeaways
  • Language models assign $P(w_1, \ldots, w_n)$ using the chain rule
  • The Markov assumption truncates history to $n-1$ words
  • MLE estimates probabilities by counting, but assigns zero to unseen n-grams
  • Smoothing redistributes mass: Laplace (too aggressive), Good-Turing (frequency of frequencies), Kneser-Ney (gold standard)
  • Interpolation outperforms backoff by always combining all n-gram orders
  • Perplexity $= 2^{H(W)}$ measures model quality via cross-entropy
  • N-grams are limited by sparse counts and fixed context, motivating neural language models
