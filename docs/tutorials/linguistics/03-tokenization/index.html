Tokenization & Subword Models

Before a language model can process text, it must answer a deceptively simple question: what is a "word"? The answer turns out to be surprisingly deep. This tutorial walks through the evolution from naive whitespace splitting to the subword algorithms that power every modern LLM — and reveals the information-theoretic principles hiding beneath the surface.

Prerequisites

Familiarity with basic probability and the concept of entropy ($H = -\sum p_i \log p_i$) is helpful. See Tutorial 01: Levels of Linguistic Analysis for background on morphology and Tutorial 02: Writing Systems & Unicode for how text is encoded in bytes.

1. Why Tokenization Matters

Tokenization is the first step in any NLP pipeline. It converts a raw string of characters into a sequence of discrete tokens — the atomic units that a model sees, embeds, and predicts. Every architectural decision downstream depends on this choice.

The Vocabulary Problem

English has over 170,000 word forms in common use. Most are rare. If we treat each unique word as a token, we face:

  • Sparsity: Most words appear only a handful of times in any corpus
  • No parameter sharing: "walk", "walks", "walking", "walked" get separate embeddings despite shared meaning
  • OOV (out-of-vocabulary): New words like "COVID-19" or "ChatGPT" cannot be represented at all

The distribution of words follows Zipf's law: the $r$-th most frequent word has frequency proportional to $1/r$. This means a few words dominate (the, of, and...) while the vast majority are extremely rare:

$$f(r) \propto \frac{1}{r^{\alpha}}, \quad \alpha \approx 1$$

In a corpus of 1 billion words, roughly half of all unique word types appear only once (these are called hapax legomena). No amount of data fully solves the long tail.

The OOV Problem in Practice

A model trained on pre-2020 text has never seen "COVID-19", "mRNA", or "Omicron". With word-level tokenization, these all map to a single <UNK> token, destroying all information. With subword tokenization, "COVID-19" might become ["CO", "VID", "-", "19"] — not perfect, but recoverable.

2. Word-Level Tokenization

The simplest approach: split text on whitespace and punctuation. 

# Naive whitespace tokenization
text = "Don't rock-climb in New York!"
tokens = text.split()
# ["Don't", "rock-climb", "in", "New", "York!"]
# Problems: "Don't" should be "Do" + "n't"?
#           "rock-climb" is one concept or two?
#           "New York" is one entity split into two tokens
#           "York!" has punctuation attached

Rule-based tokenizers like the Penn Treebank tokenizer apply language-specific rules: split contractions ("don't" → "do" + "n't"), separate punctuation, handle abbreviations. But these rules are brittle and language-dependent.

Languages Without Spaces

Chinese, Japanese, and Thai have no whitespace between words. Tokenization requires dedicated segmentation algorithms: jieba (Chinese), MeCab (Japanese), ICU (Thai/Khmer/Myanmar). These use dictionaries and statistical models to find word boundaries — a much harder problem than splitting on spaces.

The fundamental limitation of word-level tokenization is the vocabulary size. With $|V| = 100{,}000$ words, the embedding matrix alone has $|V| \times d$ parameters (e.g., 100K × 768 = 76.8M parameters just for embeddings). And there is zero parameter sharing between morphological variants.

3. Character-Level Tokenization

The opposite extreme: make each character a token. The vocabulary becomes tiny — roughly 100–300 tokens for most languages (letters, digits, punctuation, common symbols). 

# Character-level tokenization
text = "cat"
tokens = list(text)
# ["c", "a", "t"]

# No OOV problem! Any string can be represented.
# But sequences are very long:
text = "The quick brown fox"
tokens = list(text)
# ["T","h","e"," ","q","u","i","c","k"," ","b","r","o","w","n"," ","f","o","x"]
# 19 tokens instead of 4 words
Character-Level Trade-offs
Pros Cons
No OOV tokens ever Very long sequences (5–7x longer)
Tiny vocabulary (~100–300) Hard to capture word-level semantics
Works for any language Quadratic attention cost in Transformers
Handles typos and neologisms Low entropy per token

From an information-theoretic perspective: the entropy per character in English is about $H \approx 1.3$ bits/character (Shannon, 1951), while the entropy per word is about $H \approx 11$ bits/word. The total information is the same — you just spread it across more tokens. But for models with fixed context windows, longer sequences mean less content per forward pass.

4. Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) — The Breakthrough

Byte Pair Encoding (Sennrich et al., 2016; originally a data compression algorithm by Gage, 1994) finds a middle ground between words and characters. The idea is elegant: start with characters, then iteratively merge the most frequent adjacent pair.

BPE Algorithm
  1. Initialize vocabulary with all individual characters (plus an end-of-word symbol)
  2. Count the frequency of every adjacent symbol pair in the corpus
  3. Merge the most frequent pair into a single new symbol; add it to the vocabulary
  4. Repeat steps 2–3 for $K$ merges (hyperparameter: desired vocabulary size)
Worked Example: BPE on a Tiny Corpus

Corpus (with word frequencies): "low" (5), "lower" (2), "lowest" (1), "newer" (6), "wider" (3)

Initial vocabulary: {l, o, w, e, r, s, t, n, i, d, </w>}

Step 0 (character-split with frequencies):
  l o w </w>       (5)
  l o w e r </w>   (2)
  l o w e s t </w> (1)
  n e w e r </w>   (6)
  w i d e r </w>   (3)

Step 1: Most frequent pair = (e, r) count=11
  Merge "e"+"r" -> "er"
  l o w </w>       (5)
  l o w er </w>    (2)
  l o w e s t </w> (1)
  n e w er </w>    (6)
  w i d er </w>    (3)

Step 2: Most frequent pair = (er, </w>) count=11
  Merge "er"+"</w>" -> "er</w>"
  l o w </w>       (5)
  l o w er</w>     (2)
  l o w e s t </w> (1)
  n e w er</w>     (6)
  w i d er</w>     (3)

Step 3: Most frequent pair = (l, o) count=8
  Merge "l"+"o" -> "lo"
  lo w </w>        (5)
  lo w er</w>      (2)
  lo w e s t </w>  (1)
  n e w er</w>     (6)
  w i d er</w>     (3)

Step 4: Most frequent pair = (lo, w) count=8
  Merge "lo"+"w" -> "low"
  low </w>         (5)
  low er</w>       (2)
  low e s t </w>   (1)
  n e w er</w>     (6)
  w i d er</w>     (3)

... and so on.

After $K$ merges, common words like "low" become single tokens, while rare words are split into known subword pieces. At inference time, we apply the same merge rules in the same priority order.

BPE has a deep connection to information theory. By merging frequent pairs, it implicitly performs a form of minimum description length (MDL) compression. The total description length of the corpus is:

$$L = |V| \cdot C_{\text{vocab}} + \sum_{i} |T(x_i)| \cdot C_{\text{token}}$$

where $|V|$ is the vocabulary size (cost of storing the codebook), $|T(x_i)|$ is the number of tokens in the $i$-th sequence (cost of encoding the data), and $C_{\text{vocab}}$, $C_{\text{token}}$ are the per-entry costs. BPE greedily reduces the second term while incrementally growing the first.

Connection to Entropy

BPE discovers frequent substrings — these correspond to character sequences with high mutual information at their boundaries. When "e" and "r" almost always appear together, the mutual information $I(e; r)$ is high, and merging them into "er" reduces the entropy of the token sequence. BPE is, in effect, a greedy algorithm for finding entropy-optimal subword units.

5. WordPiece (Google / BERT)

WordPiece (Schuster & Nakajima, 2012) looks similar to BPE but makes a crucial change: instead of merging the most frequent pair, it merges the pair that maximizes the likelihood of the training data under a unigram language model.

The merge score for a candidate pair $(a, b)$ is:

$$\text{score}(a, b) = \frac{P(ab)}{P(a) \cdot P(b)}$$
This is Pointwise Mutual Information!

The WordPiece merge criterion is exactly the pointwise mutual information (PMI) of the two tokens:

$$\text{PMI}(a, b) = \log \frac{P(ab)}{P(a) \cdot P(b)}$$

High PMI means $a$ and $b$ co-occur much more often than chance would predict — they "want" to be a single unit. WordPiece merges the pair with the highest PMI at each step. This is more principled than raw frequency: a rare but highly cohesive pair (like "qu") may be merged before a frequent but independent pair.

WordPiece uses a special ## prefix to mark continuation tokens (tokens that are not at the start of a word): 

# WordPiece tokenization (BERT-style)
"unbelievable" -> ["un", "##believ", "##able"]
"tokenization" -> ["token", "##ization"]
"COVID"        -> ["CO", "##VI", "##D"]

WordPiece is used in BERT, DistilBERT, ELECTRA, and other models in the BERT family. BERT's vocabulary has 30,522 tokens.

6. Unigram Language Model (SentencePiece)

The Unigram model (Kudo, 2018) takes the opposite approach to BPE. Instead of starting small and building up, it starts with a large vocabulary and prunes down.

Unigram Model

Assume each subword token $x_i$ is generated independently with probability $P(x_i)$. The probability of a segmentation $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$ of a sentence is:

$$P(\mathbf{x}) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i)$$

The algorithm uses the EM algorithm to estimate the probabilities $P(x_i)$ for each candidate subword, then prunes the vocabulary by removing tokens whose removal causes the smallest increase in corpus likelihood.

Algorithm outline:

  1. Start with a large seed vocabulary (e.g., all substrings up to a maximum length, or all frequent substrings)
  2. E-step: For each sentence, find the best segmentation using the Viterbi algorithm (dynamic programming to find the highest-probability tokenization)
  3. M-step: Re-estimate token probabilities from the segmented corpus
  4. Compute the loss (negative log-likelihood) for each token if it were removed
  5. Remove the bottom $p\%$ of tokens (those whose removal hurts least)
  6. Repeat until vocabulary reaches the target size $K$
# Viterbi segmentation for Unigram model
# Given token probabilities, find best segmentation of "unbelievable"
#
# Dynamic programming: for each position j, find
#   best_score[j] = max over all tokens ending at j
#                   of best_score[start] + log P(token)
#
# Example (simplified log-probabilities):
# P("un") = -2.1,  P("un##b") = -5.3
# P("believe") = -3.2, P("believ") = -3.8
# P("able") = -2.5, P("##able") = -2.9
#
# Best path: "un" + "believe" + "able" = -2.1 + -3.2 + -2.5 = -7.8
# vs.        "un" + "believ"  + "able" = -2.1 + -3.8 + -2.5 = -8.4
# Winner: "un" + "believe" + "able"
Advantage of Unigram

Unlike BPE (which produces a single deterministic segmentation), the Unigram model can assign probabilities to different segmentations. This allows subword regularization — sampling different segmentations during training to improve robustness. For example, "international" might be tokenized as ["inter", "national"] or ["in", "ter", "national"] with different probabilities.

The Unigram model is used in T5, ALBERT, XLNet, and mBART.

7. SentencePiece: Language-Agnostic Tokenization

SentencePiece (Kudo & Richardson, 2018) is not a new algorithm — it is a framework that implements both BPE and Unigram. Its key innovation is treating the input as a raw byte stream with no language-specific pre-processing.

Key Design Principles
  • No pre-tokenization: No whitespace splitting, no language-specific rules. The raw Unicode string is the input.
  • Whitespace as a character: Spaces are treated like any other character, represented by the special symbol ▁ (U+2581, "Lower One Eighth Block")
  • Reversible: You can perfectly reconstruct the original text from tokens (including spaces)
  • Language-agnostic: Works identically for English, Chinese, Arabic, or any language
# SentencePiece tokenization
import sentencepiece as spm

sp = spm.SentencePieceProcessor()
sp.load('model.model')

tokens = sp.encode_as_pieces("Hello world")
# ['▁Hello', '▁world']
# Note: ▁ marks word boundaries (start of word after space)

tokens = sp.encode_as_pieces("New York is great")
# ['▁New', '▁York', '▁is', '▁great']

# Detokenization is lossless:
text = sp.decode_pieces(tokens)
# "New York is great"  (original string perfectly recovered)

The ▁ prefix convention is crucial: it encodes whitespace information within the tokens, so the tokenization is fully reversible. Without it, we could not distinguish "New York" from "Newyork" after tokenization.

8. Byte-Level BPE (GPT-2 / GPT-4)

Byte-level BPE (Radford et al., 2019) pushes the idea further: instead of starting from Unicode characters, start from the 256 raw bytes. This guarantees that any byte sequence can be tokenized — there are literally no unknown tokens.

Byte-Level BPE

Base vocabulary: 256 byte values (0x00 through 0xFF)

Merge process: Same as standard BPE, but operating on bytes instead of characters

Key property: Any UTF-8 encoded string is a valid byte sequence, so every possible input can be tokenized without any <UNK> token. 

# Byte-level BPE (GPT-2 style)
from transformers import GPT2Tokenizer

tok = GPT2Tokenizer.from_pretrained("gpt2")

# English: efficient encoding
tok.tokenize("Hello world")
# ['Hello', ' world']  (2 tokens)

# Chinese: less efficient (UTF-8 = 3 bytes per character)
tok.tokenize("你好世界")
# Multiple byte-level tokens per character

# Emoji: works! (UTF-8 = 4 bytes)
tok.tokenize("🎉")
# Byte-level tokens that represent the UTF-8 encoding

# Code: works for any programming language
tok.tokenize("def foo(x):")
# ['def', ' foo', '(', 'x', '):']
The Multi-Byte Trade-off

Byte-level BPE is universal but not equally efficient for all scripts. ASCII characters (Latin alphabet, digits) are 1 byte each, so they tend to form large merged tokens quickly. CJK characters are 3 bytes in UTF-8, and emoji are 4 bytes, so they require more tokens for the same semantic content. This creates an inherent bias toward Latin-script languages in token efficiency — a significant fairness consideration.

Byte-level BPE is used in GPT-2, GPT-3, GPT-4, LLaMA, and Mistral.

9. How Many Tokens? Vocabulary Size Trade-offs

The vocabulary size $|V|$ is a critical hyperparameter. It controls the trade-off between sequence length and embedding sparsity:

Vocab Size Characteristics Example
~256 Byte-level. Very long sequences. No compression. ByT5
~1K Near-character-level. Long sequences, slow inference. CANINE
~32K Good balance. Standard for most modern LLMs. LLaMA (32K), BERT (30K)
~50K Slightly shorter sequences. GPT-2's choice. GPT-2 (50,257)
~100K+ Short sequences but large embedding table. Better multilingual coverage. GPT-4 (100K), Gemini (256K)
The Entropy-Optimal Vocabulary

From an information-theoretic perspective, the optimal vocabulary minimizes the total description length:

$$L_{\text{total}} = \underbrace{|V| \cdot \log|V|}_{\text{codebook cost}} + \underbrace{N_{\text{tokens}} \cdot H_{\text{tokens}}}_{\text{encoding cost}}$$

Increasing $|V|$ decreases $N_{\text{tokens}}$ (shorter sequences) and increases $H_{\text{tokens}}$ (more entropy per token since tokens carry more information), but increases the codebook cost. The optimum depends on corpus size, language diversity, and downstream task. In practice, 32K–100K tokens is the sweet spot for large-scale language models.

10. Tokenization Artifacts and Gotchas

Tokenization is deterministic but not intuitive. The boundaries it creates can have surprising effects on model behavior.

Morphological Boundaries vs. Token Boundaries
# GPT-2 tokenizer
"Unsupervised"  -> ["Uns", "uper", "vised"]    # NOT "Un" + "supervised"!
"supervised"    -> ["super", "vised"]
"Supervised"    -> ["Super", "vised"]

# The prefix "Un-" is split across token boundaries.
# The model cannot easily learn that "Un-" negates the meaning.

BPE merges are learned from corpus statistics, not from morphological rules. The segmentation of "Unsupervised" differs from "unsupervised" because capital "U" changes the byte pair frequencies. This is a fundamental limitation: tokenization is morphology-unaware.

Leading Spaces Matter
# In many tokenizers, " hello" and "hello" produce DIFFERENT tokens
tokenize(" hello")  # [" hello"]  -- with leading space, single token
tokenize("hello")   # ["hello"]   -- different token ID!

# This means the model sees a different embedding depending on
# whether a word appears at the start of text or after a space.
Number Tokenization
# Numbers are tokenized inconsistently
tokenize("1000")    # ["1000"]           -- single token (common number)
tokenize("1729")    # ["17", "29"]       -- two tokens (rarer number)
tokenize("123456")  # ["123", "456"]     -- split at arbitrary point

# This makes arithmetic very difficult for LLMs:
# "1729" is not seen as a single number but as "17" next to "29"
# The model must learn that 17*100 + 29 = 1729
Code Tokenization
# Python indentation: 4 spaces might be 1, 2, or 4 tokens
tokenize("    x = 1")   # ["    ", "x", " =", " 1"]  or
                         # ["   ", " x", " =", " 1"]  depending on tokenizer

# This is why some models (Codex, CodeLlama) train tokenizers
# specifically on code corpora, where indentation patterns
# are merged into efficient single tokens.
Glitch Tokens

"Glitch tokens" are vocabulary entries that exist in the tokenizer but were barely seen during model training. They arise when the tokenizer is trained on a different (or larger) corpus than the model. Because the corresponding embedding is essentially random (never updated by gradient descent), feeding these tokens to the model can produce bizarre outputs. The most famous example is GPT-2's " SolidGoldMagikarp" token — a Reddit username that was frequent enough to earn its own BPE merge but rare enough in the training data that the model never learned a meaningful embedding for it.

The Big Picture

Tokenization is not just preprocessing — it is a lossy compression step that determines what information the model can access. The evolution from word → character → subword tokenization mirrors a central idea from information theory: the best encoding is the one that matches the statistical structure of the source. BPE, WordPiece, and Unigram are all different algorithms for discovering that structure, and they all converge on the same insight: the right units are not words, not characters, but something in between — determined by the data.
