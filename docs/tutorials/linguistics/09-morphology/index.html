Morphological Analysis & FSTs

Words are not atoms. They have internal structure — prefixes, roots, suffixes, infixes — that carries meaning and grammatical information. Morphology, the study of word structure, is essential for processing languages like Turkish, Finnish, or Arabic where a single word can encode what English requires an entire clause to express. The computational tool of choice for morphological analysis is the finite-state transducer (FST), which maps between surface forms and their underlying analyses.

1. What is Morphology?

Morpheme

A morpheme is the smallest meaningful unit of language. Morphemes come in two types:

  • Free morphemes: Can stand alone as words (cat, run, happy)
  • Bound morphemes: Must attach to another morpheme (un-, -ing, -ness, -s)

2. Inflection vs Derivation

Inflection

Inflection modifies a word's grammatical properties without changing its core meaning or part of speech:

ProcessExampleFeature Changed
Pluralcat → catsNumber
Past tensewalk → walkedTense
3rd personwalk → walksPerson/Number
Comparativetall → tallerDegree
Case (Russian)dom → domaCase (genitive)

Derivation

Derivation creates new words, often changing the part of speech:

ProcessExampleCategory Change
-nesshappy (Adj) → happiness (N)Adj → Noun
-izemodern (Adj) → modernize (V)Adj → Verb
un-happy (Adj) → unhappy (Adj)Adj → Adj (negation)
-erteach (V) → teacher (N)Verb → Noun
-lyquick (Adj) → quickly (Adv)Adj → Adverb

Compounding

Compounding combines free morphemes into new words: blackbird, toothpaste, software, firefighter. German is famous for productive compounding: Donaudampfschifffahrtsgesellschaft (Danube steamship transport company).

Ordering: Derivation Before Inflection

A near-universal rule: derivational morphology applies before inflectional morphology. We say "modernizations" (derive first, inflect last), never *"modernsization". This is because derivation creates new lexemes, while inflection creates forms of existing lexemes.

3. Morphological Typology

Languages differ dramatically in how much information they pack into single words. Linguists classify languages along a spectrum:

TypeCharacteristicsExamples
IsolatingOne morpheme per word, little or no affixationMandarin, Vietnamese, Thai
AgglutinativeMany morphemes per word, each clearly segmentable with one meaningTurkish, Finnish, Swahili, Japanese
FusionalAffixes encode multiple features simultaneously, boundaries often unclearRussian, Spanish, Latin, Arabic
PolysyntheticEntire sentences expressed as single complex wordsInuktitut, Mohawk, Yupik

The Same Meaning Across Types

English (analytic): "I will not be able to go"

Turkish (agglutinative): "gid-e-me-yecek-im" (go-ABIL-NEG-FUT-1SG)

Inuktitut (polysynthetic): "tusaa-tsia-runna-nngit-tu-alu-u-junga" (hear-well-can-NEG-DOER-very-be-1SG) = "I can't hear very well"

The morpheme-to-word ratio varies from ~1:1 (English) to ~8:1 (Inuktitut), but the information per sentence is comparable.

Information-Theoretic View

From an entropy perspective, isolating languages have high per-word entropy (each word is relatively unpredictable) but low word complexity. Agglutinative languages have lower per-word entropy (the morphological structure is predictable) but vastly more unique word forms. The entropy rate per unit of meaning transmitted is roughly constant across types — another manifestation of the 39 bits/second universal (Tutorial 05).

4. Stemming and Lemmatization

Reducing words to their base forms is a fundamental preprocessing step in NLP.

Stemming

Stemming applies crude rules to strip affixes, producing a stem that may not be a real word:

Porter Stemmer (Selected Rules)

Step 1a: SSES → SS, IES → I, SS → SS, S → ε
Step 1b: (m>0) EED → EE, (*v*) ED → ε, (*v*) ING → ε
Step 2: (m>0) ATIONAL → ATE, IZATION → IZE, ...

where (m>0) means the stem must have at least one vowel-consonant sequence, and (*v*) means the stem must contain a vowel.

Stemming Examples

WordPorter StemCorrect?
runningrunYes
generalizationgenerAggressive
universityuniversOver-stemmed
organizationorganOver-stemmed (conflates with "organ")
betterbetterUnder-stemmed (should → good)

Lemmatization

Lemmatization uses a dictionary and morphological analysis to return the lemma (dictionary form):

  • "better" → "good" (requires knowing it's the comparative of "good")
  • "went" → "go" (requires an irregular form table)
  • "am", "are", "is", "was", "were" → "be"
  • "mice" → "mouse"

Lemmatization is more accurate but slower and requires language-specific resources.

5. Finite-State Transducers

The computational workhorse of morphological analysis is the finite-state transducer (FST) — a finite automaton that reads an input tape and writes to an output tape.

Finite-State Transducer

An FST is a 6-tuple $(Q, \Sigma, \Gamma, \delta, q_0, F)$ where:

  • $Q$: finite set of states
  • $\Sigma$: input alphabet
  • $\Gamma$: output alphabet
  • $\delta \subseteq Q \times (\Sigma \cup \{\epsilon\}) \times (\Gamma \cup \{\epsilon\}) \times Q$: transition relation
  • $q_0 \in Q$: start state
  • $F \subseteq Q$: accepting states

Each transition reads a symbol from the input and writes a symbol to the output (either may be $\epsilon$).

Morphological Analysis as Transduction

A morphological analyzer maps surface forms to their analyses:

FST Morphological Analysis

Input (surface)Output (analysis)
catscat+N+PL
runningrun+V+PROG
geesegoose+N+PL
biggerbig+ADJ+COMP
foxesfox+N+PL

The FST handles regular patterns (cat+s) and irregular patterns (goose→geese) through different paths in the automaton.

Composition

A key advantage of FSTs is composition: if FST $T_1$ maps $A \to B$ and FST $T_2$ maps $B \to C$, then $T_1 \circ T_2$ maps $A \to C$. This allows building complex morphological systems by composing simple components:

$$\text{Lexicon} \circ \text{Rules}_1 \circ \text{Rules}_2 \circ \cdots \circ \text{Rules}_n = \text{Analyzer}$$

For example: a lexicon FST maps stems to feature bundles, a plural rule FST adds "-s" for regular nouns, and an orthographic rule FST handles spelling changes (e.g., "fox" + "s" → "foxes" with epenthetic 'e').

6. Two-Level Morphology

Koskenniemi's (1983) two-level model represents morphological processes as parallel rules relating a lexical level (underlying form) to a surface level (actual spelling).

Two-Level Rules

Each rule specifies a correspondence between lexical and surface characters in a particular context. Rule types:

  • $a$:$b$ ⇒ $L$ _ $R$ — $a$ must surface as $b$ in context $L$_$R$ (obligatory)
  • $a$:$b$ ⇐ $L$ _ $R$ — $b$ on surface implies $a$ in lexical form in this context
  • $a$:$b$ ⇔ $L$ _ $R$ — both directions (if and only if)

English Consonant Doubling

When adding "-ing" to "run," the 'n' doubles: "running" (not *"runing").

Lexical: r u n + i n g

Surface: r u n n i n g

The two-level rule: $+$:$n$ ⇔ $[C]$ _ $[V]$ (the morpheme boundary '+' surfaces as a copy of the preceding consonant before a vowel-initial suffix, when the stem has the pattern CVC with a short vowel).

Similarly:

  • stop + ing → stopping (p doubles)
  • sit + ing → sitting (t doubles)
  • jump + ing → jumping (no doubling: mp is a cluster, not single C)
  • read + ing → reading (no doubling: ea is a long vowel)

Each two-level rule compiles into an FST. The complete morphological system is the intersection of all rule transducers — a string is well-formed only if it satisfies all rules simultaneously.

Intersection vs Composition

Two-level rules apply in parallel (intersection), not sequentially (composition). This is a key theoretical distinction: in the ordered-rule approach, rules can feed or bleed each other; in the two-level approach, all rules must be simultaneously satisfied. Both approaches have been shown to be equivalent in expressive power for morphological phenomena.

7. Morphological Complexity Across Languages

Languages vary enormously in morphological complexity, and this has profound implications for NLP.

Type-Token Ratio

The type-token ratio (TTR) measures vocabulary diversity: $\text{TTR} = |V| / N$ where $|V|$ is the number of unique word types and $N$ is the total number of tokens. Morphologically rich languages have much higher TTRs:

LanguageTTR (per 10K tokens)Type
Mandarin~0.15Isolating
English~0.35Weakly fusional
Russian~0.50Fusional
Turkish~0.65Agglutinative
Finnish~0.70Agglutinative

Implications for NLP

Why Turkish NLP Needs Morphology

A Turkish verb can have thousands of inflected forms:

  • "gel" (come)
  • "geliyorum" (I am coming)
  • "gelebilecekmiydim" (was I going to be able to come?)
  • "gelememekten" (from not being able to come)

A word-level language model for Turkish would need an impossibly large vocabulary. Morphological analysis or subword tokenization (BPE, see Tutorial 03) is essential.

Entropy of Word Forms

The entropy of the word form distribution reflects morphological complexity:

$$H(\text{words}) = -\sum_{w \in V} P(w) \log_2 P(w)$$

For a corpus of equal size:

Language$H(\text{words})$ (bits)Vocabulary Size (100K tokens)
English~11.8~8,000 types
Spanish~12.5~12,000 types
Russian~13.4~22,000 types
Turkish~14.6~35,000 types
Finnish~15.1~42,000 types

Higher morphological complexity means higher word-level entropy, more data sparsity, and greater need for morphological decomposition or subword methods.

Connection to Subword Tokenization

BPE and other subword tokenizers (Tutorial 03) can be seen as data-driven morphological analyzers. They discover morpheme-like units from raw text, often recovering linguistic morphemes: BPE trained on English frequently learns "un", "ing", "tion", "ment" as subword units. For agglutinative languages, subword tokenization is even more critical, as it provides a consistent way to handle the combinatorial explosion of word forms without language-specific morphological rules.

Morphology sits at the intersection of lexicon and grammar. Understanding word-internal structure is essential for processing the world's languages, most of which are morphologically richer than English. Finite-state transducers provide an elegant and efficient computational framework, while modern subword methods offer a data-driven alternative. Both approaches grapple with the same fundamental challenge: how to handle the vast space of possible word forms while capturing the systematic patterns that make morphology learnable.
