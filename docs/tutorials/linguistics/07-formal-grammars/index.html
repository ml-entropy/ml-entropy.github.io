Formal Grammars & the Chomsky Hierarchy

At the heart of computational linguistics lies a fundamental question: what mathematical machinery is needed to describe the structure of human language? Noam Chomsky's hierarchy of formal grammars provides a framework for answering this question, classifying languages by the computational power required to generate them. This classification has profound implications for parsing algorithms, language modeling, and our understanding of what makes human language unique.

1. What is a Formal Grammar?

Formal Grammar

A formal grammar is a 4-tuple $G = (V, \Sigma, R, S)$ where:

  • $V$ is a finite set of non-terminal symbols (variables)
  • $\Sigma$ is a finite set of terminal symbols (the alphabet), disjoint from $V$
  • $R$ is a finite set of production rules of the form $\alpha \to \beta$
  • $S \in V$ is the start symbol

The language $L(G)$ generated by $G$ is the set of all strings of terminal symbols that can be derived from $S$ by repeated application of rules in $R$.

A Simple English Fragment

Consider the grammar $G$ with:

  • $V = \{S, NP, VP, Det, N, V\}$
  • $\Sigma = \{\text{the, a, cat, dog, chased, saw}\}$
  • Rules:
    $S \to NP \; VP$
    $NP \to Det \; N$
    $VP \to V \; NP$
    $Det \to \text{the} \mid \text{a}$
    $N \to \text{cat} \mid \text{dog}$
    $V \to \text{chased} \mid \text{saw}$

Derivation of "the cat chased a dog":

$S \Rightarrow NP \; VP \Rightarrow Det \; N \; VP \Rightarrow \text{the} \; N \; VP \Rightarrow \text{the cat} \; VP \Rightarrow \text{the cat} \; V \; NP \Rightarrow \text{the cat chased} \; NP \Rightarrow \text{the cat chased} \; Det \; N \Rightarrow \text{the cat chased a dog}$

Each step in the derivation replaces a non-terminal with its expansion according to some rule. The sequence of rule applications can be represented as a parse tree, which captures the hierarchical structure of the sentence.

2. The Chomsky Hierarchy

Chomsky (1956, 1959) classified grammars into four types based on the form of their production rules. Each type corresponds to a class of languages and a class of automata:

TypeGrammarRule FormAutomatonExample
3Regular$A \to aB$ or $A \to a$Finite automaton$a^*b^*$
2Context-free$A \to \gamma$Pushdown automaton$a^n b^n$
1Context-sensitive$\alpha A \beta \to \alpha \gamma \beta$Linear-bounded automaton$a^n b^n c^n$
0Unrestricted$\alpha \to \beta$ (any)Turing machineHalting problem complement

The hierarchy is strictly nested: every regular language is context-free, every context-free language is context-sensitive, and every context-sensitive language is recursively enumerable. Each level adds expressive power at the cost of computational complexity.

3. Regular Grammars (Type 3)

Regular grammars are the simplest class. Their production rules have the form $A \to aB$ (right-linear) or $A \to a$, where $A, B$ are non-terminals and $a$ is a terminal.

Regular Languages

A language is regular if and only if it can be:

  • Generated by a regular grammar
  • Recognized by a finite automaton (DFA or NFA)
  • Described by a regular expression

All three characterizations are equivalent (Kleene's theorem).

What Regular Grammars Can Describe

  • Simple morphological patterns: English plural formation (consonant + "s")
  • Phonotactic constraints: allowable consonant clusters
  • Simple token patterns: email addresses, phone numbers

What They Cannot

Regular languages cannot capture nested dependencies. The classic example:

The Language $a^n b^n$ is Not Regular

The language $\{a^n b^n \mid n \geq 1\} = \{ab, aabb, aaabbb, \ldots\}$ requires "counting" — matching the number of $a$'s to the number of $b$'s. No finite automaton can do this because it has finitely many states but must remember an arbitrarily large count.

Linguistic analog: Center-embedding in English:

"The cat [the dog [the rat bit] chased] ran."

Each noun phrase opened must be closed by a matching verb phrase — a nested dependency that regular grammars cannot capture.

4. Context-Free Grammars (Type 2)

Context-free grammars (CFGs) are the workhorse of computational syntax. Every production rule has the form $A \to \gamma$, where $A$ is a single non-terminal and $\gamma$ is any string of terminals and non-terminals.

Context-Free Grammar

A grammar $G = (V, \Sigma, R, S)$ is context-free if every rule in $R$ has the form:

$$A \to \gamma, \quad A \in V, \quad \gamma \in (V \cup \Sigma)^*$$

The key property: the left-hand side is always a single non-terminal. The replacement does not depend on the context (surrounding symbols) of $A$.

CFGs for Natural Language Syntax

A more elaborate English fragment:

An English CFG Fragment

$S \to NP \; VP$
$NP \to Det \; N \mid Det \; N \; PP \mid NP \; \text{and} \; NP$
$VP \to V \; NP \mid V \; NP \; PP \mid V \; S'$
$PP \to P \; NP$
$S' \to \text{that} \; S$
$Det \to \text{the} \mid \text{a} \mid \text{every}$
$N \to \text{man} \mid \text{telescope} \mid \text{hill}$
$V \to \text{saw} \mid \text{said} \mid \text{believed}$
$P \to \text{on} \mid \text{with} \mid \text{in}$

Structural Ambiguity

A defining feature of natural language is structural ambiguity — a single sentence can have multiple valid parse trees.

PP Attachment Ambiguity

"I saw the man with the telescope."

Parse 1 (VP attachment): I used the telescope to see the man

$VP \to V \; NP \; PP$: [saw [the man] [with the telescope]]

Parse 2 (NP attachment): The man had the telescope

$NP \to Det \; N \; PP$: [saw [the man [with the telescope]]]

The CFG generates both parses. Disambiguation requires semantic knowledge or probabilistic preferences (see Section 7).

Powers and Limitations

CFGs can describe:

  • Nested structures: $a^n b^n$ (matching parentheses, subject-verb agreement across embedding)
  • Recursive embedding: sentences within sentences
  • Most of English phrase structure

But CFGs cannot describe:

  • Cross-serial dependencies: $a^n b^n c^n$ (as seen in Swiss German and Dutch)
  • Copying: $\{ww \mid w \in \Sigma^*\}$ (exact reduplication)

5. Context-Sensitive Grammars (Type 1)

Context-sensitive grammars allow rules where the replacement depends on surrounding context:

Context-Sensitive Rule

Rules have the form $\alpha A \beta \to \alpha \gamma \beta$, where:

  • $A$ is a non-terminal
  • $\alpha, \beta$ are strings of terminals and non-terminals (the context)
  • $\gamma$ is a non-empty string

The non-terminal $A$ is replaced by $\gamma$, but only when surrounded by context $\alpha \ldots \beta$.

Evidence Natural Language Exceeds CFG Power

The strongest evidence comes from Swiss German cross-serial dependencies (Shieber, 1985):

Swiss German Cross-Serial Dependencies

In Swiss German subordinate clauses, noun phrases and verbs form cross-serial dependencies:

... dass [d'chind]$_1$ [em Hans]$_2$ [es huus]$_3$ [lönd]$_1$ [hälfe]$_2$ [aastriiche]$_3$

"... that the children$_1$ Hans$_2$ the house$_3$ let$_1$ help$_2$ paint$_3$"

The subscripts show the dependencies: each NP$_i$ pairs with verb$_i$. Furthermore, the case marking of each NP depends on its verb (dative for "help," accusative for "paint"). This creates a pattern isomorphic to $a^n b^n c^n$, which is provably not context-free.

Other evidence includes:

  • Bambara (West Africa): tonal patterns creating copying dependencies
  • Dutch: cross-serial verb clusters similar to Swiss German
  • Reduplication in many languages: exact copying of a morpheme

6. Mildly Context-Sensitive Grammars

The jump from context-free to context-sensitive is enormous in computational terms: CFG parsing is $O(n^3)$, while CSG recognition is PSPACE-complete. But natural language doesn't need the full power of CSGs. Joshi (1985) proposed that natural languages are mildly context-sensitive.

Mildly Context-Sensitive Languages (Joshi's Conjecture)

Natural languages belong to a class that:

  1. Properly contains context-free languages
  2. Can describe cross-serial dependencies and limited copying
  3. Has polynomial-time parsing algorithms
  4. Has the constant growth property: string lengths in the language grow at most linearly

Tree-Adjoining Grammars (TAGs)

TAGs, introduced by Joshi (1975), are the prototypical mildly context-sensitive formalism. They operate on trees rather than strings:

  • Initial trees: elementary phrase structures (like a simple S → NP VP tree)
  • Auxiliary trees: recursive structures with a designated "foot" node
  • Substitution: inserting an initial tree at a leaf node
  • Adjunction: splicing an auxiliary tree into an internal node

TAGs can generate $a^n b^n c^n$ but not arbitrary context-sensitive languages. Parsing is $O(n^6)$ — polynomial, though higher than CFGs.

Combinatory Categorial Grammar (CCG)

CCG (Steedman, 1987) assigns each word a syntactic category that specifies how it combines with neighbors. Categories are functions:

CCG Categories

  • "the" : $NP/N$ — looks right for an $N$, produces $NP$
  • "cat" : $N$
  • "chased" : $(S \backslash NP)/NP$ — looks right for $NP$, then left for $NP$, produces $S$

Forward application: $NP/N \;\; N \Rightarrow NP$ ("the cat")

Backward application: $NP \;\; (S\backslash NP)/NP \;\; NP \Rightarrow S$ ("the cat chased a dog")

CCG is weakly equivalent to TAGs (generates the same string languages) but assigns different structural descriptions, making it attractive for semantic composition.

7. Probabilistic Context-Free Grammars (PCFGs)

Raw CFGs generate sentences but say nothing about which are more likely. Probabilistic CFGs attach a probability to each rule, enabling disambiguation and modeling of language statistics.

PCFG

A PCFG is a CFG where each rule $A \to \gamma$ has a probability $P(\gamma \mid A)$, with the constraint that for each non-terminal $A$:

$$\sum_{\gamma: A \to \gamma \in R} P(\gamma \mid A) = 1$$

The probability of a parse tree $T$ is the product of the probabilities of all rules used in $T$:

$$P(T) = \prod_{(A \to \gamma) \in T} P(\gamma \mid A)$$

Resolving PP Attachment with PCFGs

For "I saw the man with the telescope":

  • $VP \to V \; NP \; PP$ with $P = 0.15$ (VP attachment)
  • $VP \to V \; NP$ with $P = 0.45$ (NP takes the PP instead)
  • $NP \to Det \; N \; PP$ with $P = 0.25$ (NP attachment)
  • $NP \to Det \; N$ with $P = 0.55$

Parse 1 (VP attachment): uses $VP \to V \; NP \; PP$ (0.15) and $NP \to Det \; N$ (0.55)

Parse 2 (NP attachment): uses $VP \to V \; NP$ (0.45) and $NP \to Det \; N \; PP$ (0.25)

All else equal, Parse 2 is preferred: $0.45 \times 0.25 = 0.1125 > 0.15 \times 0.55 = 0.0825$

Training PCFGs

PCFG probabilities can be estimated from a treebank — a corpus of sentences with annotated parse trees:

$$P(\gamma \mid A) = \frac{\text{Count}(A \to \gamma)}{\text{Count}(A)}$$

For the Penn Treebank, this gives a baseline PCFG with approximately 73% parsing accuracy (labeled bracket F1), which can be improved with techniques like parent annotation, lexicalization, and smoothing.

Entropy of a PCFG

The entropy of a PCFG measures the uncertainty in the language it generates:

$$H(G) = -\sum_{s \in L(G)} P(s) \log_2 P(s)$$

For an infinite language, this is the entropy rate. The cross-entropy between a test corpus and a PCFG provides an evaluation metric analogous to perplexity for n-gram models (see Tutorial 05).

Limitations of PCFGs

Standard PCFGs make a strong context-freeness assumption: the expansion of a non-terminal is independent of everything except the non-terminal itself. This fails for natural language in many ways: verbs select for specific argument structures, lexical identity matters (the parse of "the man" doesn't depend on whether it's subject of "ran" or "devoured"), and structural preferences vary by domain. Lexicalized PCFGs, head-driven models, and neural parsers address these limitations.

The Chomsky hierarchy provides the theoretical backbone for understanding what computational mechanisms language requires. The key finding is that natural language is mildly context-sensitive — more powerful than context-free (due to phenomena like cross-serial dependencies) but far less than fully context-sensitive. This sweet spot permits efficient parsing while capturing the genuine complexity of human syntax. Probabilistic grammars add the crucial statistical dimension, enabling ambiguity resolution and connecting formal language theory to the information-theoretic framework that runs through this entire tutorial series.
