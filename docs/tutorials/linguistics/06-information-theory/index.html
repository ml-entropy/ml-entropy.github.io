Information Theory in Linguistics

Shannon's information theory was originally developed for communication engineering, but its deepest applications may lie in understanding human language. Language is, after all, a communication system — one shaped by millions of years of evolution to transmit information efficiently through a noisy channel. In this tutorial, we explore how concepts like mutual information, surprisal, and channel capacity illuminate the structure and processing of natural language.

1. Mutual Information for Collocations

One of the most immediately useful applications of information theory to linguistics is discovering collocations — word pairs that occur together more often than chance would predict. The key tool is Pointwise Mutual Information (PMI).

Pointwise Mutual Information (PMI)

For two words $x$ and $y$, the PMI measures how much more likely they are to co-occur than if they were independent:

$$I(x; y) = \log_2 \frac{P(x, y)}{P(x) \cdot P(y)}$$

If $P(x,y) > P(x)P(y)$, the words attract each other (positive PMI). If $P(x,y) < P(x)P(y)$, they repel (negative PMI). If $P(x,y) = P(x)P(y)$, they are independent (PMI = 0).

Worked Example

Suppose we have a corpus of $N = 1{,}000{,}000$ words. We observe the following counts:

Example: Computing PMI for "New York"

  • $C(\text{New}) = 4{,}000$, so $P(\text{New}) = 0.004$
  • $C(\text{York}) = 3{,}500$, so $P(\text{York}) = 0.0035$
  • $C(\text{New York}) = 3{,}200$, so $P(\text{New York}) = 0.0032$

The expected co-occurrence under independence:

$$P(\text{New}) \cdot P(\text{York}) = 0.004 \times 0.0035 = 0.000014$$

PMI:

$$I(\text{New}; \text{York}) = \log_2 \frac{0.0032}{0.000014} = \log_2 228.6 \approx 7.84 \text{ bits}$$

This is very high! "New" and "York" are strongly associated.

Compare this with a non-collocation:

Example: PMI for "the the"

  • $P(\text{the}) = 0.07$ (very common word)
  • $P(\text{the the}) \approx 0.000001$ (extremely rare — ungrammatical)
$$I(\text{the}; \text{the}) = \log_2 \frac{0.000001}{0.07 \times 0.07} = \log_2 \frac{0.000001}{0.0049} = \log_2 0.000204 \approx -12.3 \text{ bits}$$

Deeply negative: "the" actively avoids following itself.

High-PMI Bigrams

The following table shows representative PMI values from a large English corpus:

PMI Bias Toward Rare Words

PMI has a known bias: it tends to give very high scores to rare word pairs. If two rare words happen to co-occur even once, the ratio $P(x,y)/P(x)P(y)$ can be enormous. In practice, a minimum count threshold (e.g., discard pairs occurring fewer than 5 times) is applied to mitigate this.

2. Pointwise MI vs Average MI

It is crucial to distinguish between PMI (a property of specific word pairs) and MI (a property of random variables).

Mutual Information (MI) Between Random Variables

The mutual information between random variables $X$ and $Y$ is the expected PMI over all possible outcomes:

$$I(X; Y) = \sum_{x \in X} \sum_{y \in Y} P(x, y) \log_2 \frac{P(x, y)}{P(x) P(y)} = \mathbb{E}[\text{PMI}(X, Y)]$$

MI is always non-negative: $I(X; Y) \geq 0$, with equality iff $X$ and $Y$ are independent.

While MI is always non-negative (it is an average), PMI for individual pairs can be negative. Negative PMI means the two words co-occur less than expected under independence — they avoid each other.

Positive vs Negative PMI Examples

Collocational Preferences

English has strong collocational preferences that cannot be predicted from semantics alone:

  • "strong tea": high positive PMI — this is the conventional way to describe concentrated tea
  • "powerful tea": negative PMI — despite "powerful" and "strong" being near-synonyms, "powerful tea" is rarely used
  • "strong argument": positive PMI
  • "powerful argument": also positive PMI (both are conventional here)
  • "heavy rain": high positive PMI
  • "strong rain": negative PMI (not the conventional choice)

These asymmetries are exactly what PMI captures and what makes it invaluable for lexicography and language learning.

In computational applications, Positive PMI (PPMI) replaces all negative values with zero:

$$\text{PPMI}(x, y) = \max(0, \text{PMI}(x, y))$$

This is widely used in distributional semantics (see Tutorial 10) because negative PMI values are often unreliable due to data sparsity.

3. Surprisal and Processing Difficulty

One of the most striking connections between information theory and psycholinguistics is the relationship between surprisal and processing difficulty.

Surprisal

The surprisal of a word $w$ given its preceding context is:

$$S(w) = -\log_2 P(w \mid \text{context})$$

Measured in bits. A word with probability 0.5 has surprisal of 1 bit; a word with probability $2^{-10} \approx 0.001$ has surprisal of 10 bits.

Eye-Tracking Evidence

When people read, their eyes do not move smoothly across the page. Instead, they make rapid jumps (saccades) and pauses (fixations). Eye-tracking studies have consistently found:

  • Fixation duration on a word is proportional to its surprisal
  • Highly predictable words are often skipped entirely
  • Unexpected words trigger regressions (the eyes jump back)

The relationship is remarkably linear: each additional bit of surprisal adds approximately 3-5 milliseconds to reading time.

Example: Garden-Path Sentences

Consider the classic garden-path sentence:

"The horse raced past the barn fell."

At the word "fell," readers experience extreme surprisal. Their initial parse treated "raced" as the main verb (the horse raced past the barn), but "fell" forces a reanalysis: "the horse [that was] raced past the barn" is the subject, and "fell" is the main verb.

In surprisal terms:

  • $P(\text{fell} \mid \text{the horse raced past the barn})$ is extremely low under the initial parse
  • Surprisal: $-\log_2 P(\text{fell} \mid \cdots) \gg 10$ bits
  • This triggers increased fixation time, regressions, and conscious processing difficulty

Surprisal Theory (Hale, 2001; Levy, 2008)

Surprisal theory formally proposes that the cognitive effort of processing a word is proportional to its surprisal. This is not merely a correlation — it has a rational justification: if the brain maintains a probability distribution over possible parses and updates it with each word (Bayesian updating), then the amount of belief revision required is exactly the surprisal.

4. Uniform Information Density (UID)

If surprisal determines processing difficulty, and if there is a maximum rate at which listeners can process information, then speakers should try to spread information evenly across an utterance. This is the Uniform Information Density hypothesis.

Uniform Information Density Hypothesis

Speakers structure their utterances so that information (surprisal) is distributed as uniformly as possible across the signal, staying close to but not exceeding the channel capacity of the listener:

$$\text{Minimize: } \text{Var}[S(w_1), S(w_2), \ldots, S(w_n)]$$

subject to conveying the intended message. Equivalently, speakers aim for a constant information rate $R$ (bits/second).

Evidence for UID

The evidence for UID comes from multiple levels of linguistic structure:

Phonetic Reduction

Predictable words are pronounced with shorter duration, reduced vowels, and less articulatory effort. For example:

  • "I don't know" → "I dunno" → practically "Idunno" (highly predictable sequence)
  • Function words ("the," "of," "is") are reduced far more than content words
  • The second mention of a word in discourse is shorter than the first

Syntactic Choice

When speakers have a choice between two syntactic forms, they tend to choose the one that distributes information more uniformly:

  • Optional "that": "I think (that) he's right." Speakers include "that" more often when the complement clause is surprising, inserting an extra low-surprisal word to smooth the information rate.
  • Genitive choice: "the king's crown" vs "the crown of the king." The possessive is preferred when the possessor is more predictable given the context.

Word Order Flexibility

In languages with flexible word order, speakers place given (predictable) information before new (surprising) information, creating a smooth ramp of information rather than an abrupt spike.

Mathematical Formulation

More formally, let $u$ be an utterance encoding message $m$. The UID principle states that speakers choose $u$ to minimize:

$$\sum_{i=1}^{|u|} \left( S(u_i \mid u_1, \ldots, u_{i-1}) - R \right)^2$$

where $R$ is the target information rate. This can be viewed as a rate-distortion problem: encode the message with minimal variance in the instantaneous information rate.

5. Entropy and Word Order

Different languages encode information through radically different strategies, yet they all need to communicate roughly the same set of meanings. Information theory reveals a fascinating trade-off between word order rigidity and morphological complexity.

Free Word Order Languages

Languages like Latin, Russian, Japanese, and Turkish allow relatively free word order. In Russian, all six orderings of "The cat caught the mouse" are grammatical:

Russian Word Order Flexibility

  • Koshka poymala mysh' (Cat caught mouse — SVO)
  • Mysh' poymala koshka (Mouse caught cat — OVS)
  • Poymala koshka mysh' (Caught cat mouse — VSO)

All are unambiguous because case endings mark the grammatical roles: koshka (nominative = subject), mysh' (accusative = object). The morphology carries the information that English encodes in word order.

In terms of entropy:

  • Per-word entropy is higher in free word order languages (more possible words at each position)
  • But morphological marking provides redundancy, reducing ambiguity
  • The total information per sentence is roughly equivalent across languages

Fixed Word Order Languages

English and Mandarin Chinese rely heavily on word order to encode grammatical relations. "Dog bites man" and "Man bites dog" use position to distinguish subject from object. This means:

  • Per-word positional entropy is lower (fewer options at each position)
  • Word order itself carries information (approximately 2-3 bits per sentence in English)
  • Less morphological complexity is needed

The Conservation Principle

There is a deep conservation law at work: the total information needed to encode a proposition is roughly constant across languages. Languages trade off between morphological information $H_{\text{morph}}$ and positional information $H_{\text{order}}$:

$$H_{\text{total}} \approx H_{\text{morph}} + H_{\text{order}} + H_{\text{lexical}} \approx \text{constant}$$

Languages that are rich in one dimension tend to be lean in another. This is not a theorem but an empirically robust generalization.

6. Information-Theoretic Syntax

Syntax — the way words are organized into sentences — can be viewed through an information-theoretic lens. One of the most striking findings is that languages systematically minimize dependency length.

Dependency Length Minimization

Dependency Length

In a dependency parse, each word (except the root) has a syntactic head. The dependency length is the linear distance (in words) between a dependent and its head. For a sentence, the total dependency length is:

$$DL = \sum_{i=1}^{n} |i - \text{head}(i)|$$

Across dozens of languages, observed dependency lengths are significantly shorter than would be expected by chance. Why? Because shorter dependencies reduce the entropy of parsing:

  • When a dependent is close to its head, the listener needs to keep less information in working memory
  • Mutual information between a head and its dependent decays with distance: $I(w_i; w_j)$ decreases as $|i - j|$ increases
  • Shorter dependencies mean higher MI between linked words, making the parse easier to recover

Mutual Information Between Heads and Dependents

Consider a verb and its arguments. The verb "devour" has high MI with animate subjects and edible objects. If we place the object far from the verb, the listener must maintain uncertainty about what was devoured for many words:

Dependency Length and Processing

Short dependency (easy): "The cat devoured the fish quickly."
→ "devoured" and "fish" are adjacent. MI between verb and object is immediately available.

Long dependency (harder): "The cat devoured, after a long pause during which nothing happened, the fish."
→ 10 words separate "devoured" from "fish." The listener must hold "devoured" in memory, maintaining a probability distribution over possible objects.

The preference for short dependencies is so strong that it shapes fundamental word order parameters across languages: verb-object languages place prepositions before nouns, while object-verb languages place postpositions after nouns — in both cases, minimizing the distance between semantically related words.

7. Channel Capacity of Human Language

Shannon showed that every communication channel has a maximum information rate — its channel capacity. What is the channel capacity of human language?

Information Rate of Speech

The information rate of a communication modality is:

$$R = H \times \text{rate}$$

where $H$ is the entropy per symbol and rate is symbols per second.

Measured Rates

Careful measurements across languages yield remarkably consistent results:

ModalityInformation RateNotes
Speech production~39 bits/secAverage across 17 languages (Coupé et al., 2019)
Speech perception~40-60 bits/secUpper bound of comfortable listening
Silent reading~45 bits/secAt average reading speed (~250 wpm)
Typing~15 bits/secProfessional typist
Sign language~38 bits/secComparable to speech

Universal Information Rate

A remarkable finding: although languages vary enormously in speech rate (syllables per second) and information per syllable, the product is nearly constant across languages. Languages that pack more information per syllable (like Mandarin) are spoken more slowly; languages with less information per syllable (like Japanese) are spoken faster. The bottleneck is cognitive, not articulatory.

Comparison with Other Modalities

How does language compare with other information channels?

ModalitySensory InputConscious Processing
Vision~10,000,000 bits/sec~40 bits/sec
Audition~100,000 bits/sec~40 bits/sec
Touch~1,000,000 bits/sec~5 bits/sec
LanguageN/A~39 bits/sec

The contrast is stunning: our sensory systems absorb millions of bits per second, but conscious processing is limited to roughly 40 bits/sec across all modalities. Language operates right at this cognitive bottleneck — it is optimized for the conscious channel, not the sensory one.

8. Applications

Information-theoretic measures have found widespread application in computational linguistics and NLP.

Keyword Extraction via TF-IDF

TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency) is an entropy-adjacent measure that identifies important words in documents:

$$\text{TF-IDF}(w, d) = \text{tf}(w, d) \times \log \frac{N}{|\{d' : w \in d'\}|}$$

The IDF component is essentially a surprisal: words that appear in fewer documents are more surprising (more informative) when they do appear. A word appearing in every document has IDF = 0 (no information). A word appearing in only one document has maximum IDF.

TF-IDF and Entropy

The IDF component can be rewritten as $\text{IDF}(w) = \log \frac{1}{P(\text{doc contains } w)} - \text{const}$, which is exactly the surprisal of encountering a document containing $w$. TF-IDF thus weights words by their information content at the document level.

Topic Modeling

Topic models like LDA (Latent Dirichlet Allocation) can be understood information-theoretically. Each topic is a probability distribution over words, and documents are mixtures of topics. The model decomposes the corpus's entropy into:

$$H(\text{words}) = I(\text{words}; \text{topics}) + H(\text{words} \mid \text{topics})$$

Good topics are those that maximize $I(\text{words}; \text{topics})$ — knowing the topic should reduce uncertainty about which words appear.

Authorship Attribution

Different authors produce text with characteristic entropy profiles. Features used in authorship attribution include:

  • Vocabulary richness: related to the entropy of the word distribution
  • Sentence length distribution: entropy of sentence lengths
  • Function word frequencies: the entropy of the distribution over function words
  • Character n-gram entropy: captures stylistic spelling and punctuation patterns

Entropy Profiles in Practice

Studies have shown that the entropy rate (bits per character) varies systematically across genres and authors:

  • Legal text: ~3.5 bits/char (formulaic, repetitive)
  • News articles: ~4.0 bits/char (conventional but varied)
  • Fiction: ~4.2 bits/char (creative, less predictable)
  • Poetry: ~4.5 bits/char (deliberately surprising)

An author's typical entropy rate is remarkably stable across works, making it a useful forensic feature.

Cross-Entropy as a Language Model Metric

The quality of a language model is measured by its cross-entropy on held-out text:

$$H_{\text{cross}}(P, Q) = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \log_2 Q(w_i \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1})$$

where $P$ is the true distribution and $Q$ is the model's distribution. Lower cross-entropy means the model better predicts the text. The related metric perplexity $= 2^{H_{\text{cross}}}$ is the standard evaluation measure for language models.

Information theory provides a unifying framework for understanding language at every level: from the micro (why specific word pairs co-occur) to the macro (why all languages transmit at the same rate). The key insight is that language is an information-optimized code — shaped by the pressure to communicate efficiently through a capacity-limited cognitive channel.
