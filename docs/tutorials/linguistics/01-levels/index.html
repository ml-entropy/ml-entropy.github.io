The Layered Architecture of Language

Language is not a monolithic phenomenon. It is organized in layers, each operating on different units and governed by different rules. A speech signal is a continuous waveform, but from it we extract discrete sounds, combine them into meaningful units, arrange those into structured sentences, and interpret the whole in context. Each layer builds on the one below while introducing its own regularities and its own sources of ambiguity.

This layered view is not merely an organizational convenience — it reflects something deep about how language works. Different NLP tasks operate at different levels: speech recognition lives at the phonetic layer, spell-checking at the orthographic layer, parsing at the syntactic layer, and dialogue systems must grapple with pragmatics. Understanding these layers is the first step toward building systems that process language at any level.

The Linguistic Stack

The levels of linguistic analysis, from lowest to highest, are: Phonetics/Phonology (sounds) → Morphology (word structure) → Syntax (sentence structure) → Semantics (meaning) → Pragmatics/Discourse (meaning in context). Each level has its own units, rules, and entropy characteristics.

From an information-theoretic perspective, each layer acts as a channel that encodes and transmits information. As we move up the stack, the units become larger but fewer, and the role of context grows. This is why Shannon's entropy decreases as we condition on more linguistic structure — a theme we will return to throughout this series.

Phonetics and Phonology

At the foundation of spoken language lie phonetics and phonology — the study of speech sounds and their abstract organization. Though often conflated, they address fundamentally different questions.

Phonetics: The Physical Reality

Phonetics deals with the physical properties of speech sounds. It divides into three branches:

  • Articulatory phonetics: how the vocal tract produces sounds (tongue position, lip rounding, voicing)
  • Acoustic phonetics: the physical properties of the sound wave (frequency, amplitude, formants)
  • Auditory phonetics: how the ear and brain perceive speech sounds

The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) provides a standardized notation for transcribing the sounds of any human language. For example, the English word "church" is transcribed as /tʃɜːtʃ/, making explicit the two affricate sounds that the spelling obscures.

Phonology: The Abstract System

Phonology studies the abstract sound system of a language — which sound distinctions matter and which do not. The central concept is the phoneme: the smallest unit of sound that can distinguish meaning.

Phonemes vs. Allophones

In English, /p/ is a phoneme: swapping it changes meaning (e.g., "pat" vs. "bat"). But the /p/ in "pin" is aspirated [pʰ] while the /p/ in "spin" is unaspirated [p]. These are allophones — different physical realizations of the same abstract phoneme. English speakers do not perceive the difference, but in Hindi, aspiration is phonemic: /pʰal/ ("knife blade") vs. /pal/ ("moment") are different words.

This illustrates a key principle: what counts as a meaningful distinction is language-specific. The same physical sound contrast can be phonemic in one language and allophonic in another.

The Phoneme Inventory: A Measure of Linguistic Diversity

Languages vary enormously in the size of their phoneme inventories. Hawaiian has approximately 13 phonemes (8 consonants and 5 vowels), making it one of the smallest known inventories. At the other extreme, !Xóõ (a Taa language of Botswana) has around 160 phonemes, including over 100 click consonants. English sits in the middle with roughly 44 phonemes. The information content per phoneme is $\log_2(|\text{inventory}|)$: about 3.7 bits for Hawaiian and 7.3 bits for !Xóõ.

Why This Matters for NLP

Phonetics and phonology underpin several key technologies: automatic speech recognition (ASR) must map acoustic signals to phoneme sequences; text-to-speech (TTS) must generate appropriate acoustic realizations from text; and pronunciation dictionaries (like CMUdict) encode the phoneme-to-grapheme mapping that enables both. Even in text-only NLP, phonological awareness helps with tasks like rhyme detection, transliteration, and understanding spelling variation.

Morphology

Morphology is the study of word structure — how meaningful pieces combine to form words. If phonemes are the atoms of sound, then morphemes are the atoms of meaning.

Morpheme

A morpheme is the smallest unit of language that carries meaning or grammatical function. The word "unbreakable" consists of three morphemes: un- (negation) + break (root) + -able (capable of). Morphemes that can stand alone are free ("break"); those that cannot are bound ("un-", "-able").

Types of Morphological Processes

Languages build words through several mechanisms:

  • Inflection: modifying a word's form to express grammatical distinctions (tense, number, case) without changing its core meaning or part of speech. "Walk" → "walks", "walked", "walking".
  • Derivation: creating a new word (often a different part of speech) from an existing one. "Happy" (adj.) → "unhappy" (adj.) → "unhappiness" (noun).
  • Compounding: combining two or more free morphemes. "Blackboard", "sunflower", "mother-in-law".

Morphological Typology

Languages fall along a spectrum of morphological complexity:

Four Morphological Types

Isolating (analytic): words are typically single morphemes. Mandarin Chinese: wǒ míngtiān huì qù (I / tomorrow / will / go) — each word is a separate morpheme.

Agglutinative: words are built from clearly segmentable morphemes, each expressing one meaning. Turkish: evlerinizden = ev+ler+iniz+den = house+PLURAL+your+from = "from your houses". Each suffix has one clear function.

Fusional (inflectional): morphemes are fused together, with a single affix expressing multiple grammatical categories simultaneously. Russian: knig-ami = book-INST.PL — the suffix -ami simultaneously encodes instrumental case AND plural number.

Polysynthetic: entire sentences can be expressed as a single word. Inuktitut: tusaatsiarunnanngittualuujunga = "I cannot hear very well." The "word" contains what would be an entire clause in English.

Why This Matters for NLP

Morphological complexity is one of the greatest challenges in NLP. Techniques like stemming (reducing words to a crude root: "running" → "run") and lemmatization (reducing to dictionary form with linguistic awareness: "better" → "good") are essential preprocessing steps. For morphologically rich languages like Turkish or Finnish, a single verb root can generate thousands of surface forms, making the vocabulary effectively infinite. This is precisely the problem that subword tokenization methods (BPE, WordPiece) were designed to address — a topic we cover in Tutorial 03.

From an information-theoretic perspective, morphological richness means that individual words carry more information in agglutinative and polysynthetic languages. The entropy per word is higher, but the entropy per morpheme may be comparable across languages — a beautiful instance of the tradeoff between unit size and information density.

Syntax

Syntax is the study of sentence structure — how words combine into phrases, clauses, and sentences according to grammatical rules. It is the level of linguistic analysis that most directly concerns the structure of language, and it is where the concept of recursion becomes central.

Constituent Structure

Sentences are not flat sequences of words. They have hierarchical structure, organized into nested constituents (phrases). The sentence "The tall student read the old book" has the structure:

$$\underbrace{[\text{The tall student}]_{\text{NP}}}_{\text{Subject}} \quad \underbrace{[\text{read} \underbrace{[\text{the old book}]_{\text{NP}}}_{\text{Object}}]_{\text{VP}}}_{\text{Predicate}}$$

This hierarchical grouping determines meaning. Constituent structure is captured by phrase structure grammars (also called context-free grammars), which we explore in depth in Tutorial 07.

Dependency Structure

An alternative view represents structure as binary relations between words: every word (except the root) depends on exactly one other word (its head). In "The cat sat on the mat", "sat" is the root, "cat" depends on "sat" (as its subject), "on" depends on "sat", "mat" depends on "on", and the determiners depend on their nouns.

Word Order Typology

Languages differ systematically in how they order the major constituents Subject (S), Verb (V), and Object (O):

  • SVO: English, French, Chinese ("The cat ate the fish")
  • SOV: Japanese, Korean, Turkish, Hindi ("The cat the fish ate")
  • VSO: Irish, Welsh, Classical Arabic ("Ate the cat the fish")
  • Free order: Russian, Latin, Warlpiri (grammatical relations marked by morphology rather than position)

SOV is the most common order across the world's languages (~45%), followed by SVO (~42%).

Recursion: The Engine of Infinity

The most remarkable property of syntax is recursion: the ability to embed structures within structures of the same type. A noun phrase can contain a relative clause, which contains a verb phrase, which contains another noun phrase, which contains another relative clause, and so on without bound:

Recursive Embedding

"The dog that chased the cat that caught the rat that ate the cheese that lay in the house that Jack built."

Each relative clause is embedded inside the previous noun phrase, theoretically without limit. This is what makes the set of grammatical sentences in any language infinite, despite being generated by a finite set of rules — and it is why we need the formal power of at least context-free grammars to describe syntax.

Syntactic Ambiguity

Structural Ambiguity

"I saw the man with the telescope."

This sentence has two parses: (1) I used a telescope to see the man [with the telescope modifies saw], or (2) I saw a man who had a telescope [with the telescope modifies the man]. Resolving such ambiguities — known as PP-attachment ambiguity — is one of the classic challenges in computational parsing.

Why This Matters for NLP

Syntactic analysis underlies parsing (both constituency and dependency), grammar checking, machine translation (where source and target languages may have very different word orders), and information extraction (determining who did what to whom requires understanding syntactic relations). Modern neural models like transformers implicitly learn syntactic structure, but understanding formal syntax remains essential for interpreting what they learn and diagnosing where they fail.

Semantics

Semantics is the study of meaning — both the meaning of individual words and how word meanings compose to form the meaning of larger expressions. It is the level where we transition from form to content.

Lexical Semantics

Words relate to each other in systematic ways:

  • Synonymy: words with similar meanings ("big" / "large")
  • Antonymy: words with opposite meanings ("hot" / "cold")
  • Polysemy: one word with multiple related meanings ("bank" = riverbank, financial institution — historically related through the concept of a raised edge)
  • Homonymy: unrelated words that happen to share a form ("bat" = flying mammal, cricket equipment — no historical connection)
  • Hyponymy: "is-a" relations ("dog" is a hyponym of "animal")

Compositional Semantics

One of the foundational ideas in semantics is Frege's Principle of Compositionality: the meaning of a complex expression is determined by the meanings of its parts and the way they are combined.

Frege's Principle

If $\mu$ is the meaning function and $E$ is a complex expression composed of parts $e_1, \ldots, e_n$ combined by syntactic rule $R$, then: $$\mu(E) = f_R(\mu(e_1), \mu(e_2), \ldots, \mu(e_n))$$ The meaning of the whole is a function of the meanings of the parts and the syntactic structure. This principle is what makes it possible to understand sentences we have never encountered before.

Thematic Roles

Verbs assign thematic roles to their arguments, capturing "who does what":

  • Agent: the entity performing the action ("The chef sliced the bread")
  • Patient (Theme): the entity affected ("The chef sliced the bread")
  • Instrument: the means used ("She cut it with a knife")
  • Experiencer: the entity experiencing a state ("John fears dogs")
  • Location: where something happens ("She works in London")

Word Sense Disambiguation

Because words are frequently polysemous, word sense disambiguation (WSD) is a core semantic task: given the word "bank" in context, does it mean the financial institution or the side of a river? Humans perform this effortlessly using context; building systems that do the same has been a long-standing challenge. Modern contextual embeddings (BERT, GPT) represent words differently depending on context, effectively performing implicit WSD.

Why This Matters for NLP

Semantics is at the heart of question answering, information extraction, semantic search, textual entailment, and natural language inference. The distributional hypothesis — that words appearing in similar contexts have similar meanings — is the foundation of word embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe), which we explore in Tutorial 10.

Pragmatics and Discourse

Pragmatics studies meaning in context — how speakers use language to do things beyond merely encoding propositions. Discourse analysis extends this to multi-sentence text and conversation. This is the level where language becomes truly situated: who is speaking, to whom, when, where, and why all matter.

Speech Acts

Austin and Searle's speech act theory distinguishes three layers of every utterance:

  • Locutionary act: the literal content ("Can you pass the salt?")
  • Illocutionary act: the intended function (a request, not a question about ability)
  • Perlocutionary act: the actual effect (the listener passes the salt)
Indirect Speech Acts

"Can you pass the salt?" is grammatically a yes/no question, but pragmatically it is a polite request. Responding "Yes, I can" (without passing the salt) would be technically accurate but communicatively uncooperative. Understanding that the illocutionary force differs from the literal meaning is essential for dialogue systems and chatbots.

Grice's Maxims and Implicature

The philosopher Paul Grice proposed that conversation is governed by a Cooperative Principle and four maxims:

  • Quantity: say enough, but not too much
  • Quality: say only what you believe to be true
  • Relation: be relevant
  • Manner: be clear and orderly

When a speaker appears to violate a maxim, listeners infer an implicature — an unstated meaning. If asked "How was the exam?" and someone replies "Well, it was an experience," the understatement (violating Quantity) implicates that it went poorly.

Anaphora and Coreference

The Coreference Challenge

"John told Bill that he was tired."

Who is "he"? It could be John or Bill. Resolving such anaphoric references — determining what a pronoun or noun phrase refers to — requires world knowledge, discourse context, and sometimes syntactic constraints (Binding Theory). Coreference resolution is a fundamental NLP task that remains challenging, especially across sentences and in complex narratives.

Why This Matters for NLP

Pragmatics and discourse are central to dialogue systems (understanding user intent beyond literal words), coreference resolution (tracking entities across a text), sentiment analysis (detecting sarcasm and implicature), and text summarization (maintaining discourse coherence). These are arguably the hardest levels for machines to master because they require modeling not just language but the minds of the speakers.

The Entropy Connection

Each level of linguistic analysis has its own entropy structure. As we move up the hierarchy and as we condition on more context, entropy — the uncertainty about the next unit — systematically changes.

Entropy of a Linguistic Source

The entropy rate $h$ of a stationary stochastic process $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ is: $$h = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} H(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n) = \lim_{n \to \infty} H(X_n \mid X_1, \ldots, X_{n-1})$$ This measures the average surprise per symbol when the full history is known. Shannon's fundamental insight was to apply this to English text.

Consider English at different levels of analysis:

  • Phoneme sequences: approximately 4.9 bits per phoneme when each phoneme is equally likely ($\log_2 44 \approx 5.5$), but with phonotactic constraints reducing this to around 4.9 bits.
  • Character sequences (unigram): $H \approx 4.08$ bits/character — treating each character independently based on frequency.
  • Character sequences (with context): Shannon estimated the true entropy rate of English at approximately $1.0$–$1.3$ bits per character when full linguistic context is available.
  • Word sequences: the entropy rate per word is higher (because words carry more information than characters), but per-character entropy is lower when word-level context is exploited.
The Key Pattern

Higher levels = more context = lower conditional entropy. Knowing the syntactic structure of a sentence reduces uncertainty about the next word far more than knowing only the previous characters. Knowing the topic of a discourse reduces uncertainty further still. This cascading reduction of entropy through linguistic structure is, in a deep sense, what language is: a system for efficiently encoding meaning by exploiting regularities at every level.

The conditional entropy at each level can be expressed as:

$$H(\text{next unit} \mid \text{context}) \leq H(\text{next unit})$$

with equality only when the context provides no information — which never happens in natural language. The gap between the unconditional and conditional entropy is the mutual information between context and prediction, and maximizing this gap is precisely what language models learn to do. We explore this in depth in Tutorials 04 and 05.

Summary: Levels at a Glance

The following table maps each level to its fundamental units, representative NLP tasks, and entropy characteristics:

Level Units NLP Tasks Entropy Notes
Phonetics / Phonology Phones, phonemes ASR, TTS, pronunciation ~4.9 bits/phoneme (English, with phonotactics)
Morphology Morphemes Stemming, lemmatization, subword tokenization Higher entropy/word in agglutinative languages
Syntax Words, phrases, clauses Parsing, grammar checking, MT Syntactic context reduces word entropy significantly
Semantics Senses, propositions WSD, QA, NLI, semantic search Semantic constraints further reduce entropy
Pragmatics / Discourse Speech acts, referents Dialogue, coreference, summarization Discourse context drives entropy toward Shannon's limit (~1.0–1.3 bits/char)
