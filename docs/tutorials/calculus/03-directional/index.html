03. Directional Derivatives

Partial derivatives measure change along coordinate axes. Directional derivatives generalize this to any direction—and reveal why the gradient is so special.

The Mountain Question

Imagine you are standing on the side of a mountain whose elevation is described by a function $f(x, y)$, where $x$ is your east–west position and $y$ is your north–south position. You already know how to compute partial derivatives: $\partial f / \partial x$ tells you the slope if you walk due east, and $\partial f / \partial y$ tells you the slope if you walk due north. But what if you want to walk northeast? Or at a bearing of 37°? The partial derivatives alone don’t answer this question—they only describe two special directions out of infinitely many.

This is exactly the problem that directional derivatives solve. They let you measure the rate of change of a function in any direction you choose, not just along the coordinate axes. The beautiful surprise is that once you know the gradient vector $\nabla f$, you can compute the directional derivative in every direction simultaneously—no new limits required. Even more remarkably, the gradient itself points in the direction of steepest ascent, which is why gradient-based optimization is the workhorse of modern machine learning.

In this tutorial, we will build the theory of directional derivatives from the ground up, prove why the gradient gives the direction of fastest increase, and then follow that insight all the way to gradient descent, momentum methods, and the information-theoretic notion of natural gradients. By the end, you will see partial derivatives, gradients, and optimization as facets of one unified geometric idea.

Definition of the Directional Derivative

Recall that the ordinary derivative $f'(a)$ measures how fast $f$ changes as $x$ increases through $a$. For a function of several variables, the directional derivative generalizes this idea: we pick a direction $\mathbf{u}$, march a tiny step $h$ in that direction, and ask how much $f$ changes relative to $h$.

Directional Derivative

Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be a function, $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ a point, and $\mathbf{u} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ a unit vector ($\|\mathbf{u}\| = 1$). The directional derivative of $f$ at $\mathbf{a}$ in the direction $\mathbf{u}$ is

$$D_{\mathbf{u}} f(\mathbf{a}) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(\mathbf{a} + h\mathbf{u}) - f(\mathbf{a})}{h}$$

The requirement that $\mathbf{u}$ is a unit vector is important: it ensures that $h$ measures genuine distance. If we used a non-unit vector $\mathbf{v}$, the derivative would be scaled by $\|\mathbf{v}\|$, conflating the magnitude of the direction with the rate of change. By insisting on $\|\mathbf{u}\| = 1$, we isolate the effect of direction alone.

Geometrically, the expression $\mathbf{a} + h\mathbf{u}$ traces out a straight line through $\mathbf{a}$ in direction $\mathbf{u}$. The function values along this line define a single-variable function $g(h) = f(\mathbf{a} + h\mathbf{u})$, and the directional derivative is simply $g'(0)$. In other words, you “slice” the multivariable function along a line and take an ordinary derivative of the resulting one-dimensional curve.

Partial Derivatives Are Special Cases

Partial derivatives are just directional derivatives along the coordinate axes. If $\mathbf{e}_1 = (1, 0, \ldots, 0)$ and $\mathbf{e}_2 = (0, 1, 0, \ldots, 0)$, then: $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} = D_{\mathbf{e}_1} f, \quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2} = D_{\mathbf{e}_2} f$$ The partial derivative notation $\partial f / \partial x_i$ is just shorthand for the directional derivative in the $i$-th coordinate direction.

Connection to the Gradient

Computing directional derivatives from the limit definition every time would be tedious. Fortunately, there is a remarkable shortcut: if $f$ is differentiable, then the directional derivative in any direction can be computed as a simple dot product with the gradient vector.

Computing Directional Derivatives via the Gradient

If $f$ is differentiable at $\mathbf{a}$, then for any unit vector $\mathbf{u}$:

$$D_{\mathbf{u}} f(\mathbf{a}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{u} = \|\nabla f(\mathbf{a})\| \cos\theta$$ where $\theta$ is the angle between $\nabla f(\mathbf{a})$ and $\mathbf{u}$.

Let us unpack why this works. The gradient $\nabla f = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, \ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right)$ encodes the rates of change along each axis. When you move in a general direction $\mathbf{u} = (u_1, \ldots, u_n)$, you are effectively moving $u_i$ units along each axis simultaneously. By the chain rule, the total rate of change is the sum of each partial derivative weighted by the corresponding component of $\mathbf{u}$:

Derivation: Gradient Dot Product Formula
1
Define $g(h) = f(\mathbf{a} + h\mathbf{u})$. By the chain rule: $g'(h) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a} + h\mathbf{u}) \cdot u_i$
2
Evaluate at $h = 0$: $g'(0) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a}) \cdot u_i$
3
Recognize this as a dot product: $D_{\mathbf{u}} f(\mathbf{a}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{u}$
4
Apply the geometric formula for the dot product: $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \|\mathbf{a}\|\|\mathbf{b}\|\cos\theta$, and since $\|\mathbf{u}\| = 1$, we get $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = \|\nabla f\| \cos\theta$.

The formula $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = \|\nabla f\| \cos\theta$ is extraordinarily informative. Since $\cos\theta$ ranges from $-1$ to $1$, we immediately see three critical cases:

  • Maximum when $\theta = 0$ (direction of $\nabla f$): $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = \|\nabla f\|$. Walking in the gradient direction gives the steepest climb.
  • Minimum when $\theta = \pi$ (opposite to $\nabla f$): $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = -\|\nabla f\|$. Walking against the gradient gives the steepest descent.
  • Zero when $\theta = \pi/2$ (perpendicular to $\nabla f$): you’re moving along a level curve.

Gradient as the Direction of Steepest Ascent

We have claimed that the gradient points in the direction of fastest increase. Let us now prove this rigorously and build geometric intuition for why it is true.

Theorem: Gradient Gives Steepest Ascent

Among all unit vectors $\mathbf{u}$, the directional derivative $D_{\mathbf{u}} f(\mathbf{a})$ is maximized when $\mathbf{u} = \frac{\nabla f(\mathbf{a})}{\|\nabla f(\mathbf{a})\|}$, and the maximum value is $\|\nabla f(\mathbf{a})\|$.

Proof
1
We want to maximize $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u}$ subject to $\|\mathbf{u}\| = 1$.
2
By the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality: $|\nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u}| \leq \|\nabla f\| \cdot \|\mathbf{u}\| = \|\nabla f\|$.
3
Equality holds iff $\mathbf{u}$ is a scalar multiple of $\nabla f$. Since $\|\mathbf{u}\| = 1$, we need $\mathbf{u} = \nabla f / \|\nabla f\|$.
4
Substituting: $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = \nabla f \cdot \frac{\nabla f}{\|\nabla f\|} = \frac{\|\nabla f\|^2}{\|\nabla f\|} = \|\nabla f\|$. $\square$

The proof is elegant: the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality immediately tells us that the dot product is maximized when the two vectors are parallel. The magnitude $\|\nabla f\|$ tells you how steep that steepest path is. If $\|\nabla f\| = 0$, you are at a critical point where every direction gives zero rate of change.

Worked Example: Steepest Ascent on a Surface

Let $f(x, y) = 100 - x^2 - 3y^2$, which describes a dome-shaped hill. At the point $(2, 1)$:

Finding the Steepest Direction
1
Compute the gradient: $\nabla f = (-2x, -6y)$, so $\nabla f(2, 1) = (-4, -6)$.
2
The steepest ascent direction is $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = \frac{(-4, -6)}{\sqrt{52}} = \left(\frac{-2}{\sqrt{13}}, \frac{-3}{\sqrt{13}}\right)$.
3
The maximum rate of increase is $\|\nabla f\| = 2\sqrt{13} \approx 7.21$.
4
The gradient points toward the origin—the dome peak at $(0, 0)$.
The Gradient’s Secret

The gradient encodes two pieces of information simultaneously: its direction tells you which way is steepest uphill, and its magnitude tells you how steep that steepest path is. In gradient descent, we simply go the opposite way: $\mathbf{x}_{t+1} = \mathbf{x}_t - \eta \nabla f(\mathbf{x}_t)$.

Worked Example: Complete Directional Derivative

Let $f(x, y) = x^2 - xy + y^2$. Find the directional derivative at $(1, 2)$ in the direction of $\mathbf{v} = (3, 4)$.

Solution
1
Compute the gradient: $\nabla f = (2x - y,\; -x + 2y)$
2
Evaluate at $(1, 2)$: $\nabla f(1, 2) = (0, 3)$.
3
Normalize: $\|\mathbf{v}\| = 5$, so $\mathbf{u} = (3/5, 4/5)$.
4
Dot product: $D_{\mathbf{u}} f(1, 2) = (0, 3) \cdot (3/5, 4/5) = 12/5 = \boxed{2.4}$

The function increases at 2.4 per unit distance in this direction. The maximum rate is $\|\nabla f\| = 3$, so our direction captures $80\%$ of the steepness ($\cos\theta = 4/5$).

Level Curves and Gradients

A level curve of $f(x, y)$ is the set of points where $f(x, y) = c$. Think of contour lines on a topographic map. The gradient is always perpendicular to level curves: along a level curve, $D_{\mathbf{t}} f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{t} = 0$, so $\nabla f \perp \mathbf{t}$.

Gradient is Perpendicular to Level Curves

The gradient always points “straight uphill”—directly away from one contour line and toward the next higher one. If contours are circular, gradient descent heads straight for the minimum. If contours are elongated ellipses, gradient descent zigzags.

This perpendicularity has beautiful consequences for optimization. When contours are elongated (as for $f(x,y) = x^2 + 100y^2$), the gradient points at an angle to the direct path to the minimum, causing iterates to zigzag—the key insight behind why advanced optimizers exist.

Directional Derivatives in Optimization

To minimize a loss $L(\boldsymbol{\theta})$, we want the direction $\mathbf{u}$ that makes $D_{\mathbf{u}} L = \nabla L \cdot \mathbf{u}$ as negative as possible. By the $\cos\theta$ formula, this happens when $\mathbf{u} = -\nabla L / \|\nabla L\|$.

Gradient Descent Update Rule

$$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \eta \nabla L(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$ where $\eta > 0$ is the learning rate. Each update moves in the negative gradient direction—the direction of steepest decrease.

The learning rate $\eta$ controls step size. Too small means slow convergence; too large means overshooting and divergence. The sweet spot depends on curvature: high curvature needs smaller steps, flat regions allow larger steps.

Geometric Picture of the Learning Rate

The step length is $\eta \|\nabla L\|$. Near a minimum where $\|\nabla L\|$ is small, steps automatically shrink. Far away where $\|\nabla L\|$ is large, steps are bigger. This built-in adaptivity is helpful but can cause trouble when gradient magnitudes vary wildly across parameter directions.

The Gradient Descent Algorithm

Algorithm: Gradient Descent
1
Initialize: Choose $\boldsymbol{\theta}_0$ and learning rate $\eta > 0$.
2
Compute gradient: Evaluate $\nabla L(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$.
3
Update: $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \eta \nabla L(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$.
4
Check convergence: If $\|\nabla L\| < \epsilon$ or loss plateaus, stop. Otherwise go to Step 2.

Convergence Conditions

For convex functions with Lipschitz gradients ($\|\nabla L(\mathbf{x}) - \nabla L(\mathbf{y})\| \leq M\|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}\|$), choosing $\eta \leq 1/M$ ensures $O(1/t)$ convergence. For strongly convex functions, convergence is exponential: error decreases as $(1 - \eta\mu)^t$ where $\mu$ is the smallest Hessian eigenvalue. The condition number $M/\mu$ controls convergence speed.

Step Size Strategies

Constant: simplest, chosen by trial. Schedules: decrease $\eta$ over time (e.g., $\eta_t = \eta_0 / (1 + \alpha t)$). Line search: choose $\eta_t$ to minimize loss along the gradient direction—expensive but precise.

Why Learning Rate Matters So Much

The learning rate is often the most important hyperparameter in deep learning. Modern practice uses learning rate warmup (start small, increase, then decay) to stabilize early training.

Beyond Steepest Descent

If the gradient gives the steepest descent direction, why would we ever want anything else? Consider $f(x, y) = x^2 + 100y^2$: the level curves are elongated ellipses, and the gradient does not point toward the minimum. It points nearly perpendicular to the direct path, causing gradient descent to zigzag wildly.

Momentum

Momentum (Polyak, 1964) maintains a running average of past gradients:

$$\mathbf{v}_{t+1} = \beta \mathbf{v}_t + \nabla L(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t), \qquad \boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \eta \mathbf{v}_{t+1}$$

Here $\beta \in [0, 1)$ is the momentum coefficient (typically 0.9). Like a heavy ball rolling downhill, inertia carries through oscillations while accelerating along consistent downhill directions. Oscillating gradient components cancel; consistent components accumulate.

Adam Optimizer

Adam (Kingma and Ba, 2015) adapts the learning rate per parameter using first and second moment estimates:

$$\mathbf{m}_t = \beta_1 \mathbf{m}_{t-1} + (1 - \beta_1)\mathbf{g}_t, \qquad \mathbf{v}_t = \beta_2 \mathbf{v}_{t-1} + (1 - \beta_2)\mathbf{g}_t^2$$ $$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \eta \frac{\hat{\mathbf{m}}_t}{\sqrt{\hat{\mathbf{v}}_t} + \epsilon}$$

Each parameter gets effective learning rate $\eta / (\sqrt{\hat{v}_i} + \epsilon)$. Parameters with large gradients get smaller rates (prevents overshooting); parameters with small gradients get larger rates (speeds convergence).

Why Steepest Descent Can Zigzag

Steepest descent is locally optimal but globally myopic. Momentum smooths the trajectory; Adam rescales axes to make effective contours circular. Both exploit the geometric insight that the gradient tells you the best direction right now, but the best trajectory considers curvature.

Information-Theoretic Connection

One of the deepest applications of directional derivatives appears in information geometry. When minimizing KL divergence $D_{\text{KL}}(p^* \| p_{\boldsymbol{\theta}})$, standard gradient descent uses Euclidean geometry of parameter space. But this may not reflect the intrinsic geometry of probability distributions—a small step in $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ might cause a tiny change in one distribution but a huge change in another.

The Fisher Information Matrix and Natural Gradients

The Fisher Information Matrix measures intrinsic curvature:

$$F_{ij}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}} \left[ \frac{\partial \log p_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(x)}{\partial \theta_i} \cdot \frac{\partial \log p_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(x)}{\partial \theta_j} \right]$$

The natural gradient (Amari, 1998) uses $\mathbf{F}^{-1} \nabla L$ instead:

$$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \eta \mathbf{F}^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t) \nabla L(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$

Natural gradient descent is invariant to reparameterization—a powerful property ordinary GD lacks. Approximations like K-FAC make this practical for deep learning.

Connection to Entropy

The Fisher Information Matrix is the Hessian of the KL divergence, and $D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q) = H(p, q) - H(p)$. So the natural gradient computes directional derivatives on the entropy landscape using the intrinsic metric that entropy itself defines—the deepest connection between calculus and information theory.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1: “The gradient is a scalar”

The gradient $\nabla f$ is a vector, not a scalar. It has $n$ components. The directional derivative $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u}$ is the scalar. Confusing the gradient (vector) with the directional derivative (scalar) is a common error.

Misconception 2: “Directional derivatives are always positive”

The sign depends on direction! If $\theta < \pi/2$ between $\nabla f$ and $\mathbf{u}$, the derivative is positive. If $\theta > \pi/2$, negative. If $\theta = \pi/2$, zero.

Misconception 3: “You must use a unit vector”

The directional derivative is defined with a unit vector, but $\nabla f \cdot \mathbf{v}$ for non-unit $\mathbf{v}$ gives $\|\mathbf{v}\|$ times the directional derivative. Forgetting to normalize is a common mistake.

Misconception 4: “The gradient always points toward the minimum”

The gradient points toward steepest increase. The negative gradient indicates the locally steepest downhill direction, but may lead to a local minimum, saddle point, or away from the global minimum. Gradient descent is a greedy local algorithm.

Misconception 5: “Differentiable means all directional derivatives exist”

This is backwards. A function can have all directional derivatives without being differentiable! Differentiability requires a linear approximation. If $f$ is differentiable, then $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u}$. This subtlety matters for non-smooth functions like ReLU.

Easy Exercises (1–10)

Exercise 1: Basic Gradient Computation

Compute the gradient of $f(x, y) = 3x^2 + 2xy + y^2$ at $(1, -1)$.

Show Solution

$\nabla f = (6x + 2y, 2x + 2y)$. At $(1,-1)$: $\nabla f = (4, 0)$.

Answer: $(4, 0)$

Exercise 2: Normalize a Direction

Find the unit vector in the direction of $\mathbf{v} = (5, 12)$.

Show Solution

$\|\mathbf{v}\| = 13$, so $\mathbf{u} = (5/13, 12/13)$.

Exercise 3: Directional Derivative Along an Axis

Let $f(x, y) = x^3 - y^2$. Compute $D_{\mathbf{e}_1} f(2, 3)$.

Show Solution

$D_{\mathbf{e}_1} f = \partial f/\partial x = 3x^2$. At $(2,3)$: $3(4) = 12$.

Exercise 4: Directional Derivative via Dot Product

Let $f(x, y) = xy$. Find $D_{\mathbf{u}} f$ at $(3, 4)$ in direction $\mathbf{u} = (1/\sqrt{2}, 1/\sqrt{2})$.

Show Solution

$\nabla f(3,4) = (4, 3)$. $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = (4,3) \cdot (1/\sqrt{2}, 1/\sqrt{2}) = 7/\sqrt{2} = 7\sqrt{2}/2 \approx 4.95$.

Exercise 5: Maximum Rate of Change

Let $f(x, y) = e^x \sin y$. What is the maximum rate of change at $(\ln 2, \pi/6)$?

Show Solution

$\nabla f = (e^x \sin y, e^x \cos y)$. At $(\ln 2, \pi/6)$: $(1, \sqrt{3})$.

Maximum rate = $\|\nabla f\| = \sqrt{1 + 3} = 2$.

Exercise 6: Zero Directional Derivative

At $(1, 1)$, $\nabla f = (2, -2)$. Find a direction $\mathbf{u}$ with $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = 0$.

Show Solution

Need $\mathbf{u} \perp (2, -2)$. Perpendicular: $(2, 2)$. Normalized: $(1/\sqrt{2}, 1/\sqrt{2})$.

Exercise 7: Gradient Descent Step

Let $L(\theta_1, \theta_2) = \theta_1^2 + 4\theta_2^2$. From $(2, 1)$ with $\eta = 0.1$, compute one step.

Show Solution

$\nabla L = (4, 8)$. Update: $(2, 1) - 0.1(4, 8) = (1.6, 0.2)$.

Exercise 8: Level Curve Tangent

At a point where $\nabla f = (3, 4)$, what is the tangent to the level curve?

Show Solution

Perpendicular to $(3, 4)$: $(-4, 3)$. Normalized: $(-4/5, 3/5)$.

Exercise 9: Steepest Descent Direction

If $\nabla f(2, 3) = (-1, 5)$, in which direction does $f$ decrease fastest?

Show Solution

$-\nabla f = (1, -5)$. Normalized: $(1/\sqrt{26}, -5/\sqrt{26})$. Rate: $\sqrt{26}$.

Exercise 10: Angle Between Gradient and Direction

If $\nabla f = (1, 1)$ and $\mathbf{u} = (1, 0)$, find $\theta$ and $D_{\mathbf{u}} f$.

Show Solution

$\cos\theta = 1/\sqrt{2}$, so $\theta = 45°$. $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = \sqrt{2} \cdot 1/\sqrt{2} = 1$.

Medium Exercises (11–20)

Exercise 11: 3D Directional Derivative

Let $f(x,y,z) = x^2 y + yz^2$. Find $D_{\mathbf{u}} f$ at $(1, 2, -1)$ in direction $(1, 2, 2)$.

Show Solution

$\nabla f = (4, 2, -4)$. $\mathbf{u} = (1/3, 2/3, 2/3)$. $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = 4/3 + 4/3 - 8/3 = 0$. This direction is along a level surface!

Exercise 12: Direction for a Given Rate

At a point where $\nabla f = (6, 8)$, find all unit directions with $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = 5$.

Show Solution

$\|\nabla f\| = 10$. Need $\cos\theta = 1/2$, so $\theta = \pm 60°$. Two directions at $\pm 60°$ from the gradient.

Exercise 13: Two Gradient Descent Steps

Let $L(\theta) = \theta^4$. From $\theta_0 = 2$, $\eta = 0.01$, compute $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$.

Show Solution

$L' = 4\theta^3$. $\theta_1 = 2 - 0.01(32) = 1.68$. $\theta_2 = 1.68 - 0.01(18.97) \approx 1.490$.

Exercise 14: Normal to a Surface

Find the normal vector to $x^2 + 2y^2 + z^2 = 9$ at $(1, 1, \sqrt{6})$.

Show Solution

$\nabla g = (2x, 4y, 2z)$. At the point: $(2, 4, 2\sqrt{6})$.

Exercise 15: Tangent Plane Equation

Find the tangent plane to $z = x^2 + y^2$ at $(1, 2, 5)$.

Show Solution

$\nabla g = (2x, 2y, -1)$ for $g = x^2 + y^2 - z$. At $(1,2,5)$: $(2, 4, -1)$.

Tangent plane: $z = 2x + 4y - 5$.

Exercise 16: Condition Number

For $f(x, y) = x^2 + 25y^2$, compute the condition number $\kappa$ of the Hessian.

Show Solution

Hessian eigenvalues: 2 and 50. $\kappa = 50/2 = 25$. Large $\kappa$ causes zigzagging in GD.

Exercise 17: Momentum Update

Starting at $\theta_0 = 3$, $v_0 = 0$, $L' = 2\theta$, $\eta = 0.1$, $\beta = 0.9$. Compute $(v_1, \theta_1)$.

Show Solution

$v_1 = 0.9(0) + 6 = 6$. $\theta_1 = 3 - 0.1(6) = 2.4$.

Exercise 18: Directional Derivative From the Definition

Using the limit definition, compute $D_{(0,1)} f(1, 0)$ for $f(x, y) = x^2 + y$.

Show Solution

$\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(1, h) - f(1, 0)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(1 + h) - 1}{h} = 1$. Check: $\nabla f \cdot (0,1) = (2,1) \cdot (0,1) = 1$. ✓

Exercise 19: Gradient of Cross-Entropy Loss

Show that $dL/d\theta = \hat{y} - y$ for binary cross-entropy with $\hat{y} = \sigma(\theta)$.

Show Solution

Using $\sigma' = \hat{y}(1 - \hat{y})$ and chain rule:

$dL/d\theta = -[y(1 - \hat{y}) - (1-y)\hat{y}] = -(y - \hat{y}) = \hat{y} - y$.

Exercise 20: Perpendicularity of Gradient and Level Curve

Verify $\nabla f \perp$ level curve $x^2 + y^2 = 2$ at $(1, 1)$.

Show Solution

$\nabla f(1,1) = (2, 2)$. Tangent to circle at $(1,1)$: $(-1, 1)$. Dot product: $-2 + 2 = 0$. ✓

Hard Exercises (21–30)

Exercise 21: Directional Derivative Without Differentiability

Let $f(x, y) = x^2 y/(x^2 + y^2)$ (with $f(0,0) = 0$). Show all directional derivatives exist at origin but $f$ is not differentiable.

Show Solution

For $\mathbf{u} = (a, b)$: $D_{\mathbf{u}} f(0,0) = a^2 b$. This is not linear in $(a, b)$, so $f$ is not differentiable at the origin despite all directional derivatives existing.

Exercise 22: Convergence Rate of Gradient Descent

For $f(x) = \frac{1}{2}ax^2$, show GD converges when $\eta < 2/a$ and find the contraction factor.

Show Solution

$x_{t+1} = (1 - \eta a)x_t$. Converges when $|1 - \eta a| < 1$, i.e., $0 < \eta < 2/a$.

Contraction factor: $|1 - \eta a|$, minimized at $\eta = 1/a$ (one-step convergence!).

Exercise 23: GD on an Ill-Conditioned Quadratic

For $f(x,y) = \frac{1}{2}(x^2 + 100y^2)$, from $(100, 1)$ with $\eta = 0.01$, compute two iterates.

Show Solution

Step 1: $(100,1) - 0.01(100, 100) = (99, 0)$. Step 2: $(99, 0) - 0.01(99, 0) = (98.01, 0)$.

The $y$-coordinate converges fast but $x$ converges 100x slower due to condition number $\kappa = 100$.

Exercise 24: Gradient of KL Divergence

For Bernoulli $q_\theta$ vs $p$ with $p_0 = 0.3$, compute $\frac{d}{d\theta} D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q_\theta)$.

Show Solution

$\frac{d}{d\theta} D_{\text{KL}} = -p_0/\theta + (1-p_0)/(1-\theta)$. Setting to zero: $\theta^* = p_0 = 0.3$.

Exercise 25: Fisher Information for Gaussian

Compute the Fisher Information $I(\mu)$ for $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with known $\sigma^2$.

Show Solution

$\partial_\mu \log p = (x - \mu)/\sigma^2$. $I(\mu) = \mathbb{E}[(x-\mu)^2/\sigma^4] = 1/\sigma^2$.

Higher variance = less information about $\mu$.

Exercise 26: Natural Gradient vs. Ordinary Gradient

For $\mathcal{N}(\mu, 1)$ with $L(\mu) = \frac{1}{2}(\mu - 3)^2$, compare ordinary and natural GD from $\mu_0 = 0$, $\eta = 0.5$.

Show Solution

$\nabla L(0) = -3$. Ordinary: $\mu_1 = 1.5$. Natural: $F^{-1} = I = 1$, so also $\mu_1 = 1.5$.

They coincide because $I = 1$. They differ when Fisher matrix is non-identity.

Exercise 27: Lagrange Multiplier via Gradients

Minimize $f(x, y) = x + y$ subject to $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ using $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$.

Show Solution

$\nabla f = (1,1)$, $\nabla g = (2x, 2y)$. So $x = y$. Constraint: $x = \pm 1/\sqrt{2}$.

Minimum: $f = -\sqrt{2}$ at $(-1/\sqrt{2}, -1/\sqrt{2})$.

Exercise 28: Adam First Step

For $L(\theta) = \theta^2$ from $\theta_0 = 4$, $\eta = 0.1$, $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.999$, compute one Adam step.

Show Solution

$g_1 = 8$. $m_1 = 0.8$, $v_1 = 0.064$. Bias-corrected: $\hat{m} = 8$, $\hat{v} = 64$.

$\theta_1 = 4 - 0.1(8/8) = 3.9$. Adam's effective step is $\approx \eta$ regardless of gradient magnitude.

Exercise 29: Gradient Flow as ODE

Solve the gradient flow ODE $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = -\nabla f$ for $f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2$.

Show Solution

$\dot{x} = -2x$, $\dot{y} = -2y$. Solution: $x(t) = x_0 e^{-2t}$, $y(t) = y_0 e^{-2t}$.

Straight-line trajectory to origin with exponential decay. No zigzagging due to isotropic curvature.

Exercise 30: Directional Derivative of Entropy

Compute $\nabla H$ for $H(\mathbf{p}) = -\sum p_i \ln p_i$ and the directional derivative shifting probability from $p_2$ to $p_1$.

Show Solution

$\partial H/\partial p_i = -(\ln p_i + 1)$. Direction $\mathbf{u} = (1, -1, 0, \ldots)/\sqrt{2}$.
