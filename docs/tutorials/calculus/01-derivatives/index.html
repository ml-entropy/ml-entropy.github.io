01. Derivatives of One Variable

The derivative is the mathematical tool that captures instantaneous change. It is the foundation of gradient descent—the algorithm that trains every neural network—and it connects deeply to information theory through the score function and Fisher information. This tutorial derives the concept from first principles, proves the key rules, and builds the intuition you need for machine learning.

The Definition

Imagine you are tracking the position of a particle moving along a line. At time $t = 2$ seconds the particle is at position $s = 10$ meters, and at $t = 5$ seconds it is at $s = 31$ meters. You can easily compute the average velocity over that interval: $(31 - 10)/(5 - 2) = 7$ m/s. But that number tells you nothing about what the particle was doing at, say, $t = 3$. Was it speeding up? Slowing down? Momentarily at rest? The average smooths away all that detail.

To recover the instantaneous behaviour, you need to shrink the time interval toward zero. If the position function is $s(t)$, the average velocity between times $t$ and $t + h$ is $\frac{s(t+h) - s(t)}{h}$. As $h \to 0$, this ratio converges (when the function is "nice enough") to a single number—the instantaneous velocity at time $t$. That number is the derivative.

Notice what is happening conceptually: we are replacing a global, coarse measurement (average rate of change over a large interval) with a local, infinitely precise one (instantaneous rate of change at a point). This idea—zooming in until a potentially complicated curve looks like a straight line—is the beating heart of differential calculus. Every function that is differentiable at a point looks linear when you zoom in enough, and the derivative tells you the slope of that local line.

Derivative (Limit Definition)

The derivative of a function $f$ at the point $x$ is defined as

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h}$$

provided this limit exists and is finite. The ratio inside the limit is called the difference quotient. Alternative notations include $\dfrac{df}{dx}$, $\dot{f}(x)$, and $Df(x)$.

Why is this the "only correct" definition? Because any other candidate—for instance using a symmetric difference $\frac{f(x+h)-f(x-h)}{2h}$—can give a finite answer even when the function has a corner (like $|x|$ at $x = 0$). The one-sided limit definition correctly detects that the left and right slopes disagree and declares the derivative undefined at a corner. It is the most conservative, most honest definition: it only reports a slope when the function truly looks linear from both sides.

Geometrically, $f'(x)$ is the slope of the tangent line to the graph of $f$ at the point $(x, f(x))$. The secant line through $(x, f(x))$ and $(x+h, f(x+h))$ has slope equal to the difference quotient; as $h \to 0$, the secant pivots into the tangent.

Worked Example: $f(x) = x^2$

Let's carefully compute the derivative of the simplest non-linear function by expanding the definition step by step.

Computing $\frac{d}{dx}(x^2)$ from the Definition
1
Form the difference: $f(x+h) - f(x) = (x+h)^2 - x^2 = x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2 = 2xh + h^2$
2
Form the difference quotient: $\dfrac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} = \dfrac{2xh + h^2}{h} = 2x + h$
We can cancel the $h$ because $h \neq 0$ (we only take $h \to 0$, never $h = 0$).
3
Take the limit: $f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0}(2x + h) = 2x$

The result $f'(x) = 2x$ says that the rate of change of $x^2$ at any point $x$ is $2x$. At $x = 3$, the function is increasing at rate $6$; at $x = -1$, it is decreasing at rate $2$ (the slope is $-2$). At $x = 0$, the derivative is zero, confirming that the parabola has a flat tangent at its vertex.

Connection to Information Theory

In information theory, the surprise (or self-information) of an event with probability $p$ is $-\log p$. If we view $p$ as a function of some parameter $\theta$, then differentiating surprise with respect to $\theta$ gives us the score function $\frac{d}{d\theta}[-\log p(\theta)] = -\frac{p'(\theta)}{p(\theta)}$. This derivative tells us how sensitive our "surprise" is to small changes in the parameter—a concept at the core of Fisher information and maximum likelihood estimation. We will return to this idea in the Derivatives & Information Theory section below.

Differentiation Rules

Computing derivatives from the limit definition every single time would be extraordinarily tedious. Fortunately, a small set of rules lets us differentiate virtually any function built from elementary pieces. These rules are not magic shortcuts—each one is a theorem that can be proved directly from the limit definition. Understanding the proofs deepens your intuition and helps you apply the rules correctly in tricky situations.

The Power Rule

Power Rule

For any real number $n$: $$\frac{d}{dx} x^n = n x^{n-1}$$

Let's prove this for positive integers using the binomial theorem. The proof for general real exponents requires logarithmic differentiation, which we defer until after the chain rule.

Proof of the Power Rule (integer $n \geq 1$)
1
Expand $(x+h)^n$ using the binomial theorem: $$(x+h)^n = x^n + n x^{n-1} h + \binom{n}{2} x^{n-2} h^2 + \cdots + h^n$$
2
Subtract $x^n$ and divide by $h$: $$\frac{(x+h)^n - x^n}{h} = n x^{n-1} + \binom{n}{2} x^{n-2} h + \cdots + h^{n-1}$$ Every term after the first contains at least one factor of $h$.
3
Take $h \to 0$: all terms with an $h$ factor vanish, leaving $$\frac{d}{dx} x^n = n x^{n-1} \quad \square$$

The Sum Rule

Sum Rule

$$(f + g)'(x) = f'(x) + g'(x)$$ Differentiation is a linear operator: the derivative of a sum is the sum of the derivatives. Combined with constant multiples, $(cf)' = c \cdot f'$, this means $\frac{d}{dx}[af(x) + bg(x)] = af'(x) + bg'(x)$ for any constants $a, b$.

The Product Rule

Product Rule

$$(fg)'(x) = f'(x)\,g(x) + f(x)\,g'(x)$$

The product rule is the first rule that surprises students—the derivative of a product is not the product of the derivatives. Intuitively, when two quantities are multiplied together and both are changing, the total rate of change has two contributions: $f$ changes while $g$ stays put, and $g$ changes while $f$ stays put. Think of the area of a rectangle with sides $f$ and $g$: if both sides grow, the area change is approximately $(\Delta f) \cdot g + f \cdot (\Delta g)$.

Proof of the Product Rule
1
Write the difference quotient and add zero cleverly: $$\frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x)}{h}$$ We add and subtract $f(x+h)g(x)$ in the numerator: $$= \frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x+h)g(x) + f(x+h)g(x) - f(x)g(x)}{h}$$
2
Factor each pair: $$= f(x+h) \cdot \frac{g(x+h) - g(x)}{h} + g(x) \cdot \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$
3
Take $h \to 0$: Since $f$ is differentiable (hence continuous), $f(x+h) \to f(x)$. The two fractions become $g'(x)$ and $f'(x)$ respectively: $$(fg)'(x) = f(x)\,g'(x) + g(x)\,f'(x) \quad \square$$

The Quotient Rule

Quotient Rule

$$\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)'(x) = \frac{f'(x)\,g(x) - f(x)\,g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2}$$

You might wonder: do we really need a separate quotient rule? Not really. A quotient $f/g$ is just the product $f \cdot g^{-1}$. If you apply the product rule to $f$ and $g^{-1}$, and use the chain rule (next section) to differentiate $g^{-1}$, you recover the quotient rule exactly. Many experienced mathematicians never memorise the quotient rule—they just use the product rule and chain rule instead. Nevertheless, the quotient rule is a convenient shorthand for divisions that arise frequently, such as $\tan x = \sin x / \cos x$.

Common Mistake

Students frequently write $(fg)' = f' \cdot g'$. This is wrong. The derivative of a product is not the product of the derivatives. Always use the product rule: $(fg)' = f'g + fg'$. A quick sanity check: differentiate $f(x) = x \cdot x = x^2$. The product rule gives $1 \cdot x + x \cdot 1 = 2x$, which matches $(x^2)' = 2x$. The incorrect formula would give $1 \cdot 1 = 1$, which is obviously wrong.

The Chain Rule

If you learn only one differentiation rule for machine learning, make it the chain rule. Every time you compute a gradient in a neural network, you are applying the chain rule—possibly thousands of times in a single backward pass. The chain rule is what makes backpropagation possible.

The fundamental idea is simple: if a change in $x$ causes a change in $u$, and that change in $u$ causes a change in $y$, then the total effect of $x$ on $y$ is the product of the two individual effects. Think of gear ratios: if gear A turns gear B at 3× speed, and gear B turns gear C at 5× speed, then gear A turns gear C at $3 \times 5 = 15$× speed.

Chain Rule

If $y = f(u)$ and $u = g(x)$, i.e., $y = f(g(x))$, then:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$

Read this as: "the derivative of the outside function, evaluated at the inside, times the derivative of the inside function." Let's build fluency with several examples of increasing complexity.

Example 1: $y = (3x + 1)^5$

Identify the outer function $f(u) = u^5$ and the inner function $g(x) = 3x + 1$. Then $f'(u) = 5u^4$ and $g'(x) = 3$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 5(3x+1)^4 \cdot 3 = 15(3x+1)^4$$

Example 2: $y = \ln(\sin x)$ — Nested Transcendentals

Outer: $f(u) = \ln u$, so $f'(u) = 1/u$. Inner: $g(x) = \sin x$, so $g'(x) = \cos x$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sin x} \cdot \cos x = \frac{\cos x}{\sin x} = \cot x$$

This is a useful result on its own: $(\ln \sin x)' = \cot x$. It appears when differentiating log-probabilities of distributions defined on angles or circular domains.

Example 3: Derivative of the Sigmoid — $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-x}}$

The sigmoid is ubiquitous in ML as an activation function and in logistic regression. Let's compute its derivative using the chain rule. Write $\sigma(x) = (1 + e^{-x})^{-1}$.

Deriving $\sigma'(x) = \sigma(x)(1 - \sigma(x))$
1
Let $u = 1 + e^{-x}$, so $\sigma = u^{-1}$. By the chain rule: $$\sigma'(x) = -u^{-2} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = -\frac{1}{(1+e^{-x})^2} \cdot (-e^{-x})$$ (We used the chain rule again inside: $\frac{d}{dx}e^{-x} = -e^{-x}$.)
2
Simplify: $$\sigma'(x) = \frac{e^{-x}}{(1+e^{-x})^2}$$
3
Now observe that $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$ and $1 - \sigma(x) = \frac{e^{-x}}{1+e^{-x}}$. Therefore: $$\sigma(x)(1 - \sigma(x)) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}} \cdot \frac{e^{-x}}{1+e^{-x}} = \frac{e^{-x}}{(1+e^{-x})^2} = \sigma'(x) \quad \square$$

This elegant identity—$\sigma' = \sigma(1 - \sigma)$—means that during backpropagation, if you have already computed $\sigma(x)$ in the forward pass, the derivative costs just one multiplication and one subtraction. No exponentials needed. This is why the sigmoid was historically popular: it is cheap to differentiate.

Example 4: Log of Sigmoid — $\log \sigma(x)$

This function appears in the binary cross-entropy loss. Applying the chain rule:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\log \sigma(x) = \frac{\sigma'(x)}{\sigma(x)} = \frac{\sigma(x)(1-\sigma(x))}{\sigma(x)} = 1 - \sigma(x)$$

A beautifully simple result. Similarly, $\frac{d}{dx}\log(1 - \sigma(x)) = -\sigma(x)$, which you can verify as an exercise. These two identities make gradient computations for logistic regression especially clean.

The Chain Rule and Computational Graphs

In modern deep learning frameworks like PyTorch and TensorFlow, computations are represented as directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) where each node is an elementary operation (add, multiply, apply $\exp$, etc.) and edges carry intermediate values. The chain rule tells us that the derivative of the final output with respect to any intermediate variable is the product of derivatives along every path from that variable to the output.

Consider a simple two-layer network: $\hat{y} = \sigma(w_2 \cdot \sigma(w_1 x + b_1) + b_2)$. To find $\frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial w_1}$, we chain together the derivatives of $\sigma$, multiplication by $w_2$, $\sigma$ again, and multiplication by $x$. Each of these is a simple, local computation. The genius of backpropagation is that it evaluates this chain efficiently by reusing intermediate results, visiting each node only once.

Why the Chain Rule IS Backpropagation

Backpropagation is not a separate algorithm from the chain rule—it is the chain rule, applied in a specific order (reverse topological order of the computation graph) to avoid redundant work. Without the chain rule, training a 100-layer neural network would require separately perturbing each of the millions of weights and measuring the change in loss, which would be computationally infeasible.

With the chain rule, a single backward pass computes all gradients in roughly the same time as the forward pass. This is the miracle that makes modern deep learning possible.

Common Derivatives

Certain derivatives appear so frequently in machine learning and statistics that they are worth memorising. Each one can be derived from the limit definition, but in practice you will simply recall them and combine them with the rules above. Let's go through the most important ones with brief justifications.

$$\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dx} e^x &= e^x \\[6pt] \frac{d}{dx} \ln x &= \frac{1}{x} \quad (x > 0)\\[6pt] \frac{d}{dx} a^x &= a^x \ln a \quad (a > 0)\\[6pt] \frac{d}{dx} \sin x &= \cos x \\[6pt] \frac{d}{dx} \cos x &= -\sin x \\[6pt] \frac{d}{dx} \sigma(x) &= \sigma(x)(1 - \sigma(x)) \quad \text{(sigmoid)} \end{aligned}$$

The first of these is perhaps the most remarkable fact in all of calculus: the exponential function is its own derivative. This property is actually what defines $e$: it is the unique base $a$ for which $\frac{d}{dx}a^x|_{x=0} = 1$. In information theory and ML, $e^x$ appears constantly because the natural logarithm $\ln x$ (its inverse) is the standard log used to measure information in nats.

The derivative $(\ln x)' = 1/x$ is the single most important derivative for statistics. Every time you take a log-likelihood and differentiate to find a maximum likelihood estimate, you are using this identity (combined with the chain rule). The $1/x$ factor is what converts multiplicative structure in the likelihood into additive structure in the log-likelihood, making optimisation vastly easier.

Why $e^x$ Is Its Own Derivative

From the definition: $\frac{d}{dx}e^x = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^{x+h} - e^x}{h} = e^x \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^h - 1}{h}$. The key limit $\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^h - 1}{h} = 1$ is essentially the definition of the number $e$. One common definition is $e = \lim_{n \to \infty}(1 + 1/n)^n$, and from this one can show the key limit equals 1. So the self-derivative property is not a coincidence—it is the reason $e$ is defined the way it is.

Worked Example: Derivative of $\tanh x$

The hyperbolic tangent $\tanh x = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}}$ is a popular activation function. Let's differentiate it using the quotient rule with $f = e^x - e^{-x}$ and $g = e^x + e^{-x}$.

Derivative of $\tanh x$
1
$f' = e^x + e^{-x}$, $g' = e^x - e^{-x}$. Apply the quotient rule: $$\frac{d}{dx}\tanh x = \frac{(e^x + e^{-x})^2 - (e^x - e^{-x})^2}{(e^x + e^{-x})^2}$$
2
Expand the numerator using the difference of squares $(a^2 - b^2) = (a+b)(a-b)$: $$(e^x+e^{-x})^2 - (e^x-e^{-x})^2 = [(e^x+e^{-x})+(e^x-e^{-x})][(e^x+e^{-x})-(e^x-e^{-x})] = 2e^x \cdot 2e^{-x} = 4$$
3
Therefore $\dfrac{d}{dx}\tanh x = \dfrac{4}{(e^x + e^{-x})^2} = 1 - \tanh^2 x$

Like the sigmoid, $\tanh$ has a derivative expressible in terms of itself: $\tanh'(x) = 1 - \tanh^2(x)$. This "self-referential" property is shared by many activation functions, and it is precisely why they are chosen: if you compute the function value in the forward pass, the derivative is essentially free.

Derivatives in Optimization

The primary reason derivatives matter for machine learning is optimisation. Training a model means finding parameter values that minimise a loss function, and the derivative tells us the direction of steepest descent. Without derivatives, we would be searching for the bottom of a valley blindfolded; with derivatives, we have a compass pointing downhill.

Critical Points and the Second Derivative Test

A critical point of $f$ is any point where $f'(x) = 0$ or where $f'$ does not exist. At a critical point, the function is momentarily "flat"—neither increasing nor decreasing. But a flat tangent could correspond to a hilltop (local maximum), a valley floor (local minimum), or a saddle (inflection point). To distinguish these, we use the second derivative:

  • $f''(x) > 0$: the slope is increasing, so the curve bends upward → local minimum
  • $f''(x) < 0$: the slope is decreasing, so the curve bends downward → local maximum
  • $f''(x) = 0$: the test is inconclusive (could be an inflection point like $x^3$ at $x=0$)

The second derivative measures the curvature of the function. In optimisation, curvature determines how aggressively we can step: high curvature means the gradient changes quickly, so we should take small steps; low curvature means the gradient is nearly constant, so larger steps are safe. This idea is the foundation of second-order optimisation methods like Newton's method.

Gradient Descent

Gradient descent is the workhorse algorithm of machine learning. The update rule is disarmingly simple:

$$x_{\text{new}} = x_{\text{old}} - \alpha \cdot f'(x_{\text{old}})$$

Here $\alpha > 0$ is the learning rate. If $f'(x) > 0$, the function is increasing at $x$, so we step left (decrease $x$) to move downhill. If $f'(x) < 0$, the function is decreasing, so we step right. The magnitude $|f'(x)|$ controls how big the step is: steeper slopes lead to bigger steps, and near a minimum (where $f' \approx 0$) the steps naturally become small, causing the algorithm to settle.

Choosing $\alpha$ is one of the most important practical decisions in training. Too large and the algorithm overshoots the minimum, bouncing back and forth or even diverging. Too small and convergence is painfully slow. Modern variants like Adam, RMSprop, and AdaGrad adaptively adjust the learning rate during training, but they all fundamentally rely on the same idea: move in the direction opposite to the gradient.

Newton's Method: Using the Second Derivative

Newton's method refines gradient descent by also using curvature information: $x_{\text{new}} = x_{\text{old}} - \frac{f'(x_{\text{old}})}{f''(x_{\text{old}})}$. This is equivalent to fitting a parabola (quadratic approximation) at the current point and jumping directly to the minimum of that parabola. In one dimension, Newton's method converges much faster than gradient descent—but it requires computing the second derivative, which is expensive in high dimensions (it becomes the Hessian matrix).

Derivatives and Information Theory

One of the central themes of this tutorial series is viewing machine learning through the lens of information theory. Derivatives play a starring role in this connection. When we differentiate probability distributions, we unlock some of the deepest concepts in statistics: the score function, Fisher information, and the information-theoretic interpretation of gradient descent.

The Score Function

Consider a parametric family of probability distributions $p(x; \theta)$, where $\theta$ is the parameter we want to estimate. The score function is defined as the derivative of the log-likelihood with respect to $\theta$:

Score Function

$$s(\theta) = \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log p(x; \theta) = \frac{1}{p(x;\theta)} \cdot \frac{\partial p(x;\theta)}{\partial \theta}$$ The score measures the sensitivity of the log-probability to changes in the parameter. When the score is large, a small change in $\theta$ causes a big change in how "surprised" we are by the data.

Notice the chain rule at work: $\frac{d}{d\theta}\log p = \frac{1}{p} \cdot \frac{dp}{d\theta}$. The logarithm converts the absolute sensitivity $dp/d\theta$ into the relative sensitivity $\frac{1}{p}\frac{dp}{d\theta}$. This is crucial because a change of $0.01$ in probability matters much more when $p = 0.02$ (a 50% relative change) than when $p = 0.98$ (a 1% relative change). The score function automatically accounts for this.

A fundamental property is that the expected value of the score is zero under the true model: $E_\theta[s(\theta)] = 0$. This makes intuitive sense: at the true parameter value, there is no systematic direction in which the log-likelihood "wants" to move.

Fisher Information

If the expected score is zero, how do we measure how much information the data carries about $\theta$? We use the variance of the score, which is called the Fisher information:

Fisher Information

$$\mathcal{I}(\theta) = E\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\log p(x; \theta)\right)^2\right] = -E\left[\frac{\partial^2}{\partial \theta^2}\log p(x; \theta)\right]$$ The Fisher information quantifies how much information a random variable $X$ carries about the parameter $\theta$. Large Fisher information means the data is very informative; the likelihood function is sharply peaked around the true $\theta$.

The second equality (involving the second derivative) is remarkable: the curvature of the log-likelihood equals the variance of the score. Sharper curvature (larger $|f''|$) means the log-likelihood has a well-defined peak, so the data tightly constrains $\theta$. This directly connects to the second derivative test from our optimisation section: the second derivative of the log-likelihood tells us how "confident" we can be in our parameter estimate.

The celebrated Cramér–Rao bound states that no unbiased estimator can have variance smaller than $1/\mathcal{I}(\theta)$. The derivative of the log-likelihood thus sets a fundamental limit on how precisely we can estimate parameters—a beautiful bridge between calculus and information theory.

Gradient Descent on Cross-Entropy

In classification, we typically minimise the cross-entropy loss:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \log p(y_i \mid x_i; \theta)$$

This is the negative average log-likelihood. Each term $-\log p(y_i \mid x_i; \theta)$ is the surprise the model experiences when seeing the true label $y_i$. When we compute $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta}$ and apply gradient descent, we are literally reducing the model's average surprise. Training a neural network is, from the information-theoretic viewpoint, a process of making the model less and less surprised by the training data.

The gradient of the cross-entropy loss is the negative average of the score function: $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_i s_i(\theta)$. So each gradient descent step adjusts parameters in the direction that maximises the log-probability of the observed data—this is exactly maximum likelihood estimation, performed iteratively.

The Big Picture

Derivatives connect three fundamental ideas: (1) the geometric notion of slope/tangent lines, (2) the optimisation notion of gradient descent, and (3) the information-theoretic notion of score functions and Fisher information. Mastering derivatives means mastering the language that links geometry, optimisation, and information—the three pillars of modern machine learning.

Common Misconceptions

Even after learning the mechanics of differentiation, many students carry subtle misunderstandings that lead to errors in proofs, computations, and ML implementations. Let's address the most common ones head-on.

Misconception 1: "Continuous implies differentiable"

This is perhaps the most widespread confusion. A function can be perfectly continuous —no jumps, no gaps—and yet fail to be differentiable. The classic example is $f(x) = |x|$ at $x = 0$: the function is continuous (you can draw it without lifting your pen), but the graph has a sharp corner at the origin. The limit $\lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{|h|}{h} = 1$ from the right does not equal $\lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{|h|}{h} = -1$ from the left, so the derivative does not exist.

The correct implication goes the other way: differentiable implies continuous. If a function has a well-defined derivative at a point, it must be continuous there (this can be proved from the definition). But continuity is a strictly weaker condition than differentiability. In fact, there exist functions that are continuous everywhere but differentiable nowhere—the Weierstrass function is the famous example.

ML Relevance: ReLU

The ReLU activation $f(x) = \max(0, x)$ is continuous but not differentiable at $x = 0$. In practice, deep learning frameworks assign $f'(0) = 0$ (or sometimes $1$) by convention. This works fine because the probability of any particular input being exactly zero is negligible with floating-point arithmetic. But it is worth knowing that the derivative technically does not exist at that point.

Misconception 2: "The derivative at a point is the same as the derivative function"

There is an important distinction between $f'(3) = 6$ (a number) and $f'(x) = 2x$ (a function). The derivative at a point $f'(a)$ is a single real number that gives the slope of the tangent line at $x = a$. The derivative function $f'(x)$ is the function that maps each point $x$ to the slope at that point. When we write $\frac{d}{dx}(x^2) = 2x$, we are defining the derivative function; when we evaluate $f'(3) = 6$, we are computing the derivative at a specific point.

This distinction matters in ML when you compute gradients. The gradient $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$ is evaluated at the current parameter values $\theta_t$. It is a specific vector of numbers, not a symbolic expression. The symbolic derivative gives you the formula; plugging in the current parameters gives you the actual gradient for the update step.

Misconception 3: "'Infinitely small' is rigorous enough"

You will often hear casual descriptions like "the derivative is the ratio of infinitely small changes $dy$ and $dx$." While this intuition can be helpful, it is not mathematically rigorous. There is no such thing as an "infinitely small" real number in standard analysis. What is rigorous is the $\epsilon$-$\delta$ definition of limits: we say $\lim_{h \to 0} g(h) = L$ if for every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $0 < |h| < \delta$ implies $|g(h) - L| < \epsilon$.

The limit formulation avoids division by zero entirely. We never set $h = 0$; instead we show that the quotient $\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$ can be made arbitrarily close to some value $L$ by choosing $h$ small enough. The derivative is this value $L$—it is a perfectly finite real number, not an "infinitely small ratio." Leibniz's notation $\frac{dy}{dx}$ looks like a fraction, and it often behaves like one (that is exactly what the chain rule says), but this is a happy consequence of the limit definition, not its foundation.

Answer: $f'(x) = \cos x - 3\sin x$

Exercise 6: Negative Exponent

Differentiate $f(x) = x^{-3}$. Express with a negative exponent.

Show Solution

Power rule with $n = -3$:

$$f'(x) = -3x^{-4} = -\frac{3}{x^4}$$

Answer: $f'(x) = -3x^{-4}$

Exercise 7: Square Root

Differentiate $f(x) = \sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}$.

Show Solution

Power rule with $n = 1/2$:

$$f'(x) = \frac{1}{2}x^{-1/2} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$$

Answer: $f'(x) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$

Exercise 8: From the Definition

Use the limit definition to find the derivative of $f(x) = 3x + 5$.

Show Solution
$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{[3(x+h)+5]-[3x+5]}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{3h}{h} = 3$$

Answer: $f'(x) = 3$

Exercise 9: Tangent Line

Find the equation of the tangent line to $f(x) = x^2$ at $x = 3$.

Show Solution

$f(3) = 9$, $f'(x) = 2x$, so $f'(3) = 6$.

Tangent line: $y - 9 = 6(x - 3)$, i.e., $y = 6x - 9$.

Answer: $y = 6x - 9$

Exercise 10: Evaluating a Derivative

If $f(x) = e^x + \ln x$, find $f'(1)$.

Show Solution

$f'(x) = e^x + 1/x$, so $f'(1) = e^1 + 1 = e + 1 \approx 3.718$.

Answer: $f'(1) = e + 1$

Medium Exercises (11–20)

Exercise 11: Product Rule

Differentiate $f(x) = x^2 \sin x$.

Show Solution

Let $u = x^2$, $v = \sin x$. Then $u' = 2x$, $v' = \cos x$.

$$f'(x) = u'v + uv' = 2x\sin x + x^2\cos x$$

Answer: $f'(x) = 2x\sin x + x^2\cos x$

Exercise 12: Quotient Rule

Differentiate $f(x) = \dfrac{x}{x^2 + 1}$.

Show Solution

$f' = \dfrac{(1)(x^2+1) - x(2x)}{(x^2+1)^2} = \dfrac{x^2+1-2x^2}{(x^2+1)^2} = \dfrac{1-x^2}{(x^2+1)^2}$

Answer: $f'(x) = \dfrac{1-x^2}{(x^2+1)^2}$

Exercise 13: Chain Rule

Differentiate $f(x) = e^{3x^2}$.

Show Solution

Let $u = 3x^2$. Then $\frac{du}{dx} = 6x$ and $\frac{d}{du}e^u = e^u$.

$$f'(x) = e^{3x^2} \cdot 6x = 6x\,e^{3x^2}$$

Answer: $f'(x) = 6x\,e^{3x^2}$

Exercise 14: Chain + Power

Differentiate $f(x) = (2x^3 - x)^4$.

Show Solution

Let $u = 2x^3 - x$. Then $u' = 6x^2 - 1$.

$$f'(x) = 4(2x^3-x)^3 \cdot (6x^2 - 1)$$

Answer: $f'(x) = 4(6x^2-1)(2x^3-x)^3$

Exercise 15: Logarithmic Chain Rule

Differentiate $f(x) = \ln(x^2 + 1)$.

Show Solution
$$f'(x) = \frac{1}{x^2+1} \cdot 2x = \frac{2x}{x^2+1}$$

Answer: $f'(x) = \dfrac{2x}{x^2+1}$

Exercise 16: Critical Points

Find all critical points of $f(x) = x^3 - 3x + 2$ and classify each as local min, max, or neither.

Show Solution

$f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3 = 3(x-1)(x+1)$. Setting $f'(x) = 0$ gives $x = 1$ and $x = -1$.

$f''(x) = 6x$. At $x = 1$: $f''(1) = 6 > 0$ → local minimum. At $x = -1$: $f''(-1) = -6 < 0$ → local maximum.

Answer: Local min at $x = 1$, local max at $x = -1$.

Exercise 17: Product + Chain

Differentiate $f(x) = x e^{-x}$.

Show Solution

Product rule: $f'(x) = 1 \cdot e^{-x} + x \cdot (-e^{-x}) = e^{-x}(1 - x)$.

Answer: $f'(x) = (1-x)e^{-x}$

Exercise 18: Gradient Descent Step

Starting at $x_0 = 3$, perform one step of gradient descent with learning rate $\alpha = 0.1$ on $f(x) = x^2 - 4x + 5$. What is $x_1$?

Show Solution

$f'(x) = 2x - 4$, so $f'(3) = 2$.

$$x_1 = x_0 - \alpha f'(x_0) = 3 - 0.1 \times 2 = 2.8$$

Answer: $x_1 = 2.8$

Exercise 19: From the Definition

Use the limit definition to prove that $\frac{d}{dx}(x^3) = 3x^2$.

Show Solution

$(x+h)^3 = x^3 + 3x^2h + 3xh^2 + h^3$.

$$\frac{(x+h)^3 - x^3}{h} = \frac{3x^2h + 3xh^2 + h^3}{h} = 3x^2 + 3xh + h^2$$

Taking $h \to 0$: $f'(x) = 3x^2$. $\square$

Exercise 20: Composite Trig

Differentiate $f(x) = \sin^2(x) = (\sin x)^2$.

Show Solution

Chain rule with outer $u^2$ and inner $\sin x$:

$$f'(x) = 2\sin x \cdot \cos x = \sin(2x)$$

We used the double-angle identity $2\sin x \cos x = \sin(2x)$.

Answer: $f'(x) = \sin(2x)$

Hard Exercises (21–30)

Exercise 21: Sigmoid Derivative

Starting from $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$, prove that $\sigma'(x) = \sigma(x)(1-\sigma(x))$.

Show Solution

Write $\sigma(x) = (1+e^{-x})^{-1}$. Chain rule:

$$\sigma'(x) = -(1+e^{-x})^{-2} \cdot (-e^{-x}) = \frac{e^{-x}}{(1+e^{-x})^2}$$

Now $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$ and $1-\sigma(x) = \frac{e^{-x}}{1+e^{-x}}$, so:

$$\sigma(x)(1-\sigma(x)) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}} \cdot \frac{e^{-x}}{1+e^{-x}} = \frac{e^{-x}}{(1+e^{-x})^2} = \sigma'(x) \quad \square$$

Exercise 22: Log-Sigmoid Identity

Show that $\frac{d}{dx}\ln\sigma(x) = 1 - \sigma(x)$.

Show Solution

Chain rule: $\frac{d}{dx}\ln\sigma = \frac{\sigma'}{\sigma} = \frac{\sigma(1-\sigma)}{\sigma} = 1 - \sigma(x)$. $\square$

Exercise 23: Logarithmic Differentiation

Use logarithmic differentiation to find the derivative of $f(x) = x^x$ for $x > 0$.

Show Solution

Take $\ln$ of both sides: $\ln f = x \ln x$. Differentiate implicitly:

$$\frac{f'}{f} = \ln x + x \cdot \frac{1}{x} = \ln x + 1$$ $$f'(x) = x^x(\ln x + 1)$$

Answer: $f'(x) = x^x(\ln x + 1)$

Exercise 24: Cross-Entropy Gradient

For binary cross-entropy $\mathcal{L} = -[y\ln\hat{y} + (1-y)\ln(1-\hat{y})]$ where $\hat{y} = \sigma(z)$, show that $\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{dz} = \hat{y} - y$.

Show Solution

Chain rule: $\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{dz} = \frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\hat{y}} \cdot \frac{d\hat{y}}{dz}$.

$\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\hat{y}} = -\frac{y}{\hat{y}} + \frac{1-y}{1-\hat{y}}$

$\frac{d\hat{y}}{dz} = \hat{y}(1-\hat{y})$ (sigmoid derivative).

$$\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{dz} = \left(-\frac{y}{\hat{y}} + \frac{1-y}{1-\hat{y}}\right)\hat{y}(1-\hat{y}) = -y(1-\hat{y}) + (1-y)\hat{y} = \hat{y} - y \quad \square$$

Exercise 25: Fisher Information

For $X \sim \text{Bernoulli}(\theta)$, compute the Fisher information $\mathcal{I}(\theta)$.

Show Solution

$\log p(x;\theta) = x\log\theta + (1-x)\log(1-\theta)$.

Score: $s = \frac{x}{\theta} - \frac{1-x}{1-\theta}$.

Second derivative: $\frac{\partial^2}{\partial\theta^2}\log p = -\frac{x}{\theta^2} - \frac{1-x}{(1-\theta)^2}$.

$$\mathcal{I}(\theta) = -E\left[\frac{\partial^2}{\partial\theta^2}\log p\right] = \frac{E[x]}{\theta^2} + \frac{1 - E[x]}{(1-\theta)^2} = \frac{\theta}{\theta^2} + \frac{1-\theta}{(1-\theta)^2} = \frac{1}{\theta} + \frac{1}{1-\theta} = \frac{1}{\theta(1-\theta)}$$

Answer: $\mathcal{I}(\theta) = \frac{1}{\theta(1-\theta)}$

Exercise 26: Prove the Product Rule

Prove the product rule $(fg)' = f'g + fg'$ from the limit definition of the derivative.

Show Solution

Start with the definition:

$$(fg)'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x)}{h}$$

Add and subtract $f(x+h)g(x)$ in the numerator:

$$= \lim_{h\to 0}\left[f(x+h)\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h} + g(x)\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\right]$$

Since $f$ is differentiable (hence continuous), $\lim_{h\to 0}f(x+h)=f(x)$. Taking the limit:

$$= f(x)g'(x) + g(x)f'(x) \quad \square$$

Exercise 27: Softmax Derivative

For the softmax function $\sigma_i(\mathbf{z}) = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_k e^{z_k}}$, show that $\frac{\partial \sigma_i}{\partial z_j} = \sigma_i(\delta_{ij} - \sigma_j)$, where $\delta_{ij}$ is the Kronecker delta.

Show Solution

Let $S = \sum_k e^{z_k}$. Then $\sigma_i = e^{z_i}/S$.

Case $i = j$: Quotient rule:

$$\frac{\partial \sigma_i}{\partial z_i} = \frac{e^{z_i} \cdot S - e^{z_i} \cdot e^{z_i}}{S^2} = \frac{e^{z_i}}{S}\cdot\frac{S - e^{z_i}}{S} = \sigma_i(1-\sigma_i)$$

Case $i \neq j$: The numerator $e^{z_i}$ does not depend on $z_j$:

$$\frac{\partial \sigma_i}{\partial z_j} = \frac{0 \cdot S - e^{z_i}\cdot e^{z_j}}{S^2} = -\sigma_i\sigma_j$$

Combining: $\frac{\partial\sigma_i}{\partial z_j} = \sigma_i(\delta_{ij} - \sigma_j)$. $\square$

Exercise 28: Newton's Method

Use Newton's method to find the minimum of $f(x) = x^4 - 4x^2 + 2$ starting from $x_0 = 3$. Perform two iterations.

Show Solution

$f'(x) = 4x^3 - 8x$, $f''(x) = 12x^2 - 8$.

Newton update for minimisation: $x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f'(x_n)}{f''(x_n)}$.

Iteration 1: $f'(3) = 108 - 24 = 84$, $f''(3) = 108 - 8 = 100$.

$$x_1 = 3 - 84/100 = 3 - 0.84 = 2.16$$

Iteration 2: $f'(2.16) = 4(10.078) - 17.28 = 40.31 - 17.28 = 23.03$, $f''(2.16) = 12(4.666) - 8 = 47.99$.

$$x_2 = 2.16 - 23.03/47.99 \approx 2.16 - 0.48 = 1.68$$

Answer: $x_1 \approx 2.16$, $x_2 \approx 1.68$ (converging toward $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.414$).

Exercise 29: Backpropagation Through Two Layers

Consider a two-layer network: $z_1 = w_1 x$, $a_1 = \sigma(z_1)$, $z_2 = w_2 a_1$, $L = (z_2 - y)^2$. Compute $\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_1}$ using the chain rule.

Show Solution

Apply the chain rule along the computation graph:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_1} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_2} \cdot \frac{\partial z_2}{\partial a_1} \cdot \frac{\partial a_1}{\partial z_1} \cdot \frac{\partial z_1}{\partial w_1}$$
  • $\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_2} = 2(z_2 - y)$
  • $\frac{\partial z_2}{\partial a_1} = w_2$
  • $\frac{\partial a_1}{\partial z_1} = \sigma(z_1)(1-\sigma(z_1))$
  • $\frac{\partial z_1}{\partial w_1} = x$
$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_1} = 2(w_2\sigma(w_1 x) - y) \cdot w_2 \cdot \sigma(w_1 x)(1-\sigma(w_1 x)) \cdot x$$

Exercise 30: Derivative of Entropy

The binary entropy function is $H(p) = -p\ln p - (1-p)\ln(1-p)$. (a) Find $H'(p)$. (b) Show that $H$ is maximised at $p = 1/2$. (c) Compute $H''(p)$ and verify it equals $-\frac{1}{p(1-p)}$, linking entropy curvature to Fisher information.

Show Solution

(a) Differentiate using the product rule on each term:

$$H'(p) = -[\ln p + 1] - [-\ln(1-p) - 1] = -\ln p + \ln(1-p) = \ln\frac{1-p}{p}$$

(b) Set $H'(p) = 0$: $\ln\frac{1-p}{p} = 0 \Rightarrow \frac{1-p}{p} = 1 \Rightarrow p = 1/2$.

(c) $H'(p) = \ln(1-p) - \ln p$, so:

$$H''(p) = \frac{-1}{1-p} - \frac{1}{p} = -\frac{p + (1-p)}{p(1-p)} = -\frac{1}{p(1-p)}$$

Since $H''(1/2) = -4 < 0$, this confirms $p = 1/2$ is a maximum. The magnitude $|H''(p)| = \frac{1}{p(1-p)}$ equals the Bernoulli Fisher information $\mathcal{I}(p)$, revealing a deep link: the curvature of entropy equals the statistical precision with which $p$ can be estimated. $\square$
