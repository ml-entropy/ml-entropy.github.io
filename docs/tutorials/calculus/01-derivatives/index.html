The Definition

The derivative of $f$ at point $x$ is the limit of the difference quotient:

Derivative Definition

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h}$$ This measures the instantaneous rate of change of $f$ at $x$.

Geometrically, $f'(x)$ is the slope of the tangent line to the graph at point $(x, f(x))$.

Example: $f(x) = x^2$

Computing from Definition
1
$f(x+h) - f(x) = (x+h)^2 - x^2 = x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2 = 2xh + h^2$
2
$\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} = \frac{2xh + h^2}{h} = 2x + h$
3
$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0}(2x + h) = 2x$

Differentiation Rules

These rules let us compute derivatives without limits:

Basic Rules
1
Power rule: $(x^n)' = nx^{n-1}$
2
Sum rule: $(f + g)' = f' + g'$
3
Product rule: $(fg)' = f'g + fg'$
4
Quotient rule: $\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}$

The Chain Rule

The chain rule is the most important rule for machine learning—it's how backpropagation computes gradients through layers of a neural network.

Chain Rule

If $y = f(g(x))$, then: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$ where $u = g(x)$.

Example: $y = (3x + 1)^5$

Let $u = 3x + 1$, so $y = u^5$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = 5u^4 \cdot 3 = 15(3x+1)^4$$
ML Connection

In a neural network, each layer is a function. The chain rule lets us compute how the loss changes with respect to weights in early layers by multiplying derivatives through the network—this is backpropagation.

Common Derivatives

These appear constantly in ML:

$$\begin{aligned} (e^x)' &= e^x \\ (\ln x)' &= \frac{1}{x} \\ (\sin x)' &= \cos x \\ (\cos x)' &= -\sin x \\ (\sigma(x))' &= \sigma(x)(1 - \sigma(x)) \quad \text{(sigmoid)} \end{aligned}$$
The Sigmoid Derivative

The sigmoid $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$ has a beautiful derivative that depends only on its own value. This makes it efficient to compute during backprop.

Derivatives in Optimization

Critical points where $f'(x) = 0$ are candidates for minima or maxima. The second derivative test tells us which:

  • $f''(x) > 0$: local minimum (curve is concave up)
  • $f''(x) < 0$: local maximum (curve is concave down)
  • $f''(x) = 0$: inconclusive (might be inflection point)

Gradient descent uses the derivative to iteratively move toward minima:

$$x_{\text{new}} = x_{\text{old}} - \alpha \cdot f'(x_{\text{old}})$$

where $\alpha$ is the learning rate.
