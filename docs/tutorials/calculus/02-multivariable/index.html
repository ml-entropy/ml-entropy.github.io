Partial Derivatives

For a function $f(x, y)$, the partial derivative with respect to $x$ treats $y$ as a constant:

Partial Derivative

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h, y) - f(x, y)}{h}$$ Notation: $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$, $f_x$, or $\partial_x f$

Example: $f(x, y) = x^2 y + \sin(y)$

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2xy \quad \text{(treat } y \text{ as constant)}$$
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = x^2 + \cos(y) \quad \text{(treat } x \text{ as constant)}$$

The Gradient

The gradient packages all partial derivatives into a vector:

Gradient

$$\nabla f = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2} \\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \end{pmatrix}$$

Key Property

The gradient points in the direction of steepest ascent. Its magnitude is the rate of change in that direction. To minimize, go opposite: $-\nabla f$.

Example: $f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2$

$$\nabla f = \begin{pmatrix} 2x \\ 2y \end{pmatrix}$$

At $(1, 2)$, the gradient is $(2, 4)$. This points away from the minimum at $(0, 0)$. Gradient descent moves in direction $(-2, -4)$, toward the minimum.

Gradient Descent

To minimize $f(\mathbf{x})$, iteratively update:

$$\mathbf{x}^{(t+1)} = \mathbf{x}^{(t)} - \alpha \nabla f(\mathbf{x}^{(t)})$$

where $\alpha$ is the learning rate.

Gradient Descent Intuition
1
Compute gradient: tells us "uphill" direction
2
Negate it: get "downhill" direction
3
Take small step ($\alpha$): move toward minimum
4
Repeat until convergence

The Hessian

The Hessian is the matrix of second partial derivatives:

$$H = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1 \partial x_2} & \cdots \\ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2 \partial x_1} & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2^2} & \cdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{pmatrix}$$

The Hessian tells us about curvature. For smooth functions, it's symmetric ($H = H^T$).

Second Derivative Test (Multivariate)

At a critical point $\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$:

  • $H$ positive definite → local minimum
  • $H$ negative definite → local maximum
  • $H$ indefinite → saddle point

Multivariable Chain Rule

If $z = f(x, y)$ where $x = g(t)$ and $y = h(t)$:

$$\frac{dz}{dt} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt}$$

In vector form:

$$\frac{dz}{dt} = \nabla f \cdot \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}$$

This generalizes beautifully to neural networks, where the chain rule computes how loss changes through many layers of composed functions.

When functions depend on multiple variables, partial derivatives measure change in one direction while holding others fixed. The gradient combines them all.
