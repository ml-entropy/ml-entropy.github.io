Why Multivariable Calculus Matters

Imagine you are training a neural network to classify images. Your model has thousands — perhaps millions — of adjustable parameters: weights and biases scattered across dozens of layers. The loss function that measures how wrong your model's predictions are depends on every single one of these parameters simultaneously. You are no longer optimizing a function of one variable; you are navigating a landscape in a space with millions of dimensions. How do you find the lowest valley in such an impossibly complex terrain?

This is the central question that multivariable calculus answers. In single-variable calculus, you learned to find slopes and optima of curves. But real-world problems — from training a GPT model to maximizing the entropy of a probability distribution — live in high-dimensional spaces where the notion of "slope" must be replaced by something richer: partial derivatives, gradients, and Hessians. These tools tell us not just "is the function increasing?" but "in which direction does it increase fastest?" and "what does the curvature look like near a critical point?"

Throughout this tutorial, we will build these ideas from the ground up. We start with the simplest generalization — how to differentiate when you have two variables instead of one — and work our way up to the machinery that powers backpropagation in deep learning and natural gradient methods in information geometry. Every concept will be motivated by a concrete problem, explained with intuition first, and then formalized with rigorous definitions. Let's begin.

Partial Derivatives

Suppose you are standing on a mountainside, and you want to know how steep the slope is. In one dimension, there is only one direction to walk, so the derivative tells you everything. But on a real mountain, you could walk north, east, or in any direction in between. A partial derivative answers a simpler question: "If I walk due east (changing only my $x$-coordinate), how steep is the slope?" You hold all other variables constant and differentiate with respect to just one.

This "hold everything else fixed" idea is the key insight. When you compute $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$, you are pretending that $y$ is just a number — a constant like 7 or $\pi$ — and using all the single-variable differentiation rules you already know. Then you do the same thing for $y$, treating $x$ as a constant. Each partial derivative gives you a "slice" of information about how the function behaves, and together they paint a complete picture.

Partial Derivative

For a function $f(x, y)$, the partial derivative with respect to $x$ is defined as:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h, y) - f(x, y)}{h}$$

Similarly, the partial derivative with respect to $y$ is:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x, y+h) - f(x, y)}{h}$$

Common notations: $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$, $f_x$, $\partial_x f$, or $D_x f$.

Notice that the definition looks identical to the ordinary derivative — we just perturb one input at a time while freezing the others. This is not a coincidence: a partial derivative is an ordinary derivative of a restricted function. If $f(x, y) = x^2 y + \sin(y)$ and we fix $y = 3$, then $g(x) = 3x^2 + \sin(3)$, and $g'(x) = 6x$. That is exactly $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$ evaluated at $y = 3$.

Worked Example: $f(x, y) = x^2 y + \sin(y)$

Computing Both Partial Derivatives
1
Partial with respect to $x$: Treat $y$ as a constant. The term $x^2 y$ differentiates to $2xy$ (power rule on $x$, with $y$ as coefficient). The term $\sin(y)$ is a constant with respect to $x$, so its derivative is $0$. $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2xy$$
2
Partial with respect to $y$: Treat $x$ as a constant. The term $x^2 y$ differentiates to $x^2$ (since $x^2$ is the coefficient of $y$). The term $\sin(y)$ differentiates to $\cos(y)$. $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = x^2 + \cos(y)$$
3
Evaluate at $(x, y) = (2, \pi)$: $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\big|_{(2,\pi)} = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot \pi = 4\pi \approx 12.57$. $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\big|_{(2,\pi)} = 4 + \cos(\pi) = 4 - 1 = 3$. This means at the point $(2, \pi)$, increasing $x$ slightly increases $f$ about 4 times faster than increasing $y$.
Higher Dimensions

For a function $f(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$ of $n$ variables, there are $n$ partial derivatives — one for each input. In machine learning, $n$ can be millions (one per model parameter). The beauty is that each partial derivative is computed the same way: hold everything else constant, differentiate with respect to one variable. No new techniques are needed — just patience and good bookkeeping.

Partial Derivatives and the Gradient

Computing partial derivatives one at a time gives us useful information, but it is scattered across $n$ separate numbers. The gradient elegantly packages all of them into a single vector. Think of it as the "master direction sign" at any point on a surface: it tells you which way is uphill and how steep the climb is.

The Gradient Vector

For a scalar-valued function $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, the gradient is the vector of all partial derivatives:

$$\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2} \\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \end{pmatrix}$$

The symbol $\nabla$ (nabla) is an upside-down triangle. The gradient $\nabla f$ is a vector field: at every point $\mathbf{x}$, it produces a vector pointing in the direction of steepest ascent.

Why does the gradient point in the direction of steepest ascent? Consider the first-order Taylor approximation: $f(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{v}) \approx f(\mathbf{x}) + \nabla f(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \mathbf{v}$ for a small step $\mathbf{v}$. The dot product $\nabla f \cdot \mathbf{v}$ is maximized when $\mathbf{v}$ points in the same direction as $\nabla f$ (by the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality). So among all directions you could step, moving along $\nabla f$ increases $f$ the most. To decrease $f$ as fast as possible, you move in the opposite direction: $-\nabla f$.

The magnitude $\|\nabla f\|$ tells you the rate of steepest ascent. If the gradient is large, the function is changing rapidly; if it is near zero, you are close to a flat region — potentially a minimum, maximum, or saddle point. This is exactly the multivariable generalization of the single-variable condition $f'(x) = 0$ for critical points.

Worked Example: $f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2$

This is the simplest "bowl" function — a paraboloid centered at the origin. Let's compute the gradient and see what it tells us geometrically.

Gradient of the Paraboloid
1
Compute partial derivatives: $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2x$ and $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 2y$.
2
Form the gradient: $\nabla f = (2x, \ 2y)$.
3
Evaluate at $(1, 2)$: $\nabla f(1, 2) = (2, 4)$. The magnitude is $\|(2, 4)\| = \sqrt{4 + 16} = \sqrt{20} \approx 4.47$. The gradient points away from the origin (the minimum). To descend, we move in the direction $(-2, -4)$, toward the minimum.
4
At the minimum $(0, 0)$: $\nabla f(0, 0) = (0, 0)$. The gradient vanishes — confirming this is a critical point.
Gradient in ML: Why It Matters

In a neural network with parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\theta_1, \theta_2, \ldots, \theta_n)$, the loss function $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ is a scalar-valued function of millions of variables. The gradient $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \mathcal{L}$ tells us how to adjust each parameter to reduce the loss. Backpropagation is simply an efficient algorithm for computing this gradient — it does not change the math, just the speed at which we evaluate it.

Gradient Descent

Now that we know the gradient points uphill, the optimization strategy is almost obvious: take a step in the opposite direction. This is gradient descent, the workhorse algorithm behind virtually all modern machine learning. Despite its simplicity, it (and its variants like Adam, SGD with momentum, and AdaGrad) trains everything from logistic regression models to trillion-parameter language models.

Gradient Descent Update Rule

Starting from an initial guess $\mathbf{x}^{(0)}$, update iteratively:

$$\mathbf{x}^{(t+1)} = \mathbf{x}^{(t)} - \alpha \, \nabla f(\mathbf{x}^{(t)})$$

where $\alpha > 0$ is the learning rate (step size), and $t$ is the iteration index.

The learning rate $\alpha$ is critical. If $\alpha$ is too large, you overshoot the minimum and the algorithm diverges — the loss actually increases each step. If $\alpha$ is too small, convergence is painfully slow; you are taking baby steps toward the minimum and may never arrive in a practical timeframe. Finding the right $\alpha$ (or using adaptive methods that adjust it automatically) is one of the core practical challenges of machine learning.

Gradient Descent on $f(x, y) = x^2 + 4y^2$, Starting at $(4, 2)$, $\alpha = 0.1$
1
Compute gradient: $\nabla f = (2x, \, 8y)$. At $(4, 2)$: $\nabla f = (8, 16)$. This points uphill (away from the minimum at the origin).
2
Update: $\mathbf{x}^{(1)} = (4, 2) - 0.1 \cdot (8, 16) = (4 - 0.8, \ 2 - 1.6) = (3.2, \ 0.4)$.
3
Check progress: $f(4, 2) = 16 + 16 = 32$. After one step, $f(3.2, 0.4) = 10.24 + 0.64 = 10.88$. The loss dropped from $32$ to $10.88$ — a big improvement in one step!
4
Next iteration: $\nabla f(3.2, 0.4) = (6.4, 3.2)$. $\mathbf{x}^{(2)} = (3.2, 0.4) - 0.1 \cdot (6.4, 3.2) = (2.56, 0.08)$. $f(2.56, 0.08) = 6.5536 + 0.0256 = 6.58$. Continuing to decrease!

Notice how the $y$-component converges much faster than $x$. This is because the curvature in the $y$-direction is steeper ($\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2} = 8$ vs. $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} = 2$). The algorithm "bounces" less along $y$ because it quickly reaches the bottom of that narrow valley. This asymmetry in curvature is exactly what the Hessian matrix captures, and it motivates more sophisticated optimizers like Newton's method.

Learning Rate Pitfalls

Try $\alpha = 0.3$ on the same function. At step 1: $\mathbf{x}^{(1)} = (4, 2) - 0.3(8, 16) = (1.6, -2.8)$. The $y$-coordinate overshot past zero to $-2.8$ — farther from the minimum than before! With $\alpha = 0.3$, the loss oscillates wildly in the $y$-direction. This is why adaptive learning rate methods (Adam, RMSProp) scale the learning rate independently for each parameter.

Contour Plots and Level Sets

Visualizing functions of two variables is challenging because the graph lives in three dimensions. Contour plots solve this by projecting the information onto a 2D plane — just like a topographic map shows the elevation of a mountain range on a flat piece of paper. Each contour line (or level curve) connects all points where $f(x, y) = c$ for some constant $c$. Closely spaced contours indicate steep terrain; widely spaced contours mean the function is nearly flat.

Level Set

The level set of $f$ at height $c$ is the set $\{(x, y) : f(x, y) = c\}$. For a function of two variables, level sets are curves in the plane. For a function of three variables, level sets are surfaces in 3D space. The loss function of a neural network has level sets that are high-dimensional manifolds embedded in parameter space.

For the paraboloid $f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2$, the level curves are circles: $x^2 + y^2 = c$. For the elongated paraboloid $f(x, y) = x^2 + 4y^2$, they are ellipses: $x^2 + 4y^2 = c$. The shape of these contours tells you about the curvature of the function — circular contours mean equal curvature in all directions, while elongated ellipses mean the curvature differs dramatically by direction.

Gradient Is Perpendicular to Level Curves

One of the most beautiful facts in multivariable calculus: the gradient $\nabla f$ at any point is perpendicular to the level curve passing through that point. Why? Along a level curve, $f$ is constant, so the rate of change of $f$ along the curve is zero. The gradient measures maximum rate of change, so it must point away from the curve — i.e., perpendicular to it.

This means gradient descent always crosses contour lines at right angles. On a topographic map, this is like water flowing straight downhill, perpendicular to the elevation contours. When the contours are circular, gradient descent heads straight for the center. When they are elongated ellipses, the path zigzags — each step is perpendicular to the local contour, but the overall trajectory bounces back and forth across the narrow valley.

Worked Example: Reading a Contour Plot

Consider $f(x, y) = (x - 1)^2 + 4(y + 2)^2$. The minimum is at $(1, -2)$ where $f = 0$. The level curve $f = 4$ is the ellipse $(x-1)^2 + 4(y+2)^2 = 4$, or equivalently $\frac{(x-1)^2}{4} + \frac{(y+2)^2}{1} = 1$. This is an ellipse centered at $(1, -2)$ with semi-axes $a = 2$ (horizontal) and $b = 1$ (vertical). The function is "steeper" in the $y$-direction — you hit higher values faster when moving vertically. The gradient at $(3, -2)$ is $(2(3-1), \ 8(-2+2)) = (4, 0)$, which points purely in the $x$-direction — perpendicular to the vertical tangent of the ellipse at that point, exactly as the theory predicts.

The Hessian Matrix

The gradient tells us about the slope of a function — its first-order behavior. But just as the second derivative in single-variable calculus tells us about curvature and concavity, we need second-order information in multiple dimensions. Enter the Hessian matrix, which encodes all second-order partial derivatives and reveals the local "shape" of the function near any point.

Why do we need second-order information? Consider gradient descent on a narrow, elongated valley. The gradient points across the valley (perpendicular to the contours), not along it. So gradient descent wastes time bouncing back and forth instead of sliding down the valley floor. The Hessian captures this elongation and allows methods like Newton's method to correct for it — essentially "reshaping" the landscape into a more isotropic bowl before stepping.

The Hessian Matrix

For $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, the Hessian is the $n \times n$ matrix of second partial derivatives:

$$\mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1 \partial x_2} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1 \partial x_n} \\ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2 \partial x_1} & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2^2} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2 \partial x_n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n \partial x_1} & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n \partial x_2} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n^2} \end{pmatrix}$$

For smooth functions (where mixed partials are continuous), $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_j \partial x_i}$ (Clairaut's theorem), so the Hessian is symmetric: $\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{H}^T$.

Worked Example: Hessian of $f(x, y) = x^3 - 3xy + y^3$

Computing the Hessian Step by Step
1
First partial derivatives: $f_x = 3x^2 - 3y$ and $f_y = -3x + 3y^2$.
2
Second partial derivatives: $f_{xx} = 6x$, $f_{yy} = 6y$, $f_{xy} = f_{yx} = -3$.
3
Assemble the Hessian: $\mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} 6x & -3 \\ -3 & 6y \end{pmatrix}$.
4
Find critical points: Set $\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$: $3x^2 = 3y$ and $3y^2 = 3x$, giving $x^2 = y$ and $y^2 = x$. Substituting: $x^4 = x$, so $x(x^3 - 1) = 0$, yielding $(0, 0)$ and $(1, 1)$.
5
Classify critical points: At $(0, 0)$: $\mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -3 \\ -3 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$. Eigenvalues: $\lambda = \pm 3$ (one positive, one negative) → saddle point. At $(1, 1)$: $\mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & -3 \\ -3 & 6 \end{pmatrix}$. Eigenvalues: $\lambda = 9$ and $\lambda = 3$ (both positive) → local minimum.
Second Derivative Test (Multivariate)

At a critical point where $\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$, the eigenvalues of the Hessian determine the nature of the critical point:

  • All eigenvalues positive ($\mathbf{H}$ positive definite) → local minimum
  • All eigenvalues negative ($\mathbf{H}$ negative definite) → local maximum
  • Mixed signs ($\mathbf{H}$ indefinite) → saddle point
  • Any eigenvalue zero → test is inconclusive

For $2 \times 2$ Hessians, you can use the determinant: $\det(\mathbf{H}) = f_{xx} f_{yy} - f_{xy}^2$. If $\det(\mathbf{H}) > 0$ and $f_{xx} > 0$: minimum. If $\det(\mathbf{H}) > 0$ and $f_{xx} < 0$: maximum. If $\det(\mathbf{H}) < 0$: saddle point.

Saddle Points in Deep Learning

In high-dimensional optimization (like training neural networks), saddle points are far more common than local minima. Intuitively, at a saddle point some eigenvalues of the Hessian are positive and others are negative. With $n$ parameters, each eigenvalue is independently likely to be positive or negative, so the probability that all are positive (true minimum) shrinks exponentially with $n$. Research has shown that in practice, the critical points encountered during neural network training are almost always saddle points rather than true local minima. SGD's noise actually helps escape saddle points — one of the hidden benefits of stochastic optimization.

Multivariable Chain Rule

The chain rule is arguably the single most important tool in machine learning calculus. In single-variable calculus, the chain rule says $\frac{d}{dx} f(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$ — the derivative of a composition is the product of derivatives. In multivariable calculus, the same idea generalizes, but the "product" becomes a sum of products (or a matrix multiplication). This generalization is exactly what makes backpropagation possible.

Multivariable Chain Rule

If $z = f(x, y)$ where $x = g(t)$ and $y = h(t)$, then:

$$\frac{dz}{dt} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt}$$

More compactly, in vector form:

$$\frac{dz}{dt} = \nabla f \cdot \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}$$

where $\mathbf{r}(t) = (g(t), h(t))$ is the path through the input space.

The intuition is straightforward: changing $t$ by a tiny amount $dt$ changes $x$ by $\frac{dx}{dt} \, dt$ and changes $y$ by $\frac{dy}{dt} \, dt$. Each of these changes affects $z$ independently — the change in $x$ contributes $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \frac{dx}{dt} \, dt$ and the change in $y$ contributes $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \frac{dy}{dt} \, dt$. Summing up all contributions gives the total rate of change.

Worked Example: Chain Rule for a Composition

If $f(x, y) = x^2 y$, $x = \cos(t)$, $y = \sin(t)$, find $\frac{dz}{dt}$
1
Partial derivatives of $f$: $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2xy$ and $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = x^2$.
2
Derivatives of the path: $\frac{dx}{dt} = -\sin(t)$ and $\frac{dy}{dt} = \cos(t)$.
3
Apply chain rule: $\frac{dz}{dt} = 2xy \cdot (-\sin t) + x^2 \cdot \cos t = -2\cos(t)\sin^2(t) + \cos^3(t)$.
4
Verify at $t = 0$: $x = 1, y = 0$, so $\frac{dz}{dt} = 0 + 1 = 1$. Indeed, $z(t) = \cos^2(t)\sin(t)$, and $z'(0) = -2\cos(0)\sin(0)\sin(0) + \cos^2(0)\cos(0) = 1$. ✓

The Jacobian and Neural Networks

When both input and output are multidimensional — say $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ where $\mathbf{f} : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ — the chain rule involves the Jacobian matrix. The Jacobian $\mathbf{J}$ is the $m \times n$ matrix whose $(i, j)$-entry is $\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}$. For a composition $\mathbf{z} = \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}))$, the chain rule becomes matrix multiplication:

$$\frac{\partial \mathbf{z}}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{J}_g \cdot \mathbf{J}_f$$

A neural network is a composition of layers: $\mathbf{y} = f_L(f_{L-1}(\cdots f_1(\mathbf{x})))$. Backpropagation computes $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{x}}$ by multiplying Jacobians from the output layer backward. In practice, we never form the full Jacobian (it would be enormous); instead we compute Jacobian-vector products (JVPs) or vector-Jacobian products (VJPs) one layer at a time. This is exactly what frameworks like PyTorch and JAX do during .backward().

Information-Theoretic Connection

This tutorial series connects every mathematical concept back to information theory. Multivariable calculus is no exception — in fact, some of the deepest ideas in modern machine learning sit at the intersection of gradient-based optimization and information geometry. Let's explore how gradients and Hessians appear naturally when we differentiate information-theoretic quantities.

Gradient of the KL Divergence

Suppose we have a parametric distribution $q_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(x)$ and we want to minimize its KL divergence from a target distribution $p(x)$. The KL divergence is:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}) = \sum_x p(x) \log \frac{p(x)}{q_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(x)}$$

Taking the gradient with respect to $\boldsymbol{\theta}$:

$$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} D_{\text{KL}} = -\sum_x p(x) \frac{\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} q_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(x)}{q_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(x)} = -\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p}\!\left[\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \log q_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(x)\right]$$

This is the score function averaged over the true distribution. Setting this gradient to zero recovers the maximum likelihood estimator — the parameters that make $q_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ closest to $p$ in the KL sense. This is why minimizing cross-entropy loss in classification is equivalent to minimizing KL divergence (up to a constant).

The Fisher Information Matrix

The Fisher information matrix is the expected Hessian of the negative log-likelihood — or equivalently, the covariance of the score function:

$$\mathbf{F} = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim q_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}}\!\left[\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \log q_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(x) \, \left(\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \log q_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(x)\right)^T\right]$$

The Fisher matrix plays the role of a "natural" Hessian — it measures how much the distribution changes when you perturb the parameters. It captures the intrinsic geometry of the statistical model, not the arbitrary parameterization you happened to choose.

Natural Gradient Descent

Ordinary gradient descent uses the update $\boldsymbol{\theta} \leftarrow \boldsymbol{\theta} - \alpha \nabla \mathcal{L}$. But this treats all parameter directions equally, even though some directions change the distribution drastically while others barely affect it. Natural gradient descent fixes this by premultiplying by the inverse Fisher matrix:

$$\boldsymbol{\theta} \leftarrow \boldsymbol{\theta} - \alpha \, \mathbf{F}^{-1} \nabla \mathcal{L}$$

This is equivalent to doing steepest descent in the space of probability distributions (using KL divergence as the distance metric) rather than in Euclidean parameter space. Algorithms like KFAC and TRPO in reinforcement learning are practical approximations of natural gradient descent.

Entropy and the Gradient

The entropy $H(p) = -\sum_x p(x) \log p(x)$ is itself a function of the probability vector $\mathbf{p}$. Its gradient (with respect to $\mathbf{p}$, subject to the constraint $\sum p_i = 1$) points toward the uniform distribution — the maximum entropy distribution. This connects to the information-theoretic principle that among all distributions consistent with our constraints, the one with maximum entropy is the "least informative" — it makes the fewest assumptions beyond what the data tells us. The gradient of entropy literally guides us toward maximal uncertainty.

Common Misconceptions

Students (and even experienced practitioners) frequently stumble on subtle points in multivariable calculus. Let's address the most common pitfalls head-on.

Misconception 1: "The gradient IS the derivative"

The gradient is not the derivative of a multivariable function — it is one representation of the derivative. The true derivative (the "total derivative") of $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ at a point is a linear map (a row vector, or covector). The gradient is the column vector obtained by transposing this row vector using the inner product structure of $\mathbb{R}^n$. In Euclidean space, the distinction rarely matters, but in information geometry (where the metric is the Fisher matrix, not the identity), it becomes critical — this is exactly why natural gradient differs from ordinary gradient.

Misconception 2: "If all partial derivatives exist, the function is differentiable"

False! Consider $f(x, y) = \frac{xy}{x^2 + y^2}$ for $(x, y) \neq (0, 0)$, with $f(0, 0) = 0$. Both $f_x(0, 0) = 0$ and $f_y(0, 0) = 0$ exist (compute them from the limit definition). But $f$ is not even continuous at the origin — approach along $y = x$ and you get $f(x, x) = \frac{1}{2}$, while along the axes you get $0$. All partial derivatives existing only means the function behaves nicely along the coordinate axes; it says nothing about behavior in other directions. Differentiability requires that the function be well-approximated by a linear map in every direction.

Misconception 3: "Gradient descent always finds the global minimum"

Gradient descent finds a critical point — not necessarily the global minimum. For convex functions (where the Hessian is positive semidefinite everywhere), every local minimum is global, so gradient descent works perfectly. But neural network loss landscapes are highly non-convex, with many local minima and saddle points. In practice, SGD's stochastic noise helps it escape shallow local minima and saddle points, and empirically the local minima it finds tend to generalize well. But there are no guarantees in the non-convex case — this remains an active area of research.

Misconception 4: "The Hessian is always expensive to compute"

The full $n \times n$ Hessian has $n^2$ entries (or $\frac{n(n+1)}{2}$ by symmetry), which is prohibitively large when $n$ is in the millions. But you rarely need the full Hessian. Hessian-vector products $\mathbf{H}\mathbf{v}$ can be computed in $O(n)$ time (the same cost as a gradient!) using a trick: $\mathbf{H}\mathbf{v} = \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} f \cdot \mathbf{v})$. This enables methods like conjugate gradients and Lanczos iteration to extract useful curvature information without ever materializing the full matrix.

Misconception 5: "The mixed partial $f_{xy}$ always equals $f_{yx}$"

Clairaut's theorem states that $f_{xy} = f_{yx}$ if the second partial derivatives are continuous. For the smooth functions encountered in most ML applications, this is always satisfied. But pathological examples exist where mixed partials differ — a classic one is $f(x,y) = \frac{xy(x^2 - y^2)}{x^2 + y^2}$ at the origin. In practice, you can safely assume symmetry of the Hessian, but it is good to know the underlying assumption.

E6. Level Curve Identification

Easy

What shape are the level curves of $f(x, y) = x^2 + 9y^2$? At what value of $c$ does the level curve pass through $(3, 1)$?

Show Solution

The level curves $x^2 + 9y^2 = c$ are ellipses (with semi-axes $\sqrt{c}$ and $\sqrt{c/9}$).

At $(3, 1)$: $c = 9 + 9 = 18$.

E7. Three-Variable Gradient

Easy

Compute the gradient of $f(x, y, z) = xyz + z^2$.

Show Solution

$\nabla f = (yz, \ xz, \ xy + 2z)$.

E8. Critical Point Identification

Easy

Find all critical points of $f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2 - 4x + 6y$.

Show Solution

$f_x = 2x - 4 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 2$. $f_y = 2y + 6 = 0 \Rightarrow y = -3$.

The only critical point is $(2, -3)$. Since $f_{xx} = 2 > 0$ and $\det(\mathbf{H}) = 4 > 0$, it is a local minimum.

E9. Gradient Perpendicularity

Easy

Verify that $\nabla f$ is perpendicular to the level curve of $f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$ at the point $(1, 1)$ by checking that the gradient is perpendicular to the tangent of the circle $x^2 + y^2 = 2$.

Show Solution

$\nabla f(1,1) = (2, 2)$. The level curve is the circle $x^2 + y^2 = 2$. At $(1,1)$, the tangent direction is $(1, -1)$ (tangent to the circle). The dot product $(2, 2) \cdot (1, -1) = 2 - 2 = 0$. ✓ Perpendicular.

E10. Simple Chain Rule

Easy

If $f(x, y) = x + y^2$, $x = 2t$, and $y = t^3$, find $\frac{dz}{dt}$ at $t = 1$.

Show Solution

$\frac{dz}{dt} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt} = 1 \cdot 2 + 2y \cdot 3t^2$.

At $t = 1$: $y = 1$, so $\frac{dz}{dt} = 2 + 2(1)(3) = 8$.

🟡 Medium

M1. Hessian Computation and Classification

Medium

Find all critical points of $f(x, y) = x^3 + y^3 - 3xy$ and classify each using the Hessian.

Show Solution

$f_x = 3x^2 - 3y = 0$, $f_y = 3y^2 - 3x = 0$. From the first: $y = x^2$. Substituting: $x^4 - x = 0$, so $x(x^3 - 1) = 0$. Critical points: $(0, 0)$ and $(1, 1)$.

Hessian: $\mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} 6x & -3 \\ -3 & 6y \end{pmatrix}$.

At $(0, 0)$: $\det(\mathbf{H}) = 0 - 9 = -9 < 0$ → saddle point.

At $(1, 1)$: $\det(\mathbf{H}) = 36 - 9 = 27 > 0$ and $f_{xx} = 6 > 0$ → local minimum.

M2. Gradient Descent Convergence

Medium

For $f(x, y) = x^2 + 25y^2$, starting at $(5, 5)$ with $\alpha = 0.01$, compute 3 steps of gradient descent. Why does convergence differ in $x$ vs $y$?

Show Solution

$\nabla f = (2x, 50y)$.

Step 0: $(5, 5)$, $f = 650$.

Step 1: $(5 - 0.1, 5 - 2.5) = (4.9, 2.5)$, $f = 24.01 + 156.25 = 180.26$.

Step 2: $(4.9 - 0.098, 2.5 - 1.25) = (4.802, 1.25)$, $f = 23.06 + 39.06 = 62.12$.

Step 3: $(4.802 - 0.096, 1.25 - 0.625) = (4.706, 0.625)$, $f = 22.15 + 9.77 = 31.92$.

$y$ converges faster because the curvature is 25× larger in the $y$-direction ($f_{yy} = 50$ vs $f_{xx} = 2$), so the gradient component is larger and the same $\alpha$ produces a bigger relative step.

M3. Gradient of Log-Likelihood

Medium

For a single data point $x$ drawn from $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, write the log-likelihood $\ell(\mu, \sigma)$ and compute $\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \mu}$ and $\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \sigma}$.

Show Solution

$\ell(\mu, \sigma) = -\frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi) - \log\sigma - \frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}$.

$\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \mu} = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma^2}$.

$\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \sigma} = -\frac{1}{\sigma} + \frac{(x - \mu)^2}{\sigma^3}$.

Setting $\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \mu} = 0$ gives $\hat{\mu} = x$ (the MLE for $\mu$). These are the score functions of the Gaussian.

M4. Chain Rule with Two Intermediate Variables

Medium

Let $f(u, v) = u^2 + uv$ where $u = x + 2y$ and $v = 3x - y$. Find $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$ and $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}$ using the multivariable chain rule.

Show Solution

$\frac{\partial f}{\partial u} = 2u + v$, $\frac{\partial f}{\partial v} = u$. Also $\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = 1$, $\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} = 3$, $\frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = 2$, $\frac{\partial v}{\partial y} = -1$.

$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = (2u + v)(1) + u(3) = 2u + v + 3u = 5u + v = 5(x + 2y) + (3x - y) = 8x + 9y$.

$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = (2u + v)(2) + u(-1) = 4u + 2v - u = 3u + 2v = 3(x+2y) + 2(3x-y) = 9x + 4y$.

M5. Contour Plot Analysis

Medium

For $f(x, y) = xy$, sketch the level curves for $c = -2, -1, 0, 1, 2$. What type of critical point is at the origin?

Show Solution

The level curves $xy = c$ are hyperbolas. For $c > 0$: hyperbolas in quadrants I and III. For $c < 0$: quadrants II and IV. For $c = 0$: the coordinate axes ($x = 0$ or $y = 0$).

$\nabla f = (y, x)$, so $(0, 0)$ is a critical point. The Hessian is $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ with eigenvalues $\pm 1$ → saddle point.

M6. Eigenvalues of a Hessian

Medium

For $f(x, y) = x^2 + xy + y^2$, compute the Hessian, find its eigenvalues, and determine whether the origin is a minimum, maximum, or saddle point.

Show Solution

$\mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$. Characteristic equation: $(2-\lambda)^2 - 1 = 0$, so $\lambda = 1$ and $\lambda = 3$.

Both eigenvalues are positive, so $\mathbf{H}$ is positive definite and the origin is a local minimum (in fact, global minimum since $f$ is convex).

M7. Gradient of Cross-Entropy

Medium

For binary cross-entropy $\mathcal{L}(w) = -[y \log(\sigma(w)) + (1 - y)\log(1 - \sigma(w))]$ where $\sigma(w) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-w}}$, show that $\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{dw} = \sigma(w) - y$.

Show Solution

Key fact: $\sigma'(w) = \sigma(w)(1 - \sigma(w))$. Let $p = \sigma(w)$.

$\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{dw} = -\left[\frac{y}{p} \cdot p(1-p) + \frac{-(1-y)}{1-p} \cdot p(1-p)\right]$

$= -\left[y(1-p) - (1-y)p\right] = -\left[y - yp - p + yp\right] = -y + p = \sigma(w) - y$.

This beautifully simple result — the gradient is just the prediction minus the label — is why logistic regression is so elegant.

M8. Verify Clairaut's Theorem

Medium

For $f(x, y) = x^3 y^2 + \sin(xy)$, verify that $f_{xy} = f_{yx}$.

Show Solution

$f_x = 3x^2 y^2 + y\cos(xy)$. Then $f_{xy} = 6x^2 y + \cos(xy) - xy\sin(xy)$.

$f_y = 2x^3 y + x\cos(xy)$. Then $f_{yx} = 6x^2 y + \cos(xy) - xy\sin(xy)$.

Indeed $f_{xy} = f_{yx}$. ✓ (Expected since all partials are continuous.)

M9. Jacobian Matrix

Medium

Compute the Jacobian matrix of the transformation $\mathbf{f}(x, y) = (x^2 + y, \ xy, \ e^x)$ at the point $(1, 2)$.

Show Solution

$\mathbf{J} = \begin{pmatrix} 2x & 1 \\ y & x \\ e^x & 0 \end{pmatrix}$. At $(1, 2)$: $\mathbf{J} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \\ e & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.

This is a $3 \times 2$ matrix because $\mathbf{f} : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^3$.

M10. Optimal Learning Rate

Medium

For the quadratic $f(x) = \frac{1}{2}x^T A x$ with $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 50 \end{pmatrix}$, what is the largest stable learning rate for gradient descent? (Hint: the condition is $\alpha < \frac{2}{\lambda_{\max}}$.)

Show Solution

$\nabla f = Ax$, so gradient descent is $x^{(t+1)} = x^{(t)} - \alpha A x^{(t)} = (I - \alpha A)x^{(t)}$.

For stability, all eigenvalues of $(I - \alpha A)$ must have magnitude $< 1$. The eigenvalues of $A$ are $2$ and $50$. We need $|1 - \alpha \lambda| < 1$ for all $\lambda$.

The binding constraint is the largest eigenvalue: $|1 - 50\alpha| < 1 \Rightarrow \alpha < \frac{2}{50} = 0.04$.

The optimal learning rate (fastest convergence) is $\alpha^* = \frac{2}{\lambda_{\max} + \lambda_{\min}} = \frac{2}{52} \approx 0.0385$.

🔴 Hard

H1. Natural Gradient for Exponential Family

Hard

For a Bernoulli distribution $p(x | \theta) = \theta^x (1-\theta)^{1-x}$ with parameter $\theta \in (0,1)$, compute the Fisher information $I(\theta)$ and show that the natural gradient update for the log-likelihood is $\Delta\theta = \alpha(x - \theta)$.

Show Solution

$\log p(x|\theta) = x \log\theta + (1-x)\log(1-\theta)$.

Score: $s(\theta) = \frac{x}{\theta} - \frac{1-x}{1-\theta} = \frac{x - \theta}{\theta(1-\theta)}$.

$I(\theta) = \text{Var}[s(\theta)] = \mathbb{E}[s^2] = \frac{1}{\theta(1-\theta)}$.

Ordinary gradient of $\ell$: $\nabla\ell = \frac{x-\theta}{\theta(1-\theta)}$.

Natural gradient: $I^{-1}\nabla\ell = \theta(1-\theta) \cdot \frac{x-\theta}{\theta(1-\theta)} = x - \theta$.

So $\Delta\theta = \alpha(x - \theta)$ — a clean, parameter-independent update!

H2. Hessian of Softmax Cross-Entropy

Hard

For the softmax cross-entropy loss $\mathcal{L} = -\sum_k y_k \log p_k$ where $p_k = \frac{e^{z_k}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}}$, show that $\frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{L}}{\partial z_i \partial z_j} = p_i(\delta_{ij} - p_j)$, i.e., the Hessian with respect to the logits is $\text{diag}(\mathbf{p}) - \mathbf{p}\mathbf{p}^T$.

Show Solution

First: $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial z_i} = p_i - y_i$ (standard softmax gradient result).

Second: $\frac{\partial p_i}{\partial z_j}$. If $i = j$: $\frac{\partial p_i}{\partial z_i} = p_i(1 - p_i)$. If $i \neq j$: $\frac{\partial p_i}{\partial z_j} = -p_i p_j$.

Combining: $\frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{L}}{\partial z_i \partial z_j} = \frac{\partial}{\partial z_j}(p_i - y_i) = \frac{\partial p_i}{\partial z_j} = p_i(\delta_{ij} - p_j)$.

In matrix form: $\mathbf{H} = \text{diag}(\mathbf{p}) - \mathbf{p}\mathbf{p}^T$. This is always positive semidefinite, which is why softmax cross-entropy is convex in the logits (though not in the weights of a neural network).

H3. Backpropagation via Chain Rule

Hard

Consider a two-layer network: $h = \sigma(w_1 x)$ and $\hat{y} = w_2 h$, with MSE loss $\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2}(\hat{y} - y)^2$ and $\sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}}$. Derive $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w_1}$ using the multivariable chain rule.

Show Solution

Forward: $z = w_1 x$, $h = \sigma(z)$, $\hat{y} = w_2 h$, $\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2}(\hat{y} - y)^2$.

$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \hat{y}} = \hat{y} - y$.

$\frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial h} = w_2$.

$\frac{\partial h}{\partial z} = \sigma(z)(1 - \sigma(z)) = h(1 - h)$.

$\frac{\partial z}{\partial w_1} = x$.

Chain rule: $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w_1} = (\hat{y} - y) \cdot w_2 \cdot h(1-h) \cdot x$.

This is exactly what backpropagation computes — multiplying local derivatives layer by layer from output to input.

H4. KL Divergence Gradient

Hard

For discrete distributions $p = (p_1, p_2, p_3)$ and $q_\theta = (\theta_1, \theta_2, 1 - \theta_1 - \theta_2)$, compute the gradient $\nabla_\theta D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q_\theta)$ and find the $\theta$ that minimizes it.

Show Solution

$D_{\text{KL}} = p_1 \log\frac{p_1}{\theta_1} + p_2 \log\frac{p_2}{\theta_2} + p_3 \log\frac{p_3}{1-\theta_1-\theta_2}$.

$\frac{\partial D_{\text{KL}}}{\partial \theta_1} = -\frac{p_1}{\theta_1} + \frac{p_3}{1-\theta_1-\theta_2}$.

$\frac{\partial D_{\text{KL}}}{\partial \theta_2} = -\frac{p_2}{\theta_2} + \frac{p_3}{1-\theta_1-\theta_2}$.

Setting both to zero: $\frac{p_1}{\theta_1} = \frac{p_2}{\theta_2} = \frac{p_3}{1-\theta_1-\theta_2}$. This implies $\theta_i = p_i$ for all $i$. The KL divergence is minimized (at zero) when $q = p$.

H5. Newton's Method Step

Hard

For $f(x, y) = x^4 + y^4 - 2x^2 - 2y^2 + 1$, compute one Newton's method step from the point $(0.5, 0.5)$. Newton's update: $\mathbf{x} \leftarrow \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{H}^{-1} \nabla f$.

Show Solution

$\nabla f = (4x^3 - 4x, \ 4y^3 - 4y)$. At $(0.5, 0.5)$: $\nabla f = (4(0.125) - 2, \ 4(0.125) - 2) = (-1.5, -1.5)$.

$\mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} 12x^2 - 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 12y^2 - 4 \end{pmatrix}$. At $(0.5, 0.5)$: $\mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$.

$\mathbf{H}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$.

Update: $(0.5, 0.5) - (-1)(-1.5, -1.5) = (0.5, 0.5) - (1.5, 1.5) = (-1, -1)$.

Note: Newton's method jumped to $(-1, -1)$, which is indeed a critical point of $f$ (a local minimum with $f = -2$).

H6. Condition Number and Convergence

Hard

The condition number $\kappa = \frac{\lambda_{\max}}{\lambda_{\min}}$ of the Hessian determines gradient descent's convergence rate. For $f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}$ with $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 100 \end{pmatrix}$, compute $\kappa$ and show that the error after $t$ steps satisfies $\|x^{(t)} - x^*\| \leq \left(\frac{\kappa - 1}{\kappa + 1}\right)^t \|x^{(0)} - x^*\|$ with optimal $\alpha$.

Show Solution

$\lambda_{\min} = 1$, $\lambda_{\max} = 100$, so $\kappa = 100$.

Optimal $\alpha = \frac{2}{\lambda_{\max} + \lambda_{\min}} = \frac{2}{101} \approx 0.0198$.

Convergence rate: $r = \frac{\kappa - 1}{\kappa + 1} = \frac{99}{101} \approx 0.98$.

After $t$ steps: $\|x^{(t)} - x^*\| \leq 0.98^t \|x^{(0)} - x^*\|$. To reduce error by $10\times$: $0.98^t = 0.1$, so $t = \frac{\log 0.1}{\log 0.98} \approx 114$ steps.

A condition number of 100 means gradient descent is slow — this motivates preconditioning (like Adam, which effectively improves conditioning).

H7. Fisher Information for Gaussian

Hard

Compute the $2 \times 2$ Fisher information matrix for $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with parameters $\theta = (\mu, \sigma)$. Show it is diagonal, meaning $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are "orthogonal" in information geometry.

Show Solution

Score vector: $\nabla_\theta \log p = \left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma^2}, \ -\frac{1}{\sigma} + \frac{(x-\mu)^2}{\sigma^3}\right)$.

$F_{11} = \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma^2}\right)^2\right] = \frac{1}{\sigma^4}\mathbb{E}[(x-\mu)^2] = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}$.

$F_{22} = \mathbb{E}\left[\left(-\frac{1}{\sigma} + \frac{(x-\mu)^2}{\sigma^3}\right)^2\right] = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\mathbb{E}\left[\left(\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{\sigma^2} - 1\right)^2\right] = \frac{2}{\sigma^2}$.

$F_{12} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma^2}\left(-\frac{1}{\sigma} + \frac{(x-\mu)^2}{\sigma^3}\right)\right] = 0$ (odd moments of centered Gaussian vanish).

$\mathbf{F} = \begin{pmatrix} 1/\sigma^2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2/\sigma^2 \end{pmatrix}$. Diagonal ✓ — $\mu$ and $\sigma$ carry independent information.

H8. Constrained Optimization via Lagrange Multipliers

Hard

Find the maximum entropy distribution over $\{1, 2, 3\}$ subject to $\sum p_i = 1$ and $\sum i \cdot p_i = 2$ using Lagrange multipliers. (Hint: maximize $H = -\sum p_i \log p_i$ subject to the constraints.)

Show Solution

Lagrangian: $\mathcal{L} = -\sum p_i \log p_i - \lambda_0(\sum p_i - 1) - \lambda_1(\sum i \cdot p_i - 2)$.

$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial p_i} = -\log p_i - 1 - \lambda_0 - \lambda_1 i = 0$.

So $p_i = e^{-1-\lambda_0} e^{-\lambda_1 i} = C \cdot r^i$ where $C = e^{-1-\lambda_0}$ and $r = e^{-\lambda_1}$.

This is an exponential (geometric) distribution over $\{1, 2, 3\}$. The constraints $\sum p_i = 1$ and $\sum i \cdot p_i = 2$ determine $C$ and $r$.

By symmetry (mean = 2 = midpoint of $\{1,2,3\}$), $r = 1$, giving $p_1 = p_2 = p_3 = \frac{1}{3}$ — the uniform distribution! Maximum entropy with mean at the midpoint is uniform.

H9. Hessian-Vector Product

Hard

For $f(x, y, z) = x^2 y + yz^2 + xz$, compute the Hessian-vector product $\mathbf{H} \mathbf{v}$ at $(1, 1, 1)$ with $\mathbf{v} = (1, 0, -1)$ using both (a) explicit Hessian multiplication and (b) the identity $\mathbf{H}\mathbf{v} = \nabla(\nabla f \cdot \mathbf{v})$.

Show Solution

(a) Explicit: $\nabla f = (2xy + z, \ x^2 + z^2, \ 2yz + x)$. $\mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} 2y & 2x & 1 \\ 2x & 0 & 2z \\ 1 & 2z & 2y \end{pmatrix}$. At $(1,1,1)$: $\mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 0 & 2 \\ 1 & 2 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$. $\mathbf{H}\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 2-1 \\ 2-2 \\ 1-2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}$.

(b) Via identity: $g = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{v} = (2xy + z)(1) + (x^2 + z^2)(0) + (2yz + x)(-1) = 2xy + z - 2yz - x$. $\nabla g = (2y - 1, \ 2x - 2z, \ 1 - 2y)$. At $(1,1,1)$: $\nabla g = (1, 0, -1)$. ✓ Same answer!

H10. Saddle Point Escape

Hard

Consider $f(x, y) = x^2 - y^2$ (a pure saddle at the origin). Starting at $(0.01, 0.01)$ with gradient descent ($\alpha = 0.1$), compute 5 iterations. Does GD escape the saddle point? In which direction? Relate this to the eigenstructure of the Hessian.

Show Solution

$\nabla f = (2x, -2y)$. Update: $x \to x - 0.2x = 0.8x$, $y \to y + 0.2y = 1.2y$.

Iteration: $(0.01, 0.01) \to (0.008, 0.012) \to (0.0064, 0.0144) \to (0.00512, 0.01728) \to (0.004096, 0.020736) \to (0.003277, 0.024883)$.

$x$ shrinks (factor $0.8$ per step) while $y$ grows (factor $1.2$ per step). GD escapes along the $y$-axis — the direction of the negative eigenvalue.

The Hessian $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$ has eigenvectors $(1, 0)$ (positive eigenvalue, stable) and $(0, 1)$ (negative eigenvalue, unstable). GD naturally moves away along the unstable direction, which is the desired behavior — it escapes saddle points toward regions where the loss decreases.

02. Partial Derivatives & Gradients

When functions depend on multiple variables, partial derivatives measure change in one direction while holding others fixed. The gradient combines them all into a single vector that points uphill. The Hessian captures curvature. Together, these tools power gradient-based optimization in machine learning and connect deeply to information geometry via the Fisher information matrix.
