From Sound to Symbol

The Phonology and Principles of the World’s Writing Systems — a 12-part series exploring how human spoken sounds were mapped into visual symbols across different cultures, focusing on the linguistic principles that guided their development.

The Series

01. Sumerian Cuneiform

The Rebus Principle and the birth of phonetic writing — how pictures of barley and arrows became the first symbols for abstract sounds.

 02. Egyptian Hieroglyphs

Acrophony and the logoconsonantal system — how pictures became consonant-spelling tools along the Nile.

 03. Phoenician & Arabic

The Abjad — why Semitic languages privilege consonantal roots and how vowels became optional.

 04. Greek & Latin

The Alphabet and the “invention” of written vowels — repurposing unused Semitic consonants.

 05. Chinese Characters

Logosyllabic writing and the phono-semantic compound — how 80%+ of characters encode both meaning and sound.

 06. Japanese Kana

Syllabaries derived from logograms — the man’yōgana pathway from Chinese characters to hiragana and katakana.

 07. Devanagari (Brahmi Family)

The Abugida and organization by articulatory phonetics — a writing system that maps the mouth.

 08. Korean Hangul

Featural writing — how King Sejong mapped the shape of the vocal tract directly into letter geometry.

 09. Maya Script

Logosyllabic blocks, synharmony, and the principle of underspelling in Mesoamerican writing.

 10. Cherokee Syllabary

Sequoyah’s cognitive feat — how a non-literate speaker isolated phonemes and engineered a syllabary.

 11. The International Phonetic Alphabet

The scientific pursuit of one-to-one sound-to-symbol mapping and the limits of perfect transcription.

 12. English Orthography

Deep orthography, the Great Vowel Shift, and why English spelling “looks wrong.”
