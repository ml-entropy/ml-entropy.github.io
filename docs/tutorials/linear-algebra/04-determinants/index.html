Geometric Meaning

The determinant of a matrix $A$ tells you how the transformation scales volumes. If you apply $A$ to a unit cube, the resulting parallelepiped has volume $|\det(A)|$.

Determinant Interpretation

$|\det(A)|$ = volume scaling factor
$\text{sign}(\det(A))$ = orientation preserved (+) or reversed (−)

2×2 Case

For a $2 \times 2$ matrix, the determinant is the signed area of the parallelogram formed by the column vectors:

$$\det\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} = ad - bc$$

Key Properties

Determinant Properties
1
$\det(I) = 1$ (identity doesn't scale volume)
2
$\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)$ (composition multiplies scaling)
3
$\det(A^T) = \det(A)$ (rows and columns are interchangeable)
4
$\det(A^{-1}) = 1/\det(A)$ (inverse reverses scaling)
5
$\det(cA) = c^n \det(A)$ for $n \times n$ matrix
Invertibility Test

$A$ is invertible if and only if $\det(A) \neq 0$. A zero determinant means the transformation collapses space (loses a dimension).

Computing Determinants

Cofactor Expansion

Expand along row $i$:

$$\det(A) = \sum_{j=1}^{n} (-1)^{i+j} a_{ij} M_{ij}$$

where $M_{ij}$ is the determinant of the submatrix with row $i$ and column $j$ removed.

3×3 Example

$$\det\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{pmatrix}$$

Expanding along the first row:

$$= 1 \cdot \det\begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 8 & 9 \end{pmatrix} - 2 \cdot \det\begin{pmatrix} 4 & 6 \\ 7 & 9 \end{pmatrix} + 3 \cdot \det\begin{pmatrix} 4 & 5 \\ 7 & 8 \end{pmatrix}$$
$$= 1(45-48) - 2(36-42) + 3(32-35) = -3 + 12 - 9 = 0$$
Why Zero?

The third row is the sum of the first two rows, so the columns are linearly dependent. The transformation collapses 3D space into a plane.

Row Operations and Determinants

  • Swap rows: multiplies determinant by $-1$
  • Scale row by $c$: multiplies determinant by $c$
  • Add multiple of one row to another: determinant unchanged

This gives a fast way to compute determinants via Gaussian elimination:

$$\det(A) = (-1)^s \cdot \prod_i u_{ii}$$

where $U$ is the row echelon form and $s$ is the number of row swaps.

04. Determinants

The determinant measures how a matrix scales volume. This geometric interpretation makes properties like $\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)$ suddenly obvious.
