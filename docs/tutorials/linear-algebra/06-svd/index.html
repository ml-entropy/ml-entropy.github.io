The SVD Theorem

Imagine you are handed an arbitrary matrix — it could be $3 \times 7$, rank-deficient, filled with messy real-world data. Eigendecomposition throws its hands up: "I only work on square matrices, and even then I might not diagonalize." The Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) has no such limitation. It works on every matrix — any shape, any rank, real or complex. That universality is why many applied mathematicians consider SVD the single most important matrix factorization in all of linear algebra.

The core idea is surprisingly elegant: every matrix, no matter how complicated, is really just doing three simple things — rotating, stretching, and rotating again. The SVD makes those three operations explicit. This is not merely an abstract theorem; it is the engine behind PCA, image compression, recommender systems, natural language processing, and countless other tools you will encounter in machine learning.

Why should we care about decomposing a matrix this way? Because once we separate the "directions" from the "magnitudes," we can do extraordinary things: discard the small magnitudes for compression, identify the dominant directions for dimensionality reduction, or invert the non-zero magnitudes for solving least-squares problems. The SVD is the Swiss Army knife of applied mathematics.

Singular Value Decomposition

Every real $m \times n$ matrix $A$ can be factored as: $$A = U\Sigma V^T$$ where:

  • $U$ is an $m \times m$ orthogonal matrix. Its columns $\mathbf{u}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{u}_m$ are called the left singular vectors.
  • $\Sigma$ is an $m \times n$ "diagonal" matrix with non-negative entries $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_{\min(m,n)} \geq 0$ on the diagonal. These are the singular values.
  • $V$ is an $n \times n$ orthogonal matrix. Its columns $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n$ are called the right singular vectors.

Notice that $\Sigma$ is not necessarily square — if $A$ is $3 \times 5$, then $\Sigma$ is also $3 \times 5$, with at most 3 non-zero diagonal entries and two extra columns of zeros. The number of non-zero singular values equals the rank of $A$. This already tells us something deep: the SVD simultaneously reveals the rank, the "important directions" in both the domain and codomain, and the magnitude of stretching along each direction.

We can also write the SVD as a sum of rank-one matrices, called the outer product form:

$$A = \sigma_1 \mathbf{u}_1 \mathbf{v}_1^T + \sigma_2 \mathbf{u}_2 \mathbf{v}_2^T + \cdots + \sigma_r \mathbf{u}_r \mathbf{v}_r^T$$

where $r = \text{rank}(A)$. Each term $\sigma_i \mathbf{u}_i \mathbf{v}_i^T$ is a rank-one "layer" of the matrix, and the singular values tell you how important each layer is. The first layer (with the largest $\sigma_1$) captures the most energy in the matrix; the last layer (with the smallest $\sigma_r$) captures the least. This layered view is the foundation for everything from image compression to latent semantic analysis.

Compact (Thin) SVD

In practice, we often use the compact SVD: $A = U_r \Sigma_r V_r^T$, where $U_r$ is $m \times r$, $\Sigma_r$ is $r \times r$, and $V_r$ is $n \times r$. This discards the parts of $U$ and $V$ that multiply zeros in $\Sigma$, saving memory. NumPy's np.linalg.svd(A, full_matrices=False) returns this compact form.

Geometric Interpretation

Here is the central geometric insight of the SVD: every linear transformation, no matter how complex, is secretly just a rotation followed by a stretch followed by another rotation. Let's unpack what that means by thinking about what $A$ does to a unit sphere.

Consider the unit sphere $S = \{\mathbf{x} : \|\mathbf{x}\| = 1\}$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$. When we apply $A$ to every point on this sphere, the image $\{A\mathbf{x} : \mathbf{x} \in S\}$ is an ellipsoid in $\mathbb{R}^m$. The SVD tells us everything about this ellipsoid: the columns of $V$ point in the directions on the sphere that get mapped to the principal axes of the ellipsoid, the columns of $U$ give the directions of those principal axes in the output space, and the singular values $\sigma_i$ give the semi-axis lengths of the ellipsoid.

Any linear transformation can be broken into three steps:

$A\mathbf{x} = U\Sigma V^T\mathbf{x}$
1
$V^T\mathbf{x}$: Rotate (or reflect) in the input space. Since $V$ is orthogonal, $V^T$ maps the standard basis to the right singular vectors. The unit sphere stays a unit sphere — we've just re-labeled the axes.
2
$\Sigma(\cdot)$: Scale each coordinate by the corresponding singular value $\sigma_i$. The unit sphere becomes an axis-aligned ellipsoid. If $m \neq n$, some coordinates are created (as zeros) or discarded.
3
$U(\cdot)$: Rotate (or reflect) in the output space. The axis-aligned ellipsoid is rotated to its final orientation. The ellipsoid axes now point along the columns of $U$.

Let's make this concrete with a 2D example. Consider the matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$. Its SVD gives singular values $\sigma_1 = 4$ and $\sigma_2 = 2$. The unit circle gets mapped to an ellipse with semi-major axis of length 4 along the direction $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,1)^T$ and semi-minor axis of length 2 along $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,-1)^T$. The matrix stretches space twice as much in the $(1,1)$ direction as in the $(1,-1)$ direction.

Singular Values as Stretching Factors

The columns of $V$ are orthogonal directions in the input that get mapped to orthogonal directions (columns of $U$) in the output. The singular values tell you the stretching factor along each direction. Specifically, $\sigma_1$ is the maximum stretching factor (the operator norm $\|A\|_2$) and $\sigma_r$ is the minimum stretching on the row space.

The condition number $\kappa(A) = \sigma_1 / \sigma_r$ measures how much the matrix distorts the sphere into an elongated ellipsoid. A large condition number means the matrix is nearly singular and numerical computations with it will be unstable.

You might wonder: what happens when some singular values are zero? If $\sigma_i = 0$, the matrix completely crushes the $i$-th input direction to zero — that direction lies in the null space of $A$. The number of non-zero singular values equals the rank of $A$, and the corresponding right singular vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_r$ span the row space while $\mathbf{v}_{r+1}, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n$ span the null space. Similarly, $\mathbf{u}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{u}_r$ span the column space and $\mathbf{u}_{r+1}, \ldots, \mathbf{u}_m$ span the left null space. The SVD simultaneously reveals all four fundamental subspaces.

Computing the SVD

How do we actually find $U$, $\Sigma$, and $V$? The key insight is that the SVD is intimately connected to the eigendecomposition of two symmetric matrices: $A^T A$ and $A A^T$. Since symmetric matrices always have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors, this connection guarantees that the SVD always exists — even when $A$ itself is not square or not diagonalizable.

Start from $A = U\Sigma V^T$ and form $A^T A$:

Deriving the Right Singular Vectors
1
$A^T A = (U\Sigma V^T)^T (U\Sigma V^T) = V \Sigma^T U^T U \Sigma V^T$
2
Since $U$ is orthogonal, $U^T U = I$, so $A^T A = V \Sigma^T \Sigma V^T = V D V^T$ where $D = \Sigma^T \Sigma$ is diagonal with entries $\sigma_i^2$.
3
This is exactly the eigendecomposition of $A^T A$! So the right singular vectors $\mathbf{v}_i$ are the eigenvectors of $A^T A$, and the singular values are $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i(A^T A)}$.

Similarly, forming $AA^T$ yields $AA^T = U \Sigma \Sigma^T U^T$, which is the eigendecomposition of $AA^T$. The left singular vectors $\mathbf{u}_i$ are the eigenvectors of $AA^T$, and the non-zero eigenvalues are again $\sigma_i^2$. This gives us a recipe:

  • Right singular vectors $V$: Eigenvectors of the symmetric matrix $A^T A$.
  • Left singular vectors $U$: Eigenvectors of the symmetric matrix $AA^T$.
  • Singular values: $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}$ where $\lambda_i$ are eigenvalues of $A^TA$ (or equivalently of $AA^T$).
$$A^TA = V\Sigma^T\Sigma V^T, \quad AA^T = U\Sigma\Sigma^T U^T$$

Why are the eigenvalues of $A^T A$ non-negative? Because for any vector $\mathbf{x}$, $\mathbf{x}^T (A^T A) \mathbf{x} = \|A\mathbf{x}\|^2 \geq 0$. This means $A^T A$ is positive semi-definite, so its eigenvalues are $\geq 0$, and we can safely take square roots to get the singular values. This also proves that singular values are always real and non-negative — a fact with no analog for eigenvalues, which can be negative or complex.

Worked Example: SVD of a 2×3 Matrix

Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$. Let's compute its SVD step by step.

Step-by-step SVD computation
1
Compute $A^T A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$
2
Find eigenvalues of $A^T A$: the characteristic polynomial gives $\lambda(\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 2) = 0$, yielding $\lambda_1 = 2$, $\lambda_2 = 1$, $\lambda_3 = 0$. So $\sigma_1 = \sqrt{2}$, $\sigma_2 = 1$, and rank$(A) = 2$.
3
For $\lambda_1 = 2$: solving $(A^TA - 2I)\mathbf{v} = 0$ gives $\mathbf{v}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, 0, 1)^T$. For $\lambda_2 = 1$: $\mathbf{v}_2 = (0, 1, 0)^T$. For $\lambda_3 = 0$: $\mathbf{v}_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(-1, 0, 1)^T$.
4
Compute $\mathbf{u}_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i} A \mathbf{v}_i$: $\mathbf{u}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} A \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,0,1)^T = (1,0)^T$ and $\mathbf{u}_2 = A \cdot (0,1,0)^T = (0,1)^T$.
5
The SVD is $A = U \Sigma V^T$ with $U = I_2$, $\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} \sqrt{2} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, and $V = \begin{pmatrix} 1/\sqrt{2} & 0 & -1/\sqrt{2} \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1/\sqrt{2} & 0 & 1/\sqrt{2} \end{pmatrix}$.
Practical Note

In practice, nobody computes the SVD by forming $A^T A$ and finding its eigenvalues — that approach squares the condition number and loses numerical precision. Production algorithms (like the Golub-Kahan bidiagonalization) work directly with $A$. But the eigenvalue connection is essential for understanding what the SVD means.

Low-Rank Approximation

One of the most powerful applications of the SVD is low-rank approximation: finding the simplest matrix that is "close" to a given matrix. This is the mathematical foundation of data compression, noise reduction, and dimensionality reduction. The key question is: if I can only afford to store $k$ rank-one components instead of all $r$, which $k$ should I pick?

The SVD gives a definitive answer: always pick the top $k$ singular values. The resulting truncated SVD is the best possible rank-$k$ approximation, in a very precise mathematical sense. No other rank-$k$ matrix — no matter how cleverly constructed — can be closer to $A$.

$$A_k = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_i \mathbf{u}_i \mathbf{v}_i^T$$

Think of this intuitively: the singular values are sorted in decreasing order, so the first term $\sigma_1 \mathbf{u}_1 \mathbf{v}_1^T$ captures the "most important" pattern in the data — the direction of maximum variance. The second term captures the next most important pattern, orthogonal to the first, and so on. By keeping only the top $k$ terms, we preserve the high-energy structure and discard the low-energy details (which are often noise).

Eckart-Young-Mirsky Theorem

Among all matrices $B$ of rank at most $k$, the truncated SVD $A_k$ uniquely minimizes the distance to $A$:

$$\min_{\text{rank}(B) \leq k} \|A - B\|_F = \|A - A_k\|_F = \sqrt{\sigma_{k+1}^2 + \cdots + \sigma_r^2}$$

This holds for both the Frobenius norm and the spectral (operator) norm. In the spectral norm, $\|A - A_k\|_2 = \sigma_{k+1}$. No other rank-$k$ matrix can do better!

The approximation error is entirely controlled by the discarded singular values. If the singular values decay rapidly (as they often do in real data), then even a small $k$ gives an excellent approximation. We can measure the fraction of "energy" (or variance) retained:

$$\text{Energy retained} = \frac{\sigma_1^2 + \cdots + \sigma_k^2}{\sigma_1^2 + \cdots + \sigma_r^2} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_i^2}{\|A\|_F^2}$$

Image Compression Example

A grayscale image is just a matrix of pixel intensities. A $1000 \times 1000$ image requires storing $10^6$ numbers. But the SVD of this image matrix typically has rapidly decaying singular values — the first few capture the overall structure (brightness gradients, major shapes), and the rest encode fine textures and noise.

Keeping only $k = 50$ singular values, we store $U_{50}$ ($1000 \times 50$), $\Sigma_{50}$ ($50$ values), and $V_{50}$ ($1000 \times 50$) — a total of $100{,}050$ numbers, about 10% of the original. Yet the reconstructed image is often visually indistinguishable from the original. This is lossy compression at its most elegant: the SVD automatically identifies the most important patterns and discards the rest.

Storage Savings

An $m \times n$ matrix requires $mn$ numbers. The rank-$k$ SVD stores $k(m + n + 1)$ numbers. The compression ratio is $\frac{mn}{k(m+n+1)}$. For a $1000 \times 1000$ matrix with $k = 50$, the compression ratio is approximately $10\times$.

Applications in ML

The SVD is not merely a theoretical tool — it is one of the most widely used algorithms in machine learning. Almost any time you need to reduce dimensionality, find latent structure, or solve an ill-conditioned linear system, the SVD (or a close relative) is the tool of choice. Let's explore the major applications.

Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

PCA is arguably the most fundamental dimensionality reduction technique, and it is essentially just the SVD of a centered data matrix. Suppose you have $n$ data points in $\mathbb{R}^d$, arranged as rows of a matrix $X$ (after subtracting the mean from each column). The covariance matrix is $C = \frac{1}{n-1} X^T X$. Its eigenvectors are the principal components — and by our earlier analysis, these are exactly the right singular vectors of $X$.

The connection is: if $X = U\Sigma V^T$, then $X^TX = V\Sigma^2 V^T$, so the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix are $\frac{\sigma_i^2}{n-1}$, and the principal components are the columns of $V$. The projected data in the lower-dimensional space is simply $XV = U\Sigma$ (keeping only the first $k$ columns). In practice, we compute PCA via SVD of $X$ rather than by forming $X^TX$, because it is more numerically stable and handles the case $d > n$ gracefully.

The fraction of variance explained by the first $k$ principal components is $\sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i^2 / \sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i^2$. A "scree plot" of singular values helps decide how many components to keep — look for an "elbow" where the singular values drop sharply.

Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA)

In natural language processing, we often represent a text corpus as a term-document matrix $A$ where $A_{ij}$ counts (or weights) how often term $i$ appears in document $j$. This matrix is huge and sparse. The truncated SVD $A_k$ discovers "latent topics" — the left singular vectors capture groups of related words, and the right singular vectors capture groups of related documents. Two documents about the same topic will be nearby in the $k$-dimensional SVD space even if they use completely different words.

This ability to discover synonymy and polysemy from raw co-occurrence data was revolutionary when LSA was introduced in the 1990s. Modern word embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe) can be understood as sophisticated extensions of this same SVD-based idea.

Recommender Systems

Netflix's famous million-dollar prize was won using matrix factorization techniques closely related to SVD. The idea: users and movies live in a shared latent space of "tastes." A user-movie rating matrix $R$ (mostly unobserved) is approximated as $R \approx U_k \Sigma_k V_k^T$, where the $k$ latent dimensions might correspond to concepts like "action vs. drama" or "mainstream vs. art-house."

To predict how user $i$ would rate movie $j$, we compute the $(i,j)$ entry of the low-rank approximation. The missing entries are "filled in" by the low-rank structure, which assumes that user preferences can be explained by a small number of latent factors. In practice, regularized variants (like SVD++ and ALS) are used to handle the massive number of missing entries.

The Pseudoinverse and Least Squares

The Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse provides the "best" solution to linear systems that may be over-determined (more equations than unknowns) or under-determined (more unknowns than equations). The SVD gives a clean formula:

$$A^+ = V\Sigma^+ U^T$$

where $\Sigma^+$ is formed by taking the reciprocal of each non-zero singular value (and leaving zeros as zeros). For the system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, the pseudoinverse gives $\mathbf{x}^+ = A^+ \mathbf{b}$, which is the minimum-norm least-squares solution. The beauty of this approach is that it handles rank-deficient matrices gracefully — we simply invert only the non-zero singular values, effectively projecting onto the row space and ignoring the null space.

In machine learning, this appears whenever you fit a linear model: ordinary least squares can be solved as $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = X^+ \mathbf{y}$. When $X$ is nearly rank-deficient (multicollinearity), small singular values cause instability. Ridge regression can be understood as replacing $\sigma_i^{-1}$ with $\sigma_i / (\sigma_i^2 + \lambda)$ in the pseudoinverse formula — shrinking the contribution of small singular values.

Information-Theoretic Connections

The SVD has deep and beautiful connections to information theory — our project's unifying thread. The singular values of a matrix can be thought of as a "spectrum" that describes how information is distributed across dimensions, and tools from information theory let us quantify this distribution precisely.

Spectral Entropy

Given a matrix $A$ with singular values $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_r$, we can define a probability distribution by normalizing the squared singular values:

$$p_i = \frac{\sigma_i^2}{\sum_{j=1}^r \sigma_j^2} = \frac{\sigma_i^2}{\|A\|_F^2}$$

The spectral entropy (or SVD entropy) of $A$ is the Shannon entropy of this distribution:

$$H_{\text{SVD}}(A) = -\sum_{i=1}^{r} p_i \log_2 p_i$$

This quantity measures the "effective dimensionality" of the matrix. If one singular value dominates (the matrix is approximately rank-1), the entropy is near zero — all the information is concentrated in one direction. If all singular values are equal (the matrix acts like a scaled identity on its row space), the entropy is $\log_2 r$ — information is uniformly spread across all dimensions.

Effective Rank via Entropy

The effective rank of a matrix $A$ can be defined as $\text{erank}(A) = 2^{H_{\text{SVD}}(A)}$. This is a continuous relaxation of the algebraic rank. A rank-10 matrix with 9 negligible singular values has effective rank close to 1. A rank-10 matrix with 10 equal singular values has effective rank 10. This measure is widely used in signal processing and statistics to assess the "true" dimensionality of data.

Rate-Distortion Theory

In information theory, rate-distortion theory asks: what is the minimum number of bits needed to represent a signal with a given level of distortion? The truncated SVD provides the optimal answer for Gaussian sources under mean-squared-error distortion. Keeping the top $k$ singular values minimizes the reconstruction error (distortion) for a given "budget" of $k$ components (rate).

This connects the Eckart-Young theorem to Shannon's rate-distortion function: the singular values of the data matrix determine the rate-distortion curve, and the optimal encoder at each rate level is exactly the truncated SVD. Rapid decay of singular values means the data is highly compressible — it has low intrinsic information content despite living in a high-dimensional space.

Mutual Information and SVD

Consider two random vectors $\mathbf{X}$ and $\mathbf{Y}$ with a joint Gaussian distribution. The mutual information $I(\mathbf{X}; \mathbf{Y})$ depends on the singular values of the cross-covariance matrix $\Sigma_{XY}$. Each canonical correlation (which comes from the SVD of a normalized version of $\Sigma_{XY}$) contributes independently to the mutual information. The SVD thus decomposes the statistical dependence between $\mathbf{X}$ and $\mathbf{Y}$ into independent channels, each with its own "information capacity" determined by the corresponding singular value.

Common Misconceptions

The SVD is often confused with related decompositions, and students frequently stumble over subtle points. Let's clear up the most common misconceptions.

Misconception 1: SVD = Eigendecomposition

Wrong. The eigendecomposition $A = P D P^{-1}$ requires $A$ to be square and diagonalizable, and the basis vectors are generally not orthogonal. The SVD $A = U\Sigma V^T$ works for any matrix (any shape, any rank), always exists, and always uses orthonormal bases. The matrices $U$ and $V$ are different (input vs. output space), whereas eigendecomposition uses a single basis. For symmetric positive semi-definite matrices, the two decompositions coincide: the singular values equal the eigenvalues, and $U = V$.

Misconception 2: SVD Might Not Exist

Wrong. The SVD always exists for every real (or complex) matrix. No exceptions. This is in sharp contrast to the eigendecomposition, which may fail for defective matrices, and the Cholesky decomposition, which requires positive definiteness. The universality of the SVD is one of its greatest strengths.

Misconception 3: Truncated SVD = Full SVD with Zeros

Subtle but important. The full SVD $A = U\Sigma V^T$ is an exact equality — no information is lost. The truncated SVD $A_k$ is an approximation that discards the smallest $r - k$ singular values. When people say "SVD" in an ML context, they almost always mean the truncated SVD, but the distinction matters. The full SVD preserves the null space information; the truncated SVD deliberately throws it away.

Misconception 4: Singular Values Can Be Negative

Wrong. Singular values are always non-negative by convention. They are square roots of eigenvalues of $A^TA$, which is positive semi-definite. Any "sign" information is absorbed into the columns of $U$ or $V$. This is different from eigenvalues, which can be negative, zero, or complex.

Misconception 5: SVD Is Unique

Partially true. The singular values are unique, but the singular vectors are not — if $\sigma_i$ has multiplicity greater than 1, the corresponding singular vectors can be rotated within their eigenspace. Even for simple singular values, the sign of each pair $(\mathbf{u}_i, \mathbf{v}_i)$ is ambiguous: flipping both signs gives the same outer product $\sigma_i \mathbf{u}_i \mathbf{v}_i^T$. This is important to remember when comparing SVD results from different software libraries.

Exercises

Easy Problems 🟢

  1. The matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$ is already diagonal. Write down its SVD. What are $U$, $\Sigma$, and $V$? (Be careful about the ordering of singular values.)
  2. If a matrix $A$ has singular values $\sigma_1 = 10$, $\sigma_2 = 3$, $\sigma_3 = 1$, compute $\|A\|_F$ (the Frobenius norm) and $\|A\|_2$ (the spectral norm).
  3. A $4 \times 3$ matrix has singular values $7, 4, 0$. What is the rank of this matrix? What are the dimensions of its null space?
  4. The matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ has rank 1. Compute $A^TA$ and find its eigenvalues. What are the singular values of $A$?
  5. If $Q$ is an orthogonal matrix ($Q^TQ = I$), what are its singular values? Justify your answer using the geometric interpretation of SVD.
  6. What fraction of the total energy (in the Frobenius norm sense) is captured by the top singular value of a matrix with singular values $\sigma_1 = 8$, $\sigma_2 = 2$, $\sigma_3 = 1$?
  7. Compute the condition number of $A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.01 \end{pmatrix}$. Is this matrix well-conditioned or ill-conditioned?
  8. A matrix has SVD $A = U\Sigma V^T$. Express $A^T$ in terms of $U$, $\Sigma$, and $V$. What is the relationship between the singular values of $A$ and $A^T$?

Medium Problems 🟡

  1. Compute the full SVD of $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$. Verify that $U\Sigma V^T = A$ by multiplying the factors together.
  2. A $100 \times 100$ data matrix has singular values that decay as $\sigma_i = 10 / i$. How many singular values do you need to keep to capture 90% of the total energy? Express your answer in terms of the harmonic series.
  3. Prove that $\|A\|_F^2 = \text{tr}(A^TA) = \sum_i \sigma_i^2$. Start from the SVD $A = U\Sigma V^T$ and use properties of the trace and orthogonal matrices.
  4. A centered data matrix $X$ ($n \times d$, mean-subtracted) has SVD $X = U\Sigma V^T$. Show that the covariance matrix $C = \frac{1}{n-1}X^TX$ has eigenvalues $\frac{\sigma_i^2}{n-1}$. What are the principal components in terms of $V$?
  5. The Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse satisfies four properties: $AA^+A = A$, $A^+AA^+ = A^+$, $(AA^+)^T = AA^+$, and $(A^+A)^T = A^+A$. Verify all four for $A^+ = V\Sigma^+ U^T$ using the SVD.
  6. Compute the spectral entropy $H_{\text{SVD}}$ for a matrix with singular values $(10, 10, 10)$ and for one with singular values $(30, 1, 1)$. Which has higher effective rank? Interpret the results.
  7. A $3 \times 3$ matrix $A$ has SVD with $\sigma_1 = 5$, $\sigma_2 = 3$, $\sigma_3 = 1$. Compute $\|A - A_1\|_F$ and $\|A - A_2\|_F$ where $A_k$ is the best rank-$k$ approximation. What percentage of the Frobenius norm error is eliminated by each additional singular value?
  8. Let $A$ be $m \times n$ with $m > n$. The system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is overdetermined. Show that the least-squares solution $\hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^+\mathbf{b}$ minimizes $\|A\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b}\|^2$. What role do the singular values play in the stability of this solution?

Hard Problems 🔴

  1. Eckart-Young Proof. Prove the Eckart-Young theorem: for any rank-$k$ matrix $B$, $\|A - A_k\|_F \leq \|A - B\|_F$. Hint: use the fact that $\text{rank}(A - B) \geq r - k$ where $r = \text{rank}(A)$, and consider the interaction of the null spaces.
  2. SVD and the Four Fundamental Subspaces. Given $A = U\Sigma V^T$ with rank $r$, prove that: (a) the row space of $A$ is spanned by $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_r$; (b) the null space is spanned by $\mathbf{v}_{r+1}, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n$; (c) the column space is spanned by $\mathbf{u}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{u}_r$; (d) the left null space is spanned by $\mathbf{u}_{r+1}, \ldots, \mathbf{u}_m$.
  3. Randomized SVD. Modern large-scale SVD algorithms use randomized methods. The key idea: project $A$ onto a random $k$-dimensional subspace by computing $Y = A\Omega$ where $\Omega$ is $n \times k$ random Gaussian, then compute the SVD of the small matrix $Q^T A$ where $Q$ is an orthonormal basis for the column space of $Y$. Explain why this works and when it gives a good approximation. Under what conditions on the singular value spectrum does randomized SVD perform well?
  4. Matrix Completion. In the Netflix problem, most entries of the user-movie matrix are unobserved. Prove that if the true rating matrix has rank $r$, then under mild "incoherence" conditions, it can be exactly recovered from $O(rn \log^2 n)$ randomly observed entries by solving a nuclear norm minimization problem (the nuclear norm is $\sum_i \sigma_i$). Why is the nuclear norm a convex relaxation of the rank?
  5. SVD Perturbation Theory. Weyl's theorem states that if $A$ and $A + E$ are two matrices, then $|\sigma_i(A+E) - \sigma_i(A)| \leq \|E\|_2$ for all $i$. Prove this using the variational characterization of singular values: $\sigma_k(A) = \min_{\dim(S)=n-k+1} \max_{\mathbf{x} \in S, \|\mathbf{x}\|=1} \|A\mathbf{x}\|$. Discuss the implications for data science: what happens to the SVD when the data has noise?
  6. Spectral Entropy and Compression. A signal matrix $A$ has spectral entropy $H_{\text{SVD}} = 3.5$ bits and rank $r = 20$. Derive upper and lower bounds on the number of singular values needed to capture 95% of the energy. How does the spectral entropy constrain the rate-distortion curve of the source? Connect this to Shannon's rate-distortion theorem for Gaussian sources.
  7. SVD of Kronecker Products. If $A = B \otimes C$ is a Kronecker product, show that the singular values of $A$ are all products $\sigma_i(B) \cdot \sigma_j(C)$, and the singular vectors are Kronecker products of the corresponding singular vectors. Use this to prove that $\text{rank}(B \otimes C) = \text{rank}(B) \cdot \text{rank}(C)$.
  8. CUR Decomposition vs. SVD. The CUR decomposition approximates $A \approx C U R$ where $C$ and $R$ are actual columns and rows of $A$. Unlike the SVD, the factors are interpretable. Prove that the CUR decomposition can achieve $\|A - CUR\|_F \leq (1+\epsilon)\|A - A_k\|_F$ with high probability when columns and rows are sampled according to their "leverage scores" (which come from the SVD). Why are leverage scores the right sampling distribution?
