Definition

Positive definite matrices occupy a central position in linear algebra, optimization, statistics, and machine learning. They are the matrices that "behave nicely" — every quadratic form they define points upward, every system of equations they appear in has a unique solution, and every algorithm that encounters them converges reliably. Before we can appreciate their importance, we need a precise definition and a firm grasp of the underlying concept.

A symmetric matrix $A$ is positive definite if for all nonzero vectors $\mathbf{x}$:

Positive Definiteness

A real symmetric $n \times n$ matrix $A$ is positive definite (PD) if: $$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0 \quad \text{for all } \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n,\; \mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$$

If the inequality is relaxed to $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} \geq 0$, the matrix is called positive semi-definite (PSD). The distinction matters: PSD matrices may have zero eigenvalues and are singular, while PD matrices are always invertible.

The expression $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}$ is called a quadratic form. Think of it as a machine that takes a vector $\mathbf{x}$ and produces a single number. Positive definite means the quadratic form is always positive (except at the origin, where it equals zero). This is a remarkably strong requirement — it must hold for every nonzero direction in $\mathbb{R}^n$, not just the coordinate axes.

Why do we require symmetry? Because for any matrix $B$, the quadratic form $\mathbf{x}^T B \mathbf{x}$ depends only on the symmetric part $\frac{1}{2}(B + B^T)$. The antisymmetric part $\frac{1}{2}(B - B^T)$ vanishes in the product (since $\mathbf{x}^T C \mathbf{x} = 0$ for any skew-symmetric $C$). Therefore, restricting attention to symmetric matrices loses no generality and simplifies the theory considerably — symmetric matrices have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors, which are properties we exploit throughout this tutorial.

As a quick example, the $2 \times 2$ identity matrix $I = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ is positive definite because $\mathbf{x}^T I \mathbf{x} = x_1^2 + x_2^2 > 0$ for any nonzero $(x_1, x_2)$. More generally, any diagonal matrix with all positive entries on the diagonal is positive definite. But positive definiteness is far richer than diagonal matrices — it encompasses any symmetric matrix whose "energy" $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}$ is always positive.

Tests for Positive Definiteness

Checking the definition directly — verifying $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0$ for every nonzero $\mathbf{x}$ — is impossible because there are infinitely many vectors. Fortunately, several equivalent conditions reduce the test to a finite computation. Each test offers a different lens on positive definiteness, and the fact that they are all equivalent is one of the beautiful results in linear algebra.

Equivalent Conditions for PD
1
Eigenvalue test: All eigenvalues satisfy $\lambda_i > 0$. Since $A$ is symmetric, it has real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors. The quadratic form evaluated at eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_i$ gives $\mathbf{v}_i^T A \mathbf{v}_i = \lambda_i \|\mathbf{v}_i\|^2$, so each $\lambda_i$ must be positive.
2
Pivot test: All pivots are positive during Gaussian elimination (without row exchanges). The pivots are the ratios of consecutive leading principal minors: $d_k = D_k / D_{k-1}$. If all pivots are positive, the matrix factors as $A = LDL^T$ with positive diagonal $D$.
3
Sylvester's criterion: All leading principal minors are positive: $D_1 > 0,\; D_2 > 0,\; \ldots,\; D_n > 0$, where $D_k = \det(A_{1:k, 1:k})$ is the determinant of the top-left $k \times k$ submatrix. This is often the most practical hand-calculation test for small matrices.
4
Cholesky factorization test: $A = LL^T$ for some lower triangular $L$ with positive diagonal entries. If the Cholesky algorithm completes without encountering a zero or negative value under the square root, the matrix is PD. This is the standard computational test.

Let's understand why these are equivalent. The eigenvalue test is the most conceptual: since $A$ is symmetric, we can diagonalize it as $A = Q \Lambda Q^T$, so $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}^T \Lambda \mathbf{y} = \sum_i \lambda_i y_i^2$ where $\mathbf{y} = Q^T \mathbf{x}$. This sum is positive for all nonzero $\mathbf{y}$ if and only if every $\lambda_i > 0$. The pivot test connects to the $LDL^T$ factorization, where the pivots are the entries of $D$. Sylvester's criterion follows because $D_k = d_1 d_2 \cdots d_k$ (the product of the first $k$ pivots), so all $D_k > 0$ if and only if all $d_k > 0$.

Example: 2×2 Case

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ b & c \end{pmatrix}$$

Applying Sylvester's criterion: the first leading minor is $D_1 = a$, and the second is $D_2 = ac - b^2 = \det(A)$. So $A$ is positive definite if and only if $a > 0$ and $ac - b^2 > 0$. Notice that $ac - b^2 > 0$ together with $a > 0$ automatically forces $c > 0$ (since $c > b^2/a \geq 0$). The eigenvalue test gives the same result: the eigenvalues are $\frac{(a+c) \pm \sqrt{(a-c)^2 + 4b^2}}{2}$, and both are positive exactly when the trace $a + c > 0$ and determinant $ac - b^2 > 0$.

Example: Checking a 3×3 Matrix

Consider the matrix:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

The leading principal minors are: $D_1 = 2 > 0$, $D_2 = 2 \cdot 2 - (-1)^2 = 3 > 0$, and $D_3 = \det(A) = 2(4-1) - (-1)(-2-0) + 0 = 6 - 2 = 4 > 0$. All three are positive, so by Sylvester's criterion, $A$ is positive definite. This particular matrix arises naturally as the stiffness matrix in finite-element analysis of a rod with three nodes, illustrating how PD matrices emerge from physical systems where energy is always non-negative.

Which Test to Use?

For hand calculations on small matrices (2×2, 3×3), Sylvester's criterion is usually fastest. For computational work, attempt the Cholesky factorization — it serves as both a test and a useful decomposition. The eigenvalue test is conceptually illuminating but computationally expensive for large matrices, since finding eigenvalues is an $O(n^3)$ iterative process.

Geometric Interpretation

The quadratic form $f(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}$ maps each vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$ to a real number, creating a surface in $(n+1)$-dimensional space. The character of the matrix $A$ — positive definite, negative definite, or indefinite — determines the shape of this surface. This geometric picture is not just a visualization aid; it is the foundation for understanding optimization, where we seek minima and maxima of functions whose local curvature is captured by the Hessian matrix.

  • Positive definite: Bowl opening upward — the surface rises in every direction from the origin, which is the unique global minimum.
  • Negative definite: Bowl opening downward — the surface falls in every direction, and the origin is the unique global maximum.
  • Positive semi-definite: A valley or trough — the surface is flat along some directions (corresponding to zero eigenvalues) but never dips below the origin.
  • Indefinite: A saddle shape — the surface rises in some directions and falls in others, so the origin is neither a minimum nor a maximum.

To build intuition, consider the 2D case $f(x_1, x_2) = ax_1^2 + 2bx_1 x_2 + cx_2^2$. If $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ b & c \end{pmatrix}$ is PD, the level curves $f(x_1, x_2) = k$ (for $k > 0$) are ellipses centered at the origin. The axes of these ellipses align with the eigenvectors of $A$, and their lengths are proportional to $1/\sqrt{\lambda_i}$. A matrix with equal eigenvalues produces circles (the identity matrix), while very different eigenvalues produce elongated ellipses — a situation that causes trouble for gradient-descent algorithms, as we'll discuss later.

In higher dimensions, level sets of a positive definite quadratic form are ellipsoids. The eigenvectors define the principal axes of the ellipsoid, and the eigenvalues determine how stretched or compressed the ellipsoid is along each axis. This geometric picture connects directly to multivariate Gaussian distributions, where the covariance matrix $\Sigma$ (which is PSD) determines the shape of the probability contours — which are exactly the level sets of $\mathbf{x}^T \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{x}$.

Connection to Convexity

A twice-differentiable function $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is convex if and only if its Hessian matrix $H(x) = \nabla^2 f(x)$ is positive semi-definite everywhere. If the Hessian is strictly positive definite everywhere, the function is strictly convex, guaranteeing a unique global minimum. This is why positive definiteness is the linchpin of convex optimization — the entire theory of gradient descent, Newton's method, and interior-point methods rests on the PD/PSD structure of the objective's Hessian.

Quadratic Forms

A quadratic form is a homogeneous polynomial of degree 2 in $n$ variables. Every quadratic form can be written as $Q(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}$ for a unique symmetric matrix $A$. Understanding quadratic forms is essential because they appear everywhere: in the energy of physical systems, the loss surfaces of machine learning models, and the log-likelihoods of Gaussian distributions.

Quadratic Form

Given a symmetric matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, the associated quadratic form is the function $Q : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by: $$Q(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} a_{ij} x_i x_j$$ In two dimensions with $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ b & c \end{pmatrix}$, this expands to $Q(x_1, x_2) = a x_1^2 + 2b x_1 x_2 + c x_2^2$.

The gradient of the quadratic form is $\nabla Q(\mathbf{x}) = 2A\mathbf{x}$, and the Hessian is $\nabla^2 Q = 2A$. Setting the gradient to zero gives $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$, which has the unique solution $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ when $A$ is positive definite (since PD implies invertibility). The Hessian $2A$ is positive definite, confirming that the origin is a strict global minimum. This is the simplest example of a convex optimization problem: minimize $Q(\mathbf{x})$ over $\mathbb{R}^n$.

When the quadratic form includes a linear term, we get $f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{x}$. Setting $\nabla f = A\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{0}$ yields the linear system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$. When $A$ is positive definite, this system has a unique solution that is the global minimizer of $f$. This elegant duality — solving a linear system is equivalent to minimizing a quadratic — underlies the conjugate gradient method and many other iterative solvers.

Level Curves and Ellipses

The level set $\{\mathbf{x} : \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = c\}$ for a PD matrix $A$ and constant $c > 0$ is an ellipsoid. To see this, diagonalize $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ and substitute $\mathbf{y} = Q^T\mathbf{x}$:

$$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}^T \Lambda \mathbf{y} = \lambda_1 y_1^2 + \lambda_2 y_2^2 + \cdots + \lambda_n y_n^2 = c$$

This is the equation of an ellipsoid in the $\mathbf{y}$-coordinates with semi-axes of length $\sqrt{c/\lambda_i}$. The rotation $Q$ maps this back to the original coordinates, aligning the semi-axes with the eigenvectors. When the eigenvalues are very different (e.g., $\lambda_1 = 100$ and $\lambda_2 = 1$), the ellipsoid is highly elongated — the condition number $\kappa(A) = \lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$ measures this elongation. High condition numbers make optimization difficult because gradient descent takes many tiny zigzagging steps along the narrow direction.

Cholesky Decomposition

The Cholesky decomposition is the "square root" of a positive definite matrix, and it is one of the most important matrix factorizations in computational mathematics. Every positive definite matrix $A$ can be uniquely written as the product of a lower triangular matrix and its transpose. This factorization is both theoretically elegant and practically indispensable — it is faster, more stable, and more memory-efficient than the general LU decomposition.

Cholesky Decomposition

If $A$ is a symmetric positive definite matrix, then there exists a unique lower triangular matrix $L$ with positive diagonal entries such that: $$A = LL^T$$ The matrix $L$ is called the Cholesky factor of $A$.

The Algorithm

The entries of $L$ are computed column by column. For the $j$-th column:

$$l_{jj} = \sqrt{a_{jj} - \sum_{k=1}^{j-1} l_{jk}^2}$$ $$l_{ij} = \frac{1}{l_{jj}} \left( a_{ij} - \sum_{k=1}^{j-1} l_{ik} l_{jk} \right), \quad i > j$$

Notice that the diagonal entries involve a square root. If $A$ is truly PD, the quantity under the square root is always positive — this is guaranteed by the positive pivots. If during computation we encounter a zero or negative value under the square root, the matrix is not PD, so the Cholesky algorithm doubles as a test for positive definiteness.

Example

Consider:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \\ 2 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$$

We compute: $l_{11} = \sqrt{4} = 2$, $l_{21} = 2/2 = 1$, $l_{22} = \sqrt{5 - 1^2} = 2$. So $L = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$ and we can verify $LL^T = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \\ 2 & 5 \end{pmatrix} = A$.

Computational Advantage

Cholesky requires about $\frac{n^3}{3}$ operations, roughly half the cost of LU decomposition ($\frac{2n^3}{3}$). It is also more numerically stable because it avoids pivoting (no row exchanges needed). In practice, Cholesky is the default method for solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ when $A$ is known to be PD.

Connection to Sampling from Gaussians

One of the most important applications of Cholesky in machine learning is sampling from multivariate Gaussian distributions. To sample $\mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \Sigma)$, we first compute $L = \text{chol}(\Sigma)$, then generate $\mathbf{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, I)$, and set $\mathbf{x} = \boldsymbol{\mu} + L\mathbf{z}$. This works because $\text{Cov}(L\mathbf{z}) = L \cdot \text{Cov}(\mathbf{z}) \cdot L^T = L I L^T = \Sigma$. This "reparameterization trick" is fundamental in variational autoencoders (VAEs) and other generative models, where we need to backpropagate through a sampling operation.

Covariance Matrices

Covariance matrices are perhaps the most ubiquitous example of positive semi-definite matrices in applied mathematics. Every covariance matrix is symmetric and PSD — this is not an assumption but a mathematical consequence of the definition. Understanding why covariance matrices are PSD, and when they are strictly PD, is essential for statistical modeling, Bayesian inference, and machine learning.

Covariance Matrix

For a random vector $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ with mean $\boldsymbol{\mu} = E[\mathbf{X}]$, the covariance matrix is: $$\Sigma = E[(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu})(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^T]$$ Each entry $\Sigma_{ij} = \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$, with $\Sigma_{ii} = \text{Var}(X_i)$.

Why is $\Sigma$ always PSD? For any nonzero $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, consider the scalar random variable $Y = \mathbf{a}^T(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu})$. Its variance is $\text{Var}(Y) = E[Y^2] = \mathbf{a}^T E[(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu})(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^T] \mathbf{a} = \mathbf{a}^T \Sigma \mathbf{a}$. Since variance is always non-negative, $\mathbf{a}^T \Sigma \mathbf{a} \geq 0$ for all $\mathbf{a}$, which is exactly the PSD condition. The covariance matrix is strictly PD if and only if no linear combination of the variables is deterministic (i.e., no variable is a linear function of the others).

Sample Covariance

Given $m$ data points $\mathbf{x}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_m \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the sample covariance matrix is $S = \frac{1}{m-1} \sum_{i=1}^{m} (\mathbf{x}_i - \bar{\mathbf{x}})(\mathbf{x}_i - \bar{\mathbf{x}})^T$. This can be written as $S = \frac{1}{m-1} X_c^T X_c$ where $X_c$ is the centered data matrix. Since $S$ has the form $B^TB$, it is automatically PSD. However, when $m \leq n$ (more features than samples), $S$ is singular (rank at most $m-1$), making it PSD but not PD. This is the "high-dimensional" setting common in genomics, NLP, and other domains.

Regularization to Ensure PD

When the sample covariance is singular or nearly singular (ill-conditioned), we regularize by adding a small multiple of the identity:

$$\hat{\Sigma} = S + \epsilon I$$

This shifts all eigenvalues up by $\epsilon > 0$, making the matrix strictly PD and invertible. In the Bayesian framework, this corresponds to a Wishart prior on the covariance matrix. The choice of $\epsilon$ trades off between faithfulness to data and numerical stability — too small and the matrix remains ill-conditioned, too large and the covariance structure is dominated by the identity (equivalent to assuming features are independent).

Mahalanobis Distance

The Mahalanobis distance between a point $\mathbf{x}$ and a distribution with mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and covariance $\Sigma$ is:

$$d_M(\mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\mu}) = \sqrt{(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^T \Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})}$$

This requires $\Sigma$ to be PD (so that $\Sigma^{-1}$ exists). Unlike Euclidean distance, Mahalanobis distance accounts for correlations between variables and scales by their variances. It measures how many "standard deviations" a point is from the mean, taking the shape of the distribution into account. Mahalanobis distance is used in anomaly detection, clustering, and as the core of Gaussian discriminant analysis.

Information-Theoretic Connections

Positive definite matrices appear naturally in information theory, particularly through their role in Gaussian distributions. Since Gaussians are the maximum-entropy distributions for a given mean and covariance, and since their entropy depends directly on the determinant of the covariance matrix, PD matrices sit at the heart of the information-theoretic framework that unifies this tutorial series.

Gaussian Entropy

The differential entropy of a multivariate Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \Sigma)$ is:

$$H(\mathbf{X}) = \frac{1}{2} \ln \det(2\pi e \, \Sigma) = \frac{n}{2} \ln(2\pi e) + \frac{1}{2} \ln \det(\Sigma)$$

The entropy depends on $\Sigma$ only through its determinant, which equals the product of its eigenvalues: $\det(\Sigma) = \prod_i \lambda_i$. For $\det(\Sigma)$ to be well-defined and positive (so the log exists), $\Sigma$ must be PD. Geometrically, $\det(\Sigma)$ measures the volume of the confidence ellipsoid — more uncertainty (larger volume) means higher entropy. If $\Sigma$ is only PSD (some eigenvalues are zero), the distribution is degenerate (concentrated on a lower-dimensional subspace), and the differential entropy is $-\infty$.

Fisher Information Matrix

The Fisher information matrix $\mathcal{I}(\theta)$ for a parametric model $p(x|\theta)$ is:

$$\mathcal{I}(\theta)_{ij} = -E\left[\frac{\partial^2}{\partial \theta_i \partial \theta_j} \ln p(X|\theta)\right]$$

The Fisher information matrix is always PSD, and it is PD when the model parameters are identifiable (no two parameter values produce the same distribution). It captures the curvature of the log-likelihood surface — directions with large Fisher information correspond to parameters that the data is informative about. The Cramér-Rao bound states that no unbiased estimator can have covariance smaller than $\mathcal{I}(\theta)^{-1}$, connecting PD matrices to the fundamental limits of statistical estimation.

Natural Gradient

In standard gradient descent, we update $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla \mathcal{L}$. The natural gradient instead uses $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \, \mathcal{I}(\theta)^{-1} \nabla \mathcal{L}$, where $\mathcal{I}(\theta)$ is the Fisher information matrix. This is analogous to Newton's method using the Hessian: it preconditions the gradient by the curvature of the statistical manifold. Since $\mathcal{I}(\theta)$ is PD (when the model is identifiable), the inverse exists and the natural gradient is well-defined. Natural gradient descent converges faster than standard gradient descent because it accounts for the geometry of the parameter space, avoiding the slow zigzagging that plagues ill-conditioned problems.

The PD Requirement in Information Geometry

In information geometry, the Fisher information matrix plays the role of a Riemannian metric on the space of probability distributions. For this metric to be well-defined (non-degenerate), $\mathcal{I}(\theta)$ must be positive definite — PSD is not enough because a degenerate metric would mean some directions in parameter space are "invisible" to the model, collapsing the geometric structure.

Applications in ML

Covariance Matrices in Probabilistic Models

Covariance matrices are always positive semi-definite, and in most practical models they must be strictly PD. The variance in any direction $\mathbf{u}$ is $\mathbf{u}^T \Sigma \mathbf{u} \geq 0$, which is the PSD condition. In Gaussian mixture models, each component has its own covariance matrix $\Sigma_k$. If $\Sigma_k$ becomes singular during EM training (which can happen when a component collapses onto a single data point), the likelihood blows up to infinity — this is the "singularity problem" of GMMs, typically handled by adding a regularization term $\epsilon I$ to each $\Sigma_k$.

Hessian in Optimization

The Hessian of the loss function being positive definite at a critical point confirms it's a local minimum. Newton's method updates parameters via $\theta \leftarrow \theta - H^{-1} \nabla \mathcal{L}$, which requires inverting the Hessian. If $H$ is PD, this inversion is well-conditioned and the update points toward the minimum. If $H$ is indefinite, Newton's method may move toward a saddle point or maximum — quasi-Newton methods like L-BFGS maintain a PD approximation to $H$ to prevent this. The condition number of the Hessian determines the convergence rate: a well-conditioned PD Hessian (eigenvalues of similar magnitude) leads to fast convergence.

Kernel Matrices

Kernel matrices (Gram matrices) in SVMs and Gaussian processes must be positive semi-definite. Given data points $\mathbf{x}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_n$ and a kernel function $k$, the Gram matrix $K_{ij} = k(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j)$ must satisfy $\mathbf{a}^T K \mathbf{a} \geq 0$ for all $\mathbf{a}$. This PSD condition ensures the optimization problem in SVMs is convex (so the global optimum can be found efficiently) and that Gaussian processes define valid probability distributions. Mercer's theorem establishes that a function is a valid kernel if and only if it produces PSD Gram matrices for all finite subsets of the input space.

Regularization

Adding $\lambda I$ (ridge regularization) to $A^TA$ shifts every eigenvalue up by $\lambda > 0$, turning a PSD matrix into a PD one. This ensures the system is well-conditioned and invertible:

$$(A^TA + \lambda I)\mathbf{x} = A^T\mathbf{b}$$

The regularized matrix $A^TA + \lambda I$ has eigenvalues $\sigma_i^2 + \lambda$ (where $\sigma_i$ are the singular values of $A$), so the condition number is $(\sigma_1^2 + \lambda)/(\sigma_n^2 + \lambda)$, which is much better than $\sigma_1^2 / \sigma_n^2$ when $\sigma_n$ is tiny. This is why ridge regression dramatically improves numerical stability in ill-posed problems, at the cost of introducing a small bias toward zero.

Common Misconceptions

Positive definiteness is a subtle concept, and students frequently make incorrect assumptions. Let's address the most common misconceptions head-on to prevent them from causing errors.

Misconception 1: "All entries positive" implies PD

False. The matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 10 \\ 10 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ has all positive entries but is not PD because $\det = 1 - 100 = -99 < 0$. Positive definiteness is about the quadratic form $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}$, not about the signs of individual entries. Conversely, a matrix with negative off-diagonal entries can be PD: $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$ has $\det = 3 > 0$ and is PD.

Misconception 2: PD and PSD are interchangeable

False. The difference between $>$ and $\geq$ is critical. PD matrices are always invertible; PSD matrices may be singular. PD matrices have all eigenvalues strictly positive; PSD matrices allow zero eigenvalues. Many algorithms (Cholesky, solving $Ax = b$, computing $\Sigma^{-1}$) require PD, not just PSD. Using a PSD matrix where PD is required can cause division by zero, numerical instability, or undefined results.

Misconception 3: Symmetry is optional

Misleading. Positive definiteness is defined for symmetric (or Hermitian) matrices. While some authors extend the definition to non-symmetric matrices via $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0$, the quadratic form only depends on the symmetric part $\frac{1}{2}(A + A^T)$. The clean spectral theory (real eigenvalues, orthogonal eigenvectors, diagonalizability) requires symmetry. In practice, if someone hands you a non-symmetric matrix and asks "is it PD?", symmetrize it first.

Misconception 4: Positive diagonal entries imply PD

False. Positive diagonal entries are necessary but not sufficient. The diagonal entry $a_{ii} = \mathbf{e}_i^T A \mathbf{e}_i$ must be positive (just apply the definition with $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{e}_i$), but the off-diagonal entries can create negative quadratic form values in other directions. Sylvester's criterion requires checking all leading principal minors, not just the diagonal.

Exercises

🟢 Easy

Exercise 1: Checking 2×2 PD

Determine whether $A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$ is positive definite using Sylvester's criterion.

Solution

$D_1 = 3 > 0$ and $D_2 = 9 - 1 = 8 > 0$. Both leading principal minors are positive, so $A$ is PD.

Exercise 2: Identity Matrix

Prove that the $n \times n$ identity matrix $I$ is positive definite.

Solution

$\mathbf{x}^T I \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{x} = \|\mathbf{x}\|^2 = \sum_i x_i^2 > 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$.

Exercise 3: Diagonal Matrix

Under what conditions is the diagonal matrix $D = \text{diag}(d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n)$ positive definite?

Solution

$\mathbf{x}^T D \mathbf{x} = \sum_i d_i x_i^2$. This is positive for all nonzero $\mathbf{x}$ if and only if all $d_i > 0$.

Exercise 4: Negative Entries

Is $B = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ -2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$ positive definite?

Solution

$D_1 = 4 > 0$, $D_2 = 16 - 4 = 12 > 0$. Yes, $B$ is PD despite having negative off-diagonal entries.

Exercise 5: PD Implies Invertible

Show that every positive definite matrix is invertible.

Solution

If $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$, then $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = 0$. By the PD condition, $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$. So the null space is trivial and $A$ is invertible.

Exercise 6: Scaling

If $A$ is PD and $c > 0$, is $cA$ also PD?

Solution

$\mathbf{x}^T(cA)\mathbf{x} = c(\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}) > 0$ since $c > 0$ and $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0$. Yes, $cA$ is PD.

Exercise 7: Cholesky of 2×2

Find the Cholesky factor $L$ of $A = \begin{pmatrix} 9 & 6 \\ 6 & 8 \end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

$l_{11} = \sqrt{9} = 3$, $l_{21} = 6/3 = 2$, $l_{22} = \sqrt{8 - 4} = 2$. So $L = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 2 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.

Exercise 8: Sum of PD Matrices

If $A$ and $B$ are both PD, is $A + B$ also PD?

Solution

$\mathbf{x}^T(A+B)\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{x}^T B \mathbf{x} > 0 + 0 = 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$. Yes, the sum of PD matrices is PD.

🟡 Medium

Exercise 9: 3×3 Sylvester

Determine whether $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 \\ 2 & 5 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$ is positive definite.

Solution

$D_1 = 1 > 0$. $D_2 = 5 - 4 = 1 > 0$. $D_3 = 1(15-1) - 2(6-0) + 0 = 14 - 12 = 2 > 0$. All positive, so $A$ is PD.

Exercise 10: $A^TA$ is PSD

Show that for any matrix $A$ (not necessarily square), $A^TA$ is positive semi-definite. When is it PD?

Solution

$\mathbf{x}^T(A^TA)\mathbf{x} = (A\mathbf{x})^T(A\mathbf{x}) = \|A\mathbf{x}\|^2 \geq 0$. It is PD when $A$ has full column rank (i.e., $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ implies $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$).

Exercise 11: Eigenvalue Shift

If $A$ is symmetric with smallest eigenvalue $\lambda_{\min}$, for what values of $\epsilon$ is $A + \epsilon I$ positive definite?

Solution

$A + \epsilon I$ has eigenvalues $\lambda_i + \epsilon$. For PD, we need all $\lambda_i + \epsilon > 0$, so $\epsilon > -\lambda_{\min}$. In particular, any $\epsilon > 0$ works if $A$ is PSD, and $\epsilon > |\lambda_{\min}|$ suffices for any symmetric $A$.

Exercise 12: Inverse of PD

Prove that if $A$ is positive definite, then $A^{-1}$ is also positive definite.

Solution

Since $A$ is PD and symmetric, it has eigendecomposition $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ with $\lambda_i > 0$. Then $A^{-1} = Q\Lambda^{-1}Q^T$ with eigenvalues $1/\lambda_i > 0$. Since all eigenvalues of $A^{-1}$ are positive and it is symmetric, $A^{-1}$ is PD.

Exercise 13: Congruence Transform

If $A$ is PD and $P$ is an invertible matrix, is $P^TAP$ also PD?

Solution

$\mathbf{x}^T(P^TAP)\mathbf{x} = (P\mathbf{x})^T A (P\mathbf{x})$. Let $\mathbf{y} = P\mathbf{x}$. Since $P$ is invertible, $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$ implies $\mathbf{y} \neq \mathbf{0}$, so $\mathbf{y}^T A \mathbf{y} > 0$. Yes, $P^TAP$ is PD. (Note: $P^TAP$ is symmetric since $(P^TAP)^T = P^TA^TP = P^TAP$.)

Exercise 14: Cholesky of 3×3

Compute the Cholesky factorization of $A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 & 0 \\ 2 & 5 & 2 \\ 0 & 2 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

$l_{11} = 2$. $l_{21} = 2/2 = 1$, $l_{31} = 0/2 = 0$. $l_{22} = \sqrt{5-1} = 2$. $l_{32} = (2-0)/2 = 1$. $l_{33} = \sqrt{5-0-1} = 2$. So $L = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.

Exercise 15: Mahalanobis Distance

Compute the Mahalanobis distance from $\mathbf{x} = (3, 1)^T$ to the origin using $\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$. Compare with Euclidean distance.

Solution

$\Sigma^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1/4 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$. $d_M = \sqrt{(3,1) \begin{pmatrix} 1/4 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}} = \sqrt{9/4 + 1} = \sqrt{13/4} \approx 1.80$. Euclidean: $\sqrt{9+1} = \sqrt{10} \approx 3.16$. Mahalanobis is smaller because the variance along the first axis is larger, so 3 units in that direction is less "surprising".

Exercise 16: Gaussian Entropy

Compute the differential entropy of a bivariate Gaussian with $\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$. How does it change if we double $\Sigma$?

Solution

$H = \frac{2}{2}\ln(2\pi e) + \frac{1}{2}\ln(\det \Sigma) = \ln(2\pi e) + \frac{1}{2}\ln 6 \approx 2.838 + 0.896 = 3.73$ nats. If $\Sigma \to 2\Sigma$, then $\det(2\Sigma) = 4 \det(\Sigma)$, so $H$ increases by $\frac{1}{2}\ln 4 = \ln 2 \approx 0.693$ nats. Doubling the covariance adds $\ln 2$ nats of entropy.

Exercise 17: Schur Complement

Show that the block matrix $M = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ B^T & C \end{pmatrix}$ is PD if and only if $A$ is PD and the Schur complement $C - B^T A^{-1} B$ is PD.

Solution

Write $\begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{x} \\ \mathbf{y} \end{pmatrix}^T M \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{x} \\ \mathbf{y} \end{pmatrix} = \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} + 2\mathbf{x}^T B \mathbf{y} + \mathbf{y}^T C \mathbf{y}$. Complete the square: $= (\mathbf{x} + A^{-1}B\mathbf{y})^T A (\mathbf{x} + A^{-1}B\mathbf{y}) + \mathbf{y}^T(C - B^T A^{-1}B)\mathbf{y}$. The first term is $\geq 0$ (positive when the expression in parentheses is nonzero), and by choosing $\mathbf{x} = -A^{-1}B\mathbf{y}$, we see the second term must be positive for all $\mathbf{y} \neq 0$, requiring $C - B^TA^{-1}B$ to be PD. Conversely, if both $A$ and the Schur complement are PD, both terms are non-negative and cannot both be zero for $(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) \neq \mathbf{0}$.

Exercise 18: PD Powers

Prove that if $A$ is PD, then $A^k$ is PD for every positive integer $k$.

Solution

Since $A$ is symmetric PD, it has eigendecomposition $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ with $\lambda_i > 0$. Then $A^k = Q\Lambda^k Q^T$ with eigenvalues $\lambda_i^k > 0$ (positive real raised to positive integer power). $A^k$ is symmetric ($(A^k)^T = (Q\Lambda^k Q^T)^T = Q\Lambda^k Q^T$) with all positive eigenvalues, hence PD.

Exercise 19: Hadamard Product

The Hadamard (entrywise) product of two PSD matrices is PSD (Schur product theorem). Verify this for $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$.

Solution

$A \circ B = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & -1 \\ -1 & 6 \end{pmatrix}$. Check: $D_1 = 6 > 0$, $D_2 = 36 - 1 = 35 > 0$. So $A \circ B$ is PD (and therefore PSD). Both $A$ and $B$ are PD (check: $\det(A) = 3, \det(B) = 8$), confirming the Schur product theorem.

Exercise 20: Matrix Square Root

If $A$ is PD, show there exists a unique PD matrix $B$ such that $B^2 = A$. How does this differ from the Cholesky factor?

Solution

Write $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$. Define $B = Q\Lambda^{1/2}Q^T$ where $\Lambda^{1/2} = \text{diag}(\sqrt{\lambda_1}, \ldots, \sqrt{\lambda_n})$. Then $B^2 = Q\Lambda^{1/2}Q^T Q\Lambda^{1/2}Q^T = Q\Lambda Q^T = A$. $B$ is symmetric and PD (eigenvalues $\sqrt{\lambda_i} > 0$). Uniqueness follows from the uniqueness of positive square roots of positive reals applied to each eigenvalue. Unlike the Cholesky factor $L$ (lower triangular), $B$ is symmetric — they are "square roots" in different senses: $A = LL^T$ vs $A = B \cdot B$.

Exercise 21: Condition Number and Convergence

For gradient descent on $f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b}^T\mathbf{x}$ with PD matrix $A$, the convergence rate is $\left(\frac{\kappa - 1}{\kappa + 1}\right)^{2k}$ where $\kappa = \lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$. Compare iterations needed for $\kappa = 10$ vs $\kappa = 1000$ to reduce error by a factor of $10^{-6}$.

Solution

We need $\left(\frac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}\right)^{2k} \leq 10^{-6}$, so $k \geq \frac{-6\ln 10}{2\ln\left(\frac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}\right)}$. For $\kappa = 10$: ratio $= 9/11 \approx 0.818$, $k \geq \frac{13.82}{2 \times 0.2007} \approx 34$ iterations. For $\kappa = 1000$: ratio $= 999/1001 \approx 0.998$, $k \geq \frac{13.82}{2 \times 0.002} \approx 3455$ iterations. The poorly-conditioned problem requires ~100× more iterations.

Exercise 22: KL Divergence and PD

The KL divergence between two Gaussians $\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_0, \Sigma_0)$ and $\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_1, \Sigma_1)$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is: $$D_{KL} = \frac{1}{2}\left[\ln\frac{\det\Sigma_1}{\det\Sigma_0} - n + \text{tr}(\Sigma_1^{-1}\Sigma_0) + (\boldsymbol{\mu}_1 - \boldsymbol{\mu}_0)^T\Sigma_1^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_1 - \boldsymbol{\mu}_0)\right]$$ Why must both $\Sigma_0$ and $\Sigma_1$ be PD (not just PSD) for this formula to work?

Solution

$\Sigma_1$ must be PD because we need $\Sigma_1^{-1}$ (appears twice in the formula). $\Sigma_0$ must be PD because we need $\det\Sigma_0 > 0$ for the $\ln$ to be defined (if $\det\Sigma_0 = 0$, the log diverges to $-\infty$). Moreover, both must be PD for the Gaussians to have proper density functions on $\mathbb{R}^n$; PSD covariances yield degenerate distributions that live on lower-dimensional subspaces and have no density with respect to Lebesgue measure.

Exercise 23: Fisher Information of Gaussian

For a univariate Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with known variance, compute the Fisher information $\mathcal{I}(\mu)$ and verify it is positive (1×1 PD).

Solution

$\ln p(x|\mu) = -\frac{1}{2}\ln(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}$. $\frac{\partial^2}{\partial\mu^2}\ln p = -\frac{1}{\sigma^2}$. So $\mathcal{I}(\mu) = -E\left[-\frac{1}{\sigma^2}\right] = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} > 0$. The Fisher information is $1/\sigma^2$, which is positive (PD in the 1×1 sense). Higher precision (smaller $\sigma^2$) means more information about $\mu$ per observation, connecting PD to the strength of statistical evidence.

Exercise 24: Simultaneous Diagonalization

If $A$ is PD and $B$ is symmetric, show there exists an invertible $P$ such that $P^TAP = I$ and $P^TBP = D$ (diagonal). Why is the PD condition on $A$ essential?

Solution

Since $A$ is PD, compute $A = LL^T$ (Cholesky). Define $C = L^{-1}BL^{-T}$, which is symmetric. Diagonalize $C = Q\tilde{D}Q^T$. Set $P = L^{-T}Q$. Then $P^TAP = Q^TL^{-1}LL^TL^{-T}Q = Q^TQ = I$, and $P^TBP = Q^TL^{-1}BL^{-T}Q = Q^TCQ = \tilde{D}$. The PD condition on $A$ is essential because: (1) we need $L$ to exist (Cholesky requires PD), (2) we need $L^{-1}$ (Cholesky factor is invertible since $A$ is), and (3) the construction $L^{-1}BL^{-T}$ must yield a well-defined symmetric matrix. This "generalized eigenvalue problem" $B\mathbf{v} = \lambda A\mathbf{v}$ arises in PCA, linear discriminant analysis, and canonical correlation analysis.
