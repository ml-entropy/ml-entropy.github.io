The Definition

Imagine you apply a linear transformation to every vector in a space. Most vectors twist, rotate, and stretch in complicated ways. But some special vectors refuse to change direction — they simply get longer or shorter, staying firmly on their original line. These stubborn vectors are called eigenvectors, from the German word eigen, meaning "own" or "characteristic." They are the directions that a matrix "owns" — the intrinsic axes along which the transformation acts by pure scaling.

Eigenvalue Equation

A nonzero vector $\mathbf{v}$ is an eigenvector of an $n \times n$ matrix $A$ if there exists a scalar $\lambda$ such that

$$A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$$

The scalar $\lambda$ is the eigenvalue corresponding to $\mathbf{v}$. The set of all eigenvectors for a given eigenvalue, together with the zero vector, forms the eigenspace $E_\lambda = \ker(A - \lambda I)$.

Let's make this concrete. If $A$ is a $2 \times 2$ matrix and $\mathbf{v}$ is an eigenvector with eigenvalue $\lambda = 3$, then applying $A$ to $\mathbf{v}$ stretches it by a factor of 3 without changing its direction. If $\lambda = -1$, the vector flips to point the opposite way (a reflection along that line). If $\lambda = 0$, the vector gets crushed to the origin — it lies in the null space of $A$. The eigenvalue tells you the fate of vectors along that particular direction.

Why must we insist that $\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}$? Because $A\mathbf{0} = \lambda\mathbf{0}$ is trivially true for any $\lambda$ — the zero vector carries no directional information and would make the definition meaningless. Every scalar would be an "eigenvalue" if we allowed it. The restriction to nonzero vectors ensures that eigenvalues genuinely capture the geometry of the transformation.

Notice that eigenvectors are only defined up to scalar multiples: if $\mathbf{v}$ is an eigenvector, then so is $c\mathbf{v}$ for any nonzero scalar $c$, with the same eigenvalue. This is why we speak of eigenspaces (entire lines or planes of eigenvectors) rather than individual eigenvectors. When we say "the eigenvector for $\lambda$," we usually mean a representative vector, often normalized to unit length.

Geometric Intuition

Think of a matrix as a physical deformation — stretching rubber, for instance. If you pull a rubber sheet in some complex pattern, most embedded arrows will rotate. But certain special arrows remain pointing in their original direction, just getting longer or shorter. Those arrows are eigenvectors; the stretch factors are eigenvalues. The eigenvectors reveal the natural axes of the deformation.

Why Eigenvalues Matter

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors are not just an abstract curiosity — they are arguably the single most important concept in applied linear algebra. Virtually every domain that uses matrices — from machine learning and physics to economics and network science — relies on eigenvalue analysis to extract meaning from data and systems. Let's survey the most important applications.

Principal Component Analysis (PCA). In machine learning, we often face datasets with hundreds or thousands of features. PCA reduces dimensionality by finding the directions of maximum variance in the data. These directions are precisely the eigenvectors of the data's covariance matrix, and the corresponding eigenvalues measure how much variance each direction captures. If a dataset in $\mathbb{R}^{1000}$ has only 10 large eigenvalues, then the data effectively lives in a 10-dimensional subspace — the remaining 990 dimensions are noise. This is the foundation of dimensionality reduction, data compression, and feature extraction across all of modern data science.

Google's PageRank. The algorithm that launched Google models the web as a giant matrix of link probabilities. The importance score of every web page is determined by the dominant eigenvector of this matrix — the eigenvector with the largest eigenvalue ($\lambda = 1$). Billions of dollars of economic value rest on the computation of a single eigenvector. We'll see the algorithm for computing it (power iteration) later in this tutorial.

Spectral Graph Theory. The eigenvalues of a graph's adjacency matrix or Laplacian matrix encode deep structural properties. The second-smallest eigenvalue of the Laplacian (the "Fiedler value") measures how well-connected the graph is. Spectral clustering algorithms partition data into groups by examining the eigenvectors of a similarity matrix — this is how Netflix recommends movies and how biologists cluster genes by expression patterns.

Vibration Analysis and Quantum Mechanics. In physics, the eigenvalues of a system's Hamiltonian operator are the allowed energy levels. The discrete spectral lines of hydrogen — the colors it emits — are eigenvalues of Schrödinger's equation. In structural engineering, the eigenvalues of a stiffness matrix determine the natural vibration frequencies of a bridge or building. An eigenvalue near zero signals a mode that can resonate catastrophically.

Dynamical Systems and Stability. Consider a system of differential equations $\mathbf{x}'(t) = A\mathbf{x}(t)$. The eigenvalues of $A$ determine the system's long-term behavior. If all eigenvalues have negative real parts, the system is stable and returns to equilibrium. If any eigenvalue has a positive real part, the system is unstable and blows up. This is how control engineers design autopilots and how epidemiologists model disease spread.

The Unifying Theme

In every application above, eigenvalues decompose a complex system into independent, one-dimensional behaviors. Instead of analyzing an $n$-dimensional coupled system, we analyze $n$ independent scalar problems. This decoupling is the fundamental reason eigenvalues matter.

Finding Eigenvalues

Now that we know why eigenvalues matter, let's see how to find them. The key insight comes from rewriting the eigenvalue equation $A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$ in a form that connects to our earlier study of determinants and systems of equations.

Start by moving everything to one side. We write $\lambda\mathbf{v}$ as $\lambda I\mathbf{v}$ (multiplying by the identity matrix changes nothing), then subtract:

$$A\mathbf{v} - \lambda I\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad (A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$$

This is a homogeneous system of linear equations. We want a nonzero solution $\mathbf{v}$. From our study of linear systems, we know that a homogeneous system has a nontrivial solution if and only if the coefficient matrix is singular — that is, its determinant is zero. Therefore:

Characteristic Equation

The eigenvalues of $A$ are the solutions to $$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$$ This polynomial equation in $\lambda$ is called the characteristic equation of $A$.

Why does this work? The matrix $(A - \lambda I)$ is obtained by subtracting $\lambda$ from every diagonal entry of $A$. When $\lambda$ equals an eigenvalue, this modified matrix becomes singular — its columns become linearly dependent — and vectors in its null space are the eigenvectors. The determinant condition $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$ is our algebraic detector for exactly these critical values of $\lambda$.

For an $n \times n$ matrix, the characteristic equation is a polynomial of degree $n$. By the fundamental theorem of algebra, it has exactly $n$ roots (counted with multiplicity, and allowing complex numbers). This means every $n \times n$ matrix has exactly $n$ eigenvalues over $\mathbb{C}$, though some may be repeated and some may not be real numbers.

Trace and Determinant Shortcuts

For a $2 \times 2$ matrix $A$, the eigenvalues satisfy $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = \text{tr}(A)$ and $\lambda_1 \cdot \lambda_2 = \det(A)$. More generally, the sum of all eigenvalues always equals the trace, and their product always equals the determinant. These are powerful sanity checks on your calculations.

The Characteristic Polynomial

The expression $p(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I)$ is a polynomial in $\lambda$, called the characteristic polynomial of $A$. For a $2 \times 2$ matrix, it is a quadratic; for $3 \times 3$, a cubic; and in general, a degree-$n$ polynomial for an $n \times n$ matrix. Its roots are the eigenvalues. Let's work through examples of increasing complexity to build fluency.

Worked Example: 2×2 Matrix
1
Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$. We want to find all eigenvalues of $A$.
2
Form $A - \lambda I = \begin{pmatrix} 4 - \lambda & 1 \\ 2 & 3 - \lambda \end{pmatrix}$. We subtract $\lambda$ from each diagonal entry, leaving off-diagonal entries unchanged.
3
Compute the determinant: $\det(A - \lambda I) = (4 - \lambda)(3 - \lambda) - (1)(2) = 12 - 4\lambda - 3\lambda + \lambda^2 - 2 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10$.
4
Set the characteristic polynomial to zero: $\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = 0$. Factor: $(\lambda - 5)(\lambda - 2) = 0$. So $\lambda_1 = 5$ and $\lambda_2 = 2$.
✓
Sanity check: $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = 5 + 2 = 7 = \text{tr}(A)$ ✓ and $\lambda_1 \cdot \lambda_2 = 5 \cdot 2 = 10 = \det(A)$ ✓.

The 2×2 case always produces a quadratic, which we can solve with the quadratic formula. Let's generalize: for $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$, the characteristic polynomial is $\lambda^2 - (a+d)\lambda + (ad - bc) = 0$, which is $\lambda^2 - \text{tr}(A)\lambda + \det(A) = 0$. This compact formula is worth memorizing — it saves significant computation time.

Worked Example: 3×3 Matrix
1
Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$. This is upper triangular, so the eigenvalues are simply the diagonal entries: $\lambda_1 = 2, \lambda_2 = 3, \lambda_3 = 1$.
2
Let's verify. $A - \lambda I = \begin{pmatrix} 2-\lambda & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 3-\lambda & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1-\lambda \end{pmatrix}$. Since this is upper triangular, $\det(A - \lambda I) = (2-\lambda)(3-\lambda)(1-\lambda)$.
3
Setting $(2-\lambda)(3-\lambda)(1-\lambda) = 0$ gives $\lambda \in \{1, 2, 3\}$. Check: $\text{tr}(A) = 6 = 1+2+3$ ✓ and $\det(A) = 6 = 1 \cdot 2 \cdot 3$ ✓.

Now let's tackle a non-triangular $3 \times 3$ case where the algebra is more involved.

Worked Example: Non-Triangular 3×3 Matrix
1
Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 0 \\ 2 & 0 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$. Form $A - \lambda I = \begin{pmatrix} 1-\lambda & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3-\lambda & 0 \\ 2 & 0 & 4-\lambda \end{pmatrix}$.
2
Expand along row 2 (which has two zeros, simplifying the cofactor expansion): $\det(A - \lambda I) = (3 - \lambda) \det\begin{pmatrix} 1-\lambda & 0 \\ 2 & 4-\lambda \end{pmatrix} = (3-\lambda)[(1-\lambda)(4-\lambda) - 0]$.
3
So $p(\lambda) = (3-\lambda)(1-\lambda)(4-\lambda)$, giving eigenvalues $\lambda \in \{1, 3, 4\}$.

A key practical observation: for matrices larger than $3 \times 3$, finding eigenvalues by hand via the characteristic polynomial is impractical. The Abel–Ruffini theorem tells us there is no general closed-form solution for polynomials of degree 5 or higher. In practice, numerical algorithms like the QR algorithm compute eigenvalues iteratively — we'll see a simpler variant, power iteration, later in this tutorial.

Multiplicity

If a factor $(\lambda - \lambda_0)^k$ appears in the characteristic polynomial, then $\lambda_0$ has algebraic multiplicity $k$. The geometric multiplicity is the dimension of the eigenspace $E_{\lambda_0}$. We always have $1 \leq \text{geometric mult.} \leq \text{algebraic mult.}$ The gap between these multiplicities has deep consequences for diagonalization.

Finding Eigenvectors

Once we know the eigenvalues, finding eigenvectors is a matter of solving a homogeneous system of linear equations. For each eigenvalue $\lambda$, we substitute back into $(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$ and find the null space. The null space of $(A - \lambda I)$ is the eigenspace $E_\lambda$ — the set of all eigenvectors for that eigenvalue, together with the zero vector.

Let's continue with our $2 \times 2$ example $A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$, which has eigenvalues $\lambda_1 = 5$ and $\lambda_2 = 2$.

Finding the Eigenvector for $\lambda_1 = 5$
1
Compute $A - 5I = \begin{pmatrix} 4-5 & 1 \\ 2 & 3-5 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$.
2
Solve $\begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$. Row 1 gives $-v_1 + v_2 = 0$, so $v_1 = v_2$. Row 2 gives $2v_1 - 2v_2 = 0$, the same constraint.
3
Setting $v_1 = 1$: $\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$. The eigenspace is $E_5 = \text{span}\left\{\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\right\}$ — the line $y = x$.
Finding the Eigenvector for $\lambda_2 = 2$
1
Compute $A - 2I = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$.
2
Both rows give $2v_1 + v_2 = 0$, so $v_2 = -2v_1$. Setting $v_1 = 1$: $\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}$.
3
Verification: $A\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix} = 2\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} = \lambda_2\mathbf{v}_2$ ✓

Always verify your eigenvectors by plugging them back into $A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$. This catches arithmetic errors that are easy to make during row reduction. Notice that when we set up $(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$, the rows must be linearly dependent (since the determinant is zero) — so you should always see at least one redundant equation. If every row gives an independent constraint, you've made an error somewhere.

What happens when an eigenvalue has algebraic multiplicity greater than 1? Then the eigenspace might be one-dimensional (a single eigenvector direction) or multi-dimensional. For the identity matrix $I$, the eigenvalue $\lambda = 1$ has multiplicity $n$, and the eigenspace is all of $\mathbb{R}^n$ — every nonzero vector is an eigenvector. On the other hand, the matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ has $\lambda = 1$ with multiplicity 2, but the eigenspace is only one-dimensional: $\text{span}\{(1, 0)^T\}$. This distinction is critical for diagonalization.

Don't Forget the Null Space

If $\lambda = 0$ is an eigenvalue, the eigenspace $E_0 = \ker(A)$ is exactly the null space of $A$. Conversely, $A$ is singular if and only if $\lambda = 0$ is one of its eigenvalues. This connects eigenvalue theory directly to invertibility.

Diagonalization

Diagonalization is the crown jewel of eigenvalue theory. The idea is breathtakingly simple: if we choose the right coordinate system — one aligned with the eigenvectors — then the matrix becomes diagonal. All the complexity of the original transformation is stripped away, leaving only the eigenvalues sitting on the diagonal. Every coupled interaction disappears, and we're left with $n$ independent scalings.

Diagonalization

An $n \times n$ matrix $A$ is diagonalizable if there exists an invertible matrix $P$ and a diagonal matrix $D$ such that

$$A = PDP^{-1}$$

where the columns of $P$ are eigenvectors of $A$ and the diagonal entries of $D$ are the corresponding eigenvalues: $D = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_n)$.

Let's see why this formula works. If we collect the eigenvector equations $A\mathbf{v}_i = \lambda_i \mathbf{v}_i$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n$ into a single matrix equation, we get $AP = PD$ where $P = [\mathbf{v}_1 | \mathbf{v}_2 | \cdots | \mathbf{v}_n]$. If the $n$ eigenvectors are linearly independent, then $P$ is invertible, and we can multiply both sides by $P^{-1}$ on the right to get $A = PDP^{-1}$.

Worked Example: Diagonalizing a 2×2 Matrix
1
For $A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$ with $\lambda_1 = 5, \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$ and $\lambda_2 = 2, \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\-2\end{pmatrix}$:
2
$P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$, $D = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$, $P^{-1} = \frac{1}{-3}\begin{pmatrix} -2 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2/3 & 1/3 \\ 1/3 & -1/3 \end{pmatrix}$.
3
Verify: $PDP^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}5&0\\0&2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}2/3&1/3\\1/3&-1/3\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}4&1\\2&3\end{pmatrix} = A$ ✓

The practical payoff of diagonalization is enormous. Computing powers of $A$ becomes trivial: $A^k = PD^kP^{-1}$, and $D^k$ just raises each diagonal entry to the $k$-th power. Need $A^{100}$? Compute $5^{100}$ and $2^{100}$ and you're done — no matrix multiplication needed. This is essential for analyzing Markov chains ($A^k$ gives the $k$-step transition probabilities) and solving systems of linear recurrences like the Fibonacci sequence.

Matrix exponentials also simplify beautifully: $e^{At} = Pe^{Dt}P^{-1}$, where $e^{Dt} = \text{diag}(e^{\lambda_1 t}, \ldots, e^{\lambda_n t})$. This is the key to solving systems of linear differential equations $\mathbf{x}'(t) = A\mathbf{x}(t)$ and is fundamental to control theory and physics.

When Does Diagonalization Fail?

A matrix is diagonalizable if and only if it has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors. This can fail when the geometric multiplicity of some eigenvalue is strictly less than its algebraic multiplicity. The classic example is $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, which has $\lambda = 0$ with algebraic multiplicity 2 but geometric multiplicity 1. Such matrices require the Jordan normal form instead.

Spectral Theorem for Symmetric Matrices

If $A$ is real and symmetric ($A = A^T$), then $A$ is always diagonalizable, and we can choose an orthogonal matrix $Q$ (meaning $Q^{-1} = Q^T$) so that $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$. This is the spectral decomposition — the most powerful form of diagonalization. It means symmetric matrices have real eigenvalues and perpendicular eigenvectors. Covariance matrices, graph Laplacians, and Hessians are all symmetric, which is why PCA, spectral clustering, and Newton's method all work so cleanly.

Symmetric Matrices

Symmetric matrices are the best-behaved matrices in all of linear algebra. Every covariance matrix, every graph Laplacian, every Hessian of a smooth function — these are all symmetric, and they enjoy spectral properties that general matrices can only dream of. The spectral theorem, stated informally above, deserves a closer look because it underpins so much of modern machine learning.

Spectral Theorem (Real Symmetric Matrices)

If $A$ is a real symmetric matrix ($A = A^T$), then:
(1) All eigenvalues of $A$ are real.
(2) Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.
(3) $A$ has a full set of $n$ orthonormal eigenvectors, and $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ where $Q$ is orthogonal and $\Lambda$ is diagonal.

Let's understand why eigenvalues of symmetric matrices must be real. Suppose $A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$ where $A$ is real symmetric. Take the conjugate transpose of both sides: $\bar{\mathbf{v}}^T A = \bar{\lambda}\bar{\mathbf{v}}^T$ (using $A^T = A$ and that $A$ is real). Now multiply the original equation on the left by $\bar{\mathbf{v}}^T$: $\bar{\mathbf{v}}^T A \mathbf{v} = \lambda \bar{\mathbf{v}}^T\mathbf{v}$. From the conjugate equation: $\bar{\mathbf{v}}^T A\mathbf{v} = \bar{\lambda}\bar{\mathbf{v}}^T\mathbf{v}$. Since $\bar{\mathbf{v}}^T\mathbf{v} = \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 > 0$, we conclude $\lambda = \bar{\lambda}$, so $\lambda$ is real.

The orthogonality of eigenvectors follows from a similar argument. If $A\mathbf{u} = \alpha\mathbf{u}$ and $A\mathbf{v} = \beta\mathbf{v}$ with $\alpha \neq \beta$, then $\alpha\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{v} = (A\mathbf{u})^T\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u}^T A^T\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u}^T A\mathbf{v} = \beta\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{v}$. So $(\alpha - \beta)\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{v} = 0$, and since $\alpha \neq \beta$, we must have $\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{v} = 0$ — the eigenvectors are orthogonal.

In machine learning, the most important symmetric matrix is the covariance matrix $\Sigma = \frac{1}{n-1}X^TX$, where $X$ is the centered data matrix. Its eigenvectors are the principal components — the directions along which the data varies most — and its eigenvalues are the variances along those directions. PCA is simply the spectral decomposition of the covariance matrix, and its power comes entirely from the spectral theorem.

Example: Spectral Decomposition of a Covariance Matrix
1
Let $\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 2 \\ 2 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$. This is symmetric and positive definite (both eigenvalues will be positive).
2
Characteristic polynomial: $\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 6 = 0$, giving $\lambda_1 = 6, \lambda_2 = 1$.
3
Eigenvectors: for $\lambda_1 = 6$: $\mathbf{q}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}$; for $\lambda_2 = 1$: $\mathbf{q}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{pmatrix}-1\\2\end{pmatrix}$. Verify $\mathbf{q}_1 \cdot \mathbf{q}_2 = \frac{1}{5}(-2 + 2) = 0$ ✓
4
Interpretation: The data has 6 units of variance along the direction $(2, 1)/\sqrt{5}$ and only 1 unit along the perpendicular direction $(-1, 2)/\sqrt{5}$. PCA would keep the first component and discard the second, reducing dimensionality from 2 to 1 while retaining $6/7 \approx 86\%$ of the variance.

Beyond PCA, symmetric eigenvalue decompositions appear in spectral clustering (eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian), kernel methods (eigenfunctions of kernel operators), and second-order optimization (eigenvalues of the Hessian determine the curvature landscape). The spectral theorem is not just a mathematical nicety — it is the structural reason why so many ML algorithms work.

Power Iteration

For large matrices — a web graph with billions of pages, a covariance matrix of millions of genes — computing eigenvalues via the characteristic polynomial is hopeless. We need iterative algorithms. The simplest and most elegant is power iteration, which finds the largest eigenvalue and its eigenvector by repeated matrix-vector multiplication.

Power Iteration Algorithm

Start with a random vector $\mathbf{b}_0$. Repeat:
1. Multiply: $\mathbf{w}_{k+1} = A\mathbf{b}_k$
2. Normalize: $\mathbf{b}_{k+1} = \mathbf{w}_{k+1} / \|\mathbf{w}_{k+1}\|$
3. Estimate eigenvalue: $\lambda_{k+1} = \mathbf{b}_{k+1}^T A\mathbf{b}_{k+1}$
Then $\mathbf{b}_k \to \mathbf{v}_1$ (dominant eigenvector) and $\lambda_k \to \lambda_1$ (dominant eigenvalue).

Why does this work? Write the initial vector in the eigenbasis: $\mathbf{b}_0 = c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_n\mathbf{v}_n$. After $k$ multiplications by $A$: $A^k\mathbf{b}_0 = c_1\lambda_1^k\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\lambda_2^k\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_n\lambda_n^k\mathbf{v}_n$. If $|\lambda_1| > |\lambda_2| \geq \cdots$, then $\lambda_1^k$ dominates, and the other terms become negligible: $A^k\mathbf{b}_0 \approx c_1\lambda_1^k\mathbf{v}_1$. After normalization, $\mathbf{b}_k \to \pm\mathbf{v}_1$. The convergence rate is $|\lambda_2/\lambda_1|^k$ — the larger the gap between the top two eigenvalues, the faster it converges.

Connection to PageRank. Google's PageRank is computed by power iteration on the web's link matrix. The web has billions of pages, making the matrix enormous — but it's also extremely sparse (each page links to only a few others), so each matrix-vector multiplication is fast. The dominant eigenvalue is $\lambda_1 = 1$ (guaranteed by the stochastic structure of the matrix), and the corresponding eigenvector gives the importance scores for every page. A typical computation converges in 50–100 iterations — remarkably few for a billion-dimensional problem.

Power iteration has important limitations. It only finds the dominant eigenvalue — the one with the largest absolute value. If $|\lambda_1| = |\lambda_2|$, it may not converge. And if $c_1 = 0$ (the initial vector is orthogonal to $\mathbf{v}_1$), it converges to the second eigenvector instead. In practice, rounding errors prevent exact orthogonality, so this is rarely a problem — but it motivates more sophisticated methods.

Beyond Power Iteration

To find multiple eigenvalues, use deflation: after finding $\lambda_1$, subtract its contribution ($A \to A - \lambda_1\mathbf{v}_1\mathbf{v}_1^T$) and run power iteration again. More sophisticated methods include the QR algorithm (which finds all eigenvalues simultaneously via a sequence of QR factorizations) and the Lanczos algorithm (optimized for sparse symmetric matrices, and the workhorse behind large-scale PCA).

Information-Theoretic Connections

This project views mathematics through the lens of information theory. Eigenvalues have deep connections to entropy, compression, and information that illuminate both subjects. Let's explore the most important of these connections.

Eigenvalues of the covariance matrix are principal variances. If $\Sigma$ has eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_n \geq 0$, then $\lambda_i$ is the variance of the data projected onto the $i$-th principal component. The total variance is $\text{tr}(\Sigma) = \sum_i \lambda_i$. The fraction of variance explained by the first $k$ components is $\sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i / \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i$ — this is the information retention when we compress the data from $n$ dimensions to $k$.

Effective rank and spectral entropy. How many "true" dimensions does a dataset have? If the covariance matrix has eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$, we can normalize them to form a probability distribution $p_i = \lambda_i / \sum_j \lambda_j$. The Shannon entropy of this distribution,

$$H_{\text{spectral}} = -\sum_{i=1}^n p_i \ln p_i$$

measures the "spreadness" of the eigenvalue spectrum. The effective rank is $e^{H_{\text{spectral}}}$. If all eigenvalues are equal (isotropic data), $H_{\text{spectral}} = \ln n$ and the effective rank is $n$. If one eigenvalue dominates (data lies on a line), $H_{\text{spectral}} \approx 0$ and the effective rank is approximately 1. This is a continuous, information-theoretic measure of dimensionality, more nuanced than just counting eigenvalues above some threshold.

Gaussian entropy and log-determinant. The differential entropy of a multivariate Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \Sigma)$ is

$$H = \frac{n}{2}\ln(2\pi e) + \frac{1}{2}\ln|\Sigma| = \frac{n}{2}\ln(2\pi e) + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^n \ln\lambda_i$$

Each eigenvalue $\lambda_i$ contributes $\frac{1}{2}\ln\lambda_i$ to the entropy. Large eigenvalues (high variance) contribute high entropy — there's lots of uncertainty along those directions. Small eigenvalues contribute low (possibly negative) entropy — the data is tightly concentrated there. The total entropy decomposes into a sum of per-axis contributions, and this decomposition is exactly the eigenvalue decomposition.

Spectral analysis of Markov chains. A Markov chain with transition matrix $P$ has eigenvalues that control its convergence to the stationary distribution. The dominant eigenvalue is $\lambda_1 = 1$ (with the stationary distribution as the corresponding left eigenvector). The spectral gap $1 - |\lambda_2|$ determines the mixing time: the number of steps required for the chain to "forget" its initial state. A large spectral gap means rapid mixing and fast convergence; a small gap means the chain mixes slowly. In information-theoretic terms, the spectral gap governs the rate of entropy production as the chain approaches stationarity.

The Deep Connection

Eigenvalues bridge linear algebra and information theory through the covariance matrix. PCA minimizes reconstruction error, which is the same as maximizing the entropy of the retained information. The "best" low-dimensional approximation to a dataset — in both the least-squares sense and the information-theoretic sense — is given by the top eigenvectors. This equivalence is no coincidence: it reflects the deep relationship between variance and entropy for Gaussian distributions.

Common Misconceptions

Eigenvalue theory is rich with subtleties that trip up students and practitioners alike. Let's address the most common misconceptions head-on, with explanations and counterexamples.

Misconception 1: "Every matrix can be diagonalized"

False. A matrix is diagonalizable if and only if it has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors. The matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ has $\lambda = 0$ with algebraic multiplicity 2 but only one linearly independent eigenvector $(1, 0)^T$. It cannot be diagonalized. Such matrices require the Jordan normal form instead, which replaces diagonal entries with Jordan blocks containing 1's on the super-diagonal.

Misconception 2: "Eigenvalues are always real numbers"

False. Real matrices can have complex eigenvalues. The rotation matrix $R = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ (90° rotation) has characteristic equation $\lambda^2 + 1 = 0$, giving $\lambda = \pm i$. Geometrically, a pure rotation has no invariant direction — no real eigenvectors exist. Complex eigenvalues always come in conjugate pairs for real matrices: if $\lambda = a + bi$ is an eigenvalue, so is $\bar{\lambda} = a - bi$. Symmetric matrices are the special case where eigenvalues are guaranteed to be real.

Misconception 3: "Algebraic multiplicity equals geometric multiplicity"

Not always. The algebraic multiplicity of an eigenvalue is its multiplicity as a root of the characteristic polynomial. The geometric multiplicity is the dimension of its eigenspace. We always have $1 \leq \text{geo. mult.} \leq \text{alg. mult.}$, but equality can fail. The matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$ has $\lambda = 2$ with algebraic multiplicity 2 but geometric multiplicity 1. When these differ, the matrix is defective (not diagonalizable).

Misconception 4: "Eigenvectors of different eigenvalues can be parallel"

False. Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are always linearly independent (not just for symmetric matrices — for all matrices). This is a theorem. If $A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$ and $A\mathbf{w} = \mu\mathbf{w}$ with $\lambda \neq \mu$, then $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{w}$ are linearly independent. Consequently, a matrix with $n$ distinct eigenvalues is always diagonalizable.

Misconception 5: "Similar matrices have the same eigenvectors"

False. Similar matrices $A$ and $B = P^{-1}AP$ have the same eigenvalues (they share the same characteristic polynomial), but their eigenvectors are generally different. If $\mathbf{v}$ is an eigenvector of $A$, then $P^{-1}\mathbf{v}$ is an eigenvector of $B$, not $\mathbf{v}$ itself. Similarity preserves the spectrum but changes the eigenbasis.

Misconception 6: "$\det(A) = 0$ means all eigenvalues are zero"

False. The determinant is the product of all eigenvalues. If $\det(A) = 0$, it means at least one eigenvalue is zero, not that all are. The matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ has $\det = 0$ but eigenvalues $\lambda_1 = 5$ and $\lambda_2 = 0$.

Exercises

Easy Problems 🟢

  1. Find the eigenvalues of $A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 7 \end{pmatrix}$. Explain why diagonal matrices make eigenvalue computation trivial.
  2. Compute the eigenvalues of $B = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$. Why can you read them directly from the matrix?
  3. The matrix $C = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$ has $\text{tr}(C) = 4$ and $\det(C) = -5$. Use the formula $\lambda^2 - \text{tr}\lambda + \det = 0$ to find the eigenvalues.
  4. If $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $A$, show that $\lambda^2$ is an eigenvalue of $A^2$. What is the corresponding eigenvector?
  5. Find an eigenvector of $D = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 2 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$ for the eigenvalue $\lambda = 7$. Verify your answer by computing $D\mathbf{v}$.
  6. A $3 \times 3$ matrix has eigenvalues $1, 2, 3$. What are its trace and determinant?
  7. The rotation matrix $R = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$ has $\text{tr}(R) = 2\cos\theta$ and $\det(R) = 1$. For which values of $\theta$ are the eigenvalues real?
  8. If $A\mathbf{v} = 5\mathbf{v}$ and $A\mathbf{w} = -3\mathbf{w}$, what is $A(2\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w})$? Is $2\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w}$ an eigenvector?

Medium Problems 🟡

  1. Find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$. Classify this matrix: is it invertible? Positive definite? What is its rank?
  2. Diagonalize $A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$. Write $A = PDP^{-1}$ explicitly, then use this to compute $A^5$.
  3. The matrix $M = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ represents a 90° rotation. Find its eigenvalues. Explain geometrically why there are no real eigenvectors — what does a rotation do to every direction?
  4. A Markov chain has transition matrix $P = \begin{pmatrix} 0.7 & 0.4 \\ 0.3 & 0.6 \end{pmatrix}$. Find the eigenvalues and the stationary distribution (the eigenvector for $\lambda = 1$, normalized so entries sum to 1). What does the second eigenvalue tell you about mixing speed?
  5. Prove that if $A$ is invertible with eigenvalue $\lambda$, then $A^{-1}$ has eigenvalue $1/\lambda$. What is the eigenvector of $A^{-1}$?
  6. The covariance matrix of a 2D dataset is $\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$. Find the principal components and compute the fraction of variance explained by the first component.
  7. Show that the matrix $N = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ is not diagonalizable. Find all eigenvalues and show that the eigenspace is one-dimensional despite the eigenvalue having algebraic multiplicity 2.
  8. Implement power iteration by hand for $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$ starting from $\mathbf{b}_0 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$. Perform 3 iterations and compare your approximate dominant eigenvalue to the exact answer.

Hard Problems 🔴

  1. Find the eigenvalues of the $3 \times 3$ matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$. This is the tridiagonal matrix that appears in discretizations of the Laplacian operator. Verify that the eigenvalues are $\lambda_k = 2 - 2\cos\!\left(\frac{k\pi}{4}\right)$ for $k = 1, 2, 3$.
  2. Prove the Cayley–Hamilton theorem for $2 \times 2$ matrices: if the characteristic polynomial of $A$ is $p(\lambda) = \lambda^2 - \text{tr}(A)\lambda + \det(A)$, then $A^2 - \text{tr}(A)\cdot A + \det(A)\cdot I = 0$. Test it on a specific matrix.
  3. A dataset has covariance matrix with eigenvalues $\lambda_1 = 10, \lambda_2 = 3, \lambda_3 = 0.5, \lambda_4 = 0.01$. Compute the spectral entropy $H = -\sum p_i \ln p_i$ (where $p_i = \lambda_i / \sum \lambda_j$) and the effective rank $e^H$. What does this tell you about the intrinsic dimensionality of the data?
  4. Prove that the convergence rate of power iteration is $O(|\lambda_2 / \lambda_1|^k)$. Under what conditions does power iteration fail to converge? Describe the deflation technique for finding $\lambda_2$ after $\lambda_1$ has been found.
  5. The Google PageRank matrix is $G = (1-\alpha)\frac{\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T}{n} + \alpha S$, where $S$ is the column-stochastic link matrix, $\alpha \approx 0.85$, and $n$ is the number of pages. Prove that $G$ has a unique dominant eigenvalue $\lambda_1 = 1$ and that $|\lambda_2| \leq \alpha$. What does this imply about the convergence rate of PageRank computation via power iteration?
  6. Prove the spectral theorem for real symmetric matrices: if $A = A^T$, then all eigenvalues are real and eigenvectors from distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal. Then explain why $A$ can be written as $A = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i \mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T$, and interpret each term $\lambda_i \mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T$ as a rank-one projection.
  7. The Rayleigh quotient $R(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\mathbf{x}^T A\mathbf{x}}{\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x}}$ for a symmetric matrix $A$ satisfies $\lambda_{\min} \leq R(\mathbf{x}) \leq \lambda_{\max}$ for all nonzero $\mathbf{x}$, with equality when $\mathbf{x}$ is the corresponding eigenvector. Prove these bounds and explain how gradient ascent on $R$ yields an eigenvector algorithm.
  8. A Markov chain with $n$ states has transition matrix $P$. The entropy rate of the chain is $h = -\sum_{i,j} \pi_i P_{ij} \ln P_{ij}$, where $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ is the stationary distribution (eigenvector for $\lambda = 1$). The spectral gap $\gamma = 1 - |\lambda_2|$ controls the mixing time $t_{\text{mix}} = O(1/\gamma)$. For a random walk on the complete graph $K_n$, compute $\lambda_2$, the spectral gap, and the mixing time. Interpret the result in terms of how quickly the walker "forgets" where it started.
