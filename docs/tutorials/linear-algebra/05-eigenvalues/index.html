The Definition

An eigenvector of a matrix $A$ is a nonzero vector $\mathbf{v}$ that, when transformed by $A$, only gets scaled:

Eigenvalue Equation

$$A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$$ where $\lambda$ is the eigenvalue corresponding to eigenvector $\mathbf{v}$.

Most vectors change direction when transformed. Eigenvectors are special—they stay on their original line, just stretched or shrunk by factor $\lambda$.

Finding Eigenvalues

Rearrange $A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$:

$$(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$$

For a nonzero solution $\mathbf{v}$ to exist, $(A - \lambda I)$ must be singular:

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$$

This is the characteristic equation. Its roots are the eigenvalues.

Example: 2×2 Matrix
1
Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$
2
$A - \lambda I = \begin{pmatrix} 4-\lambda & 1 \\ 2 & 3-\lambda \end{pmatrix}$
3
$\det(A - \lambda I) = (4-\lambda)(3-\lambda) - 2 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10$
4
Solve: $\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = 0 \implies \lambda = 5$ or $\lambda = 2$

Finding Eigenvectors

For each eigenvalue $\lambda$, solve $(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$:

For $\lambda = 5$:

$$\begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -2 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \implies \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

For $\lambda = 2$:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \implies \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Diagonalization

If $A$ has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors, we can write:

$$A = PDP^{-1}$$

where $P$ contains eigenvectors as columns and $D$ is diagonal with eigenvalues.

Why Diagonalization Matters

Powers become trivial: $A^k = PD^kP^{-1}$. Since $D$ is diagonal, $D^k$ just raises each eigenvalue to the $k$-th power. This is essential for analyzing dynamical systems and Markov chains.

Symmetric Matrices

Symmetric matrices ($A = A^T$) have beautiful eigenvalue properties:

  • All eigenvalues are real
  • Eigenvectors are orthogonal
  • Always diagonalizable: $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ where $Q$ is orthogonal
ML Connection

Covariance matrices are symmetric and positive semi-definite. Their eigenvectors are the principal components in PCA, pointing in directions of maximum variance.

05. Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Eigenvectors are the "special directions" where a matrix acts simply by scaling. They reveal the intrinsic behavior of linear transformations and are fundamental to ML.
