The Problem

Given a matrix $A$ and a vector $\mathbf{b}$, find all vectors $\mathbf{x}$ satisfying:

$$A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$$
Three Possible Outcomes

1. No solution: $\mathbf{b}$ is not in the column space of $A$
2. Unique solution: Columns of $A$ are linearly independent
3. Infinitely many: Free variables exist in the system

Gaussian Elimination

The fundamental algorithm for solving linear systems. We use elementary row operations to reduce $A$ to row echelon form.

Elementary Row Operations
1
Swap: Exchange two rows
2
Scale: Multiply a row by a nonzero constant
3
Add: Add a multiple of one row to another

Row Echelon Form

A matrix is in row echelon form if:

  • All zero rows are at the bottom
  • Each leading entry (pivot) is to the right of the pivot above it
$$\begin{pmatrix} \boxed{2} & 3 & 1 & 4 \\ 0 & \boxed{1} & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & \boxed{5} & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Worked Example

Solve the system:

$$\begin{cases} x + 2y + z = 9 \\ 2x + 4y + 3z = 21 \\ 3x + 6y + 5z = 33 \end{cases}$$
Reduction to Row Echelon Form
1
Augmented matrix: $\left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 1 & 9 \\ 2 & 4 & 3 & 21 \\ 3 & 6 & 5 & 33 \end{array}\right)$
2
$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1$, $R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 3R_1$: $\left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 1 & 9 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 & 6 \end{array}\right)$
3
$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 2R_2$: $\left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 1 & 9 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right)$

Back substitution gives: $z = 3$, and $x + 2y = 6$. Here $y$ is a free variable.

$$\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 - 2t \\ t \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 0 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} + t\begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$$
Geometric Interpretation

The solution is a line in $\mathbb{R}^3$: a particular solution plus any multiple of the null space vector $(-2, 1, 0)^T$.

LU Factorization

Instead of solving from scratch, we can factor $A = LU$ where $L$ is lower triangular and $U$ is upper triangular. Then solve in two steps:

$$A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \implies L(U\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{b}$$
  1. Solve $L\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{b}$ for $\mathbf{y}$ (forward substitution)
  2. Solve $U\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}$ for $\mathbf{x}$ (back substitution)
Why LU Factorization?

If you need to solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ for many different $\mathbf{b}$ vectors, factor once, solve cheaply each time. This is crucial for iterative methods in ML.

03. Systems of Linear Equations

Every system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ asks: can we express $\mathbf{b}$ as a linear combination of the columns of $A$? The answer reveals fundamental structure.
