07. Orthogonality and Projections

Orthogonality simplifies everything. Orthogonal bases make computations easy, and projections are the key to least squares, which underlies linear regression.

Inner Products and Orthogonality

The inner product (dot product) of vectors $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ is:

$$\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^n u_i v_i$$
Orthogonality

Vectors $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are orthogonal if $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0$.

A set of vectors is orthonormal if they are mutually orthogonal and each has unit length.

The inner product relates to the angle $\theta$ between vectors:

$$\cos\theta = \frac{\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle}{\|\mathbf{u}\| \|\mathbf{v}\|}$$

Projection onto a Vector

The projection of $\mathbf{b}$ onto $\mathbf{a}$ is the vector in the direction of $\mathbf{a}$ closest to $\mathbf{b}$:

$$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}} \mathbf{b} = \frac{\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle}{\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{a} \rangle} \mathbf{a} = \frac{\mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{b}}{\mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{a}} \mathbf{a}$$
Decomposition

Any vector $\mathbf{b}$ decomposes into a component parallel to $\mathbf{a}$ and one orthogonal: $$\mathbf{b} = \text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}} \mathbf{b} + (\mathbf{b} - \text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}} \mathbf{b})$$

Projection onto a Subspace

To project $\mathbf{b}$ onto the column space of matrix $A$, we find the closest point $A\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ in the column space:

Deriving the Projection
1
The error $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{b} - A\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ must be orthogonal to the column space
2
This means $A^T(\mathbf{b} - A\hat{\mathbf{x}}) = \mathbf{0}$
3
Normal equations: $A^T A \hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^T \mathbf{b}$
4
Projection matrix: $P = A(A^T A)^{-1}A^T$
This is Least Squares!

The normal equations $A^T A \hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^T \mathbf{b}$ minimize $\|\mathbf{b} - A\mathbf{x}\|^2$. This is the foundation of linear regression.

Gram-Schmidt Orthogonalization

Given linearly independent vectors $\mathbf{a}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{a}_n$, Gram-Schmidt produces orthonormal vectors $\mathbf{q}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{q}_n$ spanning the same space.

Gram-Schmidt Process
1
$\mathbf{q}_1 = \mathbf{a}_1 / \|\mathbf{a}_1\|$
2
$\mathbf{v}_2 = \mathbf{a}_2 - (\mathbf{q}_1^T \mathbf{a}_2)\mathbf{q}_1$, then $\mathbf{q}_2 = \mathbf{v}_2 / \|\mathbf{v}_2\|$
3
$\mathbf{v}_k = \mathbf{a}_k - \sum_{j=1}^{k-1} (\mathbf{q}_j^T \mathbf{a}_k)\mathbf{q}_j$, then $\mathbf{q}_k = \mathbf{v}_k / \|\mathbf{v}_k\|$

QR Factorization

Gram-Schmidt gives us the QR factorization: $A = QR$

  • $Q$ has orthonormal columns (the $\mathbf{q}_i$ vectors)
  • $R$ is upper triangular (stores the projection coefficients)
$$A = QR \implies A^TA = R^TQ^TQR = R^TR$$

QR is numerically more stable than computing $A^TA$ directly and is the preferred method for solving least squares in practice.
