Inner Products and Orthogonality

Why should we care about perpendicularity in abstract vector spaces? In two and three dimensions, "perpendicular" has an obvious geometric meaning — two directions that share nothing in common. But the concept generalises far beyond geometry. When two signals are orthogonal, they carry independent information; when two features in a dataset are orthogonal, knowing one tells you absolutely nothing about the other. Orthogonality is the algebraic expression of informational independence, and that is why it sits at the heart of statistics, signal processing, and machine learning.

The tool that lets us measure "how much two vectors point in the same direction" is the inner product (also called the dot product in $\mathbb{R}^n$). Given two vectors $\mathbf{u} = (u_1, \ldots, u_n)$ and $\mathbf{v} = (v_1, \ldots, v_n)$, their inner product is defined as:

$$\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^n u_i v_i$$

Notice what this formula does: it multiplies corresponding components and sums the results. If $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ "agree" in every component (both positive or both negative), the sum is large and positive. If they "disagree" (one positive where the other is negative), the positive and negative products cancel. When the cancellation is perfect — when the inner product is exactly zero — the vectors are orthogonal. They share no common direction at all.

Orthogonality and Orthonormality

Vectors $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are orthogonal (written $\mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v}$) if $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0$.

A set of vectors $\{\mathbf{q}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{q}_k\}$ is orthonormal if they are mutually orthogonal and each has unit length: $\langle \mathbf{q}_i, \mathbf{q}_j \rangle = \delta_{ij}$, where $\delta_{ij}$ is the Kronecker delta (1 if $i=j$, 0 otherwise).

The inner product also encodes the angle between two vectors. From the law of cosines in Euclidean geometry, one can show:

$$\cos\theta = \frac{\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle}{\|\mathbf{u}\| \|\mathbf{v}\|}$$

This formula is the foundation of cosine similarity, one of the most widely used similarity metrics in machine learning. When you compare word embeddings, document TF-IDF vectors, or image feature vectors, you are computing exactly this ratio. The numerator measures alignment; the denominator normalises for magnitude. Two documents about the same topic will have a cosine similarity near $+1$, unrelated documents near $0$, and (in theory) opposing documents near $-1$.

Worked Example

Let $\mathbf{u} = (1, 2, -1)$ and $\mathbf{v} = (3, 0, 3)$. Then: $$\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = (1)(3) + (2)(0) + (-1)(3) = 3 + 0 - 3 = 0$$ So $\mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v}$. The norms are $\|\mathbf{u}\| = \sqrt{6}$ and $\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{18} = 3\sqrt{2}$, and indeed $\cos\theta = 0 / (\sqrt{6} \cdot 3\sqrt{2}) = 0$, confirming $\theta = 90°$.

Now try $\mathbf{w} = (1, 1, 1)$. We get $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w} \rangle = 1 + 2 - 1 = 2 \neq 0$, so $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{w}$ are not orthogonal.

A crucial property of orthogonal sets is linear independence: if $\{\mathbf{q}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{q}_k\}$ are mutually orthogonal and nonzero, they are automatically linearly independent. To see why, suppose $c_1 \mathbf{q}_1 + \cdots + c_k \mathbf{q}_k = \mathbf{0}$. Taking the inner product of both sides with $\mathbf{q}_i$ gives $c_i \|\mathbf{q}_i\|^2 = 0$, which forces $c_i = 0$ since $\|\mathbf{q}_i\| \neq 0$. This elegant argument works for every $i$, so all coefficients must be zero. Orthogonal vectors are the strongest form of independence in a vector space.

Projection onto a Vector

Imagine you are standing in a field and the sun is directly overhead. Your shadow on the ground is the projection of your 3D body onto the 2D ground plane. The shadow captures the "horizontal" information about your position but discards the "vertical" part. Projection does the same thing algebraically: it decomposes a vector into a component that lies along a given direction and a residual that is orthogonal to it.

Given a vector $\mathbf{b}$ and a direction $\mathbf{a}$, we want to find the scalar $\hat{x}$ such that $\hat{x}\mathbf{a}$ is as close to $\mathbf{b}$ as possible. "As close as possible" means we minimise $\|\mathbf{b} - \hat{x}\mathbf{a}\|^2$. The minimiser occurs when the residual $\mathbf{b} - \hat{x}\mathbf{a}$ is orthogonal to $\mathbf{a}$:

Deriving the Projection Formula
1
Require orthogonality: $\langle \mathbf{a},\; \mathbf{b} - \hat{x}\mathbf{a} \rangle = 0$
2
Expand: $\mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{b} - \hat{x}\,\mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{a} = 0$
3
Solve: $\hat{x} = \dfrac{\mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{b}}{\mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{a}}$

The projection vector itself is:

$$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}} \mathbf{b} = \hat{x}\,\mathbf{a} = \frac{\mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{b}}{\mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{a}} \mathbf{a}$$

Notice a subtle but important point: the projection formula involves the ratio $\mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{b} / \mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{a}$, not $\mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{b}$ alone. The denominator normalises for the length of $\mathbf{a}$. If $\mathbf{a}$ is already a unit vector ($\|\mathbf{a}\| = 1$), the formula simplifies beautifully to $\text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}} \mathbf{b} = (\mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{b})\,\mathbf{a}$. This is one reason orthonormal bases are so convenient — projections become simple inner products.

Orthogonal Decomposition

Any vector $\mathbf{b}$ decomposes uniquely into a component parallel to $\mathbf{a}$ and a component orthogonal to $\mathbf{a}$: $$\mathbf{b} = \underbrace{\text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}} \mathbf{b}}_{\text{parallel}} + \underbrace{(\mathbf{b} - \text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}} \mathbf{b})}_{\text{orthogonal}}$$ You can verify orthogonality by checking that the inner product of the two components is zero.

Worked Example: Projection in $\mathbb{R}^2$

Let $\mathbf{a} = (1, 2)$ and $\mathbf{b} = (3, 1)$. Then: $$\hat{x} = \frac{\mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{b}}{\mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{a}} = \frac{(1)(3) + (2)(1)}{(1)(1) + (2)(2)} = \frac{5}{5} = 1$$ $$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}} \mathbf{b} = 1 \cdot (1, 2) = (1, 2)$$ The residual is $\mathbf{b} - \text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}} \mathbf{b} = (3,1) - (1,2) = (2, -1)$.

Check: $\langle (1,2),\,(2,-1) \rangle = 2 - 2 = 0$. ✓ The residual is indeed orthogonal.

Projection has a beautiful matrix form. The projection onto the line through $\mathbf{a}$ can be written as $P\mathbf{b}$ where $P = \frac{\mathbf{a}\mathbf{a}^T}{\mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{a}}$ is the projection matrix. This matrix is symmetric ($P^T = P$) and idempotent ($P^2 = P$): projecting twice gives the same result as projecting once, which makes geometric sense — a shadow's shadow is the same shadow.

Projection onto a Subspace

Projecting onto a single vector is useful, but the real power emerges when we project onto an entire subspace. Given a matrix $A$ whose columns $\mathbf{a}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{a}_k$ span a $k$-dimensional subspace, we want to find the point $A\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ in the column space of $A$ that is closest to a given vector $\mathbf{b}$. This is the multidimensional generalisation of the shadow analogy: we are projecting $\mathbf{b}$ onto a plane (or hyperplane) rather than a line.

The key geometric insight remains the same: the error $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{b} - A\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ must be orthogonal to every vector in the column space. Since every vector in the column space can be written as $A\mathbf{c}$ for some coefficient vector $\mathbf{c}$, the orthogonality condition says $(A\mathbf{c})^T \mathbf{e} = 0$ for all $\mathbf{c}$. This simplifies to $\mathbf{c}^T A^T \mathbf{e} = 0$ for all $\mathbf{c}$, which forces $A^T \mathbf{e} = \mathbf{0}$.

Deriving the Projection onto $\text{Col}(A)$
1
The error $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{b} - A\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ must be orthogonal to every column of $A$: $A^T \mathbf{e} = \mathbf{0}$
2
Substituting $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{b} - A\hat{\mathbf{x}}$: $A^T(\mathbf{b} - A\hat{\mathbf{x}}) = \mathbf{0}$
3
Rearranging gives the normal equations: $A^T A \hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^T \mathbf{b}$
4
If $A$ has full column rank, $A^TA$ is invertible and $\hat{\mathbf{x}} = (A^T A)^{-1}A^T \mathbf{b}$
5
The projection matrix is $P = A(A^T A)^{-1}A^T$, so $\hat{\mathbf{b}} = P\mathbf{b}$

The projection matrix $P$ inherits the same beautiful properties as the 1D case: $P^T = P$ (symmetry) and $P^2 = P$ (idempotency). Moreover, the complementary matrix $I - P$ projects onto the orthogonal complement — the subspace perpendicular to the column space. The four fundamental subspaces of $A$ connect here: the column space and left null space are orthogonal complements in $\mathbb{R}^m$, while the row space and null space are orthogonal complements in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

This is Least Squares!

The normal equations $A^T A \hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^T \mathbf{b}$ minimise $\|\mathbf{b} - A\mathbf{x}\|^2$. This is the foundation of linear regression. When $\mathbf{b}$ doesn't lie in the column space of $A$ (the system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ has no exact solution), the projection gives the best approximate solution.

Worked Example: Projection onto a Plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$

Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$. The column space of $A$ is a 2D plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

Compute: $A^TA = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$, $A^T\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}$.

Solve $A^TA\hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^T\mathbf{b}$: $(A^TA)^{-1} = \frac{1}{3}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$, so $\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \frac{1}{3}\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}$.

The projection is $\hat{\mathbf{b}} = A\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \frac{1}{3}\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 2 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$. The error $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{b} - \hat{\mathbf{b}} = \frac{1}{3}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}$. Check: $A^T\mathbf{e} = \frac{1}{3}\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$. ✓

Gram-Schmidt Process

We have seen that orthonormal bases make projections trivially easy — you just compute inner products instead of solving systems of equations. But what if you start with a basis that isn't orthonormal? The Gram-Schmidt process is an algorithm that takes any set of linearly independent vectors and systematically converts them into an orthonormal set spanning the same space. It is the constructive proof that every subspace has an orthonormal basis.

The idea is beautifully simple: process the vectors one at a time. Keep the first vector (normalised). For the second vector, subtract off its projection onto the first — what remains is the component perpendicular to the first. For the third vector, subtract its projections onto both the first and second — what remains is perpendicular to both. Continue until all vectors have been processed.

The Gram-Schmidt Algorithm

Given linearly independent vectors $\mathbf{a}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{a}_n$, produce orthonormal vectors $\mathbf{q}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{q}_n$:

Step 1: $\mathbf{q}_1 = \mathbf{a}_1 / \|\mathbf{a}_1\|$

Step $k$ (for $k = 2, \ldots, n$):
$\mathbf{v}_k = \mathbf{a}_k - \displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{k-1} \langle \mathbf{q}_j, \mathbf{a}_k \rangle\, \mathbf{q}_j$,   then   $\mathbf{q}_k = \mathbf{v}_k / \|\mathbf{v}_k\|$

Each $\mathbf{v}_k$ is $\mathbf{a}_k$ minus its projection onto the subspace spanned by $\{\mathbf{q}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{q}_{k-1}\}$.

Worked Example: Gram-Schmidt in $\mathbb{R}^3$
1
Start with $\mathbf{a}_1 = (1, 1, 0)$, $\mathbf{a}_2 = (1, 0, 1)$, $\mathbf{a}_3 = (0, 1, 1)$.
$\|\mathbf{a}_1\| = \sqrt{2}$, so $\mathbf{q}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, 1, 0)$.
2
$\langle \mathbf{q}_1, \mathbf{a}_2 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1 + 0 + 0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$.
$\mathbf{v}_2 = (1,0,1) - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,1,0) = (1,0,1) - \frac{1}{2}(1,1,0) = (\frac{1}{2}, -\frac{1}{2}, 1)$.
$\|\mathbf{v}_2\| = \sqrt{1/4 + 1/4 + 1} = \sqrt{3/2}$, so $\mathbf{q}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(1, -1, 2)$.
3
$\langle \mathbf{q}_1, \mathbf{a}_3 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(0+1+0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$. $\langle \mathbf{q}_2, \mathbf{a}_3 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(0-1+2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}$.
$\mathbf{v}_3 = (0,1,1) - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\mathbf{q}_1 - \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\mathbf{q}_2 = (0,1,1) - \frac{1}{2}(1,1,0) - \frac{1}{6}(1,-1,2)$.
$= (0-\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{6},\; 1-\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{6},\; 1-0-\frac{1}{3}) = (-\frac{2}{3},\; \frac{2}{3},\; \frac{2}{3})$.
$\|\mathbf{v}_3\| = \frac{2\sqrt{3}}{3}$, so $\mathbf{q}_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(-1, 1, 1)$.

You can verify the result: $\langle \mathbf{q}_1, \mathbf{q}_2 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{12}}(1-1+0) = 0$, $\langle \mathbf{q}_1, \mathbf{q}_3 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(-1+1+0) = 0$, $\langle \mathbf{q}_2, \mathbf{q}_3 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{18}}(-1-1+2) = 0$. All orthogonal, and each $\|\mathbf{q}_i\| = 1$.

Numerical Stability: Classical vs Modified Gram-Schmidt

The algorithm described above is called Classical Gram-Schmidt (CGS). In finite-precision arithmetic, it can lose orthogonality badly because rounding errors accumulate — the computed $\mathbf{q}_j$ are not perfectly orthogonal, so subtracting projections doesn't fully remove the component in that direction.

Modified Gram-Schmidt (MGS) fixes this by subtracting projections one at a time: first subtract the $\mathbf{q}_1$ component from $\mathbf{a}_k$, then subtract the $\mathbf{q}_2$ component from the updated vector, and so on. Mathematically equivalent but numerically superior. In practice, even MGS can be insufficient for ill-conditioned problems; Householder reflections (used in the QR decomposition) are preferred for maximum stability.

In machine learning, Gram-Schmidt appears whenever you need an orthonormal basis. For example, when computing PCA, the principal components form an orthonormal set (they are eigenvectors of a symmetric matrix). When implementing the Lanczos algorithm for large-scale eigenvalue problems — which is how libraries like SciPy and ARPACK handle sparse matrices — repeated orthogonalisation is a core step. And in recurrent neural network architectures, orthogonal weight initialisation (using Gram-Schmidt on random matrices) helps prevent vanishing and exploding gradients.

QR Decomposition

The Gram-Schmidt process doesn't just produce an orthonormal basis — it also implicitly computes a factorisation of the original matrix. If we arrange the original vectors as columns of a matrix $A$ and the orthonormal vectors as columns of $Q$, the projection coefficients form an upper triangular matrix $R$. This gives us $A = QR$, one of the most important factorisations in numerical linear algebra.

QR Decomposition

Every $m \times n$ matrix $A$ with linearly independent columns can be factored as: $$A = QR$$ where $Q$ is $m \times n$ with orthonormal columns ($Q^TQ = I_n$) and $R$ is $n \times n$ upper triangular with positive diagonal entries.

The entries of $R$ are $r_{ij} = \langle \mathbf{q}_i, \mathbf{a}_j \rangle$ for $i \leq j$ and $r_{ij} = 0$ for $i > j$, recording how much of each original vector lies in each orthonormal direction.

Why is $R$ upper triangular? Because $\mathbf{q}_k$ is constructed only from $\mathbf{a}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{a}_k$. So $\mathbf{a}_j$ has no component along $\mathbf{q}_k$ for $k > j$. The upper triangular structure encodes the sequential nature of the orthogonalisation.

Worked Example: QR from Gram-Schmidt

Continuing our earlier example with $\mathbf{a}_1 = (1,1,0)$, $\mathbf{a}_2 = (1,0,1)$:

$r_{11} = \|\mathbf{a}_1\| = \sqrt{2}$,   $r_{12} = \langle \mathbf{q}_1, \mathbf{a}_2 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$,   $r_{22} = \|\mathbf{v}_2\| = \sqrt{3/2}$.

So $A = QR = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} \\ 0 & \frac{2}{\sqrt{6}} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \sqrt{2} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\ 0 & \sqrt{3/2} \end{pmatrix}$. You can verify this product recovers $A$.

QR for solving least squares. The normal equations $A^TA\hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^T\mathbf{b}$ involve forming $A^TA$, which squares the condition number and introduces numerical error. With QR, we can do much better. Substituting $A = QR$:

$$A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \implies QR\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \implies R\mathbf{x} = Q^T\mathbf{b}$$

Since $R$ is upper triangular, this system is solved by back-substitution — no matrix inversion needed, and the condition number is that of $A$ itself (not $A^TA$). This is why every serious numerical library (NumPy's lstsq, MATLAB's \ operator, R's lm()) uses QR internally for least squares, not the normal equations.

QR Algorithm for Eigenvalues

The QR decomposition also powers the QR algorithm, the workhorse method for computing all eigenvalues of a matrix. The idea: repeatedly compute $A_k = Q_k R_k$ and then form $A_{k+1} = R_k Q_k$. Under mild conditions, the sequence $A_1, A_2, \ldots$ converges to an upper triangular matrix whose diagonal entries are the eigenvalues. This is what numpy.linalg.eig does under the hood.

In machine learning practice, you may not call the QR decomposition directly, but it is working behind the scenes every time you fit a linear regression, compute PCA on a data matrix, or use an eigenvalue solver. Understanding QR helps you understand why these algorithms are numerically stable and what can go wrong when the data matrix is nearly rank-deficient.

Orthogonal Matrices

A square matrix $Q$ whose columns form an orthonormal basis is called an orthogonal matrix. (Confusingly, the standard term is "orthogonal" even though "orthonormal" would be more accurate — the columns are not just mutually orthogonal but also unit-length. This is one of linear algebra's most persistent naming quirks.) Orthogonal matrices are the "rigid motions" of linear algebra: they rotate and reflect vectors without stretching or compressing them.

Orthogonal Matrix

A square matrix $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is orthogonal if $Q^T Q = Q Q^T = I$, or equivalently $Q^{-1} = Q^T$. Key properties:

  • $\det(Q) = \pm 1$ (reflections give $-1$, rotations give $+1$)
  • $\|Q\mathbf{x}\| = \|\mathbf{x}\|$ for all $\mathbf{x}$ — lengths are preserved
  • $\langle Q\mathbf{x}, Q\mathbf{y} \rangle = \langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle$ — angles are preserved
  • All eigenvalues have $|\lambda| = 1$ (they lie on the unit circle in $\mathbb{C}$)
  • All singular values equal $1$

The property $Q^{-1} = Q^T$ is computationally magical. Inverting a general $n \times n$ matrix costs $O(n^3)$ operations. Inverting an orthogonal matrix costs $O(n^2)$ — just transpose it. This is why algorithms based on orthogonal transformations (QR, Householder, Givens rotations) are preferred whenever possible.

Rotations are the most familiar orthogonal matrices. In 2D, the rotation by angle $\theta$ is:

$$R(\theta) = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$$

This has $\det(R) = \cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$ (proper rotation). Reflections have $\det = -1$. The reflection through the line making angle $\theta/2$ with the $x$-axis is:

$$H = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & -\cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$$
Householder Reflections

A Householder reflection is a matrix of the form $H = I - 2\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T$ where $\|\mathbf{v}\| = 1$. It reflects any vector across the hyperplane perpendicular to $\mathbf{v}$. You can verify: $H^T = H$, $H^2 = I$ (so $H^{-1} = H$), and $\det(H) = -1$.

Householder reflections are the workhorses of numerical linear algebra. They are used to implement the QR decomposition more stably than Gram-Schmidt: by choosing $\mathbf{v}$ carefully, you can zero out all entries below the diagonal in one column, building $R$ one column at a time. This is the algorithm used by LAPACK (and hence NumPy, SciPy, MATLAB, Julia, etc.).

In machine learning, orthogonal matrices appear in several important roles. Orthogonal weight initialisation (initialising weight matrices of neural networks as orthogonal matrices) helps with gradient flow — since orthogonal matrices preserve norms, gradients neither explode nor vanish as they propagate through layers. The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) matrix (scaled appropriately) is unitary (the complex analogue of orthogonal), and the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) used in JPEG compression is implemented via an orthogonal matrix. PCA itself is an orthogonal change of basis.

Least Squares via Orthogonality

We have already seen the normal equations emerge from the projection framework. Let us now develop this connection more carefully, because least squares is arguably the single most important application of orthogonality in all of applied mathematics. Every time you fit a linear regression, train a linear classifier, or compute a best-fit curve, you are solving a least squares problem — and the solution is fundamentally a projection.

The setup: we have a system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ that typically has no exact solution (the system is overdetermined — more equations than unknowns). We seek $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ minimising the squared error $\|A\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b}\|^2$.

Geometric Derivation of Least Squares
1
The set of all possible $A\mathbf{x}$ is the column space $\text{Col}(A)$. We want the point in $\text{Col}(A)$ closest to $\mathbf{b}$.
2
The closest point is the projection $\hat{\mathbf{b}} = P\mathbf{b}$ where $P = A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T$.
3
The coefficient vector satisfies $A\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \hat{\mathbf{b}}$, giving $\hat{\mathbf{x}} = (A^TA)^{-1}A^T\mathbf{b}$.
4
The minimum error is $\|\mathbf{e}\| = \|\mathbf{b} - \hat{\mathbf{b}}\|$, the distance from $\mathbf{b}$ to the column space.
Worked Example: Fitting a Line

Fit $y = c_0 + c_1 t$ to the data points $(0, 1)$, $(1, 3)$, $(2, 4)$. The system is $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ with $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$, $\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$.

$A^TA = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 3 \\ 3 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$, $A^T\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ 11 \end{pmatrix}$. Solving: $\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \begin{pmatrix} 7/6 \\ 3/2 \end{pmatrix}$.

So the best-fit line is $y = \frac{7}{6} + \frac{3}{2}t$. The predicted values are $\hat{\mathbf{b}} = (\frac{7}{6}, \frac{8}{3}, \frac{25}{6}) \approx (1.17, 2.67, 4.17)$.

Connection to linear regression in ML. In a standard linear regression with $n$ data points and $d$ features, the design matrix $A$ is $n \times d$ (with a column of ones for the intercept), and $\mathbf{b}$ is the target vector. The formula $\hat{\mathbf{x}} = (A^TA)^{-1}A^T\mathbf{b}$ gives the closed-form solution for the optimal weights. When $n \gg d$, the system is heavily overdetermined, and the least-squares solution is the maximum likelihood estimate under Gaussian noise assumptions.

In practice, as we noted, the QR approach $R\hat{\mathbf{x}} = Q^T\mathbf{b}$ is preferred to the normal equations for numerical stability. When the matrix is very large and sparse, iterative methods like conjugate gradients are used instead, but the underlying geometry — projection onto the column space — is always the same.

Information-Theoretic Connections

Orthogonality and information theory are deeply intertwined. The central theme is this: orthogonal transformations preserve information. They rearrange it, rotate it, repackage it — but they never destroy it and never create it. This section explores why this is true and why it matters for machine learning.

Parseval's Theorem (Energy Preservation)

If $Q$ is an orthogonal matrix and $\mathbf{y} = Q\mathbf{x}$, then: $$\|\mathbf{y}\|^2 = \|Q\mathbf{x}\|^2 = \mathbf{x}^TQ^TQ\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x} = \|\mathbf{x}\|^2$$ The "energy" (sum of squared components) is preserved. In the Fourier transform setting, this says the energy in the time domain equals the energy in the frequency domain.

Why does energy preservation matter for information? Because information theory measures uncertainty via entropy, and for Gaussian random vectors, entropy depends only on the covariance matrix. If $\mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \Sigma)$, then $\mathbf{y} = Q\mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, Q\Sigma Q^T)$. The differential entropy is $h(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2}\ln\det(2\pi e\,\Sigma)$. Since $\det(Q\Sigma Q^T) = \det(Q)\det(\Sigma)\det(Q^T) = \det(\Sigma)$ (because $\det(Q) = \pm 1$), we get $h(\mathbf{y}) = h(\mathbf{x})$. Orthogonal transforms preserve entropy.

Decorrelation and independence. PCA rotates the data into a coordinate system where the components are uncorrelated — the covariance matrix becomes diagonal. For Gaussian data, uncorrelated implies independent, so PCA achieves statistical independence via an orthogonal transformation. The mutual information $I(y_i; y_j) = 0$ for all $i \neq j$ in the principal component basis. This is the information-theoretic justification for why PCA is so effective as a preprocessing step: it removes redundancy without losing any information.

Whitening in Machine Learning

Whitening (also called sphering) transforms data so that the covariance matrix becomes the identity $I$. If $\Sigma = Q\Lambda Q^T$ is the eigendecomposition of the covariance, then $W = \Lambda^{-1/2}Q^T$ whitens the data: $W\Sigma W^T = I$.

Whitening is used as a preprocessing step in ICA (Independent Component Analysis), in batch normalisation variants, and in natural gradient methods. The key insight is that whitened data has maximum entropy among all distributions with the same variance — the data is "spread out" as uniformly as possible in every direction.

Compression and the KLT. The Karhunen-Loève Transform (KLT) — which is PCA by another name — is the optimal orthogonal transform for data compression. Among all orthogonal transformations, PCA packs the maximum amount of energy into the fewest coefficients. This is why the DCT (a close approximation to the KLT for natural images) is used in JPEG: it concentrates image energy into a few low-frequency coefficients that can be stored cheaply, while the many near-zero high-frequency coefficients can be discarded. The information loss from discarding small coefficients is minimal precisely because the orthogonal transform concentrated the information.

Common Misconceptions

Orthogonality is one of those topics where the intuition from 2D and 3D geometry can be misleading when you move to higher dimensions. Let us address the most common sources of confusion.

Misconception 1: "Orthogonal" = "Orthonormal"

The vectors $(2, 0)$ and $(0, 3)$ are orthogonal (their dot product is zero) but not orthonormal (their lengths are 2 and 3, not 1). Confusingly, an "orthogonal matrix" has columns that are orthonormal, not just orthogonal. The term "orthogonal matrix" really means "orthonormal-column matrix." Always check whether a problem asks for orthogonal or orthonormal vectors — the formulas differ by normalisation factors.

Misconception 2: "Perpendicular only makes sense in 2D/3D"

Students sometimes struggle with orthogonality in $\mathbb{R}^{100}$ or $\mathbb{R}^{10000}$ because they cannot visualise it. But the algebraic definition $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0$ works in any dimension. In fact, high-dimensional spaces have a remarkable property: randomly chosen vectors are almost always nearly orthogonal. In $\mathbb{R}^n$, two random unit vectors have $\mathbb{E}[\cos\theta] = 0$ and $\text{Var}[\cos\theta] = O(1/n)$. This concentration phenomenon is why random projections work so well in practice (Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma).

Misconception 3: "Projection onto a subspace = sum of projections onto basis vectors"

This is true only if the basis vectors are orthogonal. If you have a non-orthogonal basis $\{\mathbf{a}_1, \mathbf{a}_2\}$, then in general: $$\text{proj}_{V} \mathbf{b} \neq \text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}_1} \mathbf{b} + \text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}_2} \mathbf{b}$$

For a non-orthogonal basis, you must use the full projection formula $P = A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T$. This is exactly why orthonormal bases are so valuable — they make projection decomposable into independent, simple computations.

Misconception 4: "Orthogonal complement has dimension $n - 1$"

The orthogonal complement of a subspace $V$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ has dimension $n - \dim(V)$, not always $n - 1$. If $V$ is a 2D plane in $\mathbb{R}^5$, its orthogonal complement is 3-dimensional. Only when $V$ is a line (1D) in $\mathbb{R}^n$ does the complement have dimension $n - 1$.

Misconception 5: "The zero vector is not orthogonal to anything"

Actually, the zero vector is orthogonal to every vector, since $\langle \mathbf{0}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0$ for all $\mathbf{v}$. However, the zero vector is never included in an orthogonal or orthonormal set because we require the vectors to be nonzero (and for orthonormality, to have unit length). The zero vector is orthogonal but not useful as a basis element.

Exercises

Easy Problems 🟢

  1. Compute the inner product of $\mathbf{u} = (2, -1, 3)$ and $\mathbf{v} = (1, 4, -1)$. Are they orthogonal? Find the angle between them.
  2. Which of the following pairs are orthogonal? (a) $(1,1)$ and $(1,-1)$; (b) $(1,2,3)$ and $(3,0,-1)$; (c) $(1,0,1)$ and $(0,1,0)$.
  3. Project $\mathbf{b} = (3, 4)$ onto $\mathbf{a} = (1, 0)$. Then project onto $\mathbf{a} = (1, 1)$. Sketch both projections and verify that the residuals are orthogonal to the respective directions.
  4. Verify that the vectors $\mathbf{q}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,1)$ and $\mathbf{q}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,-1)$ form an orthonormal basis for $\mathbb{R}^2$. Express $\mathbf{v} = (5, 3)$ in this basis.
  5. If $P$ is the projection matrix onto the line through $\mathbf{a} = (2, 1)$, compute $P$ and verify that $P^2 = P$ and $P^T = P$.
  6. Show that any two eigenvectors of a symmetric matrix corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal. (Hint: start from $A\mathbf{u} = \lambda_1 \mathbf{u}$ and $A\mathbf{v} = \lambda_2 \mathbf{v}$, and compute $\mathbf{u}^T A \mathbf{v}$ two different ways.)
  7. The matrix $Q = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ represents a 90° rotation. Verify that $Q^TQ = I$ and $\det(Q) = 1$.
  8. Given orthonormal vectors $\mathbf{q}_1, \mathbf{q}_2, \mathbf{q}_3$ and a vector $\mathbf{b}$, the projection of $\mathbf{b}$ onto $\text{span}\{\mathbf{q}_1, \mathbf{q}_2\}$ is $(\mathbf{q}_1^T\mathbf{b})\mathbf{q}_1 + (\mathbf{q}_2^T\mathbf{b})\mathbf{q}_2$. Explain why this formula is so much simpler than the general projection formula $A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T\mathbf{b}$.

Medium Problems 🟡

  1. Apply Gram-Schmidt to $\mathbf{a}_1 = (1, 0, 1)$ and $\mathbf{a}_2 = (1, 1, 0)$ to produce orthonormal vectors $\mathbf{q}_1, \mathbf{q}_2$. Write the QR factorisation of $A = [\mathbf{a}_1 \mid \mathbf{a}_2]$.
  2. Find the projection matrix $P$ that projects onto the column space of $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$. Verify that $P\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b}$ when $\mathbf{b}$ is already in $\text{Col}(A)$, and that $P\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{0}$ when $\mathbf{e}$ is orthogonal to $\text{Col}(A)$.
  3. Use the least-squares method to fit a line $y = c_0 + c_1 t$ to the data $(0, 2)$, $(1, 1)$, $(2, 3)$, $(3, 5)$. Set up $A$ and $\mathbf{b}$, solve the normal equations, and compute the residual $\|\mathbf{e}\|$.
  4. Prove that if $P$ is a projection matrix ($P^2 = P$, $P^T = P$), then $I - P$ is also a projection matrix. What subspace does $I - P$ project onto?
  5. The Householder reflection $H = I - 2\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T$ with $\mathbf{v} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, 1, 0)^T$ reflects across a plane. Compute $H$ explicitly and verify $H^2 = I$ and $\det(H) = -1$.
  6. Show that if $Q_1$ and $Q_2$ are orthogonal matrices, then $Q_1 Q_2$ is also orthogonal. What about $Q_1^{-1}$? Do the $n \times n$ orthogonal matrices form a group under multiplication?
  7. A $3 \times 2$ matrix $A$ has QR decomposition $A = QR$. The matrix $A^TA$ has condition number $\kappa(A^TA) = 10^{6}$. What is $\kappa(R)$? Why is solving $R\mathbf{x} = Q^T\mathbf{b}$ more stable than solving the normal equations?
  8. Let $\mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \Sigma)$ where $\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$. Find an orthogonal matrix $Q$ such that $\mathbf{y} = Q^T\mathbf{x}$ has uncorrelated components. What is the covariance matrix of $\mathbf{y}$?

Hard Problems 🔴

  1. Prove Parseval's theorem: if $Q$ is orthogonal and $\mathbf{y} = Q\mathbf{x}$, then $\|\mathbf{y}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{x}\|^2$. Extend this to show that $\langle Q\mathbf{u}, Q\mathbf{v} \rangle = \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle$ for all $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}$. Then prove that orthogonal transforms preserve the differential entropy of Gaussian random vectors.
  2. Implement the Modified Gram-Schmidt algorithm. Apply it to the nearly linearly dependent vectors $\mathbf{a}_1 = (1, \epsilon, 0)$, $\mathbf{a}_2 = (1, 0, \epsilon)$, $\mathbf{a}_3 = (1, \epsilon, \epsilon)$ with $\epsilon = 10^{-8}$. Compare the orthogonality of the output with Classical Gram-Schmidt. Measure $\|Q^TQ - I\|_F$ for both methods.
  3. Derive the normal equations by minimising $f(\mathbf{x}) = \|A\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b}\|^2$ using calculus (take the gradient and set it to zero). Show that this gives the same result as the geometric (projection) derivation. When does the minimiser fail to be unique, and how does this relate to the rank of $A$?
  4. Prove that among all orthogonal transforms, PCA (the eigenvector rotation of the covariance matrix) minimises the reconstruction error $\mathbb{E}[\|\mathbf{x} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_k\|^2]$ when keeping only $k$ components. (Hint: express the error in terms of eigenvalues and use the fact that PCA sorts them in decreasing order.)
  5. Implement one iteration of the QR algorithm: given $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$, compute $A = Q_1 R_1$, form $A_2 = R_1 Q_1$, and repeat. Show that after several iterations the off-diagonal entries shrink toward zero, and the diagonal entries converge to the eigenvalues of $A$.
  6. The Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma states that $n$ points in $\mathbb{R}^d$ can be projected to $\mathbb{R}^k$ (with $k = O(\log n / \epsilon^2)$) while preserving all pairwise distances to within a factor $1 \pm \epsilon$. The projection uses a random matrix with orthonormal rows. Explain why orthogonality is essential for this result and why a non-orthogonal random projection would distort distances.
  7. A neural network layer computes $\mathbf{h} = \sigma(W\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b})$. If $W$ is initialised as an orthogonal matrix, show that the gradient $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = W^T \frac{\partial L}{\partial (W\mathbf{x})}$ preserves the norm of the backpropagated gradient (ignoring the nonlinearity). Why does this help with training deep networks?
  8. Let $A$ be an $m \times n$ matrix ($m > n$) with SVD $A = U\Sigma V^T$. Show that the "thin" QR decomposition $A = QR$ and the SVD are related by $Q = UV^T$ only when $R = V\Sigma V^T$. Under what conditions is $R$ both upper triangular and symmetric? Prove that in general $Q \neq U$ but that $Q$ and $U$ have the same column space.
