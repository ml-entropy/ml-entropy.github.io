02. Continuous Distributions

When outcomes are not countable but measured on a continuum, we need probability densities instead of probability masses. This tutorial covers the five most important continuous distributions—Uniform, Normal, Exponential, Gamma, and Beta—with full derivations of their properties and the deep relationships that connect them.

From Discrete to Continuous

In discrete probability, a probability mass function (PMF) assigns a probability $P(X = x)$ to each outcome $x$. For a fair die, $P(X = 3) = 1/6$ makes perfect sense. But what if the random variable can take any real value? What is $P(X = 1.4142135\ldots)$ for a continuous measurement? The answer is zero—for every single point.

This is not a technicality; it is the fundamental reason we need a different object. If we assigned nonzero probability to each of uncountably many points, the total would exceed 1 (in fact, it would be infinite). Instead, we assign probability to intervals:

$$P(a \le X \le b) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx$$

The function $f(x)$ is the probability density function (PDF). Notice the crucial distinction: $f(x)$ is not a probability. It is a density—probability per unit length. Just as mass density (kg/m) tells you mass per meter of a wire, $f(x)$ tells you how densely probability is packed near the point $x$. In particular, $f(x)$ can exceed 1 (and often does for narrow distributions), because it is not itself a probability.

Probability Density Function (PDF)

A function $f: \mathbb{R} \to [0, \infty)$ is a PDF if:

  1. $f(x) \ge 0$ for all $x$ (non-negativity)
  2. $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx = 1$ (normalization)

The probability that $X$ falls in an interval $[a,b]$ is $P(a \le X \le b) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx$.

Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF)

The CDF $F(x) = P(X \le x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(t)\,dt$ gives the probability that $X$ takes a value at most $x$. Every CDF is non-decreasing, right-continuous, with $\lim_{x\to -\infty}F(x) = 0$ and $\lim_{x\to \infty}F(x) = 1$. The PDF is recovered as $f(x) = F'(x)$ wherever $F$ is differentiable.

Why We Need Densities

In machine learning, continuous distributions appear everywhere: Gaussian noise models, latent spaces in VAEs, weight initializations, Bayesian posteriors. Understanding the PDF/CDF framework is essential because every loss function and every generative model makes an implicit choice of density. MSE loss assumes Gaussian noise. The reparameterization trick in VAEs requires differentiable CDFs. Flow-based models learn invertible transformations between densities.

Uniform Distribution

The simplest continuous distribution assigns equal density to every point in an interval $[a,b]$ and zero density outside it. If you know nothing about a quantity except that it lies in $[a,b]$, the Uniform distribution is the maximum-entropy choice.

Uniform Distribution $\text{Unif}(a, b)$

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{1}{b-a} & \text{if } a \le x \le b \\[6pt] 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

CDF: $F(x) = \dfrac{x - a}{b - a}$ for $x \in [a,b]$.

Mean and Variance

The mean is the midpoint of the interval:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_a^b x \cdot \frac{1}{b-a}\,dx = \frac{a+b}{2}$$

The variance measures how spread out the distribution is:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = \frac{(b-a)^2}{12}$$
Connection to Random Number Generation

Every computer's random number generator fundamentally produces $U \sim \text{Unif}(0,1)$. To sample from any distribution with CDF $F$, you apply the inverse transform: $X = F^{-1}(U)$. This single idea—transforming uniform samples—underlies Monte Carlo methods, rejection sampling, and the reparameterization trick in VAEs. The Uniform distribution is the seed from which all other distributions grow.

Normal (Gaussian) Distribution

The Normal distribution is arguably the most important distribution in all of mathematics and science. It appears whenever many small, independent effects add together—measurement errors, thermal fluctuations, stock returns, pixel noise. The Central Limit Theorem guarantees this convergence, making the Gaussian a universal attractor.

Normal (Gaussian) Distribution $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\, e^{-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$$

Parameters: mean $\mu \in \mathbb{R}$, variance $\sigma^2 > 0$. The Standard Normal $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ is obtained by setting $\mu = 0, \sigma = 1$. Any Normal can be standardized: $Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}$.

The 68-95-99.7 Rule

For a Normal distribution, probability concentrates predictably around the mean:

  • 68.27% of values fall within $\mu \pm 1\sigma$
  • 95.45% of values fall within $\mu \pm 2\sigma$
  • 99.73% of values fall within $\mu \pm 3\sigma$

The Normal distribution with the 68-95-99.7 rule: shaded regions show probability within 1, 2, and 3 standard deviations of the mean.

Why Is the Gaussian Everywhere?

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) provides the answer. If $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ are independent random variables with finite mean $\mu$ and finite variance $\sigma^2$, then their standardized sum converges in distribution to a standard Normal:

$$\frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, 1) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

This is why measurement errors (sum of many tiny perturbations), test scores (sum of many question outcomes), and financial returns (sum of many trades) all tend toward bell curves. The CLT does not require the individual $X_i$ to be Normal—they can be Bernoulli, Uniform, or any other distribution with finite variance.

Derivation: The Normalization Constant (Gaussian Integral)

We need to verify that $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2}\,dx = \sqrt{\pi}$. This is one of the most beautiful tricks in mathematics, using a conversion to polar coordinates.

The Gaussian Integral via Polar Coordinates
1
Let $I = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2}\,dx$. We want to find $I$. The trick: compute $I^2$ instead.
2
$$I^2 = \left(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2}\,dx\right)\left(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-y^2}\,dy\right) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-(x^2 + y^2)}\,dx\,dy$$
3
Switch to polar coordinates: $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$, $dx\,dy = r\,dr\,d\theta$, and $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$: $$I^2 = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\infty} e^{-r^2}\,r\,dr\,d\theta$$
4
The $\theta$ integral gives $2\pi$. For the $r$ integral, substitute $u = r^2$, $du = 2r\,dr$: $$\int_0^{\infty} r\,e^{-r^2}\,dr = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{\infty} e^{-u}\,du = \frac{1}{2}$$
5
Therefore $I^2 = 2\pi \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \pi$, so $\boxed{I = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2}\,dx = \sqrt{\pi}}$.

With this result, we can verify normalization. Substituting $u = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma\sqrt{2}}$ into $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}\,dx$ yields $\frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-u^2}\,du = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}} \cdot \sqrt{\pi} = 1$.

Mean and Variance

Mean of the Normal Distribution
1
$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x \cdot \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}\,dx$. Substitute $t = x - \mu$:
2
$= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} (t + \mu) \cdot \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{t^2}{2\sigma^2}}\,dt = \underbrace{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} t \cdot \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{t^2}{2\sigma^2}}\,dt}_{= 0 \text{ (odd function)}} + \mu \underbrace{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{t^2}{2\sigma^2}}\,dt}_{= 1}$
3
Therefore $\mathbb{E}[X] = \mu$. The variance derivation (using integration by parts) gives $\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2$.

Exponential Distribution

The Exponential distribution models waiting times—the time between events in a Poisson process. If events occur at an average rate of $\lambda$ per unit time (e.g., 3 customers per hour, 5 server requests per second), then the time until the next event follows an Exponential distribution.

Exponential Distribution $\text{Exp}(\lambda)$

$$f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x \ge 0$$

CDF: $F(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}$.   Mean: $\mathbb{E}[X] = 1/\lambda$.   Variance: $\text{Var}(X) = 1/\lambda^2$.

Connection to the Poisson Process

If the count of events in a time interval of length $t$ follows $\text{Poisson}(\lambda t)$, then the time between consecutive events follows $\text{Exp}(\lambda)$. To see why: the probability that the first event occurs after time $t$ equals the probability of zero events in $[0, t]$:

$$P(X > t) = P(\text{0 events in } [0,t]) = e^{-\lambda t}$$

Therefore $F(t) = P(X \le t) = 1 - e^{-\lambda t}$, and differentiating gives $f(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}$.

The Memoryless Property (Proof)

The Exponential distribution is the only continuous distribution that is memoryless: the probability of waiting an additional time $s$ is independent of how long you have already waited.

Proof: Memoryless Property
1
We want to show: $P(X > s + t \mid X > t) = P(X > s)$ for all $s, t \ge 0$.
2
By definition of conditional probability: $$P(X > s + t \mid X > t) = \frac{P(X > s + t \text{ and } X > t)}{P(X > t)} = \frac{P(X > s + t)}{P(X > t)}$$
3
Since $P(X > x) = e^{-\lambda x}$ for the Exponential: $$= \frac{e^{-\lambda(s+t)}}{e^{-\lambda t}} = \frac{e^{-\lambda s}\,e^{-\lambda t}}{e^{-\lambda t}} = e^{-\lambda s} = P(X > s) \quad \blacksquare$$
Physical Interpretation

A radioactive atom that has survived for 1000 years has the same probability of decaying in the next second as a freshly created atom. The system has no "memory" of how long it has been waiting. This property is what makes Poisson processes truly random—the events do not cluster or avoid each other based on past history.

Gamma Distribution

While the Exponential describes the time to the first event, the Gamma distribution describes the time to the $\alpha$-th event. It generalizes the Exponential and provides a flexible family of shapes for modeling positive-valued data.

Gamma Distribution $\text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$

$$f(x) = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)}\, x^{\alpha - 1}\, e^{-\beta x}, \quad x > 0$$

Parameters: shape $\alpha > 0$, rate $\beta > 0$.   Mean: $\alpha/\beta$.   Variance: $\alpha/\beta^2$.

The Gamma Function

The normalization constant involves the Gamma function, one of the most important special functions in mathematics:

$$\Gamma(\alpha) = \int_0^{\infty} t^{\alpha - 1}\,e^{-t}\,dt$$

Key properties:

  • Recursion: $\Gamma(\alpha + 1) = \alpha\,\Gamma(\alpha)$ (proved by integration by parts)
  • Factorial connection: For positive integers, $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!$
  • Half-integer: $\Gamma(1/2) = \sqrt{\pi}$ (this follows directly from the Gaussian integral!)

Exponential as a Special Case

Setting $\alpha = 1$ in the Gamma PDF:

$$f(x) = \frac{\beta^1}{\Gamma(1)}\, x^{0}\, e^{-\beta x} = \beta\, e^{-\beta x}$$

This is exactly $\text{Exp}(\beta)$. The Exponential is a Gamma with shape 1.

Sum of Exponentials

If $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_\alpha$ are independent $\text{Exp}(\beta)$ random variables (for integer $\alpha$), then their sum follows $\text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$. This is the waiting time interpretation: the total time for $\alpha$ events in a Poisson process with rate $\beta$.

Parameterization Warning

Different sources use different parameterizations. Some use rate $\beta$ (as we do: $f \propto e^{-\beta x}$), others use scale $\theta = 1/\beta$ (writing $f \propto e^{-x/\theta}$). Always check which convention a textbook or library uses. Python's scipy.stats.gamma uses the scale parameterization by default.

Beta Distribution

The Beta distribution is defined on the interval $[0, 1]$, making it a natural choice for modeling probabilities, proportions, and rates. In Bayesian inference, it serves as the conjugate prior for the Binomial likelihood parameter.

Beta Distribution $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$

$$f(\theta) = \frac{\theta^{\alpha - 1}(1 - \theta)^{\beta - 1}}{B(\alpha, \beta)}, \quad \theta \in [0, 1]$$

where the Beta function is $B(\alpha, \beta) = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha)\,\Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha + \beta)}$.

Mean: $\frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}$.   Variance: $\frac{\alpha\beta}{(\alpha + \beta)^2(\alpha + \beta + 1)}$.

Shape Gallery

The Beta distribution is remarkably flexible. Different choices of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ produce very different shapes:

The Beta distribution takes many shapes depending on $\alpha$ and $\beta$: uniform, left-skewed, right-skewed, symmetric peaked, or U-shaped.

Connection to Bayesian Inference

Suppose you are estimating the probability $\theta$ that a coin lands heads. In the Bayesian framework:

  • Prior: $\theta \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ — your belief about $\theta$ before seeing data
  • Likelihood: $k$ heads in $n$ flips: $\text{Binomial}(n, \theta)$
  • Posterior: $\theta \mid \text{data} \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + k, \; \beta + n - k)$

The posterior is still a Beta distribution—this is conjugacy. The parameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$ can be interpreted as "pseudo-counts": $\alpha - 1$ prior heads and $\beta - 1$ prior tails. Each observed head increments $\alpha$; each observed tail increments $\beta$. Starting from $\text{Beta}(1, 1)$ (uniform, no prior information), your belief is shaped entirely by the data.

Relationships Between Distributions

The continuous distributions are not isolated objects. They form a rich web of connections through limiting cases, transformations, sums, and conjugacy. Understanding these relationships provides deep structural insight.

Relationships between distributions: solid arrows denote exact connections; dashed arrows denote limiting cases and special-case relationships.

Summary of Key Relationships

  • Bernoulli $\xrightarrow{\text{sum}}$ Binomial: $n$ independent Bernoulli trials give a Binomial count.
  • Binomial $\xrightarrow{n\to\infty}$ Poisson: When $n$ is large and $p$ is small with $np = \lambda$ fixed.
  • Binomial $\xrightarrow{\text{CLT}}$ Normal: For large $n$, the Binomial is approximately Normal.
  • Poisson $\xrightarrow{\text{inter-arrival}}$ Exponential: Time between Poisson events is Exponential.
  • Exponential $\xrightarrow{\text{sum}}$ Gamma: Sum of $\alpha$ Exponentials is Gamma.
  • Gamma $\xrightarrow{\alpha\to\infty}$ Normal: By the CLT (sum of many Exponentials).
  • Beta $\xrightarrow{\text{conjugate}}$ Binomial: Beta is the conjugate prior for the Binomial parameter.
  • Uniform $=$ Beta$(1,1)$: The Uniform on $[0,1]$ is a special case of the Beta.
Why Relationships Matter for ML

These connections are not just mathematical curiosities—they have practical consequences. The Poisson-Exponential connection is why event-driven models (click rates, decay processes) pair naturally. The Beta-Binomial conjugacy is why Thompson sampling works for multi-armed bandits. The CLT explains why batch normalization helps: the sum of activations tends toward Gaussian regardless of the activation function. Understanding the web of distributions gives you the ability to choose the right model for the right problem.

Continuous Distributions in Python

We use scipy.stats to explore each distribution: computing PDFs, CDFs, sampling, QQ plots, and maximum likelihood estimation. All code runs in a standard Python environment with numpy, scipy, and matplotlib.

1. Uniform Distribution

The scipy.stats.uniform distribution is parameterized by loc (start) and scale (width), so $\text{Unif}(a,b)$ is uniform(loc=a, scale=b-a).

python 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats

# --- Uniform Distribution ---
a, b = 2, 7
dist = stats.uniform(loc=a, scale=b - a)

x = np.linspace(0, 9, 500)
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(12, 4))

# PDF
axes[0].plot(x, dist.pdf(x), 'b-', lw=2)
axes[0].fill_between(x, dist.pdf(x), alpha=0.2)
axes[0].set_title(f'Uniform({a}, {b}) PDF')
axes[0].set_xlabel('x')
axes[0].set_ylabel('f(x)')

# CDF
axes[1].plot(x, dist.cdf(x), 'r-', lw=2)
axes[1].set_title(f'Uniform({a}, {b}) CDF')
axes[1].set_xlabel('x')
axes[1].set_ylabel('F(x)')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

# Verify mean and variance
print(f"Mean:     {dist.mean():.4f}  (expected {(a+b)/2})")
print(f"Variance: {dist.var():.4f}  (expected {(b-a)**2/12:.4f})")

# --- Normal Distribution ---
mu, sigma = 5, 1.5
dist = stats.norm(loc=mu, scale=sigma)

x = np.linspace(mu - 4*sigma, mu + 4*sigma, 500)
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(16, 4))

# PDF with shaded regions
ax = axes[0]
ax.plot(x, dist.pdf(x), 'b-', lw=2)

    x_fill = np.linspace(mu - k*sigma, mu + k*sigma, 300)
    ax.fill_between(x_fill, dist.pdf(x_fill), alpha=alpha, color=color,
                     label=f'{k}$\\sigma$: {dist.cdf(mu+k*sigma) - dist.cdf(mu-k*sigma):.2%}')
ax.legend(fontsize=9)
ax.set_title(f'Normal($\\mu$={mu}, $\\sigma$={sigma}) PDF')

# Sampling histogram vs PDF

axes[1].set_title('10,000 Samples vs True PDF')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

3. QQ Plot: Testing for Normality

A quantile-quantile (QQ) plot compares the quantiles of your data against the quantiles of a theoretical distribution. If the points lie on a straight line, the data matches the distribution.

python 
# --- QQ Plot ---
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 4))

# Normal data -> should be linear
normal_data = np.random.normal(0, 1, 1000)
stats.probplot(normal_data, dist="norm", plot=axes[0])
axes[0].set_title('QQ Plot: Normal Data')

# Exponential data -> should curve (heavy right tail)
exp_data = np.random.exponential(1, 1000)
stats.probplot(exp_data, dist="norm", plot=axes[1])
axes[1].set_title('QQ Plot: Exponential Data')

# Uniform data -> should have S-shape (light tails)
uniform_data = np.random.uniform(0, 1, 1000)
stats.probplot(uniform_data, dist="norm", plot=axes[2])
axes[2].set_title('QQ Plot: Uniform Data')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

lambdas = [0.5, 1.0, 2.0]

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(12, 4))

for lam in lambdas:

    axes[0].plot(x, dist.pdf(x), lw=2, label=f'$\\lambda$ = {lam}')
    axes[1].plot(x, dist.cdf(x), lw=2, label=f'$\\lambda$ = {lam}')

axes[0].set_title('Exponential PDF')
axes[0].legend()
axes[0].set_xlabel('x')

axes[1].set_title('Exponential CDF')
axes[1].legend()
axes[1].set_xlabel('x')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

# Verify memoryless property empirically
lam = 2.0
samples = stats.expon(scale=1/lam).rvs(size=100000, random_state=42)
t = 1.0  # already waited 1 unit

# P(X > 1.5 | X > 1) should equal P(X > 0.5)
p_conditional = np.mean(samples[samples > t] > t + 0.5)
p_marginal = np.mean(samples > 0.5)
print(f"P(X > 1.5 | X > 1.0) = {p_conditional:.4f}")
print(f"P(X > 0.5)           = {p_marginal:.4f}")
print(f"Memoryless property verified: {abs(p_conditional - p_marginal) < 0.02}")

6. Beta Distribution and Bayesian Updating

python 
# --- Beta Distribution ---
params = [(0.5, 0.5), (1, 1), (2, 5), (5, 2), (5, 5), (10, 10)]
x = np.linspace(0.001, 0.999, 500)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 5))
for a, b in params:
    dist = stats.beta(a, b)
    ax.plot(x, dist.pdf(x), lw=2, label=f'Beta({a}, {b})')

ax.set_title('Beta Distribution PDF')
ax.set_xlabel('$\\theta$')
ax.set_ylabel('$f(\\theta)$')
ax.legend()
ax.set_ylim(0, 4)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

# --- Bayesian Updating: Beta-Binomial Conjugacy ---
# Coin flip: true probability of heads = 0.7
true_p = 0.7
np.random.seed(42)
flips = np.random.binomial(1, true_p, size=100)  # 100 flips

# Start with uniform prior Beta(1,1)
alpha_prior, beta_prior = 1, 1

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 5))
theta = np.linspace(0, 1, 500)

# Plot prior
ax.plot(theta, stats.beta(alpha_prior, beta_prior).pdf(theta),
        'k--', lw=1.5, label='Prior: Beta(1, 1)')

# Update after n = 1, 5, 20, 50, 100 observations
for n in [1, 5, 20, 50, 100]:
    k = flips[:n].sum()
    alpha_post = alpha_prior + k
    beta_post = beta_prior + n - k
    ax.plot(theta, stats.beta(alpha_post, beta_post).pdf(theta),
            lw=2, label=f'n={n}: Beta({alpha_post}, {beta_post})')

ax.axvline(true_p, color='red', ls=':', lw=2, label=f'True p = {true_p}')
ax.set_title('Bayesian Updating: Beta-Binomial Conjugacy')
ax.set_xlabel('$\\theta$ (probability of heads)')
ax.set_ylabel('Posterior density')
ax.legend(fontsize=9)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

7. Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Given data, we can fit distribution parameters using maximum likelihood estimation (MLE). scipy.stats provides the .fit() method for this purpose.

python 
# --- Maximum Likelihood Estimation ---
np.random.seed(42)

# Generate data from known distributions
normal_data = np.random.normal(loc=3.0, scale=2.0, size=500)
exp_data = np.random.exponential(scale=2.5, size=500)   # mean = 2.5
gamma_data = np.random.gamma(shape=3.0, scale=2.0, size=500)

# Fit using MLE
mu_hat, sigma_hat = stats.norm.fit(normal_data)
print(f"Normal MLE:  mu_hat = {mu_hat:.3f} (true 3.0), sigma_hat = {sigma_hat:.3f} (true 2.0)")

_, scale_hat = stats.expon.fit(exp_data, floc=0)  # fix location at 0
print(f"Exponential MLE:  scale_hat = {scale_hat:.3f} (true 2.5)")

alpha_hat, _, scale_hat = stats.gamma.fit(gamma_data, floc=0)
print(f"Gamma MLE:  alpha_hat = {alpha_hat:.3f} (true 3.0), scale_hat = {scale_hat:.3f} (true 2.0)")

# Overlay fitted PDF on histogram
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 4))

for ax, data, name, fit_dist, params in [
    (axes[0], normal_data, 'Normal', stats.norm, stats.norm.fit(normal_data)),
    (axes[1], exp_data, 'Exponential', stats.expon, stats.expon.fit(exp_data, floc=0)),
    (axes[2], gamma_data, 'Gamma', stats.gamma, stats.gamma.fit(gamma_data, floc=0)),
]:
    ax.hist(data, bins=40, density=True, alpha=0.5, color='steelblue', edgecolor='white')
    x_plot = np.linspace(data.min(), data.max(), 300)
    ax.plot(x_plot, fit_dist.pdf(x_plot, *params), 'r-', lw=2, label='Fitted PDF')
    ax.set_title(f'{name}: MLE Fit')

Easy

1. Normal Distribution Probabilities Using Z-Scores ↓

The heights of adult men in a population are normally distributed with mean $\mu = 175$ cm and standard deviation $\sigma = 7$ cm.

  1. What proportion of men are taller than 189 cm?
  2. What proportion of men have heights between 168 cm and 182 cm?
  3. What height corresponds to the 95th percentile?

a) Convert to a Z-score: $Z = \frac{189 - 175}{7} = 2$.

$P(X > 189) = P(Z > 2) = 1 - \Phi(2) \approx 1 - 0.9772 = 0.0228$.

About 2.28% of men are taller than 189 cm.

b) $Z_1 = \frac{168 - 175}{7} = -1$, $Z_2 = \frac{182 - 175}{7} = 1$.

$P(168 \le X \le 182) = \Phi(1) - \Phi(-1) \approx 0.8413 - 0.1587 = 0.6827$.

About 68.27%—exactly one standard deviation from the mean.

c) We need $x$ such that $\Phi\!\left(\frac{x - 175}{7}\right) = 0.95$.

From the standard Normal table: $z_{0.95} \approx 1.645$.

$x = 175 + 1.645 \times 7 \approx \mathbf{186.5}$ cm.

2. Exponential Distribution: Waiting Times ↓

Customers arrive at a coffee shop according to a Poisson process with an average rate of $\lambda = 4$ customers per hour.

  1. What is the probability that the next customer arrives within 10 minutes?
  2. What is the expected waiting time for the next customer?
  3. If no customer has arrived in the last 15 minutes, what is the probability that one arrives within the next 5 minutes? Explain using the memoryless property.

The inter-arrival time $T \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda = 4)$ (in hours).

a) 10 minutes $= 1/6$ hour.

$P(T \le 1/6) = 1 - e^{-4 \cdot 1/6} = 1 - e^{-2/3} \approx 1 - 0.5134 = \mathbf{0.4866}$.

b) $\mathbb{E}[T] = 1/\lambda = 1/4$ hour $= \mathbf{15}$ minutes.

c) By the memoryless property, $P(T > 15 + 5 \mid T > 15) = P(T > 5)$.

Converting to hours: $P(T > 1/12) = e^{-4/12} = e^{-1/3} \approx 0.7165$.

So $P(\text{arrives within next 5 min} \mid \text{waited 15 min}) = 1 - e^{-1/3} \approx \mathbf{0.2835}$.

This is the same as the probability of arrival within 5 minutes for a fresh start. The 15 minutes already waited provide no information about the future—the process has no memory.

Medium

3. Derive the Mean of the Gamma Distribution ↓

Starting from the definition $\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_0^{\infty} x \cdot f(x)\,dx$ where $f(x) = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} x^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta x}$, prove that $\mathbb{E}[X] = \alpha / \beta$.

Hint: Use the property $\Gamma(\alpha + 1) = \alpha\,\Gamma(\alpha)$.

Step 1: Write out the expectation:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_0^{\infty} x \cdot \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} x^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta x}\,dx = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} \int_0^{\infty} x^{\alpha} e^{-\beta x}\,dx$$

Step 2: Evaluate the integral by substituting $u = \beta x$, $du = \beta\,dx$:

$$\int_0^{\infty} x^{\alpha} e^{-\beta x}\,dx = \int_0^{\infty} \left(\frac{u}{\beta}\right)^{\alpha} e^{-u} \frac{du}{\beta} = \frac{1}{\beta^{\alpha+1}} \int_0^{\infty} u^{\alpha} e^{-u}\,du = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha + 1)}{\beta^{\alpha+1}}$$

Step 3: Substitute back:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} \cdot \frac{\Gamma(\alpha + 1)}{\beta^{\alpha+1}} = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha + 1)}{\beta\,\Gamma(\alpha)}$$

Step 4: Apply the recursion $\Gamma(\alpha + 1) = \alpha\,\Gamma(\alpha)$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{\alpha\,\Gamma(\alpha)}{\beta\,\Gamma(\alpha)} = \boxed{\frac{\alpha}{\beta}}$$

4. Show the Exponential Distribution Is Memoryless ↓

Let $X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$.

  1. Prove that $P(X > s + t \mid X > t) = P(X > s)$ for all $s, t \ge 0$.
  2. Show the converse: if $X$ is a continuous non-negative random variable with the memoryless property, then $X$ must be exponentially distributed.
    Hint: Let $g(t) = P(X > t)$. The memoryless property gives a functional equation for $g$. Find $g$ and derive the PDF.

a) This was proved in the Theory tab. For completeness:

$$P(X > s+t \mid X > t) = \frac{P(X > s+t)}{P(X > t)} = \frac{e^{-\lambda(s+t)}}{e^{-\lambda t}} = e^{-\lambda s} = P(X > s) \quad \blacksquare$$

b) Converse: Define $g(t) = P(X > t) = 1 - F(t)$, the survival function.

The memoryless property says $P(X > s+t \mid X > t) = P(X > s)$, which is:

$$\frac{g(s+t)}{g(t)} = g(s) \quad \Longrightarrow \quad g(s+t) = g(s)\,g(t)$$

This is Cauchy's functional equation for continuous positive functions with $g(0) = 1$.

Assuming $g$ is continuous (which follows from $F$ being a CDF), the only solutions are $g(t) = e^{ct}$ for some constant $c$.

Since $g(t) = P(X > t)$ must decrease to 0 as $t \to \infty$, we need $c < 0$. Writing $c = -\lambda$ with $\lambda > 0$:

$$g(t) = e^{-\lambda t} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad F(t) = 1 - e^{-\lambda t} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad f(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}$$

This is the Exponential PDF. Therefore, $X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$. $\blacksquare$

Hard

5. Derive the Gaussian Integral ↓

Prove that $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2}\,dx = \sqrt{\pi}$ using the technique of squaring the integral and converting to polar coordinates.

Then, as a corollary, show that the general Gaussian integral evaluates to:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-ax^2}\,dx = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}} \quad \text{for } a > 0$$

Finally, use this to verify that the Normal PDF $f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$ integrates to 1.

Part 1: The basic Gaussian integral.

Let $I = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2}\,dx$. Consider $I^2$:

$$I^2 = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2}\,dx \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-y^2}\,dy = \iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} e^{-(x^2+y^2)}\,dx\,dy$$

Convert to polar coordinates $(r, \theta)$ with $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$ and $dx\,dy = r\,dr\,d\theta$:

$$I^2 = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\infty} e^{-r^2}\,r\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi \int_0^{\infty} r\,e^{-r^2}\,dr$$

Substitute $u = r^2$, $du = 2r\,dr$:

$$\int_0^{\infty} r\,e^{-r^2}\,dr = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{\infty} e^{-u}\,du = \frac{1}{2}\left[-e^{-u}\right]_0^{\infty} = \frac{1}{2}(0 - (-1)) = \frac{1}{2}$$

Therefore $I^2 = 2\pi \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \pi$. Since $I > 0$:

$$\boxed{I = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2}\,dx = \sqrt{\pi}}$$

Part 2: General Gaussian integral.

For $a > 0$, substitute $u = \sqrt{a}\,x$, $du = \sqrt{a}\,dx$:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-ax^2}\,dx = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-u^2}\frac{du}{\sqrt{a}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{a}} \cdot \sqrt{\pi} = \boxed{\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}}}$$

Part 3: Verify Normal PDF normalization.

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}\,dx$$

Substitute $t = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}$, $dt = dx / \sigma$:

$$= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-t^2/2}\,dt$$

Apply the general Gaussian integral with $a = 1/2$:

$$= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{1/2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \cdot \sqrt{2\pi} = \boxed{1}$$

The Normal PDF is properly normalized. $\blacksquare$
