04. The Exponential Family

Normal, Bernoulli, Poisson, Gamma, Beta -- they look different, but they share a deep algebraic structure. The exponential family captures this structure in a single formula, unifying parameter estimation, sufficient statistics, and the foundations of generalized linear models.

Why a Unifying Framework?

Throughout probability and statistics, we encounter a zoo of distributions. The Bernoulli governs coin flips. The Normal describes measurement noise. The Poisson counts rare events. The Gamma models waiting times. The Beta parametrizes probabilities.

Each has its own formula, its own parametrization, its own set of tricks. Learning them one at a time is tedious and obscures a remarkable fact: most of these distributions are instances of a single family, differing only in their choice of a few building blocks.

The exponential family is the framework that reveals this hidden unity. Once you see the common structure, a cascade of powerful results follows automatically:

  • Sufficient statistics -- all the information in an entire dataset can be compressed into a fixed-dimensional summary, regardless of the sample size.
  • Maximum likelihood -- the MLE reduces to a simple moment-matching equation for every distribution in the family.
  • Conjugate priors -- Bayesian updating becomes a mechanical operation: just add pseudo-observations to the prior.
  • Convexity -- the log-likelihood is always concave, so optimization is tractable.
  • GLMs -- logistic regression, Poisson regression, and linear regression are all instances of one algorithm.
What is NOT in the exponential family?

Not every distribution belongs. The Cauchy distribution (no finite moments), the uniform distribution on $[0, \theta]$ (support depends on the parameter), and mixture models generally do not belong. The key requirement is that the support of the distribution must not depend on the parameters, and the density must factor into the specific $h(x)\exp(\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top \mathbf{T}(x) - A(\boldsymbol{\eta}))$ form.

The General Form

A distribution belongs to the exponential family if its probability density (or mass) function can be written in the following canonical form:

Definition: Exponential Family (Canonical Form)

A parametric family of distributions is an exponential family if its density can be expressed as:

$$p(x \mid \boldsymbol{\eta}) \;=\; h(x)\,\exp\!\Big(\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top \mathbf{T}(x) \;-\; A(\boldsymbol{\eta})\Big)$$

where:

  • $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ -- Natural (canonical) parameters. The "native" parametrization of the family. Often a nonlinear transformation of the familiar parameters (e.g., $\eta = \log\frac{p}{1-p}$ for Bernoulli).
  • $\mathbf{T}(x)$ -- Sufficient statistic. A function of the data that captures all relevant information about $\boldsymbol{\eta}$. Everything the data can tell us about the parameters flows through $\mathbf{T}(x)$.
  • $A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$ -- Log-partition function (also called the cumulant generating function). This normalizing term ensures the distribution integrates to 1. It is the single most powerful object in the theory.
  • $h(x)$ -- Base measure. A non-negative function of $x$ alone (independent of the parameters). It provides the "reference" measure against which the exponential tilt operates.

An equivalent way to write this uses the exponential of a log-normalizer:

$$p(x \mid \boldsymbol{\eta}) = \frac{h(x)\,\exp\!\bigl(\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top \mathbf{T}(x)\bigr)}{Z(\boldsymbol{\eta})} \qquad \text{where } A(\boldsymbol{\eta}) = \log Z(\boldsymbol{\eta})$$

The partition function $Z(\boldsymbol{\eta}) = \int h(x)\exp(\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top \mathbf{T}(x))\,dx$ (or the corresponding sum for discrete distributions) is precisely what ensures normalization. Taking its logarithm gives $A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$.

Why "natural" parameters?

The standard parameters we use daily ($\mu, \sigma^2$ for the Normal; $p$ for Bernoulli; $\lambda$ for Poisson) are called mean parameters or moment parameters. The natural parameters $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ are the ones that make the exponential family form clean. The mapping between them is often nonlinear and is given by $E[\mathbf{T}(x)] = \nabla A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$, which we prove below.

Worked Examples

The best way to internalize the exponential family is to convert familiar distributions into canonical form. In each case, the strategy is the same: take the standard PDF/PMF, apply logarithms, and rearrange into the $\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top \mathbf{T}(x) - A(\boldsymbol{\eta}) + \log h(x)$ form.

Example 1: Bernoulli Distribution

The Bernoulli distribution with parameter $p \in (0,1)$ has PMF:

$$P(x \mid p) = p^x (1-p)^{1-x}, \qquad x \in \{0, 1\}$$
Converting Bernoulli to Exponential Family Form
1
Take the logarithm of the PMF: $$\log P(x \mid p) = x \log p + (1-x) \log(1-p) = x \log\frac{p}{1-p} + \log(1-p)$$
2
Identify the components. Define the natural parameter: $$\eta = \log\frac{p}{1-p} \quad \text{(the log-odds, or "logit")}$$ Inverting: $p = \sigma(\eta) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-\eta}}$ (the sigmoid function!).
3
Express $\log(1-p)$ in terms of $\eta$: $$1-p = \frac{e^{-\eta}}{1+e^{-\eta}} = \frac{1}{1+e^{\eta}}, \quad \text{so } \log(1-p) = -\log(1+e^{\eta})$$
4
Final exponential family form: $$P(x \mid \eta) = \exp\!\Big(\eta \cdot x - \log(1+e^{\eta})\Big)$$

So: $\mathbf{T}(x) = x$, $A(\eta) = \log(1+e^{\eta})$, and $h(x) = 1$.

The logistic connection

The natural parameter of the Bernoulli is exactly the logit. Logistic regression models $\eta = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}$, predicting the natural parameter of a Bernoulli directly. This is no accident -- it is the defining property of a generalized linear model.

Example 2: Normal Distribution (known variance)

The Normal distribution $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with known variance $\sigma^2$ has PDF:

$$p(x \mid \mu) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$
Converting Normal to Exponential Family Form
1
Expand the quadratic in the exponent: $$-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} = -\frac{x^2}{2\sigma^2} + \frac{\mu x}{\sigma^2} - \frac{\mu^2}{2\sigma^2}$$
2
Rewrite the full density grouping terms by what depends on what: $$p(x|\mu) = \underbrace{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{x^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)}_{h(x)} \cdot \exp\!\left(\underbrace{\frac{\mu}{\sigma^2}}_{\eta} \cdot \underbrace{x}_{T(x)} - \underbrace{\frac{\mu^2}{2\sigma^2}}_{A(\eta)}\right)$$
3
Read off the components:
  • $\eta = \mu / \sigma^2$ (natural parameter)
  • $T(x) = x$ (sufficient statistic)
  • $A(\eta) = \frac{\sigma^2 \eta^2}{2} = \frac{\mu^2}{2\sigma^2}$
  • $h(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{x^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$

For unknown variance, both $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ are parameters. Then we get a two-dimensional exponential family with $\boldsymbol{\eta} = \bigl(\frac{\mu}{\sigma^2},\; -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\bigr)$ and $\mathbf{T}(x) = (x,\; x^2)$. The sufficient statistics are the sum and the sum of squares -- exactly what you need to compute the sample mean and sample variance.

Example 3: Poisson Distribution

The Poisson distribution with rate $\lambda > 0$ has PMF:

$$P(x \mid \lambda) = \frac{\lambda^x e^{-\lambda}}{x!}, \qquad x \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$$
Converting Poisson to Exponential Family Form
1
Take the logarithm: $$\log P(x|\lambda) = x\log\lambda - \lambda - \log(x!)$$
2
Define $\eta = \log\lambda$ (so $\lambda = e^\eta$), and exponentiate: $$P(x|\eta) = \frac{1}{x!}\,\exp\!\bigl(\eta \cdot x - e^{\eta}\bigr)$$
3
Read off: $T(x) = x$, $A(\eta) = e^{\eta} = \lambda$, $h(x) = 1/x!$.

Example 4: Gamma Distribution

The Gamma distribution $\text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$ with known shape $\alpha$ and rate parameter $\beta$ has PDF:

$$p(x \mid \beta) = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)}\,x^{\alpha-1}\,e^{-\beta x}, \qquad x > 0$$
Converting Gamma to Exponential Family Form
1
Take the logarithm: $$\log p(x|\beta) = \alpha\log\beta - \log\Gamma(\alpha) + (\alpha-1)\log x - \beta x$$
2
Set $\eta = -\beta$ (so $\beta = -\eta$, and we need $\eta < 0$). Rearrange: $$p(x|\eta) = \underbrace{\frac{x^{\alpha - 1}}{\Gamma(\alpha)}}_{h(x)} \exp\!\Big(\underbrace{\eta}_{} \cdot \underbrace{x}_{T(x)} \;-\; \underbrace{(-\alpha\log(-\eta))}_{A(\eta)}\Big)$$
3
Summary: $T(x) = x$, $\eta = -\beta$, $A(\eta) = -\alpha\log(-\eta) = \alpha\log\beta$, $h(x) = x^{\alpha-1}/\Gamma(\alpha)$.

Summary Table

Distribution $\eta$ (natural param) $T(x)$ (suff. stat.) $A(\eta)$ (log-partition) $h(x)$ (base measure)
Bernoulli($p$) $\log\frac{p}{1-p}$ $x$ $\log(1+e^\eta)$ $1$
Normal($\mu, \sigma^2$) $\mu/\sigma^2$ $x$ $\sigma^2\eta^2/2$ $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}e^{-x^2/2\sigma^2}$
Poisson($\lambda$) $\log\lambda$ $x$ $e^\eta$ $1/x!$
Gamma($\alpha, \beta$) $-\beta$ $x$ $-\alpha\log(-\eta)$ $x^{\alpha-1}/\Gamma(\alpha)$

Notice a pattern: for all four distributions, the sufficient statistic $T(x)$ is simply $x$ (or simple functions of $x$). This is characteristic of natural exponential families, the most important subclass.

Properties of the Log-Partition Function

The log-partition function $A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$ is the most powerful object in the exponential family. It generates all the moments of the sufficient statistics through differentiation. This single function encodes the mean, variance, and all higher cumulants.

Moment-Generating Properties of $A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$

First derivative gives the mean:

$$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\eta}} A(\boldsymbol{\eta}) = E\!\left[\mathbf{T}(x)\right]$$

Second derivative gives the covariance:

$$\nabla^2_{\boldsymbol{\eta}} A(\boldsymbol{\eta}) = \text{Cov}\!\left[\mathbf{T}(x)\right]$$

Since a covariance matrix is always positive semi-definite, this immediately proves that $A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$ is convex.

Proof: First Derivative of $A$
1
Start from the normalization condition. Since $p(x|\boldsymbol{\eta})$ integrates to 1: $$\int h(x)\exp\!\bigl(\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top \mathbf{T}(x) - A(\boldsymbol{\eta})\bigr)\,dx = 1$$
2
Differentiate both sides with respect to $\boldsymbol{\eta}$. The right side gives 0. On the left, we can exchange differentiation and integration (under regularity conditions): $$\int h(x)\exp\!\bigl(\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top \mathbf{T}(x) - A(\boldsymbol{\eta})\bigr) \cdot \bigl(\mathbf{T}(x) - \nabla A(\boldsymbol{\eta})\bigr)\,dx = 0$$
3
Recognize the expectation: The integrand is $p(x|\boldsymbol{\eta}) \cdot (\mathbf{T}(x) - \nabla A)$, so: $$E\!\left[\mathbf{T}(x)\right] - \nabla A(\boldsymbol{\eta}) = 0 \quad \implies \quad \boxed{\nabla A(\boldsymbol{\eta}) = E\!\left[\mathbf{T}(x)\right]}$$
Proof: Second Derivative of $A$
1
Differentiate again. From $E[\mathbf{T}(x)] = \nabla A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$, differentiate with respect to $\boldsymbol{\eta}$: $$\frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{\eta}} E[\mathbf{T}(x)] = \nabla^2 A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$$
2
Expand the left side by differentiating under the integral sign. After carrying through the calculus (analogous to the first derivative), we get: $$\frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{\eta}} E[\mathbf{T}(x)] = E\!\left[\mathbf{T}(x)\mathbf{T}(x)^\top\right] - E\!\left[\mathbf{T}(x)\right]E\!\left[\mathbf{T}(x)\right]^\top$$
3
This is exactly the covariance: $$\boxed{\nabla^2 A(\boldsymbol{\eta}) = \text{Cov}[\mathbf{T}(x)]}$$

Since covariance matrices are positive semi-definite (PSD), the Hessian of $A$ is PSD everywhere. A function whose Hessian is PSD everywhere is convex. This means $A$ is always convex -- and therefore the log-likelihood of any exponential family is always concave.

Verification: Bernoulli

For the Bernoulli, $A(\eta) = \log(1+e^\eta)$. Let us verify the moment-generating property:

$$A'(\eta) = \frac{e^\eta}{1+e^\eta} = \sigma(\eta) = p$$

Indeed, $E[T(x)] = E[x] = p$. For the second derivative:

$$A''(\eta) = \frac{e^\eta}{(1+e^\eta)^2} = p(1-p) = \text{Var}(x)$$

The variance of a Bernoulli is $p(1-p)$, exactly what the second derivative gives. And since $p(1-p) > 0$ for all $p \in (0,1)$, the convexity is confirmed.

Verification: Poisson

For the Poisson, $A(\eta) = e^\eta = \lambda$:

$$A'(\eta) = e^\eta = \lambda = E[x], \qquad A''(\eta) = e^\eta = \lambda = \text{Var}(x)$$

Both checks pass. For the Poisson distribution, the mean equals the variance -- and indeed the first and second derivatives of $A$ are identical.

Sufficient Statistics

The concept of sufficiency is one of the deepest ideas in statistics. A statistic $\mathbf{T}(x_1, \ldots, x_N)$ is sufficient for $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ if it captures everything the data can tell us about the parameter. Formally: the conditional distribution of the data given $\mathbf{T}$ does not depend on $\boldsymbol{\eta}$.

Neyman-Fisher Factorization Theorem

A statistic $\mathbf{T}(x)$ is sufficient for parameter $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ if and only if the joint density of the data can be factored as: $$p(x_1, \ldots, x_N \mid \boldsymbol{\eta}) = g\!\bigl(\mathbf{T}(x_1, \ldots, x_N),\; \boldsymbol{\eta}\bigr) \;\cdot\; r(x_1, \ldots, x_N)$$ where $g$ depends on the data only through $\mathbf{T}$, and $r$ does not depend on $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ at all.

For exponential family distributions, sufficiency is immediate. Given $N$ i.i.d. observations $x_1, \ldots, x_N$, the joint density is:

$$p(x_1, \ldots, x_N \mid \boldsymbol{\eta}) = \prod_{i=1}^N h(x_i) \;\cdot\; \exp\!\left(\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top \sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{T}(x_i) - N \cdot A(\boldsymbol{\eta})\right)$$

This immediately gives us the Neyman-Fisher factorization with $\mathbf{T}_N = \sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{T}(x_i)$ as the sufficient statistic. The crucial insight:

Data Compression Without Information Loss

No matter how large $N$ is -- whether you have 100 data points or 100 million -- the entire dataset can be reduced to the fixed-dimensional summary $\sum_i \mathbf{T}(x_i)$ without losing any information about $\boldsymbol{\eta}$. For the Normal distribution with both parameters unknown, this means you only need $\sum x_i$ and $\sum x_i^2$ -- two numbers, regardless of sample size.

Concrete examples of sufficient statistics:

  • Bernoulli: $\sum x_i$ (count of successes). From 10 million coin flips, you only need the total number of heads.
  • Normal (known $\sigma^2$): $\sum x_i$ (the sum). Equivalently, the sample mean $\bar{x}$.
  • Normal (unknown $\mu, \sigma^2$): $(\sum x_i, \sum x_i^2)$. Two numbers capture everything.
  • Poisson: $\sum x_i$ (total count). A million event counts compress to one number.

Maximum Likelihood Estimation

One of the most elegant consequences of the exponential family framework is that maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) reduces to a single, universal equation: moment matching.

Deriving the MLE for the Exponential Family
1
Write the log-likelihood for $N$ i.i.d. observations: $$\ell(\boldsymbol{\eta}) = \sum_{i=1}^N \log h(x_i) + \boldsymbol{\eta}^\top \sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{T}(x_i) - N \cdot A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$$
2
Differentiate with respect to $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ and set to zero: $$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\eta}} \ell = \sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{T}(x_i) - N \cdot \nabla A(\boldsymbol{\eta}) = 0$$
3
Solve for the MLE condition: $$\boxed{\nabla A(\hat{\boldsymbol{\eta}}_{\text{MLE}}) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{T}(x_i)}$$

In words: the MLE is the value of $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ for which the model's expected sufficient statistics equal the empirical average sufficient statistics. This is moment matching.

Since $\nabla A$ is the mean function and $A$ is convex, the log-likelihood is concave, which guarantees:

  • There is a unique global maximum (no local optima traps).
  • Gradient-based optimizers always converge to the MLE.
  • The Hessian of the log-likelihood is $-N \cdot \nabla^2 A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$, which is negative semi-definite (confirming concavity).

Verification: Bernoulli MLE

For the Bernoulli, the MLE condition says:

$$A'(\hat{\eta}) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N x_i \quad \implies \quad \hat{p} = \bar{x}$$

The MLE for a Bernoulli is just the sample proportion -- exactly what we expect!

Verification: Poisson MLE

$$A'(\hat{\eta}) = e^{\hat{\eta}} = \hat{\lambda} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N x_i = \bar{x}$$

The MLE for the Poisson rate is the sample mean, derived in one line from the general theory.

Verification: Normal MLE (known variance)

$$A'(\hat{\eta}) = \sigma^2 \hat{\eta} = \hat{\mu} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N x_i = \bar{x}$$

Again, the sample mean -- derived instantly from the general exponential family MLE equation.

Connection to Machine Learning

The exponential family is not just a theoretical curiosity -- it is the backbone of some of the most widely used machine learning algorithms.

Generalized Linear Models (GLMs)

A Generalized Linear Model assumes:

  1. The response variable $y$ follows an exponential family distribution.
  2. The natural parameter is a linear function of the input: $\eta = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}$.
  3. The link function connects the mean $\mu = E[y]$ to the linear predictor: $g(\mu) = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}$.
ML Model Distribution Link Function Loss Function
Linear Regression Normal Identity: $\mu = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}$ MSE (Mean Squared Error)
Logistic Regression Bernoulli Logit: $\log\frac{p}{1-p} = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}$ Binary Cross-Entropy
Poisson Regression Poisson Log: $\log\lambda = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}$ Poisson Deviance
Softmax Regression Categorical Softmax: $p_k = \frac{e^{\eta_k}}{\sum e^{\eta_j}}$ Categorical Cross-Entropy

The deep connection: the canonical link function is the identity in natural parameter space. When you use logistic regression with $\eta = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}$, you are directly parametrizing the natural parameter of the Bernoulli as a linear function of the features. This is why logistic regression uses the sigmoid function -- it maps from $\eta$ back to $p$.

Softmax as Exponential Family

The softmax function that appears at the output of every classification neural network is exactly the link function for the Categorical exponential family. For $K$ classes with natural parameters $\eta_1, \ldots, \eta_K$:

$$p_k = \frac{\exp(\eta_k)}{\sum_{j=1}^K \exp(\eta_j)}, \qquad A(\boldsymbol{\eta}) = \log\sum_{j=1}^K \exp(\eta_j)$$

The log-partition function $A$ is the famous log-sum-exp, which appears throughout ML whenever we need a smooth, differentiable approximation to the max function.

Natural Gradient Descent

Standard gradient descent treats parameter space as flat (Euclidean). But the natural parametrization of the exponential family induces a curved geometry on parameter space, described by the Fisher information matrix:

$$\mathbf{F}(\boldsymbol{\eta}) = \nabla^2 A(\boldsymbol{\eta}) = \text{Cov}[\mathbf{T}(x)]$$

The natural gradient update preconditions the gradient by the inverse Fisher information:

$$\boldsymbol{\eta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\eta}_t - \alpha \, \mathbf{F}^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\eta}_t) \nabla \ell(\boldsymbol{\eta}_t)$$

This is parameter-space invariant: the update does not depend on how you choose to parametrize the distribution. Natural gradient descent converges faster than standard gradient descent for exponential family models and forms the foundation of algorithms like TRPO and KFAC in deep learning.

Why This Matters

Every time you train a logistic regression model, choose MSE or cross-entropy as your loss, or apply softmax to your neural network's output, you are implicitly working within the exponential family framework. Understanding the exponential family does not just provide theoretical elegance -- it tells you why these design choices work, when they are appropriate, and how to extend them.

Exponential Family in Python

Complete implementations demonstrating exponential family form conversions, moment-generating properties of the log-partition function, and maximum likelihood estimation via moment matching.

1. Exponential Family Form Conversions

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats, special

# ============================================================
# Converting distributions to exponential family canonical form
# ============================================================

class ExponentialFamilyBernoulli:
    """Bernoulli distribution in exponential family form."""

    @staticmethod
    def standard_to_natural(p):
        """Convert probability p to natural parameter eta = logit(p)."""
        return np.log(p / (1 - p))

    @staticmethod
    def natural_to_standard(eta):
        """Convert natural parameter eta to probability p = sigmoid(eta)."""
        return 1 / (1 + np.exp(-eta))

    @staticmethod
    def sufficient_stat(x):
        """T(x) = x for Bernoulli."""
        return x

    @staticmethod
    def log_partition(eta):
        """A(eta) = log(1 + exp(eta))."""
        return np.log(1 + np.exp(eta))

    @staticmethod
    def base_measure(x):
        """h(x) = 1 for Bernoulli."""
        return np.ones_like(x, dtype=float)

    @staticmethod
    def log_prob(x, eta):
        """Log probability in canonical form: eta*T(x) - A(eta) + log h(x)."""
        return eta * x - np.log(1 + np.exp(eta))


class ExponentialFamilyNormal:
    """Normal distribution (known sigma^2) in exponential family form."""

    def __init__(self, sigma2=1.0):
        self.sigma2 = sigma2

    def standard_to_natural(self, mu):
        return mu / self.sigma2

    def natural_to_standard(self, eta):
        return self.sigma2 * eta

    def sufficient_stat(self, x):
        return x

    def log_partition(self, eta):
        return self.sigma2 * eta**2 / 2

    def base_measure(self, x):
        return (1 / np.sqrt(2 * np.pi * self.sigma2)) * \
               np.exp(-x**2 / (2 * self.sigma2))

    def log_prob(self, x, eta):
        return (eta * x - self.log_partition(eta)
                + np.log(self.base_measure(x)))


class ExponentialFamilyPoisson:
    """Poisson distribution in exponential family form."""

    @staticmethod
    def standard_to_natural(lam):
        return np.log(lam)

    @staticmethod
    def natural_to_standard(eta):
        return np.exp(eta)

    @staticmethod
    def sufficient_stat(x):
        return x

    @staticmethod
    def log_partition(eta):
        return np.exp(eta)

    @staticmethod
    def base_measure(x):
        return 1.0 / special.factorial(x)

    @staticmethod
    def log_prob(x, eta):
        return eta * x - np.exp(eta) - special.gammaln(x + 1)


# --- Verification ---
print("=== Bernoulli: p=0.7 ===")
ef_bern = ExponentialFamilyBernoulli()
p = 0.7
eta = ef_bern.standard_to_natural(p)
print(f"  p = {p} -> eta = {eta:.4f}")
print(f"  eta -> p = {ef_bern.natural_to_standard(eta):.4f}")
print(f"  P(x=1) via exp family: {np.exp(ef_bern.log_prob(1, eta)):.4f}")
print(f"  P(x=1) via standard:   {p:.4f}")
print(f"  P(x=0) via exp family: {np.exp(ef_bern.log_prob(0, eta)):.4f}")
print(f"  P(x=0) via standard:   {1-p:.4f}")

print("\n=== Poisson: lambda=3.5 ===")
ef_pois = ExponentialFamilyPoisson()
lam = 3.5
eta_p = ef_pois.standard_to_natural(lam)
for x in [0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5]:
    p_ef = np.exp(ef_pois.log_prob(x, eta_p))
    p_std = stats.poisson.pmf(x, lam)
    print(f"  P(X={x}): exp_family={p_ef:.6f}, scipy={p_std:.6f}")

print("\n=== Normal: mu=2, sigma^2=4 ===")
ef_norm = ExponentialFamilyNormal(sigma2=4.0)
mu = 2.0
eta_n = ef_norm.standard_to_natural(mu)
x_vals = np.array([-1.0, 0.0, 1.0, 2.0, 3.0])
for x in x_vals:
    p_ef = np.exp(ef_norm.log_prob(x, eta_n))
    p_std = stats.norm.pdf(x, loc=mu, scale=2.0)
    print(f"  p(x={x:.1f}): exp_family={p_ef:.6f}, scipy={p_std:.6f}")

2. Log-Partition Function: Moment Generation

import numpy as np
from scipy.misc import derivative

# ============================================================
# Verify that A'(eta) = E[T(x)] and A''(eta) = Var[T(x)]
# for several exponential family distributions
# ============================================================

def verify_log_partition_bernoulli(p):
    """Verify moment-generating properties for Bernoulli."""
    eta = np.log(p / (1 - p))
    A = lambda e: np.log(1 + np.exp(e))

    # Numerical derivatives
    dA = derivative(A, eta, n=1, dx=1e-8)
    d2A = derivative(A, eta, n=2, dx=1e-6)

    # True moments
    mean = p           # E[X] for Bernoulli
    var = p * (1 - p)  # Var[X] for Bernoulli

    print(f"Bernoulli(p={p}):")
    print(f"  A'(eta) = {dA:.6f},  E[X] = {mean:.6f}")
    print(f"  A''(eta) = {d2A:.6f}, Var[X] = {var:.6f}")


def verify_log_partition_poisson(lam):
    """Verify moment-generating properties for Poisson."""
    eta = np.log(lam)
    A = lambda e: np.exp(e)

    dA = derivative(A, eta, n=1, dx=1e-8)
    d2A = derivative(A, eta, n=2, dx=1e-6)

    print(f"\nPoisson(lambda={lam}):")
    print(f"  A'(eta) = {dA:.6f},  E[X] = {lam:.6f}")
    print(f"  A''(eta) = {d2A:.6f}, Var[X] = {lam:.6f}")


def verify_log_partition_normal(mu, sigma2):
    """Verify moment-generating properties for Normal (known variance)."""
    eta = mu / sigma2
    A = lambda e: sigma2 * e**2 / 2

    dA = derivative(A, eta, n=1, dx=1e-8)
    d2A = derivative(A, eta, n=2, dx=1e-6)

    print(f"\nNormal(mu={mu}, sigma^2={sigma2}):")
    print(f"  A'(eta) = {dA:.6f},  E[X] = {mu:.6f}")
    print(f"  A''(eta) = {d2A:.6f}, Var[X] = {sigma2:.6f}")

3. MLE via Moment Matching

import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import minimize_scalar, minimize

# ============================================================
# MLE for exponential families: solve A'(eta_hat) = mean(T(x))
# ============================================================

def mle_bernoulli(data):
    """
    MLE for Bernoulli via the exponential family equation:
        A'(eta) = mean(T(x)) = mean(x) = x_bar
    For Bernoulli: A'(eta) = sigmoid(eta) = p
    So: p_hat = x_bar
    """
    x_bar = np.mean(data)
    # Solve in natural parameter space
    eta_hat = np.log(x_bar / (1 - x_bar))
    p_hat = 1 / (1 + np.exp(-eta_hat))
    return eta_hat, p_hat


def mle_poisson(data):
    """
    MLE for Poisson: A'(eta) = exp(eta) = lambda
    Equation: lambda_hat = x_bar
    """
    x_bar = np.mean(data)
    eta_hat = np.log(x_bar)
    lambda_hat = np.exp(eta_hat)
    return eta_hat, lambda_hat


def mle_normal(data, sigma2_known):
    """
    MLE for Normal (known variance): A'(eta) = sigma^2 * eta = mu
    Equation: mu_hat = x_bar
    """
    x_bar = np.mean(data)
    eta_hat = x_bar / sigma2_known
    mu_hat = sigma2_known * eta_hat
    return eta_hat, mu_hat


# --- Demonstration ---
np.random.seed(42)

# Bernoulli
true_p = 0.65
data_bern = np.random.binomial(1, true_p, size=1000)
eta_hat, p_hat = mle_bernoulli(data_bern)
print(f"Bernoulli MLE: true p = {true_p}")
print(f"  eta_hat = {eta_hat:.4f}, p_hat = {p_hat:.4f}")
print(f"  Empirical sufficient stat: sum(x) = {data_bern.sum()}, "
      f"mean(T) = {data_bern.mean():.4f}")

# Poisson
true_lambda = 4.2
data_pois = np.random.poisson(true_lambda, size=1000)
eta_hat, lam_hat = mle_poisson(data_pois)
print(f"\nPoisson MLE: true lambda = {true_lambda}")
print(f"  eta_hat = {eta_hat:.4f}, lambda_hat = {lam_hat:.4f}")

# Normal
true_mu, true_sigma2 = 5.0, 2.0
data_norm = np.random.normal(true_mu, np.sqrt(true_sigma2), size=1000)
eta_hat, mu_hat = mle_normal(data_norm, true_sigma2)
print(f"\nNormal MLE: true mu = {true_mu}, sigma^2 = {true_sigma2}")
print(f"  eta_hat = {eta_hat:.4f}, mu_hat = {mu_hat:.4f}")

# --- Show convergence: MLE as N grows ---
print("\n--- MLE Convergence (Bernoulli, true p=0.65) ---")
for N in [10, 50, 100, 500, 1000, 5000, 10000]:
    data = np.random.binomial(1, 0.65, size=N)
    _, p_hat = mle_bernoulli(data)
    print(f"  N = {N:>6d}: p_hat = {p_hat:.4f}")

Exercises

Test your understanding of the exponential family, from identifying components to proving key properties.

Easy 1. Identify the Exponential Family Components

For each of the following distributions, write the density in exponential family form and identify $\boldsymbol{\eta}$, $\mathbf{T}(x)$, $A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$, and $h(x)$.

  1. The Exponential distribution with rate $\lambda > 0$: $p(x|\lambda) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}$ for $x \geq 0$.
  2. The Geometric distribution with success probability $p \in (0,1)$: $P(k|p) = (1-p)^{k-1}p$ for $k = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$

Hint: For the Geometric, take the log and group terms that depend on $p$ vs. terms that depend only on $k$. The natural parameter will involve $\log(1-p)$.

Medium 2. Full Normal with Unknown Mean and Variance

Consider the Normal distribution $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ where both $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ are unknown parameters.

  1. Write the density in two-parameter exponential family form with $\boldsymbol{\eta} = (\eta_1, \eta_2)$ and $\mathbf{T}(x) = (T_1(x), T_2(x))$.
  2. Express $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ in terms of $\eta_1$ and $\eta_2$.
  3. Compute $\nabla A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$ and verify that it equals $(E[T_1(x)], E[T_2(x)]) = (\mu, \mu^2 + \sigma^2)$.
  4. Compute $\nabla^2 A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$ (the $2\times 2$ Hessian) and verify it equals $\text{Cov}[\mathbf{T}(x)]$.
Medium 3. MLE for Gamma via Moment Matching

Given $N$ i.i.d. samples $x_1, \ldots, x_N$ from a $\text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$ distribution with known shape $\alpha$:

  1. Using the exponential family MLE equation $\nabla A(\hat{\boldsymbol{\eta}}) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i \mathbf{T}(x_i)$, derive the MLE $\hat{\beta}$.
  2. Show that $\hat{\beta} = \alpha / \bar{x}$, where $\bar{x}$ is the sample mean.
  3. Verify numerically by generating 10,000 samples from $\text{Gamma}(3, 2)$ and computing the MLE. How close is it to the true $\beta = 2$?

Hint: Recall that for the Gamma, $A(\eta) = -\alpha\log(-\eta)$ and $T(x) = x$.

Hard 4. Proving the Exponential Family is the Only Family with Fixed-Dimensional Sufficient Statistics

This exercise explores the Pitman-Koopman-Darmois theorem, one of the deepest results in mathematical statistics.

  1. State the theorem: For a parametric family of distributions with continuous, open support that does not depend on the parameter, if a sufficient statistic of fixed dimension (independent of sample size $N$) exists, then the family must be an exponential family.
  2. Prove the scalar case. Suppose $p(x|\theta) > 0$ for all $x$ in some open interval and all $\theta$ in some open set, and suppose there exists a one-dimensional sufficient statistic $T(x_1, \ldots, x_N) = \sum_i t(x_i)$ for all $N \geq 1$. Using the Neyman-Fisher factorization and the functional equation that arises from considering $N = 1$ and $N = 2$, show that $\log p(x|\theta)$ must be linear in $t(x)$ with coefficients that depend on $\theta$.
  3. Explain why the Cauchy distribution has no fixed-dimensional sufficient statistic (i.e., why you need to keep all $N$ data points).
