Why Conjugacy?

Bayesian inference requires computing the posterior distribution over parameters given observed data. By Bayes' theorem:

$$P(\theta | \mathcal{D}) = \frac{P(\mathcal{D} | \theta) \, P(\theta)}{P(\mathcal{D})} = \frac{P(\mathcal{D} | \theta) \, P(\theta)}{\int P(\mathcal{D} | \theta) \, P(\theta) \, d\theta}$$

The denominator — the evidence or marginal likelihood — requires integrating the likelihood times the prior over the entire parameter space. For most choices of prior and likelihood, this integral has no closed-form solution. We would need numerical methods (MCMC, variational inference) to approximate the posterior.

But there is a special case where the math works out exactly: when the prior and likelihood are conjugate to each other.

Conjugate Prior

A prior distribution $P(\theta)$ is conjugate to a likelihood function $P(\mathcal{D} | \theta)$ if the resulting posterior $P(\theta | \mathcal{D})$ belongs to the same family as the prior.

That is, if the prior is $P(\theta) = f(\theta; \boldsymbol{\alpha})$ for some hyperparameters $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$, then the posterior is $P(\theta | \mathcal{D}) = f(\theta; \boldsymbol{\alpha}')$ with updated hyperparameters $\boldsymbol{\alpha}' = g(\boldsymbol{\alpha}, \mathcal{D})$.

This is powerful because inference reduces to a simple hyperparameter update rule. No integrals to compute, no sampling to run — just arithmetic on the hyperparameters. The posterior is available in closed form, and sequential updating becomes trivial: today's posterior hyperparameters become tomorrow's prior hyperparameters.

The Practical Value

Conjugate priors provide (1) analytical posteriors for fast computation, (2) interpretable hyperparameters that encode prior "pseudo-observations," (3) seamless sequential updating where the posterior after batch 1 becomes the prior for batch 2, and (4) closed-form posterior predictive distributions. They are the foundation for understanding Bayesian reasoning before scaling to approximate methods.

Beta-Binomial Conjugacy

The Beta-Binomial pair is the most intuitive conjugate system. We observe $k$ successes in $n$ Bernoulli trials and want to infer the unknown success probability $\theta \in [0, 1]$.

Setup

  • Likelihood: $k$ successes in $n$ trials — $P(k | n, \theta) = \binom{n}{k} \theta^k (1 - \theta)^{n-k}$
  • Prior: $\theta \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$, with PDF $P(\theta) = \frac{\theta^{\alpha-1}(1-\theta)^{\beta-1}}{B(\alpha, \beta)}$
Derivation: Beta Prior + Binomial Likelihood = Beta Posterior
1
Write the posterior using Bayes' theorem (dropping constants in $\theta$): $$P(\theta | k, n) \propto P(k | n, \theta) \cdot P(\theta)$$
2
Expand the likelihood and prior: $$P(\theta | k, n) \propto \underbrace{\theta^k (1-\theta)^{n-k}}_{\text{Binomial}} \cdot \underbrace{\theta^{\alpha-1} (1-\theta)^{\beta-1}}_{\text{Beta prior}}$$

We dropped $\binom{n}{k}$ and $\frac{1}{B(\alpha,\beta)}$ since they do not depend on $\theta$.

3
Combine exponents: $$P(\theta | k, n) \propto \theta^{(\alpha + k) - 1} \cdot (1-\theta)^{(\beta + n - k) - 1}$$
4
Recognize this as a Beta distribution: $$\boxed{\theta | k, n \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + k, \;\beta + n - k)}$$

The posterior is Beta with updated parameters. The prior contributed $\alpha - 1$ "pseudo-successes" and $\beta - 1$ "pseudo-failures," and the data contributed $k$ observed successes and $n - k$ observed failures.

Interpreting the Hyperparameters

The prior $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ can be interpreted as encoding $\alpha + \beta - 2$ pseudo-observations: $\alpha - 1$ pseudo-successes and $\beta - 1$ pseudo-failures. The posterior mean is:

$$\mathbb{E}[\theta | k, n] = \frac{\alpha + k}{\alpha + \beta + n} = \underbrace{\frac{\alpha + \beta}{\alpha + \beta + n}}_{\text{weight on prior}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}}_{\text{prior mean}} + \underbrace{\frac{n}{\alpha + \beta + n}}_{\text{weight on data}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{k}{n}}_{\text{MLE}}$$

The posterior mean is a weighted average of the prior mean and the MLE, with weights proportional to the "effective sample sizes." As $n \to \infty$, the data overwhelms the prior and the posterior mean converges to the MLE. As $\alpha + \beta \to \infty$ (strong prior), the prior dominates.

As data accumulates, the posterior sharpens around the true parameter value and the influence of the prior vanishes. This is Bayesian consistency: with enough data, the posterior concentrates on the truth regardless of the prior (as long as the prior assigns nonzero density to the truth).

Normal-Normal Conjugacy

Suppose we observe data $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$ from a Normal distribution with known variance $\sigma^2$ and unknown mean $\mu$. We place a Normal prior on $\mu$.

Setup

  • Likelihood: $x_i | \mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n$ (known $\sigma^2$)
  • Prior: $\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)$
Derivation: Normal Prior + Normal Likelihood = Normal Posterior
1
Write the joint log-posterior (dropping constants in $\mu$): $$\log P(\mu | \mathbf{x}) = \text{const} - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \mu)^2 - \frac{1}{2\sigma_0^2}(\mu - \mu_0)^2$$
2
Expand and collect terms quadratic in $\mu$: $$= \text{const} - \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{n}{\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{\sigma_0^2}\right)\mu^2 + \left(\frac{n\bar{x}}{\sigma^2} + \frac{\mu_0}{\sigma_0^2}\right)\mu$$

where $\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i$ is the sample mean.

3
Define posterior precision (inverse variance): $$\frac{1}{\sigma_n^2} = \underbrace{\frac{1}{\sigma_0^2}}_{\text{prior precision}} + \underbrace{\frac{n}{\sigma^2}}_{\text{data precision}}$$

Precisions add. More data or tighter prior means higher posterior precision (less uncertainty).

4
Complete the square to find the posterior mean: $$\mu_n = \sigma_n^2 \left(\frac{\mu_0}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{n\bar{x}}{\sigma^2}\right)$$
5
Result: $$\boxed{\mu | \mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_n, \sigma_n^2)}$$

The posterior is Normal. The posterior mean is a precision-weighted average of the prior mean and the MLE:

$$\mu_n = \frac{\text{prior precision} \cdot \mu_0 + \text{data precision} \cdot \bar{x}}{\text{prior precision} + \text{data precision}} = \frac{\frac{1}{\sigma_0^2} \cdot \mu_0 + \frac{n}{\sigma^2} \cdot \bar{x}}{\frac{1}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{n}{\sigma^2}}$$

Precision-Weighted Average Interpretation

The posterior mean lies between the prior mean $\mu_0$ and the sample mean $\bar{x}$, with the balance determined by their respective precisions (inverse variances). This gives three illuminating limiting cases:

  • $n \to \infty$: Data precision dominates. $\mu_n \to \bar{x}$ (the MLE). The prior is washed out.
  • $\sigma_0^2 \to \infty$ (vague prior): Prior precision $\to 0$. Again $\mu_n \to \bar{x}$. An uninformative prior yields MLE-like behavior.
  • $\sigma^2 \to \infty$ (very noisy data): Data precision $\to 0$. $\mu_n \to \mu_0$. We fall back on our prior belief.

Gamma-Poisson Conjugacy

The Poisson distribution models count data: the number of emails per hour, customer arrivals per day, or mutations per genome. The unknown parameter is the rate $\lambda > 0$. The conjugate prior for $\lambda$ is the Gamma distribution.

Setup

  • Likelihood: $x_i | \lambda \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, so $P(x_i | \lambda) = \frac{\lambda^{x_i} e^{-\lambda}}{x_i!}$
  • Prior: $\lambda \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$, with PDF $P(\lambda) = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} \lambda^{\alpha - 1} e^{-\beta \lambda}$
Derivation: Gamma Prior + Poisson Likelihood = Gamma Posterior
1
Joint likelihood for $n$ observations: $$P(\mathbf{x} | \lambda) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\lambda^{x_i} e^{-\lambda}}{x_i!} \propto \lambda^{\sum x_i} e^{-n\lambda}$$
2
Posterior (dropping constants in $\lambda$): $$P(\lambda | \mathbf{x}) \propto \underbrace{\lambda^{\sum x_i} e^{-n\lambda}}_{\text{likelihood}} \cdot \underbrace{\lambda^{\alpha - 1} e^{-\beta\lambda}}_{\text{Gamma prior}}$$
3
Combine: $$P(\lambda | \mathbf{x}) \propto \lambda^{(\alpha + \sum x_i) - 1} \cdot e^{-(\beta + n)\lambda}$$
4
Recognize as Gamma: $$\boxed{\lambda | \mathbf{x} \sim \text{Gamma}\!\left(\alpha + \textstyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i, \;\; \beta + n\right)}$$

The shape parameter increases by the total count $\sum x_i$, and the rate parameter increases by $n$. The prior encodes $\alpha - 1$ pseudo-events observed over $\beta$ pseudo-time-units.

Example: Modeling Arrival Rates

A hospital emergency department wants to estimate its patient arrival rate. The prior belief, based on historical data from similar hospitals, is $\lambda \sim \text{Gamma}(30, 1)$, encoding approximately 30 arrivals per day. Over 5 days of observation they record $[28, 35, 31, 22, 34]$ arrivals (total: 150).

$$\lambda | \mathbf{x} \sim \text{Gamma}(30 + 150, \; 1 + 5) = \text{Gamma}(180, 6)$$

The posterior mean is $180/6 = 30.0$ arrivals per day. The posterior is sharply concentrated because we had both a moderately informative prior and five days of data. The 95% credible interval can be computed directly from the Gamma CDF.

Dirichlet-Multinomial Conjugacy

The Dirichlet-Multinomial pair generalizes Beta-Binomial from 2 categories to $K$ categories. Where Beta-Binomial handles coin flips (heads/tails), Dirichlet-Multinomial handles dice rolls, word frequencies, topic distributions, and any categorical data.

Setup

  • Likelihood: Multinomial with counts $\mathbf{c} = (c_1, \ldots, c_K)$, probabilities $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_K)$ $$P(\mathbf{c} | \boldsymbol{\theta}) \propto \prod_{k=1}^{K} \theta_k^{c_k}$$
  • Prior: $\boldsymbol{\theta} \sim \text{Dirichlet}(\boldsymbol{\alpha})$ where $\boldsymbol{\alpha} = (\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_K)$ $$P(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \frac{1}{B(\boldsymbol{\alpha})} \prod_{k=1}^{K} \theta_k^{\alpha_k - 1}$$
Derivation: Dirichlet Posterior
1
Posterior: $$P(\boldsymbol{\theta} | \mathbf{c}) \propto \prod_{k=1}^{K} \theta_k^{c_k} \cdot \prod_{k=1}^{K} \theta_k^{\alpha_k - 1} = \prod_{k=1}^{K} \theta_k^{(\alpha_k + c_k) - 1}$$
2
Result: $$\boxed{\boldsymbol{\theta} | \mathbf{c} \sim \text{Dirichlet}(\alpha_1 + c_1, \;\alpha_2 + c_2, \;\ldots, \;\alpha_K + c_K)}$$

Each $\alpha_k$ acts as a pseudo-count for category $k$. The update simply adds observed counts to the prior pseudo-counts.

Connection to Topic Models (LDA)

Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) uses Dirichlet-Multinomial conjugacy at two levels. Each document's topic distribution has a Dirichlet prior: $\boldsymbol{\theta}_d \sim \text{Dirichlet}(\boldsymbol{\alpha})$. Each topic's word distribution also has a Dirichlet prior: $\boldsymbol{\phi}_k \sim \text{Dirichlet}(\boldsymbol{\beta})$. Conjugacy enables efficient Gibbs sampling: after assigning a word to a topic, the conditional posterior for $\boldsymbol{\theta}_d$ and $\boldsymbol{\phi}_k$ remain Dirichlet — just with updated counts. This is why LDA was computationally feasible even before modern variational methods.

From Beta to Dirichlet

The Beta distribution is the special case $K = 2$ of the Dirichlet distribution: $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta) = \text{Dirichlet}(\alpha, \beta)$. Similarly, the Binomial is the $K=2$ case of the Multinomial. Every Beta-Binomial result generalizes naturally to Dirichlet-Multinomial.

Why Conjugacy Works: The Exponential Family

Conjugate priors are not coincidences — they arise from a deep structural property. Every member of the exponential family has a conjugate prior, and the form of that prior is determined by the likelihood's sufficient statistics.

Recall that an exponential family distribution can be written as:

$$P(x | \boldsymbol{\eta}) = h(x) \exp\!\left(\boldsymbol{\eta}^T \mathbf{T}(x) - A(\boldsymbol{\eta})\right)$$

where $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ is the natural parameter, $\mathbf{T}(x)$ is the sufficient statistic, and $A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$ is the log-partition function. The conjugate prior for $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ has the form:

$$P(\boldsymbol{\eta} | \boldsymbol{\chi}, \nu) \propto \exp\!\left(\boldsymbol{\chi}^T \boldsymbol{\eta} - \nu \, A(\boldsymbol{\eta})\right)$$

After observing $n$ data points, the posterior updates $\boldsymbol{\chi} \to \boldsymbol{\chi} + \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbf{T}(x_i)$ and $\nu \to \nu + n$. This is the universal pattern: the conjugate prior has the same functional form as the likelihood's sufficient statistics, and the update rule simply accumulates sufficient statistics.

Table of Conjugate Pairs

Likelihood Conjugate Prior Posterior Update Rule
Bernoulli / Binomial Beta($\alpha$, $\beta$) Beta($\alpha'$, $\beta'$) $\alpha' = \alpha + k$, $\beta' = \beta + n - k$
Poisson Gamma($\alpha$, $\beta$) Gamma($\alpha'$, $\beta'$) $\alpha' = \alpha + \sum x_i$, $\beta' = \beta + n$
Normal (known $\sigma^2$) Normal($\mu_0$, $\sigma_0^2$) Normal($\mu_n$, $\sigma_n^2$) Precision-weighted mean
Normal (known $\mu$) Inverse-Gamma($\alpha$, $\beta$) Inverse-Gamma($\alpha'$, $\beta'$) $\alpha' = \alpha + n/2$, $\beta' = \beta + \frac{1}{2}\sum(x_i - \mu)^2$
Multinomial Dirichlet($\boldsymbol{\alpha}$) Dirichlet($\boldsymbol{\alpha}'$) $\alpha_k' = \alpha_k + c_k$
Exponential Gamma($\alpha$, $\beta$) Gamma($\alpha'$, $\beta'$) $\alpha' = \alpha + n$, $\beta' = \beta + \sum x_i$
Multivariate Normal Normal-Inverse-Wishart Normal-Inverse-Wishart Update mean, scale, and scatter matrix

The common thread: in every case, the prior "looks like" the likelihood, and the posterior update amounts to adding the data's sufficient statistics to the prior's hyperparameters. This is not a coincidence but a fundamental consequence of the exponential family structure.

In the Normal-Normal diagram above, the posterior (blue) sits between the prior (gray) and the likelihood (red), pulled toward whichever has higher precision. The posterior is always narrower than both the prior and the likelihood individually, because combining two sources of information always reduces uncertainty.

Beyond Conjugacy

Conjugate priors are elegant but restrictive. They require the prior to have a specific parametric form dictated by the likelihood, which may not reflect our actual beliefs. Several important situations force us beyond conjugacy:

  • Non-exponential-family likelihoods: Logistic regression, neural networks, and most modern models do not have conjugate priors.
  • Hierarchical models: When hyperparameters themselves have priors, conjugacy at one level does not guarantee conjugacy at the next.
  • Mismatched beliefs: We may genuinely believe in a prior that is not conjugate to our likelihood (e.g., a multimodal prior).
  • High-dimensional parameters: Even when conjugate priors exist in theory, the parameter space may be too large for closed-form solutions to be useful.

When conjugacy fails, we turn to approximate inference methods:

Method Idea Strength
MCMC Draw correlated samples from the posterior Asymptotically exact; flexible
Variational Inference Approximate the posterior with a simpler distribution by minimizing KL divergence Fast; scalable to large datasets
Laplace Approximation Fit a Gaussian at the MAP estimate using the Hessian Simple; good when posterior is unimodal
Conjugacy as a Stepping Stone

Understanding conjugate priors is essential even when working with non-conjugate models. The intuitions — precision-weighted averages, pseudo-counts, sequential updating — carry over to approximate methods. Many variational inference algorithms use conjugate updates as inner steps within larger non-conjugate models. See the Bayesian Inference tutorial and the Variational Inference tutorial for more.

Beta-Binomial Conjugate Update

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def beta_binomial_update(alpha_prior, beta_prior, n_successes, n_failures):
    """
    Conjugate update: Beta prior + Binomial likelihood = Beta posterior.

    Args:
        alpha_prior, beta_prior: Beta prior hyperparameters
        n_successes: number of observed successes (heads)
        n_failures: number of observed failures (tails)
    Returns:
        alpha_post, beta_post: posterior Beta hyperparameters
    """
    alpha_post = alpha_prior + n_successes
    beta_post = beta_prior + n_failures
    return alpha_post, beta_post


# --- Example: Coin flipping ---
alpha_0, beta_0 = 2, 2           # Mildly informative prior
k, n = 7, 10                     # 7 heads in 10 flips

alpha_post, beta_post = beta_binomial_update(alpha_0, beta_0, k, n - k)

prior = stats.beta(alpha_0, beta_0)
posterior = stats.beta(alpha_post, beta_post)

print(f"Prior:     Beta({alpha_0}, {beta_0})")
print(f"Posterior: Beta({alpha_post}, {beta_post})")
print(f"Prior mean:     {prior.mean():.4f}")
print(f"Posterior mean: {posterior.mean():.4f}")
print(f"MLE:            {k/n:.4f}")
print(f"MAP:            {(alpha_post-1)/(alpha_post+beta_post-2):.4f}")

# 95% credible interval
ci = posterior.ppf([0.025, 0.975])
print(f"95% CI:         [{ci[0]:.4f}, {ci[1]:.4f}]")

# --- Visualization ---
theta = np.linspace(0, 1, 500)
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))
ax.plot(theta, prior.pdf(theta), '--', color='gray', lw=2,
        label=f'Prior Beta({alpha_0},{beta_0})')
ax.plot(theta, posterior.pdf(theta), '-', color='#3498db', lw=2.5,
        label=f'Posterior Beta({alpha_post},{beta_post})')
ax.axvline(k/n, color='#e74c3c', ls=':', lw=1.5, label=f'MLE = {k/n:.2f}')
ax.fill_between(theta[(theta >= ci[0]) & (theta <= ci[1])],
                posterior.pdf(theta[(theta >= ci[0]) & (theta <= ci[1])]),
                alpha=0.2, color='#3498db', label='95% Credible Interval')
ax.set_xlabel(r'$\theta$')
ax.set_ylabel('Density')
ax.set_title('Beta-Binomial Conjugate Update')
ax.legend()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Normal-Normal Conjugate Update

def normal_normal_update(mu_0, sigma_0_sq, sigma_sq, data):
    """
    Conjugate update: Normal prior + Normal likelihood (known variance).

    Args:
        mu_0: prior mean
        sigma_0_sq: prior variance
        sigma_sq: known observation variance
        data: array of observations
    Returns:
        mu_n, sigma_n_sq: posterior mean and variance
    """
    n = len(data)
    x_bar = np.mean(data)

    # Precisions add
    precision_prior = 1.0 / sigma_0_sq
    precision_data = n / sigma_sq
    precision_post = precision_prior + precision_data

    sigma_n_sq = 1.0 / precision_post
    mu_n = sigma_n_sq * (precision_prior * mu_0 + precision_data * x_bar)

    return mu_n, sigma_n_sq


# --- Example: Estimating a mean ---
np.random.seed(42)
true_mu = 5.0
sigma_sq = 4.0              # Known observation variance
mu_0, sigma_0_sq = 0.0, 9.0 # Prior: N(0, 9)

all_data = np.random.normal(true_mu, np.sqrt(sigma_sq), 100)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 4.5))
for idx, n in enumerate([1, 10, 100]):
    data = all_data[:n]
    mu_n, sigma_n_sq = normal_normal_update(mu_0, sigma_0_sq, sigma_sq, data)

    x = np.linspace(-6, 12, 500)
    prior_dist = stats.norm(mu_0, np.sqrt(sigma_0_sq))
    post_dist = stats.norm(mu_n, np.sqrt(sigma_n_sq))

    ax = axes[idx]
    ax.plot(x, prior_dist.pdf(x), '--', color='gray', lw=1.5, label='Prior')
    ax.plot(x, post_dist.pdf(x), '-', color='#3498db', lw=2.5,
            label=f'Posterior (n={n})')
    ax.axvline(true_mu, color='#27ae60', ls=':', lw=1.5,
               label=f'True $\\mu$={true_mu}')
    ax.axvline(np.mean(data), color='#e74c3c', ls=':', alpha=0.7,
               label=f'MLE={np.mean(data):.2f}')
    ax.set_title(f'n = {n}')
    ax.set_xlabel('$\\mu$')
    ax.legend(fontsize=8)

plt.suptitle('Normal-Normal Conjugate Update', fontsize=14)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Gamma-Poisson Conjugate Update

def gamma_poisson_update(alpha_prior, beta_prior, data):
    """
    Conjugate update: Gamma prior + Poisson likelihood = Gamma posterior.

    Args:
        alpha_prior: Gamma shape parameter (prior)
        beta_prior: Gamma rate parameter (prior)
        data: array of observed counts
    Returns:
        alpha_post, beta_post: posterior Gamma parameters
    """
    n = len(data)
    total_count = np.sum(data)
    alpha_post = alpha_prior + total_count
    beta_post = beta_prior + n
    return alpha_post, beta_post


# --- Example: Hospital arrival rates ---
np.random.seed(42)
true_lambda = 30.0
observed_counts = np.random.poisson(true_lambda, size=5)
print(f"Observed daily counts: {observed_counts}")
print(f"Total: {observed_counts.sum()}, Mean: {observed_counts.mean():.1f}")

# Prior: Gamma(30, 1) -> prior mean = 30
alpha_0, beta_0 = 30, 1
alpha_post, beta_post = gamma_poisson_update(alpha_0, beta_0, observed_counts)

prior_dist = stats.gamma(alpha_0, scale=1/beta_0)
post_dist = stats.gamma(alpha_post, scale=1/beta_post)

print(f"\nPrior:     Gamma({alpha_0}, {beta_0}), mean = {alpha_0/beta_0:.1f}")
print(f"Posterior: Gamma({alpha_post}, {beta_post}), mean = {alpha_post/beta_post:.2f}")

# Visualization
lam = np.linspace(15, 50, 500)
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 5))
ax.plot(lam, prior_dist.pdf(lam), '--', color='gray', lw=2,
        label=f'Prior Gamma({alpha_0}, {beta_0})')
ax.plot(lam, post_dist.pdf(lam), '-', color='#e67e22', lw=2.5,
        label=f'Posterior Gamma({alpha_post}, {beta_post})')
ax.axvline(true_lambda, color='#27ae60', ls=':', lw=1.5,
           label=f'True $\\lambda$ = {true_lambda}')
ax.axvline(observed_counts.mean(), color='#e74c3c', ls=':', lw=1.5,
           label=f'MLE = {observed_counts.mean():.1f}')
ax.set_xlabel('$\\lambda$ (arrival rate)')
ax.set_ylabel('Density')
ax.set_title('Gamma-Poisson Conjugate Update')
ax.legend()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Dirichlet-Multinomial Conjugate Update

def dirichlet_multinomial_update(alpha_prior, counts):
    """
    Conjugate update: Dirichlet prior + Multinomial likelihood.

    Args:
        alpha_prior: array of Dirichlet hyperparameters (K,)
        counts: array of observed category counts (K,)
    Returns:
        alpha_post: posterior Dirichlet hyperparameters (K,)
    """
    return alpha_prior + counts


# --- Example: Learning word category frequencies ---
categories = ['Noun', 'Verb', 'Adj', 'Adv', 'Other']

# Uniform prior (all categories equally likely)

observed_counts = np.array([45, 30, 18, 7, 20])

# Posterior mean = alpha_k / sum(alpha)
prior_mean = alpha_prior / alpha_prior.sum()
post_mean = alpha_post / alpha_post.sum()

print("Category     | Prior Mean | Observed Freq | Posterior Mean")
print("-" * 60)
for i, cat in enumerate(categories):
    obs_freq = observed_counts[i] / observed_counts.sum()
    print(f"{cat:12s} | {prior_mean[i]:.4f}     | {obs_freq:.4f}        | {post_mean[i]:.4f}")

Sequential Updating Across All Conjugate Pairs

def sequential_beta_binomial(data_stream, alpha_0=2, beta_0=2):
    """
    Demonstrate sequential Bayesian updating for Beta-Binomial.
    Each observation updates the posterior incrementally.

    Args:
        data_stream: sequence of 0/1 observations
        alpha_0, beta_0: initial prior parameters
    """
    alpha, beta = alpha_0, beta_0
    history = [(alpha, beta)]

    for x in data_stream:
        if x == 1:
            alpha += 1
        else:
            beta += 1
        history.append((alpha, beta))

    # --- Visualize the posterior evolving ---
    theta = np.linspace(0, 1, 300)
    fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 6))

    # Show a few snapshots
    n_total = len(data_stream)
    snapshots = [0, 1, 5, 10, min(25, n_total), n_total]
    snapshots = sorted(set(s for s in snapshots if s <= n_total))

    colors = plt.cm.viridis(np.linspace(0.1, 0.9, len(snapshots)))
    for i, (n_obs, color) in enumerate(zip(snapshots, colors)):
        a, b = history[n_obs]
        dist = stats.beta(a, b)
        style = '--' if n_obs == 0 else '-'
        lw = 1.5 if n_obs == 0 else 2
        label = f'n={n_obs}: Beta({a},{b})' if n_obs > 0 else f'Prior: Beta({a},{b})'
        ax.plot(theta, dist.pdf(theta), style, color=color, lw=lw, label=label)

    ax.set_xlabel(r'$\theta$', fontsize=12)
    ax.set_ylabel('Density', fontsize=12)
    ax.set_title('Sequential Beta-Binomial Updating', fontsize=14)
    ax.legend(fontsize=9)
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()

    # Verify: sequential = batch
    k_total = sum(data_stream)
    n = len(data_stream)
    a_seq, b_seq = history[-1]
    a_batch = alpha_0 + k_total
    b_batch = beta_0 + (n - k_total)
    assert a_seq == a_batch and b_seq == b_batch, "Mismatch!"
    print(f"Sequential final: Beta({a_seq}, {b_seq})")
    print(f"Batch result:     Beta({a_batch}, {b_batch})")
    print("Sequential and batch updating agree!")

    return history

def compare_conjugate_updates():
    """
    Visualize all four conjugate pairs updating from prior to posterior.
    """
    np.random.seed(42)
    fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(14, 10))

    # 1. Beta-Binomial
    ax = axes[0, 0]
    theta = np.linspace(0.001, 0.999, 500)
    for n_obs, color, ls in [(0, '#95a5a6', '--'), (5, '#f39c12', '-'),
                              (20, '#e67e22', '-'), (100, '#e74c3c', '-')]:
        k = int(0.6 * n_obs)  # ~60% success rate
        a, b = 2 + k, 2 + n_obs - k
        ax.plot(theta, stats.beta(a, b).pdf(theta), ls, color=color,
                lw=2, label=f'n={n_obs}: Beta({a},{b})')
    ax.set_title('Beta-Binomial', fontsize=13, fontweight='bold')
    ax.set_xlabel(r'$\theta$')
    ax.legend(fontsize=8)

    # 2. Normal-Normal
    ax = axes[0, 1]
    x = np.linspace(-5, 12, 500)
    mu_0, sig0sq, sigsq = 0.0, 9.0, 4.0
    for n_obs, color, ls in [(0, '#95a5a6', '--'), (1, '#f39c12', '-'),
                              (10, '#e67e22', '-'), (100, '#e74c3c', '-')]:
        data = np.random.normal(5.0, np.sqrt(sigsq), max(n_obs, 1))[:n_obs] if n_obs > 0 else []
        if n_obs == 0:
            mu_n, sig_n_sq = mu_0, sig0sq
        else:
            mu_n, sig_n_sq = normal_normal_update(mu_0, sig0sq, sigsq, data)
        ax.plot(x, stats.norm(mu_n, np.sqrt(sig_n_sq)).pdf(x), ls,
                color=color, lw=2,
                label=f'n={n_obs}: N({mu_n:.1f}, {sig_n_sq:.2f})')
    ax.set_title('Normal-Normal', fontsize=13, fontweight='bold')
    ax.set_xlabel(r'$\mu$')
    ax.legend(fontsize=8)

    # 3. Gamma-Poisson
    ax = axes[1, 0]
    lam = np.linspace(10, 55, 500)
    for n_obs, color, ls in [(0, '#95a5a6', '--'), (3, '#f39c12', '-'),
                              (10, '#e67e22', '-'), (50, '#e74c3c', '-')]:
        data = np.random.poisson(30, size=max(n_obs, 1))[:n_obs] if n_obs > 0 else []
        a = 30 + (np.sum(data) if n_obs > 0 else 0)
        b = 1 + n_obs
        ax.plot(lam, stats.gamma(a, scale=1/b).pdf(lam), ls, color=color,
                lw=2, label=f'n={n_obs}: Gamma({a},{b})')
    ax.set_title('Gamma-Poisson', fontsize=13, fontweight='bold')
    ax.set_xlabel(r'$\lambda$')
    ax.legend(fontsize=8)

    # 4. Dirichlet-Multinomial (show posterior means)
    ax = axes[1, 1]
    cats = ['A', 'B', 'C', 'D']
    true_probs = [0.4, 0.3, 0.2, 0.1]
    alpha_0 = np.ones(4)
    x_pos = np.arange(len(cats))
    width = 0.2
    for i, (n_obs, color) in enumerate([(0, '#95a5a6'), (10, '#f39c12'),
                                         (50, '#e67e22'), (200, '#e74c3c')]):
        counts = np.random.multinomial(n_obs, true_probs) if n_obs > 0 else np.zeros(4)
        alpha_post = alpha_0 + counts
        means = alpha_post / alpha_post.sum()
        ax.bar(x_pos + i*width, means, width, color=color, alpha=0.8,
               label=f'n={n_obs}')
    ax.set_xticks(x_pos + 1.5*width)
    ax.set_xticklabels(cats)
    ax.set_title('Dirichlet-Multinomial', fontsize=13, fontweight='bold')
    ax.set_ylabel('Posterior mean probability')
    ax.legend(fontsize=8)

    plt.suptitle('Conjugate Updates: Prior to Posterior Evolution',
                 fontsize=15, fontweight='bold')
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()

Exercises & Progress

Test your understanding of conjugate distributions, from basic update rules to deeper structural insights.

Easy 1. Beta-Binomial Sequential Update

You start with a $\text{Beta}(1, 1)$ prior (uniform over $[0,1]$) for a coin's probability of heads. You flip the coin 12 times and observe: H, H, T, H, T, H, H, H, T, H, H, T.

  1. What is the posterior distribution after all 12 flips?
  2. Compute the posterior mean and the MLE. How do they compare?
  3. Write down the posterior after each of the first 5 flips (sequential updating). Verify that the result after flip 5 matches what you would get by processing all 5 observations at once.
  4. Compute the 90% credible interval for $\theta$ using the final posterior. Does it contain $\theta = 0.5$?
Medium 2. Normal-Normal with Different Prior Strengths

Suppose you observe $n = 10$ data points from $\mathcal{N}(\mu, 4)$ (known $\sigma^2 = 4$) with sample mean $\bar{x} = 3.2$.

  1. For each of the following priors, compute the posterior mean $\mu_n$ and posterior variance $\sigma_n^2$:
    • (a) Vague prior: $\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 100)$
    • (b) Moderate prior: $\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 4)$
    • (c) Strong prior: $\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.25)$
  2. For each case, express the posterior mean as a weighted average of $\mu_0 = 0$ and $\bar{x} = 3.2$. What are the weights?
  3. Which prior leads to the posterior closest to the MLE? Which leads to the posterior closest to the prior mean? Explain intuitively why.
Medium 3. Gamma-Poisson Arrival Rate Estimation

A website records the number of visitor sessions per hour. Over 8 hours, the counts are: $[42, 38, 55, 47, 51, 44, 36, 49]$.

  1. Using a $\text{Gamma}(50, 1)$ prior (encoding a prior belief of about 50 visits/hour), derive the posterior distribution for the arrival rate $\lambda$.
  2. Compute the posterior mean, the MLE, and the prior mean. Show that the posterior mean lies between the MLE and prior mean.
  3. Posterior predictive: The posterior predictive distribution for the count in the next hour is a Negative Binomial. Specifically, if $\lambda | \mathbf{x} \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha', \beta')$, then $$P(x^* = k) = \binom{k + \alpha' - 1}{k} \left(\frac{\beta'}{\beta' + 1}\right)^{\alpha'} \left(\frac{1}{\beta' + 1}\right)^k$$ Compute the predictive mean and variance for the next hour's count. Why is the predictive variance larger than what you would get by plugging in the posterior mean?
Hard 4. Exponential Family and Conjugacy Proof

Consider a single-parameter exponential family likelihood: $$P(x | \eta) = h(x) \exp\!\big(\eta \, T(x) - A(\eta)\big)$$ with conjugate prior $P(\eta | \chi, \nu) \propto \exp(\chi \eta - \nu A(\eta))$.

  1. Show that after observing $x_1, \ldots, x_n$, the posterior is in the same conjugate family with updated hyperparameters $\chi' = \chi + \sum_{i=1}^{n} T(x_i)$ and $\nu' = \nu + n$.
  2. Verify this general result for the Bernoulli distribution: identify $\eta$, $T(x)$, $A(\eta)$, and $h(x)$. Show that the conjugate prior is the Beta distribution by matching the general form to the Beta PDF.
  3. Do the same for the Poisson distribution: show that the conjugate prior is Gamma.
  4. Bonus: The posterior mean of $\eta$ satisfies $\mathbb{E}[\nabla_\eta A(\eta) | \mathcal{D}] = \frac{\chi + \sum T(x_i)}{\nu + n}$. Use the fact that $\nabla_\eta A(\eta) = \mathbb{E}[T(x) | \eta]$ (a property of exponential families) to interpret this equation. What does it say about the relationship between sufficient statistics and posterior expectations?
