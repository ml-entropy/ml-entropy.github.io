06. The Central Limit Theorem

The most important theorem in probability and statistics. We prove why the average of many independent random variables is approximately Normal, regardless of the original distribution — and explore what this means for machine learning.

Why Is the Normal Distribution Everywhere?

Human heights, measurement errors, stock returns, test scores, pixel noise — the Normal (Gaussian) distribution appears everywhere in nature and science. This is not a coincidence. The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) explains why: whenever a quantity is the sum (or average) of many small, independent effects, it will be approximately Normally distributed.

Analogy: Imagine a ball falling through a Galton board (peg board). At each peg, the ball randomly bounces left or right — a tiny independent push. After many pegs, the ball's final position is the sum of many small random displacements. The resulting distribution of final positions is a perfect bell curve. It doesn't matter what the individual bounces look like — as long as they're independent and small, the sum converges to a Gaussian.

A Brief History

De Moivre (1733) proved the first version for coin flips. Laplace (1812) generalized it. Lindeberg and Feller (1920s-30s) established the modern conditions. The theorem has been called "the informal sovereign of probability theory" by the mathematician Pólya.

The Formal Statement

Central Limit Theorem (Classical / Lindeberg-Lévy)

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ be independent and identically distributed (iid) random variables with:

  • Finite mean: $\mathbb{E}[X_i] = \mu$
  • Finite variance: $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 > 0$

Define the sample mean $\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$. Then as $n \to \infty$:

$$\frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$$

Equivalently: $\bar{X}_n \overset{d}{\approx} \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)$ for large $n$. The notation $\xrightarrow{d}$ means convergence in distribution: the CDF of the left-hand side converges pointwise to the standard Normal CDF $\Phi(x)$.

Notice the remarkable features:

  • No distributional assumption: The $X_i$ can be Bernoulli, Poisson, Exponential, Uniform — anything with finite variance. The sum always approaches Normal.
  • Rate: The standard deviation of $\bar{X}_n$ shrinks as $\sigma / \sqrt{n}$. This is the origin of the $\sqrt{n}$ that appears throughout statistics.
  • Both conditions are necessary: Infinite variance (e.g., Cauchy distribution) breaks the CLT. The sum of Cauchy random variables is still Cauchy, not Normal.

Visual Demonstration

The best way to appreciate the CLT is to see it in action. Take samples from wildly non-Normal distributions, compute their averages, and watch the bell curve emerge.

The progression is striking: $n = 1$ is a flat rectangle (Uniform), $n = 2$ is a triangle, and by $n = 5$ the shape is nearly indistinguishable from a Gaussian. By $n = 30$, the match is essentially perfect. This works for any starting distribution with finite variance.

Proof via Characteristic Functions

The most elegant proof uses characteristic functions (the Fourier transform of a distribution). The key idea: the characteristic function of a sum of independent variables is the product of their characteristic functions, and products are easy to analyze via logarithms.

Characteristic Function

The characteristic function of a random variable $X$ is: $$\phi_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{itX}] = \int e^{itx} f(x) \, dx$$ This always exists (unlike the MGF) and uniquely determines the distribution. For the standard Normal: $\phi_Z(t) = e^{-t^2/2}$.

CLT Proof Sketch
1
Standardize: Let $Y_i = \frac{X_i - \mu}{\sigma}$ so that $\mathbb{E}[Y_i] = 0$, $\text{Var}(Y_i) = 1$. Define $Z_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i = \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}$.
2
Characteristic function of $Z_n$: Since the $Y_i$ are iid: $$\phi_{Z_n}(t) = \left[\phi_Y\!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\right]^n$$

This uses two facts: (a) the CF of a sum of independent variables is the product of their CFs, and (b) scaling $Y$ by $1/\sqrt{n}$ scales the CF argument by $1/\sqrt{n}$.

3
Taylor expand $\phi_Y$ around 0: Since $\mathbb{E}[Y] = 0$ and $\mathbb{E}[Y^2] = 1$: $$\phi_Y(s) = 1 + is \cdot \mathbb{E}[Y] + \frac{(is)^2}{2}\mathbb{E}[Y^2] + o(s^2) = 1 - \frac{s^2}{2} + o(s^2)$$
4
Substitute $s = t/\sqrt{n}$: $$\phi_Y\!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right) = 1 - \frac{t^2}{2n} + o\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)$$
5
Take the $n$-th power: Using $\lim_{n\to\infty}(1 + a/n)^n = e^a$: $$\phi_{Z_n}(t) = \left[1 - \frac{t^2}{2n} + o\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)\right]^n \xrightarrow{n \to \infty} e^{-t^2/2}$$
6
Recognize the limit: $e^{-t^2/2}$ is the characteristic function of $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$. By Lévy's continuity theorem, convergence of characteristic functions implies convergence in distribution: $$\boxed{Z_n = \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, 1)}$$
Why Characteristic Functions?

Moment generating functions (MGFs) don't always exist (e.g., for heavy-tailed distributions). Characteristic functions always exist because $|e^{itx}| = 1$ for all real $t, x$. Moreover, Lévy's continuity theorem gives us the convergence in distribution for free. This is why the CF-based proof is the standard one.

Rate of Convergence: Berry-Esseen

The CLT tells us the convergence happens, but how fast? This is critical for practice: is $n = 30$ enough? $n = 100$? The Berry-Esseen theorem gives a quantitative bound.

Berry-Esseen Theorem

If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are iid with $\mathbb{E}[X] = 0$, $\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \sigma^2$, and $\mathbb{E}[|X|^3] = \rho < \infty$, then:

$$\sup_x \left|F_n(x) - \Phi(x)\right| \leq \frac{C \cdot \rho}{\sigma^3 \sqrt{n}}$$

where $F_n$ is the CDF of the standardized sum, $\Phi$ is the standard Normal CDF, and $C \leq 0.4748$ is a universal constant. The bound is $O(1/\sqrt{n})$.

Key implications:

  • Heavier tails → slower convergence: The ratio $\rho / \sigma^3$ measures "how far from Gaussian" the original distribution is. Distributions with large third absolute moments (skewed or heavy-tailed) converge more slowly.
  • Symmetric distributions converge faster: Symmetry zeroes out the third moment, effectively improving the rate to $O(1/n)$.
  • The $\sqrt{n}$ rule of thumb: For "nice" distributions, $n \geq 30$ gives a good Normal approximation. For skewed distributions (e.g., Exponential), you may need $n \geq 100$.

The Multivariate CLT

The CLT extends naturally to vector-valued random variables.

Multivariate Central Limit Theorem

Let $\mathbf{X}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{X}_n$ be iid random vectors in $\mathbb{R}^d$ with mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$. Then:

$$\sqrt{n}\left(\bar{\mathbf{X}}_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}\right) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\left(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\right)$$

The proof follows the same CF approach, using the multivariate characteristic function $\phi(\mathbf{t}) = \mathbb{E}[e^{i\mathbf{t}^T\mathbf{X}}]$ and the Cramér-Wold device (convergence of all linear combinations implies joint convergence).

Applications in Machine Learning

The CLT is not just a theoretical curiosity — it underpins much of modern ML practice.

1. Stochastic Gradient Descent

SGD estimates the full gradient $\nabla \mathcal{L}$ using a mini-batch average: $\hat{g} = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^B \nabla \ell_i$. By the CLT, this estimate is approximately Normal: $\hat{g} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\nabla\mathcal{L}, \frac{\Sigma}{B}\right)$. This justifies why SGD works and why larger batches give more stable gradients (variance decreases as $1/B$).

2. Confidence Intervals and Hypothesis Testing

The CLT lets us construct confidence intervals for any population mean: $\bar{X}_n \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}$. This is the basis of A/B testing, statistical significance testing, and model comparison.

3. Why Normal Priors Are Natural

In Bayesian inference, placing Normal priors on parameters is not arbitrary. If a parameter is influenced by many small independent factors (which is often the case for neural network weights initialized randomly), the CLT tells us its prior distribution should be approximately Normal.

4. Batch Normalization

BatchNorm computes the mean and variance of activations across a mini-batch. The CLT ensures these batch statistics are approximately Normally distributed around the true population statistics, which is why BatchNorm works well even with moderate batch sizes.

When the CLT Fails

The CLT requires finite variance. This fails for:
- Cauchy distribution: $E[X^2] = \infty$. The average of $n$ Cauchy variables has the same distribution as a single Cauchy — no convergence.
- Pareto with $\alpha \leq 2$: Infinite variance. The appropriate limit theorem is the Generalized CLT, where sums converge to stable distributions (not Gaussian).
- Dependent data: For time series or spatial data, we need specialized versions (e.g., CLT for mixing processes, martingale CLT).

Central Limit Theorem in Python

Simulations and visualizations demonstrating the CLT in action.

CLT Demonstration for Different Source Distributions

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats

def demonstrate_clt(dist_name, sampler, true_mean, true_var,
                    sample_sizes=[1, 2, 5, 10, 30, 100],
                    n_simulations=10000):
    """
    Demonstrate the CLT by plotting the distribution of sample means
    for increasing sample sizes.
    """
    fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 3, figsize=(15, 8))
    axes = axes.flatten()

    for idx, n in enumerate(sample_sizes):
        # Draw n_simulations samples of size n, compute means
        samples = sampler(size=(n_simulations, n))
        means = samples.mean(axis=1)

        # Standardize
        std_means = (means - true_mean) / (np.sqrt(true_var / n))

        ax = axes[idx]
        ax.hist(std_means, bins=50, density=True, alpha=0.7,
                color='#3498db', edgecolor='white', linewidth=0.5)

        # Overlay standard Normal
        x = np.linspace(-4, 4, 200)
        ax.plot(x, stats.norm.pdf(x), 'r-', linewidth=2,
                label='N(0,1)')

        ax.set_title(f'n = {n}', fontsize=13, fontweight='bold')
        ax.set_xlim(-4, 4)
        ax.legend(fontsize=9)

    plt.suptitle(f'CLT: Sample means from {dist_name}',
                 fontsize=15, fontweight='bold')
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()


# 1. Uniform[0, 1]: mean=0.5, var=1/12
demonstrate_clt('Uniform[0,1]',
                lambda size: np.random.uniform(0, 1, size),
                true_mean=0.5, true_var=1/12)

# 2. Exponential(1): mean=1, var=1 (very skewed!)
demonstrate_clt('Exponential(1)',
                lambda size: np.random.exponential(1, size),
                true_mean=1.0, true_var=1.0)

# 3. Bernoulli(0.3): mean=0.3, var=0.21 (discrete!)
demonstrate_clt('Bernoulli(0.3)',
                lambda size: np.random.binomial(1, 0.3, size),
                true_mean=0.3, true_var=0.21)

Berry-Esseen Bound Visualization

def berry_esseen_demo():
    """
    Compare the actual CDF of standardized sample means
    to the Normal CDF, and overlay the Berry-Esseen bound.
    """
    np.random.seed(42)
    fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 5))

    # Exponential(1): rho = E[|X-1|^3] = 2 + e^(-1) ≈ 2.37, sigma=1
    sample_sizes = [5, 30, 200]
    n_sim = 50000

    for idx, n in enumerate(sample_sizes):
        samples = np.random.exponential(1.0, (n_sim, n))
        means = samples.mean(axis=1)
        standardized = (means - 1.0) / (1.0 / np.sqrt(n))

        # Empirical CDF
        x_sorted = np.sort(standardized)
        ecdf = np.arange(1, n_sim + 1) / n_sim

        # Normal CDF
        x_range = np.linspace(-4, 4, 500)
        normal_cdf = stats.norm.cdf(x_range)

        ax = axes[idx]
        ax.plot(x_sorted, ecdf, color='#3498db', linewidth=1.5,
                label='Empirical CDF')
        ax.plot(x_range, normal_cdf, 'r--', linewidth=1.5,
                label='Normal CDF')

        # Berry-Esseen bound
        rho = 2.0  # approximate E[|X-1|^3] for Exp(1)
        C = 0.4748
        bound = C * rho / np.sqrt(n)
        ax.fill_between(x_range, normal_cdf - bound, normal_cdf + bound,
                        alpha=0.15, color='red', label=f'B-E bound: {bound:.3f}')

        ax.set_title(f'n = {n}', fontsize=13, fontweight='bold')
        ax.set_xlim(-4, 4)
        ax.legend(fontsize=8)

    plt.suptitle('Berry-Esseen: Exponential(1) sample means',
                 fontsize=14, fontweight='bold')
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()

CLT Failure: The Cauchy Distribution

def cauchy_no_clt():
    """
    Show that the CLT does NOT hold for the Cauchy distribution
    (infinite variance). The average of n Cauchy variables
    is still Cauchy — no convergence to Normal.
    """
    np.random.seed(42)
    fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 4))

    for idx, n in enumerate([1, 10, 1000]):
        samples = np.random.standard_cauchy((50000, n))
        means = samples.mean(axis=1)

        # Clip for visualization (Cauchy has extreme outliers)
        means_clipped = means[(means > -10) & (means < 10)]

        ax = axes[idx]
        ax.hist(means_clipped, bins=100, density=True, alpha=0.7,
                color='#e74c3c', edgecolor='white', linewidth=0.5)

        # Overlay Cauchy PDF (which should match)
        x = np.linspace(-10, 10, 500)
        ax.plot(x, stats.cauchy.pdf(x), 'k-', linewidth=2,
                label='Cauchy PDF')
        ax.plot(x, stats.norm.pdf(x), 'b--', linewidth=1.5,
                label='Normal PDF')

        ax.set_title(f'n = {n}', fontsize=13, fontweight='bold')
        ax.set_xlim(-10, 10)
        ax.legend(fontsize=8)

    plt.suptitle('CLT FAILS for Cauchy: Average stays Cauchy',
                 fontsize=14, fontweight='bold')
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()

SGD Gradient Distribution

def sgd_clt_demo():
    """
    Demonstrate that mini-batch gradient estimates are
    approximately Normal by the CLT.
    """
    np.random.seed(42)

    # Simulate: true gradient = 2.5, individual gradients are Gamma-distributed
    # (skewed, definitely not Normal)
    true_grad = 2.5
    shape_param = 2.5  # Gamma shape (mean = shape/rate)

    n_sim = 20000
    batch_sizes = [1, 4, 16, 64, 256]

    fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, len(batch_sizes), figsize=(18, 3.5))

    for idx, B in enumerate(batch_sizes):
        # Individual per-sample gradients: Gamma(shape, rate) with mean=true_grad
        rate = shape_param / true_grad
        individual_grads = np.random.gamma(shape_param, 1/rate, (n_sim, B))
        batch_means = individual_grads.mean(axis=1)

        ax = axes[idx]
        ax.hist(batch_means, bins=50, density=True, alpha=0.7,
                color='#2ecc71', edgecolor='white')

        # CLT approximation
        pop_var = shape_param / (rate ** 2)
        x = np.linspace(batch_means.min(), batch_means.max(), 200)
        ax.plot(x, stats.norm.pdf(x, true_grad, np.sqrt(pop_var / B)),
                'r-', linewidth=2)

        ax.set_title(f'Batch size = {B}', fontsize=11, fontweight='bold')
        ax.axvline(true_grad, color='k', linestyle=':', alpha=0.5)

    plt.suptitle('Mini-batch gradients approach Normal (CLT)',
                 fontsize=13, fontweight='bold')
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()

Exercises & Progress

Test your understanding of the Central Limit Theorem and its applications.

Easy 1. CLT for Dice

You roll a fair six-sided die $n = 36$ times and compute the average.

  1. What is the mean and variance of a single die roll?
  2. Using the CLT, approximate the probability that the average exceeds 4.0.
  3. How large must $n$ be for the probability $P(\bar{X}_n > 4) < 0.01$?
Easy 2. Normal Approximation to Binomial

A coin with $p = 0.4$ is flipped $n = 100$ times. Let $S = \sum X_i$ be the total heads.

  1. What is $E[S]$ and $\text{Var}(S)$?
  2. Using the CLT, approximate $P(35 \leq S \leq 45)$.
  3. Compare your answer to the exact Binomial probability (use Python if needed).
Medium 3. SGD Variance and Batch Size

In SGD, the mini-batch gradient estimate is $\hat{g}_B = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^B \nabla\ell_i$.

  1. Show that $\text{Var}(\hat{g}_B) = \frac{\sigma^2_g}{B}$ where $\sigma^2_g$ is the per-sample gradient variance.
  2. By the CLT, $\hat{g}_B$ is approximately Normal. What is its distribution?
  3. If you want to halve the standard deviation of the gradient estimate, by what factor must you increase the batch size? Comment on the efficiency implications.
Medium 4. Berry-Esseen for Bernoulli

For $X_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$:

  1. Compute $\sigma^2 = p(1-p)$ and $\rho = \mathbb{E}[|X - p|^3]$.
  2. Show that the Berry-Esseen bound is tightest (worst) when $p = 0.5$ and loosest when $p$ is near 0 or 1.
  3. For $p = 0.5$ and $p = 0.01$, how large must $n$ be for the Berry-Esseen bound to guarantee the CDF error is below 0.01?
Hard 5. CLT Proof: Complete the Details

Fill in the gaps of the CLT proof from the Theory tab:

  1. Prove that $\phi_Y(s) = 1 - \frac{s^2}{2} + o(s^2)$ by expanding $e^{isY}$ in Taylor series and taking expectations term by term. State the conditions under which this term-by-term approach is valid.
  2. Rigorously justify the step $\left(1 - \frac{t^2}{2n} + o(1/n)\right)^n \to e^{-t^2/2}$ using the fact that $\log(1+x) = x + o(x)$ for small $x$.
  3. State Lévy's continuity theorem precisely and explain why pointwise convergence of $\phi_{Z_n}(t) \to e^{-t^2/2}$ is sufficient for convergence in distribution.
