03. Multivariate Distributions

Real-world data lives in high dimensions. Height correlates with weight. Stock prices move together. Pixel intensities depend on their neighbors. To model these relationships, we need distributions over multiple random variables simultaneously — and the mathematical machinery of joint, marginal, and conditional distributions.

Why Multivariate?

In tutorials 01 and 02 we studied distributions of a single random variable: the number of heads in 10 flips, or the time until the next bus arrives. But almost every real dataset has multiple features, and those features are typically correlated.

Consider a dataset of human body measurements. Height $X$ and weight $Y$ are not independent — taller people tend to weigh more. Knowing someone's height changes your prediction of their weight. A univariate Normal for height and a separate univariate Normal for weight cannot capture this dependency. We need a joint distribution $f(x, y)$ that encodes both the individual behavior of each variable and their relationship.

The Core Questions

Multivariate distribution theory answers three fundamental questions:
1. Joint: What is the probability of $X$ and $Y$ occurring together?
2. Marginal: If I only care about $X$, how do I "remove" $Y$?
3. Conditional: Given that I know $Y = y$, what can I say about $X$?

Joint Distributions

A joint distribution describes the simultaneous behavior of two or more random variables. It assigns a probability (discrete case) or a probability density (continuous case) to every combination of outcomes.

Joint PMF (Discrete)

For discrete random variables $X$ and $Y$, the joint probability mass function is:

$$P(X = x, Y = y) = p(x, y) \quad \text{for all } x, y$$

satisfying $p(x, y) \geq 0$ and $\displaystyle\sum_x \sum_y p(x, y) = 1$.

Joint PDF (Continuous)

For continuous random variables $X$ and $Y$, the joint probability density function is a function $f(x, y)$ satisfying:

$$f(x, y) \geq 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x, y)\, dx\, dy = 1$$

Probabilities are obtained by integrating: $P(a \leq X \leq b, \; c \leq Y \leq d) = \int_a^b \int_c^d f(x,y)\, dy\, dx$.

Key insight: A joint distribution contains all probabilistic information about the relationship between variables. Everything else — marginals, conditionals, expectations, correlations — can be derived from it.

Marginal Distributions

Often we have a joint distribution but only care about one of the variables. Marginalization is the process of "summing out" (or "integrating out") the variables we do not care about.

The intuition is simple: to find the probability of $X = x$ regardless of $Y$, we add up the joint probability over every possible value of $Y$. We are, in a sense, projecting the joint distribution onto the $X$-axis.

Deriving the Marginal
1
Start from the law of total probability. The event $\{X = x\}$ can be partitioned by the values of $Y$: $$P(X = x) = \sum_y P(X = x, Y = y)$$
2
Discrete case: Sum over all values of $Y$: $$p_X(x) = \sum_y p(x, y)$$
3
Continuous case: Replace the sum with an integral: $$f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x, y) \, dy$$
4
Symmetrically for $Y$: $$f_Y(y) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x, y) \, dx$$

A helpful mantra: "Marginalization is summing over what you don't care about." This principle extends to any number of variables — to get the marginal over $(X_1, X_3)$ from a joint over $(X_1, X_2, X_3, X_4)$, integrate out $X_2$ and $X_4$.

Caution: Information Loss

Marginalization is a lossy operation. Knowing the marginals $f_X(x)$ and $f_Y(y)$ is not enough to reconstruct the joint $f(x,y)$, unless $X$ and $Y$ are independent. The marginals tell you about each variable in isolation but lose all information about their relationship.

Conditional Distributions

If marginalization answers "what if I ignore $Y$?", the conditional distribution answers the opposite: "what if I know $Y = y$?" This is the foundation of prediction, Bayesian inference, and generative modeling.

Conditional Distribution

The conditional density of $Y$ given $X = x$ is:

$$f_{Y|X}(y \mid x) = \frac{f(x, y)}{f_X(x)}, \quad \text{provided } f_X(x) > 0$$

This is a proper density in $y$: it integrates to 1 over $y$ for each fixed $x$.

Interpretation: We "slice" the joint distribution at $X = x$, then renormalize so the slice integrates to 1. The denominator $f_X(x)$ is exactly the normalizing constant needed.

Connection to Bayes' Theorem
1
Start from the definition of conditional density. We can write the joint in two ways: $$f(x, y) = f_{Y|X}(y|x) \cdot f_X(x) = f_{X|Y}(x|y) \cdot f_Y(y)$$
2
Equate and solve for $f_{X|Y}(x|y)$: $$f_{X|Y}(x \mid y) = \frac{f_{Y|X}(y \mid x) \cdot f_X(x)}{f_Y(y)}$$
3
This is Bayes' theorem in continuous form. Replacing $X$ with parameters $\theta$ and $Y$ with data $\mathcal{D}$ gives exactly the posterior from Bayesian inference: $$\underbrace{f(\theta | \mathcal{D})}_{\text{posterior}} = \frac{\overbrace{f(\mathcal{D}|\theta)}^{\text{likelihood}} \cdot \overbrace{f(\theta)}^{\text{prior}}}{\underbrace{f(\mathcal{D})}_{\text{evidence}}}$$

Independence

Two random variables are independent if knowing one tells you absolutely nothing about the other. This is simultaneously the simplest and most powerful structural assumption in probability.

Independence of Random Variables

$X$ and $Y$ are independent (written $X \perp Y$) if and only if:

$$f(x, y) = f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y) \quad \text{for all } x, y$$

Equivalent conditions (any one implies the others):
(a) $f_{Y|X}(y|x) = f_Y(y)$ for all $x, y$
(b) $f_{X|Y}(x|y) = f_X(x)$ for all $x, y$
(c) $E[g(X)h(Y)] = E[g(X)] \cdot E[h(Y)]$ for all functions $g, h$

Independence means the joint factorizes as a product of marginals. This is a massive simplification — instead of modeling an entire 2D surface, you only need two 1D functions. In high dimensions, independence assumptions are what make computation tractable (e.g., the naive Bayes classifier assumes features are independent given the class).

Conditional Independence

Variables that are not independent overall can become independent once we condition on a third variable. We write $X \perp Y \mid Z$ to mean: $$f(x, y \mid z) = f(x \mid z) \cdot f(y \mid z)$$ Example: Height and vocabulary size are correlated (both increase with age). But given a person's age, they become approximately independent. The apparent correlation was induced by the confounding variable "age." Conditional independence is the backbone of graphical models (Bayesian networks, Markov random fields).

Covariance and Correlation

We need a way to quantify how two random variables relate. Covariance and correlation measure the linear dependence between variables.

Deriving Covariance
1
Definition. The covariance of $X$ and $Y$ is the expected value of their centered product: $$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E\big[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])\big]$$
2
Expand the product: $$= E[XY - X \cdot E[Y] - E[X] \cdot Y + E[X] \cdot E[Y]]$$
3
Use linearity of expectation: $$= E[XY] - E[X] \cdot E[Y] - E[X] \cdot E[Y] + E[X] \cdot E[Y]$$
4
Simplify to the computational formula: $$\boxed{\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[XY] - E[X] \cdot E[Y]}$$

Covariance has units of (units of $X$) $\times$ (units of $Y$), which makes it hard to interpret across scales. Correlation normalizes this:

Pearson Correlation Coefficient

$$\rho(X, Y) = \frac{\text{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \cdot \sigma_Y}$$

where $\sigma_X = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}$ and $\sigma_Y = \sqrt{\text{Var}(Y)}$. The correlation satisfies $-1 \leq \rho \leq 1$.
$\rho = 1$: perfect positive linear relationship
$\rho = -1$: perfect negative linear relationship
$\rho = 0$: no linear relationship (uncorrelated)

Key properties of covariance:

  • $\text{Cov}(X, X) = \text{Var}(X)$
  • $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = \text{Cov}(Y, X)$ (symmetric)
  • $\text{Cov}(aX + b, \; cY + d) = ac \cdot \text{Cov}(X, Y)$ (bilinear)
  • If $X \perp Y$, then $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = 0$ (independence implies zero covariance)
  • $\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) + 2\text{Cov}(X, Y)$
Zero Correlation Does NOT Imply Independence

This is one of the most important subtleties in probability. Independence implies zero correlation, but the converse is false.

Classic counterexample: Let $X \sim \text{Uniform}(-1, 1)$ and $Y = X^2$. Then $Y$ is completely determined by $X$ (maximal dependence!), yet:

$$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[XY] - E[X] \cdot E[Y] = E[X^3] - 0 \cdot E[X^2] = 0$$

because $E[X^3] = 0$ by symmetry of the uniform distribution. Correlation only captures linear relationships. The quadratic dependence $Y = X^2$ is invisible to it. This is why assuming independence based on zero correlation alone can be dangerously wrong.

The Multivariate Normal Distribution

The multivariate Normal (MVN) is the most important multivariate distribution in statistics and machine learning. It generalizes the familiar bell curve to $d$ dimensions and is entirely characterized by a mean vector $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and a covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$.

Multivariate Normal Distribution

A random vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ follows a multivariate Normal, written $\mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})$, if its density is:

$$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2} |\boldsymbol{\Sigma}|^{1/2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^T \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})\right)$$

where $\boldsymbol{\mu} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is the mean vector and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is a symmetric positive definite covariance matrix.

Understanding Each Component
1
The normalization constant $\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2} |\boldsymbol{\Sigma}|^{1/2}}$ ensures the density integrates to 1. The factor $(2\pi)^{d/2}$ generalizes $\sqrt{2\pi}$ from the univariate case to $d$ dimensions. The determinant $|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}|$ measures the "volume" of the covariance ellipsoid — larger variance means the density must be lower at the center to still integrate to 1.
2
The Mahalanobis distance $\Delta^2 = (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^T \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})$ measures how far $\mathbf{x}$ is from the mean, accounting for the covariance structure. Unlike Euclidean distance, it stretches and rotates space so that one unit of Mahalanobis distance corresponds to one standard deviation in every direction. Contours of constant density are ellipsoids of constant Mahalanobis distance.
3
The exponential $\exp(-\frac{1}{2}\Delta^2)$ produces the bell-shaped decay: density drops off as a Gaussian function of the Mahalanobis distance. Points at Mahalanobis distance $k$ from the mean lie on the $k$-sigma ellipsoid.
4
Connection to $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}$: If we eigendecompose $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = Q \Lambda Q^T$, then $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} = Q \Lambda^{-1} Q^T$. The eigenvectors of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ give the principal axes of the density ellipsoids, and the eigenvalues give the variance along each axis. This is the geometric link between the MVN and PCA.

The three cases illustrate how the covariance matrix controls the geometry of the MVN:

  • Spherical ($\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \sigma^2 I$): Equal variance in all directions, no correlation. Contours are circles. Each component is independent with the same variance.
  • Diagonal ($\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \text{diag}(\sigma_1^2, \sigma_2^2)$): Different variances along axes, but no correlation. Contours are axis-aligned ellipses. Components are independent with different variances.
  • Full (general $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$): Off-diagonal entries encode correlation. Contours are tilted ellipses. The tilt angle is determined by the eigenvectors of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$.
Marginals and Conditionals of the MVN

One of the most remarkable properties of the multivariate Normal is that marginal and conditional distributions are also Normal.

Partition $\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{x}_1 \\ \mathbf{x}_2 \end{pmatrix}$ with corresponding mean $\boldsymbol{\mu} = \begin{pmatrix} \boldsymbol{\mu}_1 \\ \boldsymbol{\mu}_2 \end{pmatrix}$ and covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \begin{pmatrix} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{11} & \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{12} \\ \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{21} & \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{22} \end{pmatrix}$. Then:

Marginal: $\mathbf{x}_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_1, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{11})$

Conditional: $\mathbf{x}_1 \mid \mathbf{x}_2 \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_1 + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{12}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{22}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}_2 - \boldsymbol{\mu}_2), \;\; \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{11} - \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{12}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{22}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{21}\right)$

The conditional mean is a linear function of the observed values $\mathbf{x}_2$, and the conditional covariance does not depend on $\mathbf{x}_2$ at all. This is the mathematical foundation of linear regression and Gaussian process prediction.

The MVN in Machine Learning

The multivariate Normal is not just a theoretical construct — it is the workhorse distribution behind many of the most important algorithms in ML:

  • Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs): Model complex, multi-modal distributions as weighted sums of MVNs. Each cluster is an MVN with its own $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k$. The EM algorithm fits these parameters.
  • Gaussian Processes (GPs): Define a distribution over functions by specifying that any finite collection of function values is jointly MVN. The conditional distribution formula above gives exact posterior predictions.
  • Variational Autoencoders (VAEs): The encoder maps inputs to MVN parameters ($\boldsymbol{\mu}$, $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$) in latent space. The reparameterization trick $\mathbf{z} = \boldsymbol{\mu} + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{1/2}\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$, $\boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, I)$, enables gradient-based training.
  • Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA): Assumes each class has an MVN distribution with a shared covariance matrix. The optimal decision boundary is linear — determined by the Mahalanobis distance.
  • Principal Component Analysis (PCA): The eigenvectors of the sample covariance matrix $\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}$ are the principal components. The eigendecomposition $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = Q\Lambda Q^T$ decomposes the variance into orthogonal directions — this is the link between the MVN and linear algebra.
The Eigendecomposition Connection

The covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ is symmetric positive definite, so it has a spectral decomposition $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = Q\Lambda Q^T$ where $Q$ is orthogonal and $\Lambda = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_d)$. In the rotated coordinate system $\mathbf{z} = Q^T(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})$, the components of $\mathbf{z}$ are independent with $z_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \lambda_i)$. Every MVN is really just independent univariate Normals in a rotated frame. This is why linear algebra and distributions are deeply intertwined.

Multivariate Distributions in Python

Complete implementations using NumPy, SciPy, and Matplotlib. We cover sampling from the multivariate Normal, computing marginal and conditional distributions, and visualizing covariance structures.

1. Sampling and Visualizing the Bivariate Normal

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.stats import multivariate_normal

# --- Define three different covariance structures ---
mu = np.array([0, 0])

# Spherical: equal variance, no correlation
Sigma_spherical = np.array([[1, 0],
                             [0, 1]])

# Diagonal: unequal variance, no correlation
Sigma_diagonal = np.array([[3, 0],
                            [0, 0.5]])

# Full: correlated
Sigma_full = np.array([[2.0, 1.2],
                        [1.2, 1.0]])

# Sample from each
np.random.seed(42)
n_samples = 1000
samples_sph = np.random.multivariate_normal(mu, Sigma_spherical, n_samples)
samples_diag = np.random.multivariate_normal(mu, Sigma_diagonal, n_samples)
samples_full = np.random.multivariate_normal(mu, Sigma_full, n_samples)

# --- Contour plots ---
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 5))
x_grid = np.linspace(-4, 4, 200)
y_grid = np.linspace(-4, 4, 200)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x_grid, y_grid)
pos = np.dstack((X, Y))

configs = [
    (Sigma_spherical, samples_sph, "Spherical: $\\Sigma = I$"),
    (Sigma_diagonal, samples_diag, "Diagonal: $\\sigma_1^2 \\neq \\sigma_2^2$"),
    (Sigma_full, samples_full, "Full: $\\sigma_{12} \\neq 0$"),
]

for ax, (Sigma, samples, title) in zip(axes, configs):
    rv = multivariate_normal(mu, Sigma)
    Z = rv.pdf(pos)
    ax.contour(X, Y, Z, levels=6, cmap="Blues", linewidths=1.2)
    ax.scatter(samples[:, 0], samples[:, 1], alpha=0.15, s=5, c="steelblue")
    ax.set_title(title, fontsize=12)
    ax.set_xlabel("$x_1$")
    ax.set_ylabel("$x_2$")
    ax.set_aspect("equal")
    ax.set_xlim(-4, 4)
    ax.set_ylim(-4, 4)

plt.suptitle("Bivariate Normal: Three Covariance Structures", fontsize=14)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

2. Computing Marginal Distributions

import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm, multivariate_normal
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Joint distribution: bivariate Normal
mu = np.array([1.0, 2.0])
Sigma = np.array([[2.0, 1.2],
                   [1.2, 1.5]])

# --- Marginals of MVN are Normal ---
# Marginal of X1: N(mu_1, Sigma_11)
mu_x1, var_x1 = mu[0], Sigma[0, 0]
# Marginal of X2: N(mu_2, Sigma_22)
mu_x2, var_x2 = mu[1], Sigma[1, 1]

print(f"Marginal X1: N({mu_x1}, {var_x1})")
print(f"Marginal X2: N({mu_x2}, {var_x2})")

# --- Verify by sampling ---
np.random.seed(0)
samples = np.random.multivariate_normal(mu, Sigma, 50000)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(12, 4))

# X1 marginal
x1_grid = np.linspace(-3, 5, 300)
axes[0].hist(samples[:, 0], bins=80, density=True, alpha=0.5, label="Samples")
axes[0].plot(x1_grid, norm.pdf(x1_grid, mu_x1, np.sqrt(var_x1)),
             'r-', lw=2, label=f"$N({mu_x1}, {var_x1})$")
axes[0].set_title("Marginal $f_{X_1}(x_1)$")
axes[0].legend()

# X2 marginal
x2_grid = np.linspace(-2, 6, 300)
axes[1].hist(samples[:, 1], bins=80, density=True, alpha=0.5, label="Samples")
axes[1].plot(x2_grid, norm.pdf(x2_grid, mu_x2, np.sqrt(var_x2)),
             'r-', lw=2, label=f"$N({mu_x2}, {var_x2})$")
axes[1].set_title("Marginal $f_{X_2}(x_2)$")
axes[1].legend()

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

3. Computing Conditional Distributions

import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm, multivariate_normal
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Joint: (X1, X2) ~ N(mu, Sigma)
mu = np.array([1.0, 2.0])
Sigma = np.array([[2.0, 1.2],
                   [1.2, 1.5]])

def conditional_normal(mu, Sigma, x2_observed, idx_1=0, idx_2=1):
    """
    Compute the conditional distribution X1 | X2 = x2_observed
    for a bivariate Normal.

    Returns: (conditional_mean, conditional_variance)
    """
    mu1, mu2 = mu[idx_1], mu[idx_2]
    s11 = Sigma[idx_1, idx_1]
    s22 = Sigma[idx_2, idx_2]
    s12 = Sigma[idx_1, idx_2]

    # Conditional mean: mu1 + (s12 / s22) * (x2 - mu2)
    cond_mean = mu1 + (s12 / s22) * (x2_observed - mu2)

    # Conditional variance: s11 - s12^2 / s22
    cond_var = s11 - s12**2 / s22

    return cond_mean, cond_var

# Condition on different values of X2
x2_values = [0.5, 2.0, 3.5]
x1_grid = np.linspace(-4, 6, 300)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))

for x2_obs in x2_values:
    cond_mu, cond_var = conditional_normal(mu, Sigma, x2_obs)
    cond_std = np.sqrt(cond_var)
    label = f"$X_1 | X_2={x2_obs}$: $N({cond_mu:.2f}, {cond_var:.2f})$"
    ax.plot(x1_grid, norm.pdf(x1_grid, cond_mu, cond_std), lw=2, label=label)

# Also plot the marginal for comparison
ax.plot(x1_grid, norm.pdf(x1_grid, mu[0], np.sqrt(Sigma[0, 0])),
        'k--', lw=1.5, alpha=0.5, label=f"Marginal $X_1$: $N({mu[0]}, {Sigma[0,0]})$")

ax.set_xlabel("$x_1$")
ax.set_ylabel("Density")
ax.set_title("Conditional Distributions $X_1 | X_2 = x_2$")
ax.legend()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

print(f"Note: conditional variance = {cond_var:.4f} (same for all x2)")
print(f"      marginal variance    = {Sigma[0,0]:.4f}")
print(f"      Conditioning always REDUCES variance (we gain information).")

4. Joint PMF and Marginalization (Discrete Example)

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

# --- Discrete joint PMF as a table ---
# X = number of defects (0, 1, 2)
# Y = machine type (A=0, B=1)
joint_pmf = np.array([
    [0.15, 0.25],   # X=0: P(X=0,Y=A), P(X=0,Y=B)
    [0.10, 0.20],   # X=1
    [0.05, 0.25],   # X=2
])

# Display as DataFrame
df = pd.DataFrame(joint_pmf,
                  index=["X=0", "X=1", "X=2"],
                  columns=["Y=A", "Y=B"])

# Marginal of X: sum over Y (columns)
marginal_X = joint_pmf.sum(axis=1)
df["P(X=x)"] = marginal_X

# Marginal of Y: sum over X (rows)
marginal_Y = joint_pmf.sum(axis=0)
df.loc["P(Y=y)"] = list(marginal_Y) + [1.0]

print("Joint PMF with Marginals:")
print(df.to_string())
print()

# Conditional: P(X | Y=B) = P(X, Y=B) / P(Y=B)
p_Y_B = marginal_Y[1]
conditional_X_given_B = joint_pmf[:, 1] / p_Y_B
print(f"P(Y=B) = {p_Y_B:.2f}")
print(f"P(X=0 | Y=B) = {conditional_X_given_B[0]:.4f}")
print(f"P(X=1 | Y=B) = {conditional_X_given_B[1]:.4f}")
print(f"P(X=2 | Y=B) = {conditional_X_given_B[2]:.4f}")
print(f"Sum = {conditional_X_given_B.sum():.4f}  (should be 1)")

# Check independence: P(X,Y) = P(X)*P(Y)?
outer_product = np.outer(marginal_X, marginal_Y)
print(f"\nIndependence check: P(X)*P(Y) vs P(X,Y)")
print(f"Max absolute difference: {np.max(np.abs(joint_pmf - outer_product)):.4f}")
print(f"Independent? {np.allclose(joint_pmf, outer_product)}")

5. Zero Correlation but Dependent

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# X ~ Uniform(-1, 1), Y = X^2
np.random.seed(42)
n = 100000
X = np.random.uniform(-1, 1, n)
Y = X**2

# Compute correlation
correlation = np.corrcoef(X, Y)[0, 1]
covariance = np.cov(X, Y)[0, 1]

print(f"Cov(X, Y)  = {covariance:.6f}  (approximately 0)")
print(f"Corr(X, Y) = {correlation:.6f}  (approximately 0)")
print(f"But Y = X^2, so Y is FULLY DETERMINED by X!")
print(f"This proves: zero correlation ≠ independence")

# Visualize
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(12, 5))

axes[0].scatter(X[:2000], Y[:2000], alpha=0.2, s=3)
axes[0].set_xlabel("$X$")
axes[0].set_ylabel("$Y = X^2$")
axes[0].set_title(f"$Y = X^2$: Corr = {correlation:.4f}")

# Compare with truly independent
Y_indep = np.random.uniform(0, 1, n)
axes[1].scatter(X[:2000], Y_indep[:2000], alpha=0.2, s=3)
axes[1].set_xlabel("$X$")
axes[1].set_ylabel("$Y$ (independent)")
axes[1].set_title(f"Independent: Corr = {np.corrcoef(X, Y_indep)[0,1]:.4f}")

plt.suptitle("Zero Correlation Does NOT Imply Independence", fontsize=13)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

  4. Compute $\text{Cov}(X, Y)$ and verify it is consistent with your independence conclusion.
Medium 3. Conditional Distribution of the Bivariate Normal

Let $(X_1, X_2) \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})$ with $\boldsymbol{\mu} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$.

  1. Derive the conditional distribution $X_1 \mid X_2 = 2$ using the formula $\mu_{1|2} = \mu_1 + \frac{\sigma_{12}}{\sigma_{22}}(x_2 - \mu_2)$ and $\sigma^2_{1|2} = \sigma_{11} - \frac{\sigma_{12}^2}{\sigma_{22}}$.
  2. Compute the correlation coefficient $\rho$ between $X_1$ and $X_2$.
  3. Verify that the conditional variance $\sigma^2_{1|2} = \sigma_{11}(1 - \rho^2)$.
  4. Explain intuitively why the conditional variance is always less than the marginal variance.
Medium 4. Show Correlation = 0 Doesn't Imply Independence

Let $X \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ and define $Y = X^2$.

  1. Compute $E[X]$, $E[Y]$, and $E[XY] = E[X^3]$ using properties of the standard Normal.
  2. Show that $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = 0$ and therefore $\rho(X, Y) = 0$.
  3. Now prove that $X$ and $Y$ are not independent by finding an event where $P(X \in A, Y \in B) \neq P(X \in A) \cdot P(Y \in B)$. Hint: Consider $A = \{|X| > 2\}$ and $B = \{Y > 4\}$.
  4. Construct a second example where $X$ is discrete, $Y$ is a deterministic function of $X$, and yet $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = 0$.
Hard 5. Prove Marginals and Conditionals of the MVN are Normal

Let $\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{x}_1 \\ \mathbf{x}_2 \end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})$ with the block structure $\boldsymbol{\mu} = \begin{pmatrix} \boldsymbol{\mu}_1 \\ \boldsymbol{\mu}_2 \end{pmatrix}$, $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \begin{pmatrix} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{11} & \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{12} \\ \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{21} & \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{22} \end{pmatrix}$.

  1. Marginal: Show that $\mathbf{x}_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_1, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{11})$ by integrating out $\mathbf{x}_2$ from the joint density. Hint: Use the block matrix inversion formula (Schur complement) to write the exponent as a sum of a term in $\mathbf{x}_1$ only and a term that is a perfect square in $\mathbf{x}_2$, then integrate the Gaussian in $\mathbf{x}_2$.
  2. Conditional: Derive the conditional distribution $\mathbf{x}_1 \mid \mathbf{x}_2$ by using $f(\mathbf{x}_1 | \mathbf{x}_2) = \frac{f(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2)}{f(\mathbf{x}_2)}$. Show that: $$\mathbf{x}_1 \mid \mathbf{x}_2 \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_1 + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{12}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{22}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}_2 - \boldsymbol{\mu}_2), \;\; \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{11} - \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{12}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{22}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{21}\right)$$
  3. Explain why the conditional covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{11} - \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{12}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{22}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{21}$ (the Schur complement) does not depend on the observed value $\mathbf{x}_2$. What is the geometric interpretation?
  4. Special case: Show that for the bivariate case ($d=2$), the formulas reduce to the scalar conditional mean and variance from Exercise 3.
