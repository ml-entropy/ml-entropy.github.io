The SDE/ODE Framework

Discrete diffusion already works, but the continuous-time viewpoint reveals a deeper structure. Once the number of steps becomes large and each corruption becomes infinitesimal, the forward process is naturally described by a stochastic differential equation rather than a discrete Markov chain. This is not merely an aesthetic preference — it is the difference between working with a finite chain of Gaussian transitions and working with a smooth family of distributions parameterized by continuous time. The continuous picture makes it transparent why the same score network can be used at any noise level, not just the finitely many levels chosen for training, and it opens the door to adaptive step-size integrators that automatically concentrate computation where the dynamics change fastest.

This reframing matters because it imports the full toolkit of stochastic calculus and numerical differential equations. Reverse-time sampling, deterministic probability-flow paths, and exact likelihood formulas all become easier to express in the continuous language. In the SDE picture, the forward process, the reverse process, and the probability-flow ODE are all manifestations of the same underlying object — a time-dependent vector field defined by the score function. The framework was unified in the landmark paper by Song et al. (2021), which showed that DDPM (Ho et al., 2020), SMLD / NCSN (Song & Ermon, 2019), and continuous normalizing flows can all be recovered as special cases of SDEs with particular choices of drift and diffusion coefficients.

8.1 Forward SDE

The discrete forward process becomes a continuous SDE when we let the step size shrink to zero. In the discrete world we wrote $x_{t+1} = \alpha_t x_t + \sigma_t \epsilon$; in the continuous limit this becomes an Itô stochastic differential equation:

$$dx = f(x, t)\, dt + g(t)\, dw$$

Here $f(x,t)$ is the drift coefficient — it specifies the deterministic tendency of the state. The function $g(t)$ is the diffusion coefficient — it controls the intensity of the Brownian noise injected at time $t$. The term $dw$ denotes an infinitesimal Wiener increment, a Gaussian random variable with mean zero and variance $dt$. Together, drift and diffusion fully characterize the law of the process: the drift tells probability mass where to go on average, while the diffusion smears it out.

For VP-SDE: $f(x,t) = -\frac{1}{2}\beta(t)x$, $g(t) = \sqrt{\beta(t)}$. The drift is linear and contractive — it pulls the state toward the origin at a rate proportional to $\beta(t)$. This is what makes the signal shrink over time. The diffusion coefficient $\sqrt{\beta(t)}$ injects noise at a rate that is perfectly balanced so that the total variance of the marginal $p_t(x)$ stays bounded (hence "variance-preserving"). As $t \to \infty$, the marginal approaches a standard Gaussian regardless of the initial data distribution.

8.2 Reverse SDE

The central question of diffusion generative modelling is: given that we know how to corrupt data into noise, can we reverse that corruption? Anderson (1982) answered this in the affirmative, showing that any Itô diffusion running forward in time induces a well-defined reverse-time diffusion with an explicit formula for its drift. The reverse-time SDE is:

$$dx = [f(x,t) - g(t)^2 \nabla_x \log p_t(x)]\, dt + g(t)\, d\bar{w}$$

The score $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ is the only unknown — once learned, we can reverse the process. Notice the structure: the original drift $f(x,t)$ is corrected by a term proportional to $g(t)^2$ times the score. The diffusion coefficient $g(t)$ stays exactly the same as in the forward process, but the Brownian motion $\bar{w}$ now runs backward in time. Intuitively, the score tells us the direction in which the log-density increases most steeply — it points from low-probability regions toward high-probability regions. By following this gradient, the reverse process "un-diffuses" noise back into data. The factor $g(t)^2$ ensures that the correction scales with the amount of noise present: when diffusion is strong, the score correction must also be strong.

Reverse-time SDE (Anderson's theorem)
1
Start from the forward SDE. Assume the forward process obeys $dx = f(x,t)\,dt + g(t)\,dw$ with marginal densities $p_t(x)$. The associated Fokker–Planck equation (also called the Kolmogorov forward equation) describes how the density evolves: $$\frac{\partial p_t}{\partial t} = -\nabla \cdot \bigl(f(x,t)\,p_t\bigr) + \frac{1}{2}g(t)^2 \Delta p_t$$ This PDE is the density-level counterpart of the sample-level SDE.
2
Time-reverse the Fokker–Planck equation. Substitute $\tau = T - t$ so that $\tau$ increases as we go backward. The density $q_\tau(x) = p_{T-\tau}(x)$ must satisfy a Fokker–Planck equation for the reverse process. After rewriting the time derivative and spatial operators, one finds that the reverse Fokker–Planck equation has the form: $$\frac{\partial q_\tau}{\partial \tau} = -\nabla \cdot \Bigl(\bigl[f - g^2 \nabla \log p_t\bigr]\,q_\tau\Bigr) + \frac{1}{2}g^2 \Delta q_\tau$$ The key algebraic step uses the identity $\nabla p_t = p_t \,\nabla \log p_t$ to absorb the second-order term's contribution into a modified drift.
3
Read off the reverse SDE. Since a Fokker–Planck equation of the form $\partial_\tau q = -\nabla\cdot(\tilde{f}\,q) + \frac{1}{2}\tilde{g}^2 \Delta q$ corresponds to the Itô SDE $dx = \tilde{f}\,d\tau + \tilde{g}\,d\bar{w}$, we can directly identify the reverse-time drift and diffusion: $$\tilde{f}(x,t) = f(x,t) - g(t)^2\,\nabla_x \log p_t(x), \qquad \tilde{g}(t) = g(t)$$
4
Interpret each term. The reverse drift $\tilde{f} = f - g^2 \nabla_x \log p_t$ has two parts. The first part, $f$, is the original forward drift — if we naively ran the forward SDE backward, we would get this. The second part, $-g^2 \nabla_x \log p_t$, is the score correction: it accounts for the information destroyed by the forward diffusion. The diffusion coefficient is unchanged ($\tilde{g} = g$), meaning the reverse process is just as noisy as the forward — only the drift direction changes.
5
Practical implication. The only quantity in the reverse SDE that we cannot compute analytically is the score $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$, because it depends on the unknown data distribution. This is precisely what the score network $s_\theta(x,t) \approx \nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ is trained to approximate. Once we plug in the learned score, the reverse SDE becomes a concrete stochastic process we can simulate with standard numerical integrators.

8.3 Probability Flow ODE

A remarkable fact discovered by Song et al. (2021) is that for every SDE there exists a deterministic ordinary differential equation whose solutions have exactly the same marginal distributions $p_t(x)$ at every time $t$. This is the probability-flow ODE:

$$dx = \left[f(x,t) - \frac{1}{2}g(t)^2 \nabla_x \log p_t(x)\right] dt$$

This is equivalent to a continuous normalizing flow — connecting back to Tutorial 17! Notice the structural similarity to the reverse SDE: the drift is identical except the factor of $g^2$ in front of the score is halved from $1$ to $\frac{1}{2}$, and the noise term $g(t)\,d\bar{w}$ is dropped entirely. Why does halving the score coefficient and removing the noise give the same marginals? Because in the SDE the noise spreads probability mass, and the full score correction concentrates it; when we remove the noise, we need less concentration — exactly half as much — to maintain the same density evolution.

The probability-flow ODE is powerful for two reasons. First, it provides deterministic sampling: given the same initial noise sample, the ODE always produces the same output, making generation reproducible and enabling latent-space interpolation. Second, it enables exact likelihood computation via the instantaneous change-of-variables formula for ODEs, something that is impossible with the stochastic path.

Probability-flow ODE derivation
1
Start from the Fokker–Planck equation of the forward SDE. The marginal density $p_t(x)$ satisfies: $$\frac{\partial p_t}{\partial t} = -\nabla \cdot (f\,p_t) + \frac{1}{2}g^2\,\Delta p_t$$ We want to find a drift-only ODE $dx = \tilde{f}(x,t)\,dt$ (no noise) whose density $\tilde{p}_t$ satisfies the same PDE, so that $\tilde{p}_t = p_t$ for all $t$.
2
Write the continuity equation for a deterministic flow. A pure ODE $dx = \tilde{f}\,dt$ evolves densities via the continuity (Liouville) equation: $$\frac{\partial \tilde{p}_t}{\partial t} = -\nabla \cdot (\tilde{f}\,\tilde{p}_t)$$ For $\tilde{p}_t = p_t$ we need $-\nabla\cdot(\tilde{f}\,p_t) = -\nabla\cdot(f\,p_t) + \frac{1}{2}g^2 \Delta p_t$.
3
Absorb the Laplacian into the drift. Use the identity $\Delta p_t = \nabla \cdot (p_t\,\nabla \log p_t)$ (which follows from $\nabla p_t = p_t \nabla \log p_t$). Substituting: $$-\nabla\cdot(\tilde{f}\,p_t) = -\nabla\cdot(f\,p_t) + \frac{1}{2}g^2\,\nabla\cdot(p_t\,\nabla\log p_t)$$ This is satisfied by choosing $\tilde{f} = f - \frac{1}{2}g^2\,\nabla\log p_t$, giving the probability-flow ODE.
4
Key insight. The $\frac{1}{2}$ factor (compared to the full $g^2$ in the reverse SDE) arises because the noise term in the SDE contributes its own spreading effect. When we remove the noise, we must compensate by reducing the score correction proportionally. The reverse SDE needs the full score to fight against both drift and diffusion; the ODE only needs to account for drift, so half the score suffices.
From a discrete DDPM step to the VP-SDE limit
1
Write one forward step as $x_{t+\Delta t} = \sqrt{1-\beta(t)\Delta t}\,x_t + \sqrt{\beta(t)\Delta t}\,\epsilon$.
2
Use $\sqrt{1-u} \approx 1-\tfrac12u$ for small $u$ to obtain $x_{t+\Delta t}-x_t \approx -\tfrac12\beta(t)x_t\Delta t + \sqrt{\beta(t)}\sqrt{\Delta t}\,\epsilon$.
3
Interpret $\sqrt{\Delta t}\,\epsilon$ as an infinitesimal Brownian increment $dw$ and let $\Delta t \to 0$.
4
The limit is the VP-SDE: $dx = -\tfrac12\beta(t)x\,dt + \sqrt{\beta(t)}\,dw$.

Worked example: the VP-SDE as noisy exponential decay

Ignore the random term for a moment and solve $\tfrac{dx}{dt} = -\tfrac12\beta x$ with constant $\beta$. The deterministic solution is $x(t) = e^{-\beta t/2}x(0)$, so the clean signal decays exponentially as time grows.

The stochastic term changes the story from simple decay into diffusion. Brownian kicks keep injecting uncertainty while the drift keeps shrinking the signal, so the distribution both spreads and forgets structure smoothly. That is the continuous-time version of the familiar DDPM picture in which $\bar{\alpha}_t$ falls while the noise weight rises.

What does this mean?

The continuous-time language does not replace DDPM intuition; it refines it. The SDE coefficients are just the infinitesimal version of the same schedule-controlled trade-off between signal decay and noise growth that you already met in the discrete forward process.

Worked example: one reverse-SDE step vs. one probability-flow ODE step

Let us make the reverse dynamics concrete by computing a single step with real numbers. Suppose we are at time $t = 0.8$ with current state $x = 0.3$, using the VP-SDE with $\beta(t) = 0.1 + 19.9t$. At $t = 0.8$ we have $\beta(0.8) = 0.1 + 19.9 \times 0.8 = 16.02$. The drift coefficient is $f(x,t) = -\frac{1}{2}\beta(t)x = -\frac{1}{2}(16.02)(0.3) = -2.403$ and the diffusion coefficient is $g(t) = \sqrt{\beta(t)} = \sqrt{16.02} \approx 4.003$.

Now suppose the score network estimates $s_\theta(x, t) = \nabla_x \log p_t(x) \approx -0.25$ at this point. We will take a reverse step of size $\Delta t = 0.01$ (stepping from $t = 0.80$ to $t = 0.79$). For the reverse SDE (Euler–Maruyama), the update is:

$$x_{t-\Delta t} = x_t - \bigl[f(x,t) - g(t)^2 \,s_\theta(x,t)\bigr]\Delta t + g(t)\sqrt{\Delta t}\,z$$

where $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. Computing the drift correction: $g(t)^2 \cdot s_\theta = (16.02)(-0.25) = -4.005$, so the total reverse drift is $f - g^2 s_\theta = -2.403 - (-4.005) = 1.602$. The deterministic displacement is $-1.602 \times 0.01 = -0.01602$. The stochastic kick is $4.003 \times \sqrt{0.01} \times z = 0.4003z$. If $z = 0.5$, we get $x_{0.79} = 0.3 - 0.01602 + 0.2002 = 0.4842$.

For the probability-flow ODE, the update uses half the score coefficient and no noise:

$$x_{t-\Delta t} = x_t - \bigl[f(x,t) - \tfrac{1}{2}g(t)^2\,s_\theta(x,t)\bigr]\Delta t$$

The ODE drift is $f - \frac{1}{2}g^2 s_\theta = -2.403 - \frac{1}{2}(-4.005) = -2.403 + 2.0025 = -0.4005$. So the displacement is $-(-0.4005) \times 0.01 = 0.004005$, giving $x_{0.79} = 0.3 + 0.004005 = 0.3040$.

What does this mean?

The ODE step is small and deterministic — it nudges the state gently toward higher-density regions. The SDE step has the same expected drift direction but adds a random perturbation whose magnitude ($\approx 0.40$) dwarfs the drift displacement ($\approx 0.02$). Over many steps, the SDE explores more of the distribution while the ODE traces a single smooth trajectory. Both converge to the same distribution of outputs, but any individual SDE sample path looks much noisier than the corresponding ODE path.

A short SDE primer

An SDE has a drift term and a diffusion term. The drift says where the state would move deterministically, while the diffusion coefficient controls how strongly Brownian motion perturbs that deterministic motion. Formally, the general Itô SDE $dx = f(x,t)\,dt + g(x,t)\,dw$ says: in a tiny interval $[t, t+dt]$, the state shifts by a deterministic amount $f\,dt$ plus a random Gaussian kick of standard deviation $g\sqrt{dt}$. The randomness is Markovian — future increments are independent of the past given the present — which is why the process has no memory beyond the current state $x_t$.

For diffusion models, the beauty is that the unknown part of the reverse-time SDE is exactly the score. Once the score network is available, the reverse dynamics are specified and can be simulated like any other stochastic system. The simplest numerical integrator is the Euler–Maruyama method, which discretises the SDE by taking $x_{t+\Delta t} = x_t + f\,\Delta t + g\,\sqrt{\Delta t}\,z$ with $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$. This is the stochastic analog of the forward Euler method for ODEs, and it has weak order 1 (meaning expectations of smooth functions converge at rate $O(\Delta t)$). Higher-order methods like the stochastic Runge–Kutta scheme or predictor–corrector approaches from Song et al. can improve accuracy per step, but they require additional score evaluations.

One important practical concern is numerical stiffness. Near $t = 0$ (the data end), the drift coefficients in the reverse SDE can become very large because the score $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ grows as the distribution becomes sharply peaked. This means the ODE/SDE becomes stiff in that regime, and naive fixed-step integrators may require extremely small steps to remain stable. Adaptive-step ODE solvers (such as Dormand–Prince RK45) handle this automatically by shrinking the step when the vector field changes rapidly and enlarging it when the dynamics are smooth, which is why they are preferred for probability-flow ODE sampling in practice.

VE-SDE formal definition and comparison with VP-SDE
1
Define the Variance Exploding SDE. The VE-SDE has zero drift and a time-dependent diffusion coefficient tied to an increasing noise schedule $\sigma(t)$: $$dx = \sqrt{\frac{d[\sigma^2(t)]}{dt}}\,dw$$ Here $f(x,t) = 0$ (no signal decay!) and $g(t) = \sqrt{\frac{d[\sigma^2(t)]}{dt}}$. Because the drift is zero, the signal is never scaled down — only noise is added. The variance of $x_t$ grows without bound as $t$ increases, hence the name "variance exploding."
2
Connection to SMLD / NCSN. In the Score Matching with Langevin Dynamics framework (Song & Ermon, 2019), the forward corruption is $x_i = x_0 + \sigma_i \epsilon$ for a geometric sequence of noise levels $\sigma_1 < \sigma_2 < \cdots < \sigma_L$. Setting $\sigma(t) = \sigma_{\min} \cdot \left(\frac{\sigma_{\max}}{\sigma_{\min}}\right)^t$ for $t \in [0,1]$, the continuous limit is: $$g(t) = \sigma(t)\sqrt{2 \log\!\left(\frac{\sigma_{\max}}{\sigma_{\min}}\right)}$$ This is the VE-SDE that unifies all discrete NCSN noise levels into a single continuous process.
3
Side-by-side comparison. The VP-SDE and VE-SDE differ in a fundamental way:

VP-SDE: $f(x,t) = -\frac{1}{2}\beta(t)x$,   $g(t) = \sqrt{\beta(t)}$ — signal shrinks, noise is injected, total variance stays $\approx 1$.
VE-SDE: $f(x,t) = 0$,   $g(t) = \sqrt{\frac{d[\sigma^2(t)]}{dt}}$ — signal is unchanged, noise is added on top, total variance grows to $\sigma_{\max}^2$.

Both end at approximately the same place (a wide Gaussian), but they get there differently. VP-SDE compresses the signal into a unit ball and fills it with noise; VE-SDE leaves the signal alone and buries it under an ever-growing pile of noise.
4
Practical implications. Because the VE-SDE never rescales the signal, the score $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ at large $t$ is approximately $-x/\sigma^2(t)$, which is small and smooth. This can make training easier at high noise levels. However, the large dynamic range of $\sigma(t)$ (often spanning 3–4 orders of magnitude) requires careful loss weighting. VP-SDE, by contrast, keeps all quantities order-one throughout the process, which simplifies optimisation but introduces coupling between signal decay and noise injection that VE-SDE avoids.

Worked example: VP-SDE vs VE-SDE signal evolution

Let us trace what happens to a single 1D data point $x_0 = 1$ at time $t = 0.5$ under each SDE. We will compute the mean and variance of the conditional distribution $p(x_t \mid x_0)$.

VP-SDE with $\beta(t) = 0.1 + 19.9t$. The integrated noise schedule is $\int_0^{0.5} \beta(s)\,ds = \int_0^{0.5}(0.1+19.9s)\,ds = 0.05 + 19.9 \cdot 0.125 = 0.05 + 2.4875 = 2.5375$. For the VP-SDE, $p(x_t \mid x_0) = \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(x_0 \,e^{-\frac{1}{2}\int_0^t \beta(s)ds},\; 1 - e^{-\int_0^t \beta(s)ds}\bigr)$. So:

$$\text{mean} = 1 \cdot e^{-2.5375/2} = e^{-1.269} \approx 0.281$$
$$\text{variance} = 1 - e^{-2.5375} \approx 1 - 0.0791 = 0.921$$

The signal has shrunk from $1.0$ to $0.281$ (a $72\%$ reduction), and the variance is $0.921$ — close to the terminal value of $1.0$. The VP-SDE aggressively compresses the signal.

VE-SDE with $\sigma(t) = 0.01 \cdot 100^t$. At $t = 0.5$: $\sigma(0.5) = 0.01 \cdot 100^{0.5} = 0.01 \cdot 10 = 0.1$. The conditional distribution is $p(x_t \mid x_0) = \mathcal{N}(x_0, \sigma^2(t) - \sigma^2(0))$. Since $\sigma(0) = 0.01$:

$$\text{mean} = 1.0 \quad (\text{unchanged!})$$
$$\text{variance} = 0.1^2 - 0.01^2 = 0.01 - 0.0001 = 0.0099$$

The signal is exactly $1.0$ — the VE-SDE has not touched it at all. The added noise variance is only $0.0099$, so at $t = 0.5$ the data point is barely corrupted. The VE-SDE's corruption is back-loaded: most of the noise arrives at later times as $\sigma(t)$ grows geometrically.

What does this mean?

The VP-SDE and VE-SDE reach the same destination (a broad Gaussian) but take very different routes. VP-SDE simultaneously shrinks the signal and adds noise, giving a smooth, roughly linear transition from data to noise. VE-SDE leaves the signal intact and piles noise on top, with most corruption concentrated at late times. This has practical consequences: VP-SDE models tend to be easier to train uniformly across time, while VE-SDE models may need careful loss weighting to handle the extreme dynamic range of $\sigma(t)$.

Exact likelihood and the continuous normalizing-flow view

The probability-flow ODE removes the stochastic term while preserving the same one-time marginals as the reverse SDE. That is remarkable, because it means a diffusion model can also be seen as a continuous normalizing flow whose vector field is defined by the same learned score. A continuous normalizing flow (CNF) is an ODE $\frac{dx}{dt} = v_\theta(x,t)$ that transports samples from a simple base distribution (like a Gaussian) to the data distribution. The probability-flow ODE is exactly such a CNF, except the vector field is not parameterised freely — it is derived from the score function, which constrains it to be consistent with the forward diffusion.

Once the process is deterministic, log-likelihood can be tracked with the instantaneous change-of-variables formula. For an ODE flow $\frac{dx}{dt} = v(x,t)$, the log-density evolves as:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\log p_t(x(t)) = -\mathrm{tr}\!\left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}\right) = -\nabla \cdot v(x(t), t)$$

This is the instantaneous change-of-variables formula (also called the continuous normalizing flow identity). To compute $\log p_0(x_0)$ — the log-likelihood of a data point — we integrate the ODE forward from $t=0$ to $t=T$ and accumulate the divergence along the trajectory: $\log p_0(x_0) = \log p_T(x_T) + \int_0^T \nabla \cdot v(x(t),t)\,dt$. Since $p_T$ is approximately Gaussian, its log-density is trivial to evaluate, so the entire computation reduces to a single ODE solve. In practice the Jacobian trace is estimated with stochastic techniques such as Hutchinson estimators, which makes exact or near-exact density evaluation feasible even in high dimension.

Why Hutchinson's trace estimator works

The change-of-variables formula needs the trace of the Jacobian of the flow field, which is the divergence term $\mathrm{tr}(J_f(x))$. Forming a full Jacobian at image dimension would be completely impractical — for a 64×64 RGB image the state has $d = 64 \times 64 \times 3 = 12{,}288$ dimensions, so the Jacobian is a $12{,}288 \times 12{,}288$ matrix with over 150 million entries. Computing it would require $d$ backward passes through the score network. Instead, diffusion likelihood methods rely on the identity $\mathbb{E}[v^T J v] = \mathrm{tr}(J)$ when $v$ is a zero-mean random vector with identity covariance.

Why does this identity hold? Expand: $\mathbb{E}[v^T J v] = \mathbb{E}\bigl[\sum_{ij} v_i J_{ij} v_j\bigr] = \sum_{ij} J_{ij}\,\mathbb{E}[v_i v_j]$. Since $\mathbb{E}[v_i v_j] = \delta_{ij}$ (the Kronecker delta), only the diagonal terms survive, giving $\sum_i J_{ii} = \mathrm{tr}(J)$. In practice, $v$ is drawn from a Rademacher distribution ($\pm 1$ with equal probability) rather than a Gaussian, because this gives lower variance while still satisfying the identity-covariance requirement.

That is exactly what the code block is doing. It samples a random probe vector, computes a Jacobian-vector product instead of the whole Jacobian, and then takes the inner product with the same probe. Averaging a few such probes gives an unbiased trace estimate at a tiny fraction of the memory cost, which is why exact-likelihood-style diffusion calculations are computationally feasible at all. Each probe requires only one Jacobian–vector product (computable via a single backward pass), so $k$ probes cost $k$ backward passes instead of $d$ — a reduction from 12,288 to perhaps 1–5 passes.

Worked example: likelihood computation via instantaneous change of variables

Consider a 64×64 grayscale image, so $d = 4{,}096$. The probability-flow ODE is $\frac{dx}{dt} = v_\theta(x,t)$, and we want $\log p_0(x_0)$ for a given data image $x_0$. We integrate forward from $t=0$ to $t=T=1$ using an adaptive ODE solver and accumulate:

$$\log p_0(x_0) = \log p_T(x_T) + \int_0^T \mathrm{tr}\!\left(\frac{\partial v_\theta}{\partial x}(x(t),t)\right) dt$$

At $t = T$ the distribution is approximately $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$, so $\log p_T(x_T) = -\frac{d}{2}\log(2\pi) - \frac{1}{2}\|x_T\|^2$. For $d = 4{,}096$ and $\|x_T\| \approx 64$ (typical for a standard normal sample in this dimension, since $\mathbb{E}[\|z\|^2] = d$), $\log p_T \approx -\frac{4096}{2}\log(2\pi) - \frac{64^2}{2} = -2048 \times 1.838 - 2048 \approx -3764 - 2048 = -5812$.

Now for the divergence integral. Without the Hutchinson trick, each evaluation of $\mathrm{tr}(J)$ would require building the full $4{,}096 \times 4{,}096$ Jacobian — about 16.8 million entries — needing 4,096 backward passes per function evaluation. With the Hutchinson estimator, we draw $v \sim \text{Rademacher}(d)$ and compute $v^T \frac{\partial v_\theta}{\partial x} v$ using a single vector–Jacobian product (one backward pass). If the ODE solver takes 100 function evaluations and we use 1 probe each, that is 100 backward passes total — a $41\times$ reduction compared to the naive approach's 409,600 backward passes.

What does this mean?

The Hutchinson trace estimator is what makes diffusion models competitive as density estimators, not just samplers. Without it, computing $\log p(x)$ for a single image would be prohibitively expensive. With it, we get unbiased likelihood estimates at a cost comparable to generating a single sample — opening the door to model comparison, out-of-distribution detection, and hybrid training objectives that combine score matching with maximum likelihood.

Connection to physics

The name “diffusion model” is not metaphorical. The forward process is genuinely a diffusion process in the stochastic-calculus sense, closely tied to Brownian motion, the heat equation, and the Fokker-Planck equation for density evolution. In 1827, Robert Brown observed pollen grains jittering randomly in water; a century later, Einstein and Smoluchowski formalized this as a consequence of molecular bombardment. The mathematics of that physical phenomenon — Brownian motion driving a particle through a viscous fluid — is exactly the mathematics of the forward corruption process in diffusion generative models. The only difference is interpretation: instead of a pollen grain, we have a data point; instead of thermal energy, the noise is injected deliberately by the modeller.

The Fokker-Planck equation describes how an entire probability density moves under drift and noise. In the pure-diffusion limit it reduces to the heat equation, whose job is to smooth away sharp structure over time. That is exactly the intuition of the forward process: detailed data distributions blur into broader, simpler noise distributions as entropy increases. The Fokker-Planck viewpoint is powerful because it operates at the distribution level rather than the sample level — it tells us how the entire ensemble of possible states evolves, not just one trajectory.

Reverse diffusion therefore is not “undoing randomness” by magic. It supplies the missing feedback term — the score — that tells probability mass how to flow back toward concentrated data regions instead of continuing to disperse. The reverse SDE is a physically meaningful diffusion process whose drift has been corrected by learned information about the data manifold. In a physical analogy, imagine an inkdrop spreading in water (forward diffusion). Normally you cannot un-spread ink. But if at every point in the fluid you knew the local concentration gradient (the “score”), you could design a counter-flow that reconcentrates the ink. That is precisely what the reverse SDE does.

This makes the information-theoretic interpretation especially vivid. Uncontrolled diffusion spends information by washing structure away; the learned reverse field spends model capacity to inject the right directional bits back in. Sampling is successful precisely because the network has learned how entropy should be reversed locally at every noise scale.

The Fokker–Planck equation

For the SDE $dx = f(x,t)\,dt + g(t)\,dw$, the probability density $p_t(x)$ satisfies the Fokker–Planck (or Kolmogorov forward) equation:

$$\frac{\partial p_t}{\partial t} = -\nabla \cdot \bigl(f(x,t)\,p_t(x)\bigr) + \frac{1}{2}g(t)^2\,\Delta p_t(x)$$

The first term, $-\nabla \cdot (f\,p_t)$, is advection: it transports probability mass along the drift field $f$, like a current carrying a dye through fluid. The second term, $\frac{1}{2}g^2 \Delta p_t$, is diffusion: the Laplacian $\Delta p_t$ measures how the density curves in space, and the diffusion coefficient causes mass to flow from regions of high concentration to low concentration (Fick's law). In the special case $f = 0$ and $g = \text{const}$, this reduces to the classical heat equation $\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = \frac{g^2}{2}\Delta p$, which smooths any initial distribution toward a uniform (or Gaussian) steady state — precisely the forward corruption process of a VE-SDE.

Common misconception: the reverse SDE and probability-flow ODE do NOT give the same samples

The reverse SDE and the probability-flow ODE share the same marginal densities $p_t(x)$ at every time $t$, but they do not produce the same sample paths. If you start both processes from the same noise vector $x_T$, the ODE will follow a single deterministic streamline, while the SDE will wander stochastically around it. After many samples, the distribution of endpoints will match — but any individual sample pair will differ. This distinction matters in practice: the ODE gives reproducible outputs (useful for interpolation and editing), while the SDE's stochasticity can improve sample diversity and quality by allowing the sampler to explore multiple modes.

Why the score appears everywhere

You may have noticed that $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ — the score function — appears in nearly every equation in this chapter. This is not a coincidence; it is the central object of the entire framework. In the reverse SDE, the score tells probability mass which direction to flow to undo diffusion. In the probability-flow ODE, it provides the "missing information" that makes deterministic transport possible without stochastic noise. In the likelihood computation, it appears inside the divergence term of the instantaneous change-of-variables formula. In the Fokker–Planck equation, the score converts the Laplacian of the density into a first-order drift. The score is the universal bridge between the sample-level dynamics (SDEs/ODEs operating on individual points $x$) and the distribution-level description (Fokker–Planck operating on the density $p_t$). This is why score matching — training a network to approximate $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ — is the single most important objective in diffusion modelling.

The SDE/ODE Framework

The continuous-time view reveals diffusion as a family of stochastic and deterministic transport equations. It unifies DDPMs, score models, and flow-like likelihood methods under one mathematical roof. The forward SDE defines noise corruption; Anderson's reverse-time SDE provides the stochastic denoising path; and the probability-flow ODE offers a deterministic alternative with the same marginal densities. Together, these three equations — connected by the score function $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ — form the theoretical backbone of modern diffusion generative modelling, enabling not just sampling but also exact likelihood computation, latent-space manipulation, and principled connections to physics via the Fokker–Planck equation.

Simulating the VP-SDE forward process

The Euler–Maruyama method is the stochastic analogue of forward Euler. For a VP-SDE it gives a direct numerical bridge from the continuous-time equation back to the familiar DDPM corruption path.

Python 

import torch
import math

@torch.no_grad()
def simulate_vp_sde(x0, beta_fn, n_steps=1000, t_max=1.0):
    dt = t_max / n_steps
    x = x0.clone()
    states = [x.clone()]
    for i in range(n_steps):
        t = i * dt
        beta_t = beta_fn(t)
        drift = -0.5 * beta_t * x
        diffusion = math.sqrt(beta_t * dt) * torch.randn_like(x)
        x = x + drift * dt + diffusion
        states.append(x.clone())
    return states
Why this implementation matters

As the discretization becomes finer, this simulation approaches the continuous VP-SDE and visually matches the intuition behind the forward diffusion chain.

Reverse-time Euler–Maruyama sampler

Once a score network is available, the reverse SDE can be simulated with the same numerical machinery. The only difference is the drift now contains the learned score term.

Python 

@torch.no_grad()
def sample_reverse_sde(score_model, xT, beta_fn, n_steps=1000, t_max=1.0):
    dt = t_max / n_steps
    x = xT.clone()
    for i in reversed(range(n_steps)):
        t = i * dt
        beta_t = beta_fn(t)
        score = score_model(x, torch.full((x.size(0),), t, device=x.device))
        drift = -0.5 * beta_t * x - beta_t * score
        noise = math.sqrt(beta_t * dt) * torch.randn_like(x)
        x = x - drift * dt + noise
    return x
Why this implementation matters

This is the stochastic counterpart of deterministic ODE solvers. It preserves randomness all the way to the clean-data end.

Probability-flow ODE integration

For deterministic sampling, remove the stochastic term and keep half of the score correction. Standard ODE integrators can then be used.

Python 

@torch.no_grad()
def probability_flow_step(score_model, x, t, dt, beta_t):
    score = score_model(x, torch.full((x.size(0),), t, device=x.device))
    drift = -0.5 * beta_t * x - 0.5 * beta_t * score
    return x - drift * dt


@torch.no_grad()
def sample_probability_flow(score_model, xT, beta_fn, n_steps=100):
    x = xT.clone()
    dt = 1.0 / n_steps
    for i in reversed(range(n_steps)):
        t = i * dt
        x = probability_flow_step(score_model, x, t, dt, beta_fn(t))
    return x
Why this implementation matters

The probability-flow ODE is deterministic but still shares marginals with the stochastic reverse SDE, which is one of the deepest facts in diffusion theory.

Exact log-likelihood sketch with Hutchinson trace

The continuous normalizing-flow view turns density evaluation into an ODE over both state and log-density. Hutchinson’s trick estimates the trace term without explicitly forming the full Jacobian.

Python 

def hutchinson_trace(fn, x):
    v = torch.randint_like(x, low=0, high=2).float() * 2 - 1
    jvp = torch.autograd.functional.jvp(fn, (x,), (v,), create_graph=True)[1]
    return (jvp * v).reshape(x.size(0), -1).sum(dim=1)


def logp_dynamics(flow_fn, x):
    trace = hutchinson_trace(flow_fn, x)
    return -trace

# Integrate state and log-density together along the probability-flow ODE.
Why this implementation matters

This idea is what lets a diffusion-like model double as a continuous flow with likelihood accounting.

Easy Exercises

Start with direct computations and concept checks. These should feel like guided repetitions of the core formulas.

🟢 Exercise 1: Identify drift and diffusion ▼

In an SDE of the form $dx = f(x,t)dt + g(t)dw$, which term is drift and which is diffusion?

$f(x,t)dt$ is the drift term because it describes deterministic motion, and $g(t)dw$ is the diffusion term because it multiplies the Brownian increment.

🟢 Exercise 2: Read the VP-SDE ▼

What are the drift and diffusion coefficients of the VP-SDE?

The drift is $-\tfrac12\beta(t)x$ and the diffusion coefficient is $\sqrt{\beta(t)}$. The first term shrinks the state and the second injects Brownian noise.

🟢 Exercise 3: VE-SDE intuition ▼

Why is the VE-SDE called variance-exploding?

Because its marginal variance grows over time instead of being kept roughly constant. The process keeps adding noise without the same variance-preserving shrinkage used in VP formulations.

🟢 Exercise 4: Reverse score term ▼

What new ingredient appears in the reverse-time SDE that is not present in the forward SDE?

The score $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ appears in the drift. It is the learned term that tells the process how to move back toward data rather than further into noise.

🟢 Exercise 5: Probability-flow ODE ▼

What is the practical benefit of the probability-flow ODE?

It gives a deterministic sampler with the same marginals as the reverse SDE. That opens the door to ODE solvers and likelihood tracking.

🟢 Exercise 6: Brownian increment meaning ▼

What does the symbol $dw$ represent intuitively?

It represents an infinitesimal Brownian-motion increment: a tiny random perturbation with variance proportional to elapsed time. It is the continuous-time analogue of adding Gaussian noise at each discrete step.

🟢 Exercise 7: Discretization method ▼

What is Euler–Maruyama used for?

It is the standard first-order numerical method for simulating SDEs. It plays the same role for stochastic systems that Euler’s method plays for ordinary differential equations.

🟢 Exercise 8: Same marginals claim ▼

What does it mean when we say the reverse SDE and probability-flow ODE have the same marginals?

At each fixed time, both processes produce the same distribution over states even though one is stochastic and the other deterministic. Their trajectories differ, but their one-time densities match.

🟢 Exercise 9: Connection to heat flow ▼

Why is the forward process related to the heat equation?

Both describe the smoothing or spreading of information under diffusion. As time grows, sharp structure blurs into broader, simpler distributions.

🟢 Exercise 10: Role of score accuracy ▼

Why does reverse-time sampling depend critically on accurate score estimates?

Because the score is the only learned ingredient in the reverse dynamics. If it is wrong, both the stochastic and deterministic reverse trajectories drift away from the true data distribution.

Medium Exercises

Now move from recall to derivation. These questions ask you to justify formulas, compare viewpoints, and explain why the method works.

🟡 Exercise 11: Discrete-to-continuous limit ▼

Why does shrinking the timestep in DDPM-like corruption lead naturally to an SDE description?

Each discrete update becomes a tiny drift plus a tiny Gaussian perturbation. In the limit of infinitely many infinitesimal steps, those perturbations accumulate as Brownian motion and the update law becomes an SDE.

🟡 Exercise 12: VP versus VE trade-off ▼

Why might one prefer a VP-SDE over a VE-SDE in image generation?

The VP formulation keeps state scale more controlled, which often makes parameterization and optimization easier. VE formulations are elegant in score modeling but can involve a wider dynamic range across time.

🟡 Exercise 13: Reverse-time correction ▼

Why does the score appear multiplied by $g(t)^2$ in the reverse SDE?

The diffusion coefficient determines how much randomness the forward process injected, so it also sets how strongly the reverse dynamics must correct for that randomness. The score is therefore scaled by the same variance budget that created the noise.

🟡 Exercise 14: ODE likelihood formula ▼

Why does the probability-flow ODE permit likelihood computation while the reverse SDE does not directly?

A deterministic flow admits a change-of-variables formula relating densities along the path. A stochastic process requires a different probabilistic treatment and does not yield the same simple Jacobian-based likelihood accounting.

🟡 Exercise 15: Fokker–Planck intuition ▼

What does the Fokker–Planck equation describe in this context?

It describes how the full density over states evolves under the combined effect of drift and diffusion. In diffusion modeling it gives the population-level picture corresponding to individual SDE sample paths.

🟡 Exercise 16: Trace estimation need ▼

Why is Hutchinson trace estimation useful in diffusion likelihood computation?

The exact Jacobian trace of a high-dimensional neural vector field is expensive to compute directly. Hutchinson estimators turn that trace into an unbiased stochastic inner-product estimate that is much cheaper.

🟡 Exercise 17: SDE solver error ▼

How does decreasing the numerical step size affect Euler–Maruyama simulation?

Smaller steps reduce discretization error and make the simulated path closer to the underlying SDE, at the cost of more model evaluations. This is the same quality-speed trade-off seen everywhere in diffusion sampling.

🟡 Exercise 18: Unification with flows ▼

In what sense does the probability-flow ODE unify diffusion and continuous normalizing flows?

It shows that a diffusion-trained score field also defines a deterministic transport map. The same learned object can therefore be read as both a denoiser and a flow vector field.

🟡 Exercise 19: Physical interpretation of reverse time ▼

Why does reverse diffusion look physically unusual?

Ordinary physical diffusion increases entropy and erases structure. Reverse diffusion requires a carefully chosen drift derived from the score so that entropy is removed in a coordinated, data-consistent way.

🟡 Exercise 20: Marginal equality check ▼

Why does “same marginals” not imply “same trajectories”?

Two processes can agree on every one-time distribution while moving through state space very differently path by path. That is exactly the relationship between the reverse SDE and the probability-flow ODE.

Hard Exercises

These problems push you toward research-level thinking: asymptotics, design choices, edge cases, and deeper theoretical interpretation.

🔴 Exercise 21: Reverse-time SDE derivation ▼

What is the central idea behind deriving the reverse-time SDE from the forward one?

The derivation matches how infinitesimal transition densities must transform when time is reversed and then expresses the correction in terms of the score of the time-marginal density. The result is Anderson’s theorem, the backbone of continuous-time diffusion theory.

🔴 Exercise 22: Why half the score in the ODE ▼

Why does the probability-flow ODE contain half the score correction used in the reverse SDE?

The deterministic flow must reproduce the same marginal density evolution without stochastic diffusion, so only half of the stochastic correction remains in the drift. The missing half is effectively replaced by the removed noise term.

🔴 Exercise 23: Likelihood robustness ▼

Why can exact-likelihood computation be numerically fragile even when the theory is clean?

Trace estimates are noisy, ODE integration accumulates error, and stiff score fields can be difficult to resolve accurately. In high-dimensional neural models, these numerical issues can dominate the theoretical neatness of the formula.

🔴 Exercise 24: Design a custom SDE ▼

How would you design a custom corruption SDE for data with highly anisotropic structure?

The diffusion coefficient could be made state- or direction-dependent so that sensitive directions are corrupted more slowly than redundant ones. The challenge is preserving tractable marginals and a usable reverse-time correction formula.

🔴 Exercise 25: VP-SDE variance proof ▼

Why does the VP-SDE preserve bounded variance when initialized from a unit-variance distribution?

The negative linear drift shrinks the state at exactly the rate needed to offset the injected diffusion variance. Solving the second-moment dynamics shows the marginal variance stays controlled rather than exploding.

🔴 Exercise 26: Path-space differences ▼

If the ODE and SDE have identical marginals, what statistics can still differ between them?

Any path-dependent statistic can differ: hitting times, trajectory roughness, path entropy, and temporal correlations are not fixed by marginals alone. That is why sampler behavior can feel different even when endpoint quality is similar.

🔴 Exercise 27: Stiffness near the data manifold ▼

Why can the reverse dynamics become numerically stiff near clean data?

The score field may change sharply in regions of high curvature or fine detail, forcing small integration steps for stability. This is one reason late-stage denoising often benefits from more careful solvers or finer step spacing.

🔴 Exercise 28: SDE to flow-matching bridge ▼

How does the continuous-time viewpoint help explain the connection to flow matching?

Both frameworks define time-indexed vector fields that transport one distribution into another. Diffusion obtains that field through scores of noised marginals, while flow matching can specify alternative paths more directly.

🔴 Exercise 29: Thermodynamic reading ▼

What is the thermodynamic meaning of training a reverse-time diffusion model?

It amounts to learning how to produce low-entropy structured samples from a high-entropy reference by applying the correct microscopic drift at each time. The model learns an information-restoring direction field against natural noise growth.

🔴 Exercise 30: Research-level solver design ▼

What property would you target when designing a better reverse SDE solver?

You would target low global error per denoiser evaluation in the regimes where score fields are most curved or perceptually important. Exploiting schedule structure, adaptive step sizing, and problem-specific linearization are natural directions.
