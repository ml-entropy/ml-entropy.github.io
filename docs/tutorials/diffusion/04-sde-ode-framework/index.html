The SDE/ODE Framework

Discrete diffusion already works, but the continuous-time viewpoint reveals a deeper structure. Once the number of steps becomes large and each corruption becomes infinitesimal, the forward process is naturally described by a stochastic differential equation rather than a discrete Markov chain.

This reframing matters because it imports the full toolkit of stochastic calculus and numerical differential equations. Reverse-time sampling, deterministic probability-flow paths, and exact likelihood formulas all become easier to express in the continuous language.

8.1 Forward SDE

The discrete forward process becomes a continuous SDE:

$$dx = f(x, t) dt + g(t) dw$$

For VP-SDE: $f(x,t) = -\frac{1}{2}\beta(t)x$, $g(t) = \sqrt{\beta(t)}$

8.2 Reverse SDE

Anderson (1982) showed the reverse-time SDE is:

$$dx = [f(x,t) - g(t)^2 \nabla_x \log p_t(x)] dt + g(t) d\bar{w}$$

The score $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ is the only unknown — once learned, we can reverse the process.

8.3 Probability Flow ODE

A deterministic ODE with the same marginals:

$$dx = \left[f(x,t) - \frac{1}{2}g(t)^2 \nabla_x \log p_t(x)\right] dt$$

This is equivalent to a continuous normalizing flow — connecting back to Tutorial 17!

From a discrete DDPM step to the VP-SDE limit
1
Write one forward step as $x_{t+\Delta t} = \sqrt{1-\beta(t)\Delta t}\,x_t + \sqrt{\beta(t)\Delta t}\,\epsilon$.
2
Use $\sqrt{1-u} \approx 1-\tfrac12u$ for small $u$ to obtain $x_{t+\Delta t}-x_t \approx -\tfrac12\beta(t)x_t\Delta t + \sqrt{\beta(t)}\sqrt{\Delta t}\,\epsilon$.
3
Interpret $\sqrt{\Delta t}\,\epsilon$ as an infinitesimal Brownian increment $dw$ and let $\Delta t \to 0$.
4
The limit is the VP-SDE: $dx = -\tfrac12\beta(t)x\,dt + \sqrt{\beta(t)}\,dw$.

Worked example: the VP-SDE as noisy exponential decay

Ignore the random term for a moment and solve $\tfrac{dx}{dt} = -\tfrac12\beta x$ with constant $\beta$. The deterministic solution is $x(t) = e^{-\beta t/2}x(0)$, so the clean signal decays exponentially as time grows.

The stochastic term changes the story from simple decay into diffusion. Brownian kicks keep injecting uncertainty while the drift keeps shrinking the signal, so the distribution both spreads and forgets structure smoothly. That is the continuous-time version of the familiar DDPM picture in which $\bar{\alpha}_t$ falls while the noise weight rises.

What does this mean?

The continuous-time language does not replace DDPM intuition; it refines it. The SDE coefficients are just the infinitesimal version of the same schedule-controlled trade-off between signal decay and noise growth that you already met in the discrete forward process.

A short SDE primer

An SDE has a drift term and a diffusion term. The drift says where the state would move deterministically, while the diffusion coefficient controls how strongly Brownian motion perturbs that deterministic motion.

For diffusion models, the beauty is that the unknown part of the reverse-time SDE is exactly the score. Once the score network is available, the reverse dynamics are specified and can be simulated like any other stochastic system.

Exact likelihood and the continuous normalizing-flow view

The probability-flow ODE removes the stochastic term while preserving the same one-time marginals as the reverse SDE. That is remarkable, because it means a diffusion model can also be seen as a continuous normalizing flow whose vector field is defined by the same learned score.

Once the process is deterministic, log-likelihood can be tracked with the instantaneous change-of-variables formula. In practice the Jacobian trace is estimated with stochastic techniques such as Hutchinson estimators, which makes exact or near-exact density evaluation feasible even in high dimension.

Why Hutchinson's trace estimator works

The change-of-variables formula needs the trace of the Jacobian of the flow field, which is the divergence term $\mathrm{tr}(J_f(x))$. Forming a full Jacobian at image dimension would be completely impractical, so diffusion likelihood methods rely on the identity $\mathbb{E}[v^T J v] = \mathrm{tr}(J)$ when $v$ is a zero-mean random vector with identity covariance.

That is exactly what the code block is doing. It samples a random probe vector, computes a Jacobian-vector product instead of the whole Jacobian, and then takes the inner product with the same probe. Averaging a few such probes gives an unbiased trace estimate at a tiny fraction of the memory cost, which is why exact-likelihood-style diffusion calculations are computationally feasible at all.

Connection to physics

The name “diffusion model” is not metaphorical. The forward process is genuinely a diffusion process in the stochastic-calculus sense, closely tied to Brownian motion, the heat equation, and the Fokker-Planck equation for density evolution.

The Fokker-Planck equation describes how an entire probability density moves under drift and noise. In the pure-diffusion limit it reduces to the heat equation, whose job is to smooth away sharp structure over time. That is exactly the intuition of the forward process: detailed data distributions blur into broader, simpler noise distributions as entropy increases.

Reverse diffusion therefore is not "undoing randomness" by magic. It supplies the missing feedback term — the score — that tells probability mass how to flow back toward concentrated data regions instead of continuing to disperse. The reverse SDE is a physically meaningful diffusion process whose drift has been corrected by learned information about the data manifold.

This makes the information-theoretic interpretation especially vivid. Uncontrolled diffusion spends information by washing structure away; the learned reverse field spends model capacity to inject the right directional bits back in. Sampling is successful precisely because the network has learned how entropy should be reversed locally at every noise scale.

Common misconception: same marginals do not mean same paths

The reverse SDE and the probability-flow ODE match at the level of one-time distributions, not sample-by-sample trajectories. A stochastic path can wander while the ODE path stays smooth, and both can still induce the same marginal density at each time.

The SDE/ODE Framework

The continuous-time view reveals diffusion as a family of stochastic and deterministic transport equations. It unifies DDPMs, score models, and flow-like likelihood methods under one mathematical roof.

Simulating the VP-SDE forward process

The Euler–Maruyama method is the stochastic analogue of forward Euler. For a VP-SDE it gives a direct numerical bridge from the continuous-time equation back to the familiar DDPM corruption path.

Python 

import torch
import math

@torch.no_grad()
def simulate_vp_sde(x0, beta_fn, n_steps=1000, t_max=1.0):
    dt = t_max / n_steps
    x = x0.clone()
    states = [x.clone()]
    for i in range(n_steps):
        t = i * dt
        beta_t = beta_fn(t)
        drift = -0.5 * beta_t * x
        diffusion = math.sqrt(beta_t * dt) * torch.randn_like(x)
        x = x + drift * dt + diffusion
        states.append(x.clone())
    return states
Why this implementation matters

As the discretization becomes finer, this simulation approaches the continuous VP-SDE and visually matches the intuition behind the forward diffusion chain.

Reverse-time Euler–Maruyama sampler

Once a score network is available, the reverse SDE can be simulated with the same numerical machinery. The only difference is the drift now contains the learned score term.

Python 

@torch.no_grad()
def sample_reverse_sde(score_model, xT, beta_fn, n_steps=1000, t_max=1.0):
    dt = t_max / n_steps
    x = xT.clone()
    for i in reversed(range(n_steps)):
        t = i * dt
        beta_t = beta_fn(t)
        score = score_model(x, torch.full((x.size(0),), t, device=x.device))
        drift = -0.5 * beta_t * x - beta_t * score
        noise = math.sqrt(beta_t * dt) * torch.randn_like(x)
        x = x - drift * dt + noise
    return x
Why this implementation matters

This is the stochastic counterpart of deterministic ODE solvers. It preserves randomness all the way to the clean-data end.

Probability-flow ODE integration

For deterministic sampling, remove the stochastic term and keep half of the score correction. Standard ODE integrators can then be used.

Python 

@torch.no_grad()
def probability_flow_step(score_model, x, t, dt, beta_t):
    score = score_model(x, torch.full((x.size(0),), t, device=x.device))
    drift = -0.5 * beta_t * x - 0.5 * beta_t * score
    return x - drift * dt


@torch.no_grad()
def sample_probability_flow(score_model, xT, beta_fn, n_steps=100):
    x = xT.clone()
    dt = 1.0 / n_steps
    for i in reversed(range(n_steps)):
        t = i * dt
        x = probability_flow_step(score_model, x, t, dt, beta_fn(t))
    return x
Why this implementation matters

The probability-flow ODE is deterministic but still shares marginals with the stochastic reverse SDE, which is one of the deepest facts in diffusion theory.

Exact log-likelihood sketch with Hutchinson trace

The continuous normalizing-flow view turns density evaluation into an ODE over both state and log-density. Hutchinson’s trick estimates the trace term without explicitly forming the full Jacobian.

Python 

def hutchinson_trace(fn, x):
    v = torch.randint_like(x, low=0, high=2).float() * 2 - 1
    jvp = torch.autograd.functional.jvp(fn, (x,), (v,), create_graph=True)[1]
    return (jvp * v).reshape(x.size(0), -1).sum(dim=1)


def logp_dynamics(flow_fn, x):
    trace = hutchinson_trace(flow_fn, x)
    return -trace

# Integrate state and log-density together along the probability-flow ODE.
Why this implementation matters

This idea is what lets a diffusion-like model double as a continuous flow with likelihood accounting.

Easy Exercises

Start with direct computations and concept checks. These should feel like guided repetitions of the core formulas.

🟢 Exercise 1: Identify drift and diffusion ▼

In an SDE of the form $dx = f(x,t)dt + g(t)dw$, which term is drift and which is diffusion?

$f(x,t)dt$ is the drift term because it describes deterministic motion, and $g(t)dw$ is the diffusion term because it multiplies the Brownian increment.

🟢 Exercise 2: Read the VP-SDE ▼

What are the drift and diffusion coefficients of the VP-SDE?

The drift is $-\tfrac12\beta(t)x$ and the diffusion coefficient is $\sqrt{\beta(t)}$. The first term shrinks the state and the second injects Brownian noise.

🟢 Exercise 3: VE-SDE intuition ▼

Why is the VE-SDE called variance-exploding?

Because its marginal variance grows over time instead of being kept roughly constant. The process keeps adding noise without the same variance-preserving shrinkage used in VP formulations.

🟢 Exercise 4: Reverse score term ▼

What new ingredient appears in the reverse-time SDE that is not present in the forward SDE?

The score $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ appears in the drift. It is the learned term that tells the process how to move back toward data rather than further into noise.

🟢 Exercise 5: Probability-flow ODE ▼

What is the practical benefit of the probability-flow ODE?

It gives a deterministic sampler with the same marginals as the reverse SDE. That opens the door to ODE solvers and likelihood tracking.

🟢 Exercise 6: Brownian increment meaning ▼

What does the symbol $dw$ represent intuitively?

It represents an infinitesimal Brownian-motion increment: a tiny random perturbation with variance proportional to elapsed time. It is the continuous-time analogue of adding Gaussian noise at each discrete step.

🟢 Exercise 7: Discretization method ▼

What is Euler–Maruyama used for?

It is the standard first-order numerical method for simulating SDEs. It plays the same role for stochastic systems that Euler’s method plays for ordinary differential equations.

🟢 Exercise 8: Same marginals claim ▼

What does it mean when we say the reverse SDE and probability-flow ODE have the same marginals?

At each fixed time, both processes produce the same distribution over states even though one is stochastic and the other deterministic. Their trajectories differ, but their one-time densities match.

🟢 Exercise 9: Connection to heat flow ▼

Why is the forward process related to the heat equation?

Both describe the smoothing or spreading of information under diffusion. As time grows, sharp structure blurs into broader, simpler distributions.

🟢 Exercise 10: Role of score accuracy ▼

Why does reverse-time sampling depend critically on accurate score estimates?

Because the score is the only learned ingredient in the reverse dynamics. If it is wrong, both the stochastic and deterministic reverse trajectories drift away from the true data distribution.

Medium Exercises

Now move from recall to derivation. These questions ask you to justify formulas, compare viewpoints, and explain why the method works.

🟡 Exercise 11: Discrete-to-continuous limit ▼

Why does shrinking the timestep in DDPM-like corruption lead naturally to an SDE description?

Each discrete update becomes a tiny drift plus a tiny Gaussian perturbation. In the limit of infinitely many infinitesimal steps, those perturbations accumulate as Brownian motion and the update law becomes an SDE.

🟡 Exercise 12: VP versus VE trade-off ▼

Why might one prefer a VP-SDE over a VE-SDE in image generation?

The VP formulation keeps state scale more controlled, which often makes parameterization and optimization easier. VE formulations are elegant in score modeling but can involve a wider dynamic range across time.

🟡 Exercise 13: Reverse-time correction ▼

Why does the score appear multiplied by $g(t)^2$ in the reverse SDE?

The diffusion coefficient determines how much randomness the forward process injected, so it also sets how strongly the reverse dynamics must correct for that randomness. The score is therefore scaled by the same variance budget that created the noise.

🟡 Exercise 14: ODE likelihood formula ▼

Why does the probability-flow ODE permit likelihood computation while the reverse SDE does not directly?

A deterministic flow admits a change-of-variables formula relating densities along the path. A stochastic process requires a different probabilistic treatment and does not yield the same simple Jacobian-based likelihood accounting.

🟡 Exercise 15: Fokker–Planck intuition ▼

What does the Fokker–Planck equation describe in this context?

It describes how the full density over states evolves under the combined effect of drift and diffusion. In diffusion modeling it gives the population-level picture corresponding to individual SDE sample paths.

🟡 Exercise 16: Trace estimation need ▼

Why is Hutchinson trace estimation useful in diffusion likelihood computation?

The exact Jacobian trace of a high-dimensional neural vector field is expensive to compute directly. Hutchinson estimators turn that trace into an unbiased stochastic inner-product estimate that is much cheaper.

🟡 Exercise 17: SDE solver error ▼

How does decreasing the numerical step size affect Euler–Maruyama simulation?

Smaller steps reduce discretization error and make the simulated path closer to the underlying SDE, at the cost of more model evaluations. This is the same quality-speed trade-off seen everywhere in diffusion sampling.

🟡 Exercise 18: Unification with flows ▼

In what sense does the probability-flow ODE unify diffusion and continuous normalizing flows?

It shows that a diffusion-trained score field also defines a deterministic transport map. The same learned object can therefore be read as both a denoiser and a flow vector field.

🟡 Exercise 19: Physical interpretation of reverse time ▼

Why does reverse diffusion look physically unusual?

Ordinary physical diffusion increases entropy and erases structure. Reverse diffusion requires a carefully chosen drift derived from the score so that entropy is removed in a coordinated, data-consistent way.

🟡 Exercise 20: Marginal equality check ▼

Why does “same marginals” not imply “same trajectories”?

Two processes can agree on every one-time distribution while moving through state space very differently path by path. That is exactly the relationship between the reverse SDE and the probability-flow ODE.

Hard Exercises

These problems push you toward research-level thinking: asymptotics, design choices, edge cases, and deeper theoretical interpretation.

🔴 Exercise 21: Reverse-time SDE derivation ▼

What is the central idea behind deriving the reverse-time SDE from the forward one?

The derivation matches how infinitesimal transition densities must transform when time is reversed and then expresses the correction in terms of the score of the time-marginal density. The result is Anderson’s theorem, the backbone of continuous-time diffusion theory.

🔴 Exercise 22: Why half the score in the ODE ▼

Why does the probability-flow ODE contain half the score correction used in the reverse SDE?

The deterministic flow must reproduce the same marginal density evolution without stochastic diffusion, so only half of the stochastic correction remains in the drift. The missing half is effectively replaced by the removed noise term.

🔴 Exercise 23: Likelihood robustness ▼

Why can exact-likelihood computation be numerically fragile even when the theory is clean?

Trace estimates are noisy, ODE integration accumulates error, and stiff score fields can be difficult to resolve accurately. In high-dimensional neural models, these numerical issues can dominate the theoretical neatness of the formula.

🔴 Exercise 24: Design a custom SDE ▼

How would you design a custom corruption SDE for data with highly anisotropic structure?

The diffusion coefficient could be made state- or direction-dependent so that sensitive directions are corrupted more slowly than redundant ones. The challenge is preserving tractable marginals and a usable reverse-time correction formula.

🔴 Exercise 25: VP-SDE variance proof ▼

Why does the VP-SDE preserve bounded variance when initialized from a unit-variance distribution?

The negative linear drift shrinks the state at exactly the rate needed to offset the injected diffusion variance. Solving the second-moment dynamics shows the marginal variance stays controlled rather than exploding.

🔴 Exercise 26: Path-space differences ▼

If the ODE and SDE have identical marginals, what statistics can still differ between them?

Any path-dependent statistic can differ: hitting times, trajectory roughness, path entropy, and temporal correlations are not fixed by marginals alone. That is why sampler behavior can feel different even when endpoint quality is similar.

🔴 Exercise 27: Stiffness near the data manifold ▼

Why can the reverse dynamics become numerically stiff near clean data?

The score field may change sharply in regions of high curvature or fine detail, forcing small integration steps for stability. This is one reason late-stage denoising often benefits from more careful solvers or finer step spacing.

🔴 Exercise 28: SDE to flow-matching bridge ▼

How does the continuous-time viewpoint help explain the connection to flow matching?

Both frameworks define time-indexed vector fields that transport one distribution into another. Diffusion obtains that field through scores of noised marginals, while flow matching can specify alternative paths more directly.

🔴 Exercise 29: Thermodynamic reading ▼

What is the thermodynamic meaning of training a reverse-time diffusion model?

It amounts to learning how to produce low-entropy structured samples from a high-entropy reference by applying the correct microscopic drift at each time. The model learns an information-restoring direction field against natural noise growth.

🔴 Exercise 30: Research-level solver design ▼

What property would you target when designing a better reverse SDE solver?

You would target low global error per denoiser evaluation in the regimes where score fields are most curved or perceptually important. Exploiting schedule structure, adaptive step sizing, and problem-specific linearization are natural directions.
