Score Matching & Langevin Dynamics

The score view of diffusion asks a sharper question than “what clean sample produced this noise?” It asks for the local direction in which log-density increases, which turns sampling into repeated motion toward higher-probability regions combined with carefully calibrated randomness. This reframing is not merely aesthetic — it unlocks an entirely different class of training objectives and sampling algorithms. Instead of modeling the full probability density (which requires knowing an intractable normalizing constant), we model only its gradient, which is both easier to learn and sufficient for generation. The result is a framework where training reduces to regression and sampling reduces to following a vector field with added noise.

This perspective is powerful because it removes the normalization constant from the problem. Gradients of log-densities do not care about unknown partition functions, which is exactly why score-based models can reason about complicated distributions without ever writing them in normalized form. Consider a distribution $p(x) = \\frac{1}{Z}\\tilde{p}(x)$ where $Z = \\int \\tilde{p}(x)dx$ is unknown. Taking the gradient of the log gives $\\nabla_x \\log p(x) = \\nabla_x \\log \\tilde{p}(x) - \\nabla_x \\log Z = \\nabla_x \\log \\tilde{p}(x)$, because $Z$ is a constant with zero gradient. This seemingly simple observation is the foundation of the entire score-matching paradigm: we can work with unnormalized densities, energy-based models, and Boltzmann distributions without ever computing partition functions — a task that is computationally intractable in high dimensions.

7.1 The Score Function

The score of a distribution is the gradient of its log-density:

$$s(x) = \nabla_x \log p(x)$$

It points toward regions of higher probability — like a compass pointing "uphill." At any point $x$ in data space, the score vector tells you the direction in which you should move to most rapidly increase the probability density. Where $p(x)$ is large (near a mode), the score is close to zero because you are already near a peak. Where $p(x)$ is small but increasing, the score is large and points toward the nearest mode. This vector field encodes the entire geometry of the distribution without ever requiring us to evaluate $p(x)$ itself.

The score function has a deep connection to physics: if we interpret $-\log p(x)$ as an energy landscape, then the score $\nabla_x \log p(x) = -\nabla_x E(x)$ is the negative gradient of the energy — exactly the force that would push a particle toward low-energy (high-probability) states. This is why Langevin dynamics, which we will see shortly, borrows its name from statistical mechanics. The score is also intimately related to the Fisher information matrix $\mathcal{I} = \mathbb{E}[s(x)s(x)^\top]$, which measures how much the score varies. Higher Fisher information means the score varies more, which in turn means the distribution has sharper features that are easier to distinguish.

Common misconception: score matching does NOT require knowing the normalizing constant

This is the whole point of the score-based approach and what makes it revolutionary compared to maximum likelihood estimation. In maximum likelihood, you need to evaluate $\log p_\theta(x) = \log \tilde{p}_\theta(x) - \log Z_\theta$, and the partition function $Z_\theta = \int \tilde{p}_\theta(x)dx$ changes with every parameter update, making each gradient step require an intractable integral. The score $\nabla_x \log p_\theta(x)$ cancels $Z_\theta$ completely because the normalizing constant does not depend on $x$. This means we can train generative models on distributions defined only up to a normalizing constant — including energy-based models, Markov random fields, and any model specified by an unnormalized density or energy function.

Worked example: Score of a 1D Gaussian

Let us compute the score function explicitly for the simplest non-trivial case: a univariate Gaussian $p(x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with $\mu = 3$ and $\sigma^2 = 4$. The density is:

$$p(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \cdot 4}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x - 3)^2}{2 \cdot 4}\right)$$

Taking the log:

$$\log p(x) = -\frac{1}{2}\log(8\pi) - \frac{(x-3)^2}{8}$$

Differentiating with respect to $x$:

$$s(x) = \nabla_x \log p(x) = -\frac{x - 3}{4}$$

Now let us evaluate at three specific points:

At $x = 1$ (below the mean): $s(1) = -\frac{1 - 3}{4} = \frac{2}{4} = +0.5$. The score is positive, pointing rightward toward the mean. The magnitude $0.5$ reflects that we are 2 units away from the mean, scaled by the variance.

At $x = 3$ (at the mean): $s(3) = -\frac{3-3}{4} = 0$. The score is zero at the mode — there is no direction to move because we are already at the peak of the density.

At $x = 5$ (above the mean): $s(5) = -\frac{5-3}{4} = -0.5$. The score is negative, pointing leftward back toward the mean. By symmetry, the magnitude matches the $x=1$ case.

What does this mean?

For a Gaussian, the score is a simple linear function: $s(x) = -(x - \mu)/\sigma^2$. It always points toward the mean with magnitude proportional to the distance from the mean, divided by the variance. Larger variance means gentler score (the distribution is flatter, so there is less urgency to move), while smaller variance means steeper score (the distribution is sharply peaked). This linear structure is special to Gaussians — for more complex distributions the score field will be highly nonlinear, which is why we need neural networks to approximate it.

7.2 Score Matching Objective

Directly matching the score is intractable (we don’t know $\nabla_x \log p(x)$). But denoising score matching is tractable. The key idea, due to Vincent (2011), is that instead of matching the score of the unknown data distribution, we can match the score of a known conditional distribution — the Gaussian perturbation kernel $q(x_t|x_0)$. Since we chose this kernel ourselves (it is the forward process), we can compute its score analytically. The training objective becomes:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{DSM}} = \mathbb{E}_{t, x_0, \epsilon}\left[\|s_\theta(x_t, t) - \nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t|x_0)\|^2\right]$$

Since $\nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t|x_0) = -\epsilon / \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}$, this is equivalent to $\epsilon$-prediction! But where does this crucial identity come from? Let us derive it step by step.

Derivation: Score of a Gaussian perturbation kernel
1
Write the forward process. The forward process produces $x_t$ from $x_0$ via $x_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\, x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\, \epsilon$ where $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$. This means the conditional distribution is Gaussian: $q(x_t|x_0) = \mathcal{N}(x_t;\, \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\, x_0,\; (1-\bar{\alpha}_t)I)$.
2
Write the log-density. The log of a Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2 I)$ evaluated at $x$ is $\log q(x_t|x_0) = -\frac{d}{2}\log(2\pi) - \frac{d}{2}\log(1-\bar{\alpha}_t) - \frac{\|x_t - \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\, x_0\|^2}{2(1-\bar{\alpha}_t)}$, where $d$ is the dimensionality. The first two terms are constants with respect to $x_t$.
3
Differentiate with respect to $x_t$. Only the quadratic term survives: $\nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t|x_0) = -\frac{x_t - \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\, x_0}{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t}$.
4
Substitute $x_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\, x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\, \epsilon$. The numerator becomes $\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\, x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\, \epsilon - \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\, x_0 = \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\, \epsilon$. Therefore: $$\nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t|x_0) = -\frac{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\, \epsilon}{1-\bar{\alpha}_t} = -\frac{\epsilon}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}}$$ This is the key identity: the score of the perturbation kernel is just the noise $\epsilon$ rescaled by $-1/\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}$.

But why is denoising score matching a valid proxy for the true objective? The ideal (but intractable) score matching objective is the Fisher divergence: $\mathbb{E}_{p_{\text{data}}}[\|s_\theta(x) - \nabla_x \log p_{\text{data}}(x)\|^2]$. We cannot compute this because $\nabla_x \log p_{\text{data}}(x)$ is unknown. The following derivation shows that the denoising objective equals the Fisher divergence up to a constant that does not depend on $\theta$.

Derivation: Denoising score matching equivalence (Hyvärinen’s trick)
1
Start with the Fisher divergence. The explicit (intractable) score matching objective at noise level $t$ is: $$J_{\text{ESM}}(\theta) = \frac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}_{q(x_t)}\!\left[\|s_\theta(x_t, t) - \nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t)\|^2\right]$$ Expanding the squared norm gives three terms: $\|s_\theta\|^2 - 2\, s_\theta \cdot \nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t) + \|\nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t)\|^2$. The last term is a constant (independent of $\theta$), so we can ignore it for optimization.
2
Apply integration by parts to the cross term. The problematic middle term is $-\mathbb{E}_{q(x_t)}[s_\theta(x_t,t) \cdot \nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t)]$. Writing $\nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t) = \frac{\nabla_{x_t} q(x_t)}{q(x_t)}$ and using integration by parts (assuming boundary terms vanish): $$\int q(x_t)\, s_\theta \cdot \frac{\nabla q(x_t)}{q(x_t)}\, dx_t = \int s_\theta \cdot \nabla q(x_t)\, dx_t = -\int q(x_t)\, \nabla \cdot s_\theta\, dx_t$$ where $\nabla \cdot s_\theta = \text{tr}(\nabla_{x_t} s_\theta)$ is the divergence (trace of the Jacobian) of the score model.
3
Write Hyvärinen’s implicit score matching objective. Combining the surviving terms, we get an objective that depends only on the model $s_\theta$ and samples from $q(x_t)$ — the true score has been eliminated: $$J_{\text{ISM}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{q(x_t)}\!\left[\frac{1}{2}\|s_\theta(x_t,t)\|^2 + \nabla \cdot s_\theta(x_t,t)\right]$$ This is computable but requires the Jacobian trace $\nabla \cdot s_\theta$, which is expensive for high-dimensional models.
4
Switch to denoising score matching. Vincent (2011) showed that when we introduce corruption via a known kernel $q(x_t|x_0)$, we can equivalently minimize: $$J_{\text{DSM}}(\theta) = \frac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}_{q(x_0)q(x_t|x_0)}\!\left[\|s_\theta(x_t,t) - \nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t|x_0)\|^2\right]$$ This equals $J_{\text{ESM}}(\theta)$ up to a constant independent of $\theta$. The proof uses the tower property of expectations: $\mathbb{E}_{q(x_t)}[f(x_t)] = \mathbb{E}_{q(x_0)}[\mathbb{E}_{q(x_t|x_0)}[f(x_t)]]$, and the fact that the cross terms between the conditional score and the marginal score integrate to the same value.
5
Why this matters. The denoising objective $J_{\text{DSM}}$ is a simple regression loss: sample $x_0$ from data, sample noise $\epsilon$, form $x_t$, and regress $s_\theta(x_t,t)$ onto $-\epsilon/\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}$. No Jacobian trace, no access to the true score, no partition function. This is what makes modern diffusion model training practical — a deep theoretical result reduced to a one-line loss function.

Worked example: Score of a Gaussian mixture

Consider the two-component Gaussian mixture $p(x) = 0.5\,\mathcal{N}(x;\,-2, 1) + 0.5\,\mathcal{N}(x;\, 2, 1)$. The score at any point $x$ is:

$$s(x) = \frac{\nabla_x p(x)}{p(x)} = \frac{0.5\,\phi(x; -2, 1)\cdot(-(x+2)) + 0.5\,\phi(x; 2, 1)\cdot(-(x-2))}{0.5\,\phi(x; -2, 1) + 0.5\,\phi(x; 2, 1)}$$

where $\phi(x; \mu, \sigma^2)$ denotes the Gaussian density. Let us evaluate at the midpoint $x = 0$. By symmetry, $\phi(0; -2, 1) = \phi(0; 2, 1)$ (both modes are equidistant from the origin). Call this common value $c$. Then:

$$s(0) = \frac{0.5c \cdot (-(0+2)) + 0.5c \cdot (-(0-2))}{0.5c + 0.5c} = \frac{0.5c(-2) + 0.5c(+2)}{c} = \frac{-c + c}{c} = 0$$

The score is exactly zero at $x=0$! This is an unstable equilibrium — a particle following the score field from exactly $x=0$ would stay put, but the slightest perturbation to the left would send it toward the left mode at $x=-2$, and similarly for the right. For $x$ slightly positive (say $x=0.1$), the right mode at $x=2$ exerts a slightly stronger pull than the left mode at $x=-2$, so $s(0.1) > 0$. The score field thus has a saddle-like structure between the modes, revealing the topology of the distribution.

What does this mean?

Unlike the single Gaussian where the score is a simple linear function, the score of a mixture is a weighted combination of component scores, where the weights depend on the relative densities of each component at the current point. Between modes, the score field has zero crossings and saddle points that encode the geometry of the distribution. A neural network learning this score must capture these nonlinear transitions — it cannot simply learn a linear map. This is why multi-modal distributions are fundamentally harder to model, and why noise smoothing (which blurs the modes together) makes the score field easier to learn.

7.3 Langevin Dynamics

Given the score function, we can sample using Langevin dynamics, a method borrowed from statistical physics. The basic idea is beautiful in its simplicity: if we know the direction of increasing probability at every point (the score), we can generate samples by following that direction with added noise. The noise is not a nuisance — it is essential. Without it, the dynamics would simply converge to the mode (the MAP estimate), not to samples from the distribution. The noise ensures that the stationary distribution of the chain is the target $p(x)$, not a point mass at its maximum. The update rule is:

$$x_{t+1} = x_t + \eta \nabla_x \log p(x_t) + \sqrt{2\eta} \, z, \quad z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$$

This converges to samples from $p(x)$ as $\eta \to 0$ and number of steps $\to \infty$. The step size $\eta$ controls the tradeoff between speed and accuracy: larger $\eta$ explores faster but introduces discretization error, while smaller $\eta$ follows the continuous dynamics more faithfully but requires more steps. In practice, one uses an annealing schedule that starts with large $\eta$ for global exploration and gradually decreases it for local refinement. The discrete update rule above is an Euler–Maruyama discretization of the continuous-time Langevin stochastic differential equation (SDE):

$$dx = \nabla_x \log p(x)\, dt + \sqrt{2}\, dw$$

where $dw$ is a standard Wiener process (Brownian motion). The drift term $\nabla_x \log p(x)\, dt$ pushes the particle toward high-probability regions, while the diffusion term $\sqrt{2}\, dw$ adds isotropic Gaussian noise. The coefficient $\sqrt{2}$ is not arbitrary — it is precisely the value needed to ensure that $p(x)$ is the stationary distribution. Let us prove this using the Fokker–Planck equation.

Derivation: Langevin dynamics converges to $p(x)$
1
Write the Fokker–Planck equation. For a general Itô SDE $dx = f(x)dt + g\, dw$, the probability density $\rho(x,t)$ of the particle evolves according to the Fokker–Planck (Kolmogorov forward) equation: $$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = -\nabla \cdot (f\, \rho) + \frac{g^2}{2}\nabla^2 \rho$$ For Langevin dynamics, $f(x) = \nabla \log p(x)$ and $g = \sqrt{2}$, giving: $$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = -\nabla \cdot (\rho\, \nabla \log p) + \nabla^2 \rho$$
2
Substitute $\rho = p$ and check stationarity. At stationarity, $\partial \rho / \partial t = 0$. We need to verify that $\rho = p(x)$ satisfies this. The drift term becomes: $\nabla \cdot (p\, \nabla \log p) = \nabla \cdot (p \cdot \frac{\nabla p}{p}) = \nabla \cdot (\nabla p) = \nabla^2 p$.
3
Verify cancellation. Plugging into the Fokker–Planck equation with $\rho = p$: $$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = -\nabla^2 p + \nabla^2 p = 0 \quad \checkmark$$ The drift and diffusion terms cancel perfectly, confirming that $p(x)$ is a stationary solution. This is why the noise coefficient must be $\sqrt{2}$ — any other value would break this cancellation.
4
Uniqueness and convergence. Under mild regularity conditions (e.g., $p(x) > 0$ everywhere and $\log p$ has bounded Hessian), the stationary distribution is unique and the dynamics converge to it regardless of initialization. More precisely, the KL divergence $D_{\text{KL}}(\rho_t \| p)$ decreases monotonically — in fact it satisfies $\frac{d}{dt}D_{\text{KL}}(\rho_t \| p) = -\mathbb{E}_{\rho_t}[\|\nabla \log \rho_t - \nabla \log p\|^2]$ (the Fisher divergence), which is always non-negative and zero only when $\rho_t = p$. This guarantees convergence to the target distribution.

Hyvärinen score matching and Fisher divergence

Classical score matching, introduced by Aapo Hyvärinen in 2005, minimizes the squared gap between the true score and a model score field. The Fisher divergence $\frac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}_p[\|s_\theta(x) - \nabla_x \log p(x)\|^2]$ measures exactly this gap. After an integration-by-parts trick (detailed in the derivation above), the objective can be written without the unknown data score itself, which is why the method is appealing despite operating on derivatives of densities. This was a breakthrough: for the first time, one could fit a model’s score to data without ever needing the score of the data distribution or the model’s normalizing constant. The price paid is the need to compute the trace of the score model’s Jacobian, $\text{tr}(\nabla_x s_\theta)$, which for a neural network in $d$ dimensions nominally requires $d$ backpropagation passes.

In diffusion practice, denoising score matching (DSM) is even more useful and completely avoids the Jacobian trace. Rather than estimating the score on the raw data manifold — where low-density regions are treacherous — we estimate the score of a smoothed family of noisy distributions. That smoothing is what makes modern diffusion training robust. The connection to Fisher divergence remains: DSM minimizes the Fisher divergence between the model score and the score of each noise-smoothed distribution $q_t(x_t) = \int q(x_t|x_0)q(x_0)dx_0$. Since these smoothed distributions have full support (no zero-density regions), the score estimation problem is well-posed at every noise level.

Why multiple noise scales are necessary (not just helpful)

At very low noise levels, the data distribution can look like a thin, sharp manifold and score estimation becomes unstable away from the data. Injecting larger noise smooths the density, produces gentler score fields, and gives Langevin dynamics room to move globally before refining local detail. But the need for multiple scales goes deeper than just smoothing. In high dimensions, natural data (images, audio, text embeddings) concentrates on low-dimensional manifolds embedded in the ambient space. The score perpendicular to the manifold is essentially undefined in the zero-noise limit — it points in arbitrary directions where there is no data to learn from. Adding noise at scale $\sigma$ effectively “thickens” the manifold by $\sigma$ in every direction, giving the score network meaningful gradients everywhere within that tube. The multi-scale hierarchy $\sigma_1 > \sigma_2 > \cdots > \sigma_L$ ensures that at the largest scale the manifold is so thick that score estimation is easy (the distribution looks nearly Gaussian), while at the smallest scale the score captures fine-grained detail on the manifold itself. Without this hierarchy, either the model cannot learn global structure (if $\sigma$ is too small) or it cannot resolve local detail (if $\sigma$ is too large).

Worked example: Noise-scale annealing

Consider the same Gaussian mixture $p(x) = 0.5\,\mathcal{N}(-2, 1) + 0.5\,\mathcal{N}(2, 1)$, but now we convolve it with Gaussian noise of different scales. The noisy distribution at scale $\sigma$ is $p_\sigma(x) = \int p(x')\, \mathcal{N}(x; x', \sigma^2)\, dx'$. For our mixture, this yields $p_\sigma(x) = 0.5\,\mathcal{N}(-2, 1+\sigma^2) + 0.5\,\mathcal{N}(2, 1+\sigma^2)$.

At $\sigma = 10$ (high noise): Each component has variance $1 + 100 = 101$, so standard deviation $\approx 10.05$. The two modes at $\pm 2$ are completely merged — the distribution is essentially a single broad Gaussian centered at $0$ with variance $\approx 105$. The score field $s_\sigma(x)$ is nearly linear: $s_{10}(x) \approx -x/105$. This is trivially easy to learn with a neural network. A linear model would suffice.

At $\sigma = 1$ (medium noise): Each component has variance $1 + 1 = 2$. The modes are still at $\pm 2$ but each has spread to standard deviation $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41$. The modes overlap significantly but are distinguishable. The score field is nonlinear with a zero crossing at $x=0$ and magnitude that varies between about $-1$ and $+1$. This requires a nonlinear model but the score is smooth and well-behaved.

At $\sigma = 0.01$ (low noise): Each component has variance $1.0001 \approx 1$, nearly unchanged from the original. The two modes are sharp and well-separated. Between the modes (around $x=0$), the density is extremely low: $p_{0.01}(0) \propto \exp(-2) \approx 0.135$, while at the peaks $p_{0.01}(\pm 2) \propto 1$. The score field is spiky near each mode (changing rapidly) and nearly zero in the low-density valley between them. In high dimensions, this low-density region would be even more extreme, and any finite dataset would have essentially no training examples there, making score estimation unreliable.

What does this mean?

This example illustrates the fundamental tradeoff in noise-conditional score estimation. High noise makes the score smooth and globally informative (easy to learn from limited data) but destroys fine detail. Low noise preserves sharp features but creates vast low-density regions where the score is poorly estimated. The genius of multi-scale models like NCSN and DDPM is that they learn the score at all noise levels simultaneously, using high-noise estimates to guide global structure and low-noise estimates to refine details. During sampling, annealed Langevin dynamics traverses this hierarchy from $\sigma_{\text{max}}$ down to $\sigma_{\text{min}}$, using each level’s score to progressively sharpen the sample.

Noise-conditional score networks

NCSN-style models (Noise-Conditional Score Networks, Song & Ermon, 2019) train one score network $s_\theta(x, \sigma)$ across many noise scales $\sigma_1 > \sigma_2 > \cdots > \sigma_L$. Conceptually, the high-noise regime teaches the model global geometry, while the low-noise regime teaches local detail; annealed Langevin dynamics then traverses that hierarchy from broad exploration to sharp refinement. The noise level is provided as a conditioning input to the network, typically through a sinusoidal positional encoding or a learned embedding, allowing a single set of parameters to represent the score at all scales simultaneously. This parameter sharing is crucial: the score at adjacent noise levels is strongly correlated (adding a tiny bit more noise only slightly blurs the score field), and sharing parameters lets the network exploit this correlation for better generalization.

Diffusion models and score-based models are therefore not rival camps so much as different coordinate systems for the same underlying object. Predicting the forward noise $\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)$ and predicting the smoothed score $s_\theta(x_t, t)$ are linked by a simple scale factor determined by the noise schedule. This equivalence was made precise by Song et al. (2021), who showed that DDPM, NCSN, and SDE-based diffusion models are all special cases of a unified framework based on score-parameterized SDEs. Understanding this equivalence is essential because it means insights from one formulation immediately transfer to the other.

$$s_\theta(x_t,t) = -\frac{\epsilon_\theta(x_t,t)}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}$$

Let us derive this equivalence formally.

Derivation: Score-to-epsilon equivalence
1
Recall the denoising score matching target. From our earlier derivation, the ground-truth conditional score is $\nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t|x_0) = -\epsilon / \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}$, where $\epsilon$ is the noise that was actually added to produce $x_t$. The DSM loss trains $s_\theta(x_t, t)$ to approximate this target.
2
Define the noise-prediction network. Suppose instead we train a network $\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)$ to predict the noise $\epsilon$ directly, minimizing $\mathbb{E}[\|\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) - \epsilon\|^2]$. This is the standard DDPM training objective.
3
Show the equivalence. If $s_\theta(x_t, t) = -\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)/\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}$, then: $$\|s_\theta - \nabla_{x_t}\log q(x_t|x_0)\|^2 = \left\|\frac{-\epsilon_\theta}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}} - \frac{-\epsilon}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}}\right\|^2 = \frac{1}{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\|\epsilon_\theta - \epsilon\|^2$$ The DSM loss and the $\epsilon$-prediction loss differ only by the time-dependent weight $1/(1-\bar{\alpha}_t)$. With appropriate weighting, they produce identical optimal networks. A score-prediction network and a noise-prediction network are the same model with different output scaling — you can convert between them at inference time by multiplying or dividing by $\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}$.
Information geometry perspective

The score function has a beautiful interpretation in information geometry — the study of statistical models as Riemannian manifolds. The space of all probability distributions forms a manifold where the Fisher information matrix $\mathcal{I}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}[s_\theta(x)\, s_\theta(x)^\top]$ serves as the natural Riemannian metric. The score $s_\theta(x) = \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x)$ is the tangent vector that tells you how the log-likelihood changes as you move in parameter space. Fisher information measures how much the score varies — higher Fisher information means the distribution is more “curved” and parameters are more identifiable. Score matching minimizes the Fisher divergence, which is the natural distance in this geometry. This connects score-based diffusion models to natural gradient descent (Amari, 1998): both use the Fisher metric to define the “right” notion of distance. When we train a score network, we are essentially learning the geometry of the data manifold — not just where data points are, but how the probability landscape curves around them.

Score Matching & Langevin Dynamics

The score viewpoint reveals diffusion as gradient information about a family of smoothed densities. Once the score is known, sampling becomes a controlled combination of uphill motion and stochastic exploration. We saw that the score of the Gaussian perturbation kernel has a closed form ($-\epsilon/\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}$), which reduces training to simple noise-prediction regression. The denoising score matching equivalence — proved via integration by parts — guarantees that this tractable objective minimizes the same Fisher divergence as the intractable explicit score matching loss. Finally, the Fokker–Planck analysis of Langevin dynamics shows that score-guided sampling provably converges to the target distribution, with the KL divergence decreasing at a rate governed by the Fisher divergence between the current and target densities.

Exact scores for a Gaussian mixture

Before training a neural score network, it is useful to work with a distribution whose score can be computed analytically. Gaussian mixtures are perfect for that purpose because their score is explicit but still rich enough to have multiple modes.

Python 

import torch
import math

def gaussian_mixture_score(x, means, sigma, logits=None):
    # x: [N, D], means: [K, D]
    if logits is None:
        logits = torch.zeros(len(means), device=x.device)
    weights = torch.softmax(logits, dim=0)

    diff = x[:, None, :] - means[None, :, :]
    log_comp = -0.5 * diff.pow(2).sum(dim=-1) / sigma**2
    log_comp = log_comp + torch.log(weights)[None, :]

    responsibilities = torch.softmax(log_comp, dim=1)
    component_scores = -(diff / sigma**2)
    score = (responsibilities[..., None] * component_scores).sum(dim=1)
    return score
Why this implementation matters

This implementation mirrors the analytic derivation: a mixture score is a responsibility-weighted average of component scores.

Denoising score matching loss

For Gaussian corruption, the target score of the noised distribution is available in closed form. That is what makes denoising score matching practical.

Python 

import torch.nn.functional as F

def dsm_loss(model, x0, sigma):
    noise = torch.randn_like(x0)
    xt = x0 + sigma * noise
    target_score = -(xt - x0) / (sigma ** 2)
    pred_score = model(xt, sigma)
    return F.mse_loss(pred_score, target_score)


def epsilon_loss_from_score(pred_score, noise, sigma):
    pred_eps = -sigma * pred_score
    return F.mse_loss(pred_eps, noise)
Why this implementation matters

The two losses differ only by a scale factor. This is the algebraic heart of the claim that diffusion and score matching are the same game in different notation.

Langevin dynamics sampler

Once a score is available, Langevin dynamics turns it into samples. The deterministic part follows the score uphill, while the random part keeps the chain from collapsing to a single mode.

Python 

@torch.no_grad()
def langevin_sample(score_fn, x_init, step_size=1e-2, n_steps=200):
    x = x_init.clone()
    trajectory = [x.clone()]
    for _ in range(n_steps):
        grad = score_fn(x)
        noise = torch.randn_like(x)
        x = x + step_size * grad + math.sqrt(2 * step_size) * noise
        trajectory.append(x.clone())
    return x, trajectory
Why this implementation matters

If the step size is too large, the chain becomes unstable. If it is too small, the chain mixes painfully slowly. That trade-off motivates annealed schedules and better solvers.

Annealed multi-scale sampling

Annealed Langevin dynamics uses a ladder of noise levels. High noise gives broad, global movement; low noise refines details.

Python 

@torch.no_grad()
def annealed_langevin(model, x_init, sigmas, steps_per_level=50, eps=1e-2):
    x = x_init.clone()
    for sigma in sigmas:
        alpha = eps * (sigma / sigmas[-1]) ** 2
        for _ in range(steps_per_level):
            grad = model(x, sigma)
            noise = torch.randn_like(x)
            x = x + alpha * grad + math.sqrt(2 * alpha) * noise
    return x

# Example sigma ladder
sigmas = torch.tensor([1.0, 0.5, 0.25, 0.1, 0.05])
Why this implementation matters

This annealed strategy is historically important because it makes visible how diffusion-like generation can emerge from score fields alone.

Easy Exercises

Start with direct computations and concept checks. These should feel like guided repetitions of the core formulas.

🟢 Exercise 1: Score of a standard normal ▼

Compute the score of $p(x)=\mathcal N(0,1)$.

The log-density is $-x^2/2$ up to a constant, so the derivative is $-x$. The score always points back toward the origin.

🟢 Exercise 2: Score at the mean ▼

What is the score of a Gaussian at its mean?

It is zero because the log-density is locally maximized there. A score field vanishes at stationary points of the density.

🟢 Exercise 3: Read the Langevin update ▼

In Langevin dynamics, which term follows the distribution and which term injects randomness?

The gradient term follows the score and therefore moves toward higher probability. The Gaussian term injects randomness so the chain explores rather than collapsing deterministically.

🟢 Exercise 4: Explain why normalization constants disappear ▼

Why does the score not depend on the partition function of a density?

Because differentiating $\log p(x)$ turns multiplicative constants into additive constants, and derivatives of constants vanish. That makes score methods attractive for unnormalized models.

🟢 Exercise 5: Compute a Gaussian corruption score ▼

If $x = x_0 + \sigma \epsilon$, what is $\nabla_x \log q_\sigma(x\mid x_0)$?

It is $-(x-x_0)/\sigma^2$. For Gaussian corruption the score is simply the negative residual divided by the variance.

🟢 Exercise 6: Direction of the score ▼

If a point lies to the right of a unimodal Gaussian mean, in which direction does the score point?

It points left, back toward the mean. More generally, the score points toward regions of higher density.

🟢 Exercise 7: Interpret NCSN qualitatively ▼

Why train on multiple noise scales instead of only one?

Different scales teach different geometric aspects of the distribution. High noise smooths the density for global guidance, while low noise sharpens local detail.

🟢 Exercise 8: Link epsilon to score ▼

If $\epsilon_\theta$ is known at timestep $t$, how do you recover the score estimate?

Use the scaling relation $s_\theta(x_t,t) = -\epsilon_\theta(x_t,t)/\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}$. The schedule determines the conversion factor.

🟢 Exercise 9: Low-density regions ▼

Why are low-density regions hard for direct score estimation?

Because the score can change rapidly there and the model receives little data support. Errors in those regions can badly distort sampling trajectories.

🟢 Exercise 10: Annealing intuition ▼

What is the intuitive purpose of annealed Langevin dynamics?

It starts with a smooth landscape that is easy to navigate globally and then gradually sharpens the landscape so fine detail can be resolved near the end.

Medium Exercises

Now move from recall to derivation. These questions ask you to justify formulas, compare viewpoints, and explain why the method works.

🟡 Exercise 11: Responsibility-weighted mixture score ▼

Why does the score of a Gaussian mixture become a responsibility-weighted average of component scores?

The derivative of the mixture density is the sum of derivatives of its components. Dividing by the mixture density introduces posterior responsibilities, which act as adaptive weights on the component score vectors.

🟡 Exercise 12: Integration by parts idea ▼

What role does integration by parts play in classical score matching?

It transfers derivatives from the unknown data density onto the model score field under suitable boundary conditions. That is how the objective becomes computable without direct access to the true score.

🟡 Exercise 13: DSM as denoising ▼

Why can denoising score matching be viewed as a denoising problem?

For Gaussian corruption, the target score is proportional to the negative residual between noisy and clean samples. Estimating that residual is exactly what a denoiser does.

🟡 Exercise 14: Annealed step size scaling ▼

Why are step sizes in annealed Langevin often scaled with the noise level?

Because the geometry of the score field changes with smoothing. Large-noise levels permit larger global moves, while low-noise levels require smaller steps to avoid overshooting fine structure.

🟡 Exercise 15: From score to density geometry ▼

What geometric information is lost if you know only the density values but not the score field?

You lose the local directional guidance encoded by gradients. The score says how to move, not just how good the current location is.

🟡 Exercise 16: Failure mode near a manifold ▼

Why can a score network trained only at tiny noise levels fail catastrophically during sampling?

The data manifold is extremely thin, so the network has little incentive to learn meaningful directions away from it. Sampling then wanders into unsupported regions where the estimated score is unreliable.

🟡 Exercise 17: Diffusion equivalence ▼

Explain why a DDPM noise predictor can be reinterpreted as a score network.

The forward corruption kernel is Gaussian, so the score of the noised conditional is proportional to the negative forward noise. Predicting that noise therefore yields the score after dividing by the appropriate schedule-dependent scale.

🟡 Exercise 18: Langevin stationary distribution ▼

Why does Langevin dynamics sample from the target distribution in the small-step limit?

The associated Fokker-Planck equation has the target density as its stationary solution. As discretization error vanishes and mixing is sufficient, the chain approaches that equilibrium.

🟡 Exercise 19: Score scaling with variance ▼

How does the magnitude of the Gaussian score change when corruption variance increases?

The score magnitude shrinks because it is divided by $\sigma^2$. Smoother distributions therefore produce gentler, more globally informative score fields.

🟡 Exercise 20: Sampling versus optimization ▼

Why is Langevin dynamics not just gradient ascent on log-density?

Pure gradient ascent would converge to a mode and stop exploring. Langevin keeps adding noise so the chain spreads according to the whole target distribution instead of collapsing to a point estimate.

Hard Exercises

These problems push you toward research-level thinking: asymptotics, design choices, edge cases, and deeper theoretical interpretation.

🔴 Exercise 21: Fisher divergence interpretation ▼

Why is classical score matching minimizing a Fisher divergence rather than KL divergence?

The objective compares gradients of log-densities directly, which is exactly the Fisher divergence. This is a derivative-based discrepancy and behaves differently from KL because it emphasizes local geometry rather than only density ratios.

🔴 Exercise 22: Boundary conditions in score matching ▼

What technical assumption is hidden inside the integration-by-parts derivation?

One assumes boundary terms vanish at infinity so that the surface integral drops out. In practice this means densities and score fields must decay sufficiently fast.

🔴 Exercise 23: Noise-level design ▼

How would you choose a ladder of noise levels for an NCSN-style model on a highly multimodal dataset?

You want the largest scale high enough to connect distant modes through smoothing and the smallest scale low enough to recover sharp detail. A geometric or log-spaced ladder is often sensible because densities change multiplicatively across scales.

🔴 Exercise 24: Score error amplification ▼

Why can small score errors create large sampling artifacts after many Langevin steps?

Each update uses the model estimate as a direction field, so systematic bias compounds over time. Small rotational or scaling errors can therefore send trajectories into the wrong basin or prevent proper mixing.

🔴 Exercise 25: High-dimensional score estimation ▼

Why does score estimation become more difficult in high dimension even with Gaussian smoothing?

Volume grows rapidly away from the data concentration region, so the model must generalize score behavior over a much larger ambient space. The signal available from finite data becomes comparatively sparse.

🔴 Exercise 26: Annealed versus reverse diffusion ▼

In what sense is annealed Langevin a precursor to reverse diffusion sampling?

Both methods start from a heavily smoothed distribution and progressively refine samples toward the data manifold. Reverse diffusion makes the same idea explicit through a learned time-indexed denoising process with schedule-controlled noise.

🔴 Exercise 27: Consistency of score conversion ▼

Why must the score-to-noise conversion include the exact schedule coefficient rather than an arbitrary constant?

The scale of the score is determined by the variance of the forward corruption kernel. Using the wrong coefficient miscalibrates every reverse step and effectively asks the sampler to denoise at the wrong SNR.

🔴 Exercise 28: Mode hopping trade-off ▼

How does increasing Langevin noise help and hurt mode exploration?

More noise helps the chain cross low-density barriers between modes, but too much noise overwhelms the gradient signal and destroys refinement. Good samplers balance exploration and local correction.

🔴 Exercise 29: Smooth densities as curriculum ▼

Why can one view multi-scale score training as a curriculum learning strategy?

High-noise distributions are easier because their geometry is smoother and more global. Training then gradually exposes the model to harder, sharper densities as noise decreases.

🔴 Exercise 30: Research extension ▼

Propose a principled way to combine score matching with learned feature spaces rather than raw pixels.

Train the score network in a latent representation whose encoder preserves semantics while reducing dimensionality. The key requirement is that the latent corruption process and its score remain well defined so the sampler still corresponds to a valid generative path.
