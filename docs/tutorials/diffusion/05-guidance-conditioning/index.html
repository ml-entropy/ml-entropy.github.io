Guidance & Conditioning

Unconditional generation asks for samples from $p(x)$, but most creative applications ask for samples from $p(x\mid y)$. The conditioning variable $y$ might be a class label, a text prompt, a mask, a depth map, a pose skeleton, or any other side signal that tells the model what to create.

Guidance works by bending the denoising field toward outputs that are more compatible with the condition. The cleverness of modern diffusion systems lies in doing that without retraining a separate generative model for every new condition type.

9.1 Classifier Guidance

Condition on class $y$ by modifying the score:

$$\nabla_x \log p(x_t | y) = \nabla_x \log p(x_t) + \nabla_x \log p(y | x_t)$$

Train a separate classifier $p(y|x_t)$ on noisy data and add its gradient. The classifier must handle inputs at every noise level the diffusion process uses, which means it needs to recognize classes even in heavily corrupted images. At high noise levels this is genuinely difficult — the class signal is almost completely buried under Gaussian noise. This practical difficulty is one of the key motivations behind the shift to classifier-free methods: maintaining and training a robust noisy-data classifier adds significant engineering and computational overhead to the entire generation pipeline.

9.2 Classifier-Free Guidance

Combine conditional and unconditional scores:

$$\tilde{\epsilon}_\theta = (1 + w) \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t, y) - w \cdot \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t, \emptyset)$$

where $w > 0$ is the guidance weight. Higher $w$ means stronger adherence to the condition but less sample diversity. When $w = 0$ the model is fully unconditional; when $w = 1$ it uses the raw conditional prediction; and when $w > 1$ it amplifies the conditional direction beyond what the model would naturally produce. In practice, values of $w$ between 5 and 15 are common for Stable Diffusion, while DALL-E typically uses $w$ between 2 and 5. The optimal scale depends on the prompt complexity, the model architecture, and the desired trade-off between creative variety and precise prompt matching.

This simple formula is what makes text-to-image models like DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, and Imagen work so well. Despite its apparent simplicity — just a weighted combination of two noise predictions — classifier-free guidance implicitly performs Bayesian conditioning without ever training an explicit classifier. The idea was introduced by Ho and Salimans (2022) and rapidly became the default steering mechanism across nearly all production diffusion systems. Its elegance lies in the fact that a single denoising network, trained with random conditioning dropout, simultaneously learns both the unconditional and conditional data distributions.

Classifier guidance from Bayes' rule

Classifier guidance starts from Bayes' rule. Because $\log p(x\mid y) = \log p(x) + \log p(y\mid x) - \log p(y)$, differentiating with respect to $x$ removes the constant term $\log p(y)$ and leaves a clean steering identity.

$$\nabla_x \log p(x\mid y) = \nabla_x \log p(x) + \nabla_x \log p(y\mid x)$$

The first term is the unconditional score supplied by the diffusion model. The second term comes from a noisy-image classifier and points in the direction that makes the condition $y$ more likely. Sampling then becomes "follow the usual denoising field, plus an extra push toward the condition."

Classifier guidance: from Bayes' rule to temperature-controlled steering
1
Start from Bayes' rule applied to the joint: $p(x \mid y) = \frac{p(x)\,p(y \mid x)}{p(y)}$. Taking logarithms gives $\log p(x \mid y) = \log p(x) + \log p(y \mid x) - \log p(y)$.
2
Differentiate with respect to $x$. Since $p(y)$ does not depend on $x$, the gradient of $\log p(y)$ vanishes: $$\nabla_x \log p(x \mid y) = \nabla_x \log p(x) + \nabla_x \log p(y \mid x)$$ The first term is the unconditional score (from the diffusion model) and the second is the classifier gradient.
3
Introduce a guidance scale $w$ that controls the strength of the classifier signal. Replace the classifier gradient with a scaled version: $$\nabla_x \log p_w(x \mid y) = \nabla_x \log p(x) + w \cdot \nabla_x \log p(y \mid x)$$ When $w = 1$ we recover exact Bayesian conditioning. When $w > 1$ we sharpen the conditional distribution, effectively sampling from a "temperature-reduced" version $p(y \mid x)^w$ that concentrates more probability mass on regions where $y$ is highly likely.
4
In practice, this means the diffusion sampling update at each step $t$ becomes: $$\hat{\mu}_t = \mu_t(x_t) - w \cdot \sigma_t^2 \,\nabla_{x_t} \log p(y \mid x_t)$$ where $\mu_t(x_t)$ is the mean predicted by the unconditional denoiser and $\sigma_t^2$ scales the classifier gradient to match the noise schedule. Larger $w$ bends the denoising trajectory more aggressively toward the condition.
How classifier and classifier-free guidance are related
1
Classifier guidance adds an explicit classifier gradient to the unconditional score.
2
Classifier-free guidance avoids a separate classifier by training one denoiser that sometimes sees the condition and sometimes sees a null token.
3
Subtracting the unconditional prediction from the conditional one isolates a learned condition direction.
4
Scaling that difference plays the same conceptual role as scaling the classifier gradient: stronger adherence to $y$, usually at the cost of diversity.

This works beautifully in principle, but maintaining a separate classifier is expensive and somewhat awkward. Classifier-free guidance is so successful because it bakes both unconditional and conditional behavior into the same denoiser and recovers a similar steering effect with one extra forward pass.

Worked example: what the CFG scale is actually doing

Imagine the unconditional denoiser predicts a direction saying "make this look more like a generic natural image," while the conditional denoiser adds "specifically make it look like a red fox in snow." Classifier-free guidance subtracts those two vectors, keeps the difference as the prompt-specific direction, and then multiplies that direction by the guidance scale.

At moderate scales this is exactly what we want: weak prompt information becomes strong enough to shape the sample. At extreme scales the same multiplication also amplifies modeling error, which is why too much CFG often produces oversaturated colors, brittle composition, repeated motifs, or strange prompt over-literalness.

Common misconception: higher guidance is not always better

CFG does not create information the model never learned. It only reweights conditional and unconditional directions, so pushing the scale too high often improves prompt adherence while making realism, diversity, and layout stability worse.

Worked example: CFG guidance scale sweep

Let's make the effect of the guidance scale concrete with a simplified numerical example. Consider the prompt "photo of a cat" and suppose, at some denoising step, our model produces the following two-dimensional noise predictions (we compress to 2D for illustration, but real models operate in thousands of dimensions):

$$\epsilon_\emptyset = [0.5,\; 0.3] \qquad \text{(unconditional prediction)}$$ $$\epsilon_c = [0.2,\; 0.8] \qquad \text{(conditional prediction for "photo of a cat")}$$

The conditional direction — the difference that captures "cat-ness" — is $\epsilon_c - \epsilon_\emptyset = [-0.3,\; 0.5]$. The CFG formula $\tilde{\epsilon} = \epsilon_\emptyset + w(\epsilon_c - \epsilon_\emptyset)$ amplifies this direction by the guidance scale $w$. Let's compute the guided prediction at several scales:

Guided noise prediction at increasing scales
1
$w = 1$ (no boost): $\tilde{\epsilon} = [0.5, 0.3] + 1 \cdot [-0.3, 0.5] = [0.2, 0.8]$. This just recovers the raw conditional prediction — no amplification at all. Magnitude: $\|\tilde{\epsilon}\| = 0.82$.
2
$w = 3$: $\tilde{\epsilon} = [0.5, 0.3] + 3 \cdot [-0.3, 0.5] = [-0.4, 1.8]$. The prediction shifts noticeably toward the "cat" direction. Magnitude: $\|\tilde{\epsilon}\| = 1.84$. The model now pushes harder toward cat-like features.
3
$w = 7$: $\tilde{\epsilon} = [0.5, 0.3] + 7 \cdot [-0.3, 0.5] = [-1.6, 3.8]$. The guided vector is strongly dominated by the conditional direction. Magnitude: $\|\tilde{\epsilon}\| = 4.12$. This is the sweet spot for many Stable Diffusion workflows — strong prompt adherence with acceptable image quality.
4
$w = 15$: $\tilde{\epsilon} = [0.5, 0.3] + 15 \cdot [-0.3, 0.5] = [-4.0, 7.8]$. The magnitude has exploded to $\|\tilde{\epsilon}\| = 8.77$ — more than 10× the raw conditional prediction. At this point the noise estimate is far outside the range the model was trained on, causing color saturation, loss of fine detail, and repetitive textures.

Notice how the direction stays roughly the same across all scales (pointing toward "cat"), but the magnitude grows linearly with $w$. This is exactly the quality-diversity trade-off in action. In practice, Stable Diffusion defaults to $w = 7.5$, which sits in the range $[5, 15]$ where prompt fidelity is strong but artifacts are manageable. DALL-E models typically use lower scales around $w \in [2, 5]$, partly because their training and architecture choices already produce strong conditional predictions.

Worked example: computational cost of classifier-free guidance

One aspect of CFG that practitioners encounter immediately is its computational cost. Because the formula requires both an unconditional prediction $\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t, \emptyset)$ and a conditional prediction $\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t, y)$, every single denoising step needs two forward passes through the full U-Net. This effectively doubles the inference cost compared to an unconditional sampler.

Consider Stable Diffusion v1.5, whose U-Net has roughly 860 million parameters. At fp16 precision the model weights occupy about 1.72 GB of GPU memory. On an NVIDIA A100, a single forward pass through this U-Net takes approximately 30–40 ms for a 512×512 latent. With CFG, each denoising step now takes 60–80 ms because we must run two passes.

If we use a DDIM sampler with $T = 50$ steps, the total inference involves $50 \times 2 = 100$ forward passes. At ~35 ms per pass, that's roughly 3.5 seconds of pure compute on an A100 — before accounting for the VAE decoder, text encoding, and memory transfers. The standard optimization is to batch the two predictions together: stack the unconditional and conditional inputs into a single batch of size 2, run one batched forward pass (which the GPU can parallelize efficiently), and then split the outputs. This doesn't reduce the total FLOPs, but it eliminates the overhead of two separate kernel launches and often saves 10–20% wall-clock time.

Failure modes of high guidance scales

At very high guidance scales ($w > 20$), generated images develop a characteristic set of artifacts that experienced practitioners learn to recognize. Colors become oversaturated and "neon-like," textures take on a plastic or waxy quality, and fine details like hair strands or fabric weave are replaced by smooth, featureless surfaces. Human faces become unnervingly "perfect" — symmetrical to a degree that looks artificial, with skin that appears airbrushed beyond recognition. Anatomical errors also increase: extra fingers, malformed hands, and impossible body proportions become more common because the model is being pushed far from its training distribution. Compositionally, high guidance tends to collapse diversity — run the same prompt ten times and you may get nearly identical outputs. The Stable Diffusion default of $w = 7.5$ exists precisely because it sits at the empirical sweet spot where prompt adherence is strong but these failure modes remain rare.

Why CFG works with any sampler

A common source of confusion is whether CFG is "compatible" with different sampling algorithms. The answer is yes — universally. CFG modifies the noise prediction $\tilde{\epsilon}_\theta$, not the sampling algorithm itself. Once you have the guided noise prediction, you can feed it into DDPM, DDIM, DPM-Solver, DPM-Solver++, Euler, Heun, or any other sampler without modification. The sampler only cares about receiving a noise estimate at each step; it is agnostic to how that estimate was computed. This composability is one of the reasons CFG became the universal standard: it slots cleanly into any sampling pipeline as a drop-in replacement for the raw model prediction.

Text conditioning and cross-attention

In text-to-image systems, the condition is usually encoded by a language model such as CLIP or T5. Cross-attention lets image features query those text embeddings so that different spatial regions can focus on different words or concepts during denoising.

Classifier-free guidance then blends the conditional and unconditional denoiser outputs. Small guidance scales preserve diversity, moderate scales sharpen prompt fidelity, and extreme scales can oversaturate or collapse variety.

ControlNet, inpainting, and compositional prompts

ControlNet augments a pretrained denoiser with trainable side branches that inject spatial control signals such as edges, depth, or pose. The base model stays largely intact, which is why ControlNet can add new control modes without relearning the entire visual prior from scratch.

The key engineering trick is the zero-initialized convolution. At initialization the new branch contributes almost nothing, so the copied pretrained U-Net behaves exactly like the original model. During finetuning, the control branch gradually earns influence only where the optimization signal consistently says the structural condition should matter.

A helpful mental model is "frozen broad knowledge plus trainable precise structure." The pretrained backbone remembers what realistic images look like, while the control branch learns how edges, depth maps, segmentation masks, or pose skeletons should bend that realism into a desired spatial arrangement.

Inpainting and image-to-image methods work by partially constraining the reverse process: known pixels are reinserted or softly preserved while unknown regions are regenerated. Negative prompts and compositional prompting can be understood in the same algebraic spirit — they shape the denoising field by adding or subtracting concept directions.

Negative prompting as vector arithmetic in epsilon-space
1
Standard CFG uses the null-conditioned prediction $\epsilon_\emptyset$ as its baseline: $\hat{\epsilon} = \epsilon_\emptyset + w\,(\epsilon_c - \epsilon_\emptyset)$. A negative prompt replaces that null baseline with an explicit "anti-condition" — a text description of what we want to avoid. Let $\epsilon_n = \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t, y_{\text{neg}})$ denote the model's prediction conditioned on the negative prompt $y_{\text{neg}}$.
2
The simplest negative-prompt formula substitutes $\epsilon_n$ for $\epsilon_\emptyset$: $$\hat{\epsilon} = \epsilon_n + w\,(\epsilon_c - \epsilon_n)$$ This moves the denoising prediction toward the positive concept $y$ and away from the negative concept $y_{\text{neg}}$. The guidance direction $\epsilon_c - \epsilon_n$ now spans from the negative concept to the positive one, which can be a larger and more informative vector than $\epsilon_c - \epsilon_\emptyset$.
3
A more general formulation uses separate scales for the positive and negative guidance: $$\hat{\epsilon} = \epsilon_\emptyset + w\,(\epsilon_c - \epsilon_\emptyset) - w_n\,(\epsilon_n - \epsilon_\emptyset)$$ Geometrically, this is vector arithmetic in epsilon-space: we take the null prediction as our origin, add $w$ times the "toward positive concept" direction, and subtract $w_n$ times the "toward negative concept" direction. The result is a noise prediction that pulls the sample toward what we want and pushes it away from what we don't — all through simple linear combinations of the model's own predictions.
Worked example: why zero convolutions matter

If the copied control branch started with large random weights, it would immediately distort the hidden states of the pretrained denoiser and destroy sample quality. Zero initialization makes the branch a no-op on day one, so finetuning can add structure gradually instead of overwriting the visual prior all at once.

Guidance & Conditioning

Guidance is how diffusion models stop being generic and start being useful. By steering the denoising field with prompts, labels, masks, or structural maps, the same generative prior becomes a flexible conditional generator. Classifier guidance provides the cleanest theoretical derivation — Bayes' rule decomposes the conditional score into an unconditional score plus a classifier gradient — but classifier-free guidance has become the universal practical standard because it avoids training a separate noisy-image classifier entirely.

The guidance scale $w$ is the single most important user-facing hyperparameter in modern image generation. It controls the trade-off between prompt fidelity and sample diversity, and getting it right (typically $w \in [5, 15]$ for Stable Diffusion) is the difference between a useful tool and a gallery of oversaturated artifacts. Beyond basic prompting, ControlNet injects spatial structure through zero-initialized side branches, inpainting constrains known pixels at every step, and negative prompting performs concept subtraction in epsilon-space. Together, these techniques turn a single pretrained denoiser into a remarkably versatile conditional generation engine.

Classifier-free guidance in one function

The essence of CFG is a linear combination of two denoiser outputs: one conditional and one unconditional. This tiny line of code is arguably the most economically important equation in modern image generation.

Python 

@torch.no_grad()
def classifier_free_guidance(model, x, t, cond, scale=7.5):
    eps_cond = model(x, t, cond)
    eps_uncond = model(x, t, None)
    return eps_uncond + scale * (eps_cond - eps_uncond)

# equivalent form
# guided = (1 + scale) * eps_cond - scale * eps_uncond
Why this implementation matters

When scale=0, the sampler is unconditional. As the scale grows, prompt fidelity improves but diversity usually drops.

Classifier-guided reverse step

If a noisy-image classifier is available, its gradient can be added directly to the reverse score. The classifier-free trick above is usually preferred in practice, but the classifier form is still the cleanest Bayes-rule derivation.

Python 

@torch.no_grad()
def classifier_guided_eps(model, classifier, x, t, y, sigma_t, guidance_scale=1.0):
    x = x.detach().requires_grad_(True)
    eps = model(x, t, None)
    logits = classifier(x, t)
    logp = torch.log_softmax(logits, dim=-1)
    selected = logp[torch.arange(x.size(0), device=x.device), y].sum()
    grad = torch.autograd.grad(selected, x)[0]
    guided_score = -eps / sigma_t + guidance_scale * grad
    guided_eps = -sigma_t * guided_score
    return guided_eps.detach()
Why this implementation matters

This is elegant but expensive because every denoising step now also requires a backward pass through the classifier.

Text conditioning with cross-attention embeddings

A practical text-to-image pipeline first encodes prompts into context vectors. The denoiser then consumes both image latents and text features.

Python 

def encode_prompts(text_encoder, prompts, device):
    tokens = text_encoder.tokenize(prompts).to(device)
    with torch.no_grad():
        context = text_encoder(tokens)
    return context


@torch.no_grad()
def conditioned_step(unet, x, t, text_context):
    # the U-Net internally uses cross-attention blocks
    return unet(x, t, context=text_context)
Why this implementation matters

The heavy lifting happens inside the denoiser architecture, where image features query the text representation repeatedly at multiple resolutions.

Inpainting loop sketch

Inpainting keeps the known region anchored while allowing unknown regions to evolve under the denoiser. The mask is reimposed at every step so the sampler cannot drift away from observed pixels.

Python 

@torch.no_grad()
def inpaint(model, init_latent, known_latent, mask, timesteps):
    x = init_latent.clone()
    for t in reversed(timesteps):
        batch_t = torch.full((x.size(0),), t, device=x.device, dtype=torch.long)
        eps = model(x, batch_t)
        x = denoise_step_from_eps(x, t, eps)
        x = mask * known_latent + (1 - mask) * x
    return x
Why this implementation matters

This same pattern generalizes to image-to-image generation, where the “known” latent is a noisy version of an input image rather than a hard mask.

Easy Exercises

Start with direct computations and concept checks. These should feel like guided repetitions of the core formulas.

🟢 Exercise 1: Conditional versus unconditional generation ▼

What distribution does a guided diffusion model try to sample from?

It tries to sample from a conditional distribution such as $p(x\mid y)$ rather than only the unconditional $p(x)$. The condition may be a label, prompt, or spatial control signal.

🟢 Exercise 2: Read the CFG formula ▼

In classifier-free guidance, what are the two denoiser evaluations being combined?

They are the conditional prediction and the unconditional prediction. Guidance takes their difference and amplifies it to steer the reverse process.

🟢 Exercise 3: Effect of zero guidance ▼

What happens when the CFG scale is set to zero?

The conditional correction disappears, so the sampler behaves like the unconditional model. Prompt adherence drops, but diversity is usually higher.

🟢 Exercise 4: High guidance intuition ▼

Why can extremely high guidance scales produce brittle or oversaturated images?

Because the conditional direction is over-amplified relative to the base denoising field. The sampler then sacrifices diversity and can exaggerate textures or colors unnaturally.

🟢 Exercise 5: Classifier guidance input ▼

What must a classifier-guidance classifier be trained to do?

It must classify noisy inputs at the same timesteps used by the diffusion process. A clean-image classifier is not enough because the sampler queries it on noisy states.

🟢 Exercise 6: Negative prompt idea ▼

What is a negative prompt doing conceptually?

It pushes the denoising trajectory away from features associated with an unwanted concept. In practice it alters the unconditional branch used in CFG-style interpolation.

🟢 Exercise 7: Role of text encoder ▼

Why is a separate text encoder often used in text-to-image systems?

It converts variable-length language into dense context embeddings that the denoiser can attend to repeatedly. The denoiser itself is then free to focus on image-space computation.

🟢 Exercise 8: Inpainting constraint ▼

Why are known pixels reinserted during inpainting?

To stop the reverse process from drifting away from observed content. Reimposing the known region each step preserves consistency with the input image.

🟢 Exercise 9: ControlNet purpose ▼

What does ControlNet add that ordinary text conditioning does not?

It adds explicit spatial control, such as edges or depth, rather than only semantic guidance from text. That lets generation obey detailed layouts and structures.

🟢 Exercise 10: Compositional prompting ▼

Why are diffusion models naturally compatible with combining conditions?

Because guidance acts on denoising directions, and those directions can often be added, subtracted, or blended. Composition is therefore an operation on vector fields rather than on hard symbolic rules.

Medium Exercises

Now move from recall to derivation. These questions ask you to justify formulas, compare viewpoints, and explain why the method works.

🟡 Exercise 11: Bayes-rule derivation ▼

Why does classifier guidance follow from Bayes\' rule?

The conditional log-density splits as $\log p(x\mid y)=\log p(x)+\log p(y\mid x)-\log p(y)$. Differentiating with respect to $x$ removes the constant term $\log p(y)$ and yields the sum of the unconditional score and classifier gradient.

🟡 Exercise 12: Implicit classifier in CFG ▼

Why is classifier-free guidance often described as learning an implicit classifier?

The difference between conditional and unconditional denoiser outputs acts like a learned estimate of how the condition changes the score. No separate classifier is trained, but the same directional information is encoded in the denoiser pair.

🟡 Exercise 13: Guidance-diversity trade-off ▼

Why does stronger guidance usually reduce diversity?

Because more of the sampler\'s freedom is spent following the condition-specific direction rather than exploring the unconditional data prior. The path becomes more concentrated around a narrower subset of satisfying samples.

🟡 Exercise 14: Noisy classifier challenge ▼

Why is training a classifier on noisy images harder than training on clean images?

At large timesteps the semantic signal is faint and class evidence is heavily corrupted. The classifier must therefore infer labels under severe information loss, which is exactly why classifier-free guidance became so attractive.

🟡 Exercise 15: Cross-attention role ▼

What does cross-attention let image features do during denoising?

It lets spatial image tokens query the text context and focus on relevant prompt fragments. Different locations can therefore attend to different words as generation unfolds.

🟡 Exercise 16: ControlNet zero-conv idea ▼

Why are zero-initialized convolutions useful in ControlNet?

They ensure the added control branch starts with no effect on the pretrained model. The network can then learn control gradually without immediately disrupting the base denoiser\'s behavior.

🟡 Exercise 17: Img2img strength parameter ▼

Why does stronger initial noising produce bigger edits in image-to-image generation?

The more corruption you add to the input image, the less tightly the sampler remains tied to the original content. Strong noising therefore grants more freedom to recompose shape, texture, and semantics.

🟡 Exercise 18: Negative prompt algebra ▼

How can negative prompts be interpreted algebraically in CFG?

They modify the reference branch against which the positive condition is contrasted. That effectively subtracts features associated with the negative concept from the final guided direction.

🟡 Exercise 19: Multiple conditions ▼

Why can multiple conditions often be combined by weighted sums of guidance directions?

Because each condition contributes a direction that says how denoising should change to satisfy it. Weighted sums are a natural first approximation when those conditions do not conflict too strongly.

🟡 Exercise 20: Prompt overfitting ▼

What does it mean for a sampler to overfit to a prompt?

It means the guidance is so strong that outputs lose variety and repeatedly instantiate the same narrow interpretation of the request. The images become on-topic but less creative or robust.

Hard Exercises

These problems push you toward research-level thinking: asymptotics, design choices, edge cases, and deeper theoretical interpretation.

🔴 Exercise 21: Optimal guidance scale ▼

Why is there unlikely to be one globally optimal guidance scale for every prompt?

The ideal scale depends on prompt ambiguity, dataset bias, sampler noise level, and user preference between fidelity and diversity. Different prompts require different amounts of steering to land in a satisfying regime.

🔴 Exercise 22: Mode collapse under guidance ▼

How can aggressive guidance create a diffusion analogue of mode collapse?

When the conditional direction overwhelms the unconditional prior, many distinct latent starting points are pulled into the same narrow family of outputs. The system is still generative, but effective output diversity collapses.

🔴 Exercise 23: Conditional score composition ▼

Under what assumption is it most reasonable to add guidance vectors from multiple conditions?

It is most reasonable when the conditions are approximately compatible and their score contributions combine linearly over the relevant region of latent space. Strongly conflicting conditions break that approximation.

🔴 Exercise 24: Classifier-free versus classifier guidance ▼

Why has CFG largely displaced classifier guidance in large-scale image generation?

CFG avoids training and backpropagating through a separate noisy classifier, integrates naturally with text conditioning, and typically offers a better practical trade-off between quality, compute, and engineering complexity.

🔴 Exercise 25: Control fidelity versus prior fidelity ▼

What is the main tension in ControlNet-style methods?

The control branch should impose external structure without destroying the pretrained model\'s rich visual prior. Too little control ignores the conditioning signal; too much control can make images rigid or unnatural.

🔴 Exercise 26: Inpainting boundary artifacts ▼

Why do seams appear near mask boundaries in naive inpainting?

The generated region and preserved region may follow inconsistent denoising histories near the boundary. Better masking schedules, latent blending, or boundary-aware conditioning are needed to smooth the transition.

🔴 Exercise 27: Prompt entanglement ▼

Why can text prompts interact nonlinearly even when each phrase seems simple in isolation?

The denoiser does not represent text as independent symbolic slots; it learns a shared embedding space where concepts influence one another. Guidance therefore acts on an entangled semantic geometry rather than a clean Boolean algebra.

🔴 Exercise 28: Robust negative prompting ▼

Why can negative prompts sometimes accidentally remove desirable features?

Concepts in embedding space are correlated, so steering away from one attribute may also weaken nearby desirable attributes. This is a geometric side effect of operating in a learned representation rather than an explicit symbolic ontology.

🔴 Exercise 29: Guidance at different noise levels ▼

Why might the best guidance scale depend on the timestep?

High-noise stages determine global composition and semantics, while low-noise stages refine details. The amount of useful steering can therefore vary across the trajectory rather than remaining constant.

🔴 Exercise 30: Research extension ▼

Propose a way to make guidance adaptive rather than using one fixed scale.

One approach is to let a small policy network predict the guidance scale from timestep, prompt embedding, and current latent statistics. That policy could optimize a downstream reward balancing fidelity, diversity, and artifact suppression.
