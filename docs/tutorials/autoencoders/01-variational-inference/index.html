Variational Inference

Probabilistic models are only useful if we can perform inference in them. Given observed data $x$, we want to compute the posterior distribution over latent variables $p(z|x)$, which tells us everything the model believes about the hidden structure behind the observation. In simple models -- conjugate exponential families, for example -- the posterior has a closed form. But in the vast majority of interesting models, including deep generative models, the posterior is intractable. Variational inference is the principled framework for turning the intractable inference problem into a tractable optimization problem, and it is the mathematical backbone of variational autoencoders.

The core challenge is straightforward to state. Bayes' theorem gives us the posterior:

$$p(z|x) = \frac{p(x|z)\,p(z)}{p(x)}$$

The numerator is easy: $p(x|z)$ is the likelihood (our model's prediction given latents), and $p(z)$ is the prior (our belief about the latents before seeing data). The problem lies entirely in the denominator, the marginal likelihood or evidence:

$$p(x) = \int p(x|z)\,p(z)\,dz$$

This integral sums the likelihood over every possible configuration of the latent variables, weighted by the prior. For a Gaussian mixture model with $K$ components and $N$ data points, this involves summing over $K^N$ possible assignments. For a neural network decoder with a continuous latent space $z \in \mathbb{R}^d$, it involves integrating over all of $\mathbb{R}^d$ where the integrand is a highly nonlinear function of $z$. Neither computation is remotely feasible. This is the intractability problem that variational inference solves.

Definition: Variational Inference

Variational inference (VI) is a method for approximating intractable posterior distributions. Instead of computing $p(z|x)$ exactly, we posit a family of tractable distributions $\mathcal{Q} = \{q_\phi(z|x) : \phi \in \Phi\}$ and find the member of this family closest to the true posterior by solving an optimization problem. The "variational" in the name comes from the calculus of variations, since we are optimizing over a space of functions (distributions) rather than a finite-dimensional parameter vector -- though in practice we parameterize $q$ and optimize over those parameters.

The central idea is to replace an intractable integration problem with a tractable optimization problem. We choose a family of simple distributions $q_\phi(z|x)$ -- for instance, factorized Gaussians -- and then find the member of that family that is closest to the true posterior $p(z|x)$. Closeness is measured by the Kullback-Leibler divergence:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z|x)) = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}\left[\log \frac{q_\phi(z|x)}{p(z|x)}\right]$$

This quantity is always non-negative (by Gibbs' inequality or Jensen's inequality applied to the convex function $-\log$) and equals zero if and only if $q_\phi(z|x) = p(z|x)$ almost everywhere. So minimizing this KL divergence with respect to $\phi$ gives us the best approximation to the true posterior within our chosen family. But there is a catch: expanding the KL divergence requires evaluating $\log p(z|x)$, which itself requires the intractable evidence $p(x)$. This seems circular -- we wanted to avoid computing $p(x)$, yet the objective we want to minimize depends on it. The resolution of this apparent circularity is the evidence lower bound.

The Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO)

The ELBO is the central quantity in variational inference. It provides a tractable objective that, when maximized, simultaneously gives us a good approximation to the posterior and a lower bound on the log-evidence. The derivation is one of the most important calculations in modern machine learning, and it can be approached from several angles. We present the most illuminating route.

Derivation: The Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO)
1
Start from the log-evidence. We begin with the quantity we wish we could compute but cannot: $$\log p(x) = \log \int p(x, z)\, dz$$ This is intractable because the integral inside the logarithm couples all dimensions of $z$.
2
Introduce $q$ via importance sampling. Multiply and divide by $q_\phi(z|x)$ inside the integral: $$\log p(x) = \log \int \frac{p(x, z)}{q_\phi(z|x)} q_\phi(z|x)\, dz = \log\, \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}\left[\frac{p(x, z)}{q_\phi(z|x)}\right]$$ This is an identity -- it holds for any distribution $q_\phi(z|x)$ that has the same support as $p(z|x)$.
3
Apply Jensen's inequality. Since $\log$ is a concave function, Jensen's inequality gives us: $$\log\, \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}\left[\frac{p(x, z)}{q_\phi(z|x)}\right] \geq \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}\left[\log \frac{p(x, z)}{q_\phi(z|x)}\right]$$ The right-hand side is the ELBO: $\mathcal{L}(\phi) = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}\left[\log \frac{p(x, z)}{q_\phi(z|x)}\right]$.
4
Show that the gap is the KL divergence. We can also derive the relationship algebraically. Start from the KL divergence definition: $$D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z|x)) = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}\left[\log \frac{q_\phi(z|x)}{p(z|x)}\right] = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}\left[\log q_\phi(z|x) - \log p(x,z) + \log p(x)\right]$$ Since $\log p(x)$ does not depend on $z$, it comes out of the expectation: $$D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi \| p) = -\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}\left[\log \frac{p(x,z)}{q_\phi(z|x)}\right] + \log p(x) = -\mathcal{L}(\phi) + \log p(x)$$
5
Rearrange to obtain the fundamental identity. Solving for $\log p(x)$: $$\log p(x) = \mathcal{L}(\phi) + D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z|x))$$ Since $D_{\text{KL}} \geq 0$, we have $\log p(x) \geq \mathcal{L}(\phi)$, confirming that the ELBO is indeed a lower bound. Maximizing $\mathcal{L}(\phi)$ is equivalent to minimizing $D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi \| p)$, because $\log p(x)$ is a constant with respect to $\phi$.

The identity $\log p(x) = \text{ELBO} + D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi \| p)$ is perhaps the single most important equation in variational inference. It tells us three things simultaneously. First, the ELBO is always a lower bound on the log-evidence, hence its name. Second, the gap between the ELBO and the true log-evidence is exactly the KL divergence between our approximation and the true posterior -- so we can gauge how good our approximation is by measuring how tight the bound is. Third, maximizing the ELBO with respect to $\phi$ is equivalent to minimizing the KL divergence, which means we can find the best approximation to the posterior without ever evaluating $p(z|x)$ directly.

ELBO Decomposition: Reconstruction + Regularization

The ELBO can be decomposed into two terms with clear interpretations. Starting from the definition and expanding the joint $p(x,z) = p_\theta(x|z)\,p(z)$:

$$\mathcal{L}(\phi, \theta) = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z))$$

The first term is the reconstruction term (or expected log-likelihood). It measures how well the model can reconstruct the data $x$ from latent codes $z$ drawn from the approximate posterior $q_\phi(z|x)$. Maximizing this term alone would encourage the model to encode as much information as possible into $z$ -- the encoder would try to make $z$ a perfect, lossless representation of $x$.

The second term is the KL penalty (or regularization term). It penalizes the approximate posterior $q_\phi(z|x)$ for deviating from the prior $p(z)$. Minimizing this term alone would force $q_\phi(z|x) = p(z)$ for all $x$, which means the latent code would carry zero information about the input -- it would just be noise from the prior.

The tension between these two terms is the fundamental tradeoff of variational inference applied to generative models. The reconstruction term pushes for informative latent codes; the KL penalty pushes for simple, prior-like latent codes. The optimal balance encodes exactly the information needed for good reconstruction and no more. This is directly connected to the rate-distortion tradeoff from information theory, which we will explore in Chapter 03.

Why the KL penalty matters for generation

Without the KL penalty, the encoder could learn to map each training example to a unique, isolated point in latent space. Reconstruction would be perfect, but the latent space would have no structure -- sampling a new $z$ from the prior would land in a region the decoder has never seen, producing garbage. The KL penalty forces the encoder to spread its latent codes across the prior's support, creating a smooth, continuous latent space where nearby points decode to similar outputs. This is what makes the model generative: we can sample $z \sim p(z)$ and decode it into a realistic new data point.

Mean-Field Approximation

The simplest and most widely used variational family is the mean-field family, where the approximate posterior fully factorizes over the latent variables:

$$q(z) = \prod_{i=1}^{d} q_i(z_i)$$

This means we assume the latent dimensions are independent under the approximate posterior, even though they may be highly correlated under the true posterior $p(z|x)$. The mean-field assumption is a strong simplification, but it makes optimization tractable and often gives surprisingly good results. The factorization means we only need to store and optimize $d$ univariate distributions rather than one $d$-dimensional joint distribution, reducing the problem from exponential to linear complexity in the latent dimension.

The mean-field assumption has a well-known limitation: it systematically underestimates posterior correlations and variances. If the true posterior has strong dependencies between $z_i$ and $z_j$, the mean-field approximation cannot capture them -- it will find the best product distribution, which typically has narrower marginals than the true posterior. This is a direct consequence of using the reverse KL divergence $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p)$, which is mode-seeking and prefers to concentrate mass where $p$ has high density rather than spreading mass to cover all of $p$'s support.

Coordinate Ascent Variational Inference (CAVI)

Given the mean-field factorization, we can derive the optimal update for each factor $q_j(z_j)$ while holding all other factors fixed. This is the coordinate ascent variational inference (CAVI) algorithm, and it is the classical workhorse of variational methods in Bayesian statistics.

The optimal form for $q_j(z_j)$, holding all other factors $q_{-j}$ fixed, is:

$$\log q_j^*(z_j) = \mathbb{E}_{q_{-j}}[\log p(x, z)] + \text{const}$$

where the expectation is taken over all latent variables except $z_j$. The constant ensures $q_j^*$ integrates to one. This result is derived by taking the functional derivative of the ELBO with respect to $q_j$ and setting it to zero. For models in the conjugate exponential family, these expectations have closed-form solutions, making each CAVI update an analytic computation. The algorithm iterates over all factors, updating each one in turn, and is guaranteed to monotonically increase the ELBO at each step.

Definition: CAVI Algorithm

Coordinate Ascent Variational Inference (CAVI) is an iterative algorithm for mean-field variational inference. At each iteration, it cycles through the latent variable factors $q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_d$ and updates each one to its conditional optimum given the current values of all others. Each update maximizes the ELBO with respect to one factor while holding the rest fixed. The algorithm converges to a local optimum of the ELBO (which is generally non-convex in the variational parameters) and is guaranteed to never decrease the objective.

CAVI is elegant and theoretically clean, but it has significant practical limitations. Each update requires computing expectations over all other factors, which can be expensive. The algorithm processes the entire dataset at each step, making it ill-suited for large-scale problems. And the convergence can be slow, especially when the factors are strongly coupled. These limitations motivated the development of stochastic variational inference and, ultimately, amortized inference with neural networks.

Connection to the EM Algorithm

The expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm can be viewed as a special case of variational inference. In the E-step, we compute the posterior $q(z) = p(z|x, \theta^{\text{old}})$ exactly (when this is tractable), which is equivalent to setting the variational distribution to the true posterior and driving the KL divergence to zero. In the M-step, we maximize the expected complete-data log-likelihood $\mathbb{E}_{q(z)}[\log p(x, z | \theta)]$ with respect to $\theta$, which is equivalent to maximizing the ELBO with respect to the model parameters.

The connection becomes precise when we write the EM objective. After the E-step sets $q(z) = p(z|x, \theta^{\text{old}})$, the ELBO becomes:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{p(z|x, \theta^{\text{old}})}[\log p(x, z | \theta)] + H(q)$$

where $H(q)$ is the entropy of $q$, which does not depend on $\theta$. So the M-step maximization of $\mathcal{L}$ with respect to $\theta$ is identical to maximizing the expected complete-data log-likelihood, which is exactly what EM does. This perspective reveals that EM is variational inference with the exact posterior as the variational distribution. When the exact E-step is intractable, replacing it with an approximate variational E-step gives variational EM, which is the bridge between classical EM and modern deep generative models.

Amortized Inference

Classical variational inference optimizes a separate set of variational parameters $\phi_n$ for each data point $x_n$. This means that at test time, encountering a new observation requires running a full optimization from scratch. Amortized inference replaces this per-example optimization with a single neural network $f_\phi$ that maps any input $x$ directly to the variational parameters of $q_\phi(z|x)$.

$$q_\phi(z|x) = \mathcal{N}(z;\, \mu_\phi(x),\, \text{diag}(\sigma^2_\phi(x)))$$

where $\mu_\phi(x)$ and $\sigma^2_\phi(x)$ are outputs of a neural network (the encoder or inference network). The term "amortized" comes from the idea that the computational cost of inference is amortized across all data points: instead of paying a separate optimization cost for each $x_n$, we pay once to train the shared network $f_\phi$, and then inference on any new point is a single forward pass. This is what makes VAEs practical for large datasets -- the encoder performs approximate inference in milliseconds rather than minutes.

The tradeoff is the amortization gap: the shared network $f_\phi$ may not be as accurate as per-example optimization for any individual data point. The amortization gap is the difference between the ELBO achieved by the amortized encoder and the ELBO that could be achieved by optimizing the variational parameters for each data point individually. In practice, the gap is usually small for well-trained networks, and the massive speedup in inference time more than compensates for the slight loss in approximation quality.

Stochastic Variational Inference

Stochastic variational inference (SVI) scales variational methods to massive datasets by using stochastic gradient ascent on the ELBO. Instead of computing the ELBO gradient using the entire dataset, SVI subsamples a minibatch of data points and computes a noisy but unbiased estimate of the gradient. This reduces the per-iteration cost from $O(N)$ to $O(B)$ where $B$ is the minibatch size, enabling variational inference on datasets with millions or billions of observations.

The key insight of SVI is that the ELBO for the full dataset decomposes as a sum over data points (assuming i.i.d. observations), so the gradient also decomposes as a sum. Subsampling and rescaling gives an unbiased gradient estimator with standard SGD convergence guarantees. Combined with amortization, SVI is what makes modern variational inference practical: amortization eliminates per-example optimization, and stochastic gradients eliminate the need to process the full dataset at each step. Together, they turn variational inference into a procedure that looks and scales like standard deep learning training.

KL Divergence: Properties and Intuitions

The KL divergence is the distance measure that drives variational inference, and understanding its properties is essential for understanding why VI behaves the way it does. The KL divergence from $q$ to $p$ is defined as:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) = \mathbb{E}_{q}\left[\log \frac{q(z)}{p(z)}\right] = \int q(z) \log \frac{q(z)}{p(z)}\, dz$$

Non-negativity. The KL divergence satisfies $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) \geq 0$ with equality if and only if $q = p$ almost everywhere. This follows directly from Jensen's inequality applied to $-\log$, which is convex: $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) = -\mathbb{E}_q[\log(p/q)] \geq -\log \mathbb{E}_q[p/q] = -\log 1 = 0$.

Asymmetry. The KL divergence is not symmetric: $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) \neq D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q)$ in general. This asymmetry has profound consequences for variational inference. The "reverse KL" $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p)$ used in standard VI is mode-seeking: it forces $q$ to be zero wherever $p$ is zero, which means $q$ tends to concentrate on a single mode of $p$ and underestimate the posterior's spread. The "forward KL" $D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q)$ is mode-covering: it forces $q$ to have support wherever $p$ has support, which means $q$ tends to spread out and cover all modes but may place mass in low-density regions between modes.

Consider a bimodal posterior $p(z|x)$ with modes at $z = -3$ and $z = +3$. Minimizing the reverse KL $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p)$ with a unimodal Gaussian $q$ will place $q$ on one of the two modes, ignoring the other entirely. Minimizing the forward KL $D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q)$ will place $q$ centered at $z = 0$ with large variance, covering both modes but also placing substantial mass in the low-density valley between them. Neither approximation is ideal, but they fail in characteristically different ways.

Information-Theoretic Interpretation of the ELBO

The ELBO has a beautiful interpretation in terms of information theory. The reconstruction term $\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)]$ measures the expected number of bits needed to encode $x$ given $z$ under the decoder model. The KL term $D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z))$ measures the number of extra bits needed to encode $z$ under the approximate posterior $q_\phi$ compared to the prior $p(z)$ -- this is the information that the encoder transmits about $x$ through the bottleneck $z$.

From this perspective, maximizing the ELBO means finding the best tradeoff between compression (minimizing the bits used to encode $z$) and reconstruction quality (minimizing the bits needed to recover $x$ from $z$). This is precisely the rate-distortion problem: the KL term is the rate (bits used by the code), and the negative reconstruction term is the distortion (reconstruction error). The ELBO traces out the rate-distortion curve as we vary the relative weight of the two terms.

The diagram above illustrates the key difference between mode-seeking (reverse KL) and mode-covering (forward KL) behavior. When the true posterior is multimodal, the reverse KL used in standard variational inference will concentrate the approximation $q$ on a single mode, producing a tight but incomplete approximation. The forward KL would produce a broad approximation that covers both modes but is diffuse. This asymmetry is not a bug but a feature: in many applications, having a confident estimate of one mode is more useful than a vague estimate that averages over multiple modes.

The relationship between variational inference and other approximate inference methods is worth noting. Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods are asymptotically exact -- given infinite computation, they converge to the true posterior -- but they can be very slow and it is hard to diagnose convergence. Variational inference converges to a specific (possibly incorrect) answer quickly, and we can monitor the ELBO to track progress. The choice between VI and MCMC depends on whether you need accuracy (MCMC) or speed (VI). In practice, VI dominates in deep learning because the models are so large that MCMC is computationally infeasible, and the approximate posteriors from VI are good enough for downstream tasks.

Variational inference can also be understood through the lens of optimization on statistical manifolds. The space of all distributions $q_\phi$ forms a Riemannian manifold with the Fisher information metric, and minimizing the KL divergence corresponds to a projection of $p(z|x)$ onto the variational family $\mathcal{Q}$ with respect to this metric. This information-geometric perspective explains why natural gradient methods -- which account for the manifold structure -- converge faster than ordinary gradient methods for variational inference. The natural gradient update takes a step of fixed size in distribution space rather than parameter space, avoiding the problem that small parameter changes can correspond to large distribution changes (or vice versa) depending on the local geometry.

To summarize the key ideas before moving to the code examples: variational inference replaces intractable posterior computation with optimization. The ELBO is both the objective we maximize and a lower bound on the log-evidence. The gap between the ELBO and the true log-evidence is the KL divergence between the approximate and true posteriors. Mean-field approximations factorize the posterior, CAVI optimizes one factor at a time, amortized inference uses neural networks to share computation across data points, and stochastic methods scale everything to large datasets. These ideas form the mathematical foundation of variational autoencoders, which we build in the next chapter.

Computing KL Divergence Between Two Gaussians

The KL divergence between two multivariate Gaussians has a closed-form expression that is used constantly in variational inference. Given $q = \mathcal{N}(\mu_1, \Sigma_1)$ and $p = \mathcal{N}(\mu_2, \Sigma_2)$, the KL divergence is:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) = \frac{1}{2}\left[\log\frac{|\Sigma_2|}{|\Sigma_1|} - d + \text{tr}(\Sigma_2^{-1}\Sigma_1) + (\mu_2 - \mu_1)^T \Sigma_2^{-1} (\mu_2 - \mu_1)\right]$$

When $p$ is the standard normal $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$ and $q$ has diagonal covariance $\text{diag}(\sigma^2)$, this simplifies dramatically and is the formula used in every VAE implementation.

Python 

import torch
import torch.distributions as dist

def kl_divergence_gaussians(mu1, logvar1, mu2, logvar2):
    """KL(q || p) between two diagonal Gaussians.

    Args:
        mu1, logvar1: mean and log-variance of q
        mu2, logvar2: mean and log-variance of p
    Returns:
        KL divergence, shape (batch_size,)
    """
    var1 = logvar1.exp()
    var2 = logvar2.exp()
    kl = 0.5 * (
        logvar2 - logvar1           # log(sigma2/sigma1) per dim
        - 1.0                        # -1 per dim
        + var1 / var2                # tr(Sigma2^{-1} Sigma1) diagonal
        + (mu2 - mu1).pow(2) / var2  # Mahalanobis term
    )
    return kl.sum(dim=-1)  # sum over latent dimensions


def kl_to_standard_normal(mu, logvar):
    """KL(q || N(0,I)) -- the VAE regularization term.

    This is the special case used in every VAE.
    """
    return -0.5 * (1 + logvar - mu.pow(2) - logvar.exp()).sum(dim=-1)


# Example usage
batch_size, latent_dim = 32, 16
mu_q = torch.randn(batch_size, latent_dim)
logvar_q = torch.randn(batch_size, latent_dim) * 0.5

# KL to standard normal
kl_values = kl_to_standard_normal(mu_q, logvar_q)
print(f"KL to N(0,I): mean={kl_values.mean():.3f}, shape={kl_values.shape}")

# KL between two arbitrary Gaussians
mu_p = torch.zeros(batch_size, latent_dim)
logvar_p = torch.zeros(batch_size, latent_dim)
kl_general = kl_divergence_gaussians(mu_q, logvar_q, mu_p, logvar_p)
print(f"General KL: mean={kl_general.mean():.3f}")

# Verify they match when p = N(0,I)
print(f"Match: {torch.allclose(kl_values, kl_general, atol=1e-5)}")
Why this implementation matters

Working with log-variances instead of variances is a standard numerical trick: it ensures positivity without constrained optimization and improves gradient flow. The special case kl_to_standard_normal is used in virtually every VAE implementation and is worth memorizing.

CAVI for a Gaussian Mixture Model

We implement coordinate ascent variational inference for a univariate Gaussian mixture model. The model has $K$ components with unknown means $\mu_k$ and known variance $\sigma^2$. Each data point $x_n$ has a latent cluster assignment $c_n \in \{1, \ldots, K\}$. The CAVI updates alternate between updating the cluster responsibilities (like the E-step in EM) and updating the variational distributions over the means.

Python 

import numpy as np
from scipy.special import digamma, logsumexp

def cavi_gmm(X, K, n_iters=100, sigma2=1.0, prior_mean=0.0, prior_var=10.0):
    """CAVI for a Gaussian mixture model.

    Model: x_n | c_n, mu ~ N(mu_{c_n}, sigma2)
           c_n ~ Categorical(1/K, ..., 1/K)
           mu_k ~ N(prior_mean, prior_var)

    Variational family:
        q(c, mu) = prod_n q(c_n) * prod_k q(mu_k)
        q(c_n) = Categorical(r_{n1}, ..., r_{nK})
        q(mu_k) = N(m_k, s_k^2)
    """
    N = len(X)

    # Initialize variational parameters
    r = np.random.dirichlet(np.ones(K), size=N)  # responsibilities (N, K)
    m = np.random.randn(K) * 0.1 + X.mean()      # variational means
    s2 = np.ones(K) * 1.0                         # variational variances

    elbos = []

    for iteration in range(n_iters):
        # === Update q(c_n) for each data point ===
        # Optimal: log r_{nk} propto E_q[log p(x_n | c_n=k, mu_k)]
        # = -1/(2*sigma2) * E_q[(x_n - mu_k)^2]
        # = -1/(2*sigma2) * (x_n^2 - 2*x_n*m_k + m_k^2 + s2_k)
        log_r = np.zeros((N, K))
        for k in range(K):
            log_r[:, k] = (-0.5 / sigma2) * (
                X**2 - 2 * X * m[k] + m[k]**2 + s2[k]
            )
        # Normalize responsibilities
        log_r -= logsumexp(log_r, axis=1, keepdims=True)
        r = np.exp(log_r)

        # === Update q(mu_k) for each component ===
        # Optimal: q(mu_k) = N(m_k, s_k^2) with:
        for k in range(K):
            N_k = r[:, k].sum()
            precision = 1.0 / prior_var + N_k / sigma2
            s2[k] = 1.0 / precision
            m[k] = s2[k] * (
                prior_mean / prior_var + (r[:, k] * X).sum() / sigma2
            )

        # === Compute ELBO ===
        elbo = 0.0
        # E[log p(x | c, mu)]
        for k in range(K):
            elbo += -0.5 / sigma2 * (
                r[:, k] * (X**2 - 2*X*m[k] + m[k]**2 + s2[k])
            ).sum()
        # E[log p(c)] - E[log q(c)]
        elbo += -np.sum(r * np.log(r + 1e-10)) + N * np.log(1.0/K)
        # E[log p(mu)] - E[log q(mu)]
        for k in range(K):
            elbo += -0.5/prior_var * (m[k]**2 + s2[k] - prior_var)
            elbo += 0.5 * np.log(2 * np.pi * np.e * s2[k])

        elbos.append(elbo)

    return r, m, s2, elbos


# Generate synthetic data from a 3-component mixture
np.random.seed(42)
true_means = [-3.0, 0.0, 4.0]
X = np.concatenate([
    np.random.randn(100) + mu for mu in true_means
])
np.random.shuffle(X)

r, m, s2, elbos = cavi_gmm(X, K=3, n_iters=50)
print(f"Inferred means: {sorted(m)}")
print(f"True means:     {sorted(true_means)}")
print(f"Final ELBO:     {elbos[-1]:.2f}")
Why this implementation matters

CAVI for a GMM is the canonical example of classical variational inference. Each update has a closed form because the model is in the conjugate exponential family. Comparing the inferred means to the true means shows that VI recovers the ground truth, while the ELBO trace confirms monotonic convergence.

Amortized Inference Network

An amortized inference network (encoder) maps inputs directly to variational parameters, replacing per-example optimization with a single forward pass. This is the architecture at the heart of the VAE encoder.

Python 

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class AmortizedEncoder(nn.Module):
    """Amortized inference network: x -> (mu, log_var).

    Maps input data to the parameters of the approximate
    posterior q_phi(z|x) = N(mu(x), diag(sigma^2(x))).
    """
    def __init__(self, input_dim, hidden_dim, latent_dim):
        super().__init__()
        self.shared = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(input_dim, hidden_dim),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, hidden_dim),
            nn.ReLU(),
        )
        self.mu_head = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, latent_dim)
        self.logvar_head = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, latent_dim)

    def forward(self, x):
        h = self.shared(x)
        mu = self.mu_head(h)
        logvar = self.logvar_head(h)
        return mu, logvar

    def sample(self, x, n_samples=1):
        """Sample from q(z|x) using the reparameterization trick."""
        mu, logvar = self.forward(x)
        std = (0.5 * logvar).exp()
        # Reparameterization: z = mu + std * epsilon
        eps = torch.randn(n_samples, *mu.shape, device=mu.device)
        z = mu.unsqueeze(0) + std.unsqueeze(0) * eps
        return z, mu, logvar


class PerExampleVI(nn.Module):
    """Non-amortized baseline: separate (mu, logvar) per data point."""
    def __init__(self, n_data, latent_dim):
        super().__init__()
        self.mu = nn.Parameter(torch.randn(n_data, latent_dim) * 0.01)
        self.logvar = nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(n_data, latent_dim))

    def forward(self, idx):
        return self.mu[idx], self.logvar[idx]


# Compare parameter counts
input_dim, hidden_dim, latent_dim, n_data = 784, 256, 32, 60000

amortized = AmortizedEncoder(input_dim, hidden_dim, latent_dim)
per_example = PerExampleVI(n_data, latent_dim)

n_amortized = sum(p.numel() for p in amortized.parameters())
n_per_example = sum(p.numel() for p in per_example.parameters())

print(f"Amortized encoder:  {n_amortized:,} parameters")
print(f"Per-example VI:     {n_per_example:,} parameters")
print(f"Ratio:              {n_per_example / n_amortized:.1f}x more for per-example")
Why this implementation matters

The amortized encoder uses a fixed number of parameters regardless of dataset size, while per-example VI scales linearly with $N$. For MNIST ($N = 60{,}000$), per-example VI needs roughly 50x more parameters. More importantly, the amortized encoder generalizes to unseen inputs -- it can perform inference on new data without retraining.

Visualizing ELBO Optimization

We visualize how the ELBO evolves during training for a simple 2D latent variable model. We also plot the reconstruction and KL terms separately to show their tension.

Python 

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.optim as optim
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Simple generative model: z ~ N(0, I), x | z ~ N(Wz + b, sigma2*I)
torch.manual_seed(42)
true_W = torch.randn(10, 2)
true_b = torch.randn(10)
sigma2 = 0.1

# Generate data
N = 1000
z_true = torch.randn(N, 2)
x_data = z_true @ true_W.T + true_b + torch.randn(N, 10) * sigma2**0.5

# Amortized encoder
encoder = nn.Sequential(
    nn.Linear(10, 64), nn.ReLU(), nn.Linear(64, 64), nn.ReLU()
)
mu_head = nn.Linear(64, 2)
logvar_head = nn.Linear(64, 2)
decoder_W = nn.Parameter(torch.randn(10, 2) * 0.1)
decoder_b = nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(10))

params = (
    list(encoder.parameters())
    + list(mu_head.parameters())
    + list(logvar_head.parameters())
    + [decoder_W, decoder_b]
)
optimizer = optim.Adam(params, lr=1e-3)

# Training loop tracking ELBO components
elbo_history, recon_history, kl_history = [], [], []

for epoch in range(300):
    h = encoder(x_data)
    mu = mu_head(h)
    logvar = logvar_head(h)

    # Reparameterization trick
    std = (0.5 * logvar).exp()
    z = mu + std * torch.randn_like(std)

    # Reconstruction: log p(x|z)
    x_recon = z @ decoder_W.T + decoder_b
    recon_loss = -0.5 / sigma2 * ((x_data - x_recon)**2).sum(dim=1).mean()

    # KL: D_KL(q(z|x) || N(0,I))
    kl_loss = -0.5 * (1 + logvar - mu.pow(2) - logvar.exp()).sum(dim=1).mean()

    elbo = recon_loss - kl_loss
    loss = -elbo  # minimize negative ELBO

    optimizer.zero_grad()
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()

    elbo_history.append(elbo.item())
    recon_history.append(recon_loss.item())
    kl_history.append(kl_loss.item())

# Plot
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 4))
axes[0].plot(elbo_history, color='#2563eb')
axes[0].set_title('ELBO')
axes[0].set_xlabel('Epoch')
axes[1].plot(recon_history, color='#16a34a')
axes[1].set_title('Reconstruction Term')
axes[1].set_xlabel('Epoch')
axes[2].plot(kl_history, color='#dc2626')
axes[2].set_title('KL Divergence Term')
axes[2].set_xlabel('Epoch')
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('elbo_optimization.png', dpi=150)
plt.show()
print(f"Final ELBO: {elbo_history[-1]:.2f}")
print(f"Final Recon: {recon_history[-1]:.2f}, Final KL: {kl_history[-1]:.2f}")
Why this implementation matters

Plotting the ELBO components separately reveals the reconstruction-regularization tension in action. Early in training, the KL term rises as the encoder starts encoding information; later, it stabilizes as the model finds the optimal rate. The reconstruction term improves throughout, but its rate of improvement slows as the KL penalty pushes back.

🟢 Exercise 1: KL between two univariate Gaussians ▼

Compute $D_{\text{KL}}(\mathcal{N}(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2) \| \mathcal{N}(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2))$ in closed form. Verify that it equals zero when $\mu_1 = \mu_2$ and $\sigma_1 = \sigma_2$.

Starting from the definition $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) = \mathbb{E}_q[\log q - \log p]$ and substituting the Gaussian densities:

$$D_{\text{KL}} = \mathbb{E}_q\left[-\frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma_1^2) - \frac{(z-\mu_1)^2}{2\sigma_1^2} + \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma_2^2) + \frac{(z-\mu_2)^2}{2\sigma_2^2}\right]$$

Using $\mathbb{E}_q[(z-\mu_1)^2] = \sigma_1^2$ and $\mathbb{E}_q[(z-\mu_2)^2] = \sigma_1^2 + (\mu_1 - \mu_2)^2$:

$$D_{\text{KL}} = \log\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1} + \frac{\sigma_1^2 + (\mu_1 - \mu_2)^2}{2\sigma_2^2} - \frac{1}{2}$$

When $\mu_1 = \mu_2$ and $\sigma_1 = \sigma_2$: $D_{\text{KL}} = \log 1 + \frac{\sigma_1^2}{2\sigma_1^2} - \frac{1}{2} = 0 + \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{2} = 0$. Verified.

🟢 Exercise 2: Non-negativity of KL from Jensen's inequality ▼

Show that $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) \geq 0$ using Jensen's inequality applied to the concave function $\log$.

Write $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) = -\mathbb{E}_q\left[\log \frac{p(z)}{q(z)}\right]$. Since $\log$ is concave, Jensen's inequality gives:

$$\mathbb{E}_q\left[\log \frac{p(z)}{q(z)}\right] \leq \log \mathbb{E}_q\left[\frac{p(z)}{q(z)}\right] = \log \int q(z)\frac{p(z)}{q(z)}dz = \log \int p(z)\,dz = \log 1 = 0$$

Therefore $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) = -\mathbb{E}_q[\log(p/q)] \geq 0$. Equality holds iff $p(z)/q(z)$ is constant $q$-almost everywhere, i.e., $q = p$.

🟢 Exercise 3: Write the ELBO for a specific model ▼

🟢 Exercise 4: ELBO when q equals p exactly ▼

What happens to the ELBO when the approximate posterior $q_\phi(z|x)$ equals the true posterior $p(z|x)$ exactly? What does the ELBO equal in this case?

From the fundamental identity $\log p(x) = \mathcal{L}(\phi) + D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z|x))$, when $q_\phi = p(z|x)$, the KL divergence is zero, so:

$$\mathcal{L}(\phi) = \log p(x)$$

The ELBO equals the log-evidence exactly. The bound is tight. This is the best possible ELBO achievable by any variational distribution, and it confirms that the ELBO is indeed a lower bound: it can reach $\log p(x)$ but never exceed it.

🟢 Exercise 5: Mean-field update for a Gaussian ▼

Consider $p(z_1, z_2 | x) \propto \exp(-\frac{1}{2}(z_1^2 + z_2^2 + 2\rho z_1 z_2 - 2xz_1 - 2xz_2))$. Derive the mean-field update for $q_1(z_1)$ given a current estimate $q_2(z_2) = \mathcal{N}(m_2, s_2^2)$.

The optimal $q_1^*(z_1)$ satisfies $\log q_1^*(z_1) = \mathbb{E}_{q_2}[\log p(z_1, z_2 | x)] + \text{const}$.

Keeping only terms involving $z_1$: $\log q_1^*(z_1) \propto -\frac{1}{2}z_1^2 - \rho z_1 \mathbb{E}_{q_2}[z_2] + x z_1 = -\frac{1}{2}z_1^2 + (x - \rho m_2)z_1$.

🟢 Exercise 6: Why is KL asymmetric? ▼

Give a concrete example showing that $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) \neq D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q)$. Use $q = \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ and $p = \mathcal{N}(0, 4)$.

Using the univariate Gaussian KL formula: $D_{\text{KL}}(\mathcal{N}(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2) \| \mathcal{N}(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2)) = \log\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1} + \frac{\sigma_1^2}{2\sigma_2^2} - \frac{1}{2}$.

$D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) = \log\frac{2}{1} + \frac{1}{8} - \frac{1}{2} = \log 2 - 0.375 \approx 0.318$

$D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q) = \log\frac{1}{2} + \frac{4}{2} - \frac{1}{2} = -\log 2 + 1.5 \approx 0.807$

These are clearly different: $0.318 \neq 0.807$. Intuitively, $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p)$ is small because the narrow $q$ sits comfortably inside the wide $p$. But $D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q)$ is large because the wide $p$ places mass in the tails where the narrow $q$ assigns very low probability.

🟢 Exercise 7: Identify reconstruction and regularization terms ▼

For a VAE with Bernoulli decoder $p_\theta(x|z) = \prod_i \text{Bern}(x_i; f_\theta(z)_i)$ and standard normal prior, write the ELBO and identify which part is reconstruction and which is regularization.

The ELBO is:

$$\mathcal{L} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}\left[\sum_i x_i \log f_\theta(z)_i + (1-x_i)\log(1-f_\theta(z)_i)\right]}_{\text{Reconstruction: binary cross-entropy}} - \underbrace{D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| \mathcal{N}(0,I))}_{\text{Regularization: KL to prior}}$$

The reconstruction term is the expected binary cross-entropy between the input $x$ and the decoder's output $f_\theta(z)$, averaged over $z \sim q_\phi(z|x)$. The regularization term is the closed-form KL between the Gaussian encoder and the standard normal prior: $-\frac{1}{2}\sum_j(1 + \log\sigma_j^2 - \mu_j^2 - \sigma_j^2)$.

🟢 Exercise 8: Log-evidence for a simple model ▼

For the model $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$, $x|z \sim \mathcal{N}(z, \sigma^2)$, compute $p(x) = \int p(x|z)p(z)\,dz$ analytically. What distribution does $x$ follow?

Since both $p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and $p(x|z) = \mathcal{N}(z, \sigma^2)$ are Gaussian, the marginal is also Gaussian. We can compute it by noting that $x = z + \epsilon$ where $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ are independent. Therefore:

$$p(x) = \mathcal{N}(x; 0, 1 + \sigma^2)$$

The mean is $\mathbb{E}[x] = \mathbb{E}[z] + \mathbb{E}[\epsilon] = 0$ and the variance is $\text{Var}(x) = \text{Var}(z) + \text{Var}(\epsilon) = 1 + \sigma^2$. This is one of the rare cases where the evidence is tractable, making it a perfect test case for verifying that variational inference recovers the correct answer.

🟢 Exercise 9: What does "amortized" mean? ▼

Explain the term "amortized" in amortized inference. What cost is being amortized, and across what?

In classical variational inference, computing $q_\phi(z|x_n)$ for each data point $x_n$ requires a separate optimization over variational parameters $\phi_n$. The cost is $O(N \cdot T)$ where $N$ is the number of data points and $T$ is the number of optimization steps per point.

In amortized inference, a single neural network $f_\phi$ is trained to map any input $x$ to the variational parameters. The training cost is paid once, and then inference on any (including unseen) data point is a single forward pass $O(1)$. The optimization cost is "amortized" across all data points: instead of paying $T$ steps per point, we pay a shared training cost that covers all points simultaneously. The analogy is financial amortization, where a large upfront cost is spread over many uses.

🟢 Exercise 10: Variational inference vs MCMC ▼

List at least four key tradeoffs between variational inference and Markov chain Monte Carlo for approximate posterior inference.

1. Speed vs accuracy: VI is much faster (optimization converges quickly), while MCMC is asymptotically exact but can be very slow to converge.

2. Scalability: VI scales to large datasets via stochastic gradients and amortization. MCMC scales poorly because each sample requires a full pass through the data (though stochastic MCMC variants exist).

3. Approximation quality: VI produces a biased approximation (restricted to the variational family $\mathcal{Q}$). MCMC samples from the true posterior given enough time.

4. Diagnostics: The ELBO provides a clear convergence metric for VI. MCMC convergence diagnostics (R-hat, ESS, trace plots) are harder to interpret and never definitively confirm convergence.

5. Multimodality: VI with reverse KL tends to collapse to one mode. MCMC can in principle explore all modes, though it may get stuck in practice.

6. Differentiability: VI naturally integrates with gradient-based deep learning. MCMC requires special techniques (e.g., HMC) to exploit gradient information.

🟡 Exercise 11: KL between two multivariate Gaussians ▼

Derive the closed-form KL divergence $D_{\text{KL}}(\mathcal{N}(\mu_1, \Sigma_1) \| \mathcal{N}(\mu_2, \Sigma_2))$ for $d$-dimensional Gaussians with full covariance matrices.

Start from $D_{\text{KL}} = \mathbb{E}_q[\log q - \log p]$ and substitute the multivariate Gaussian densities:

$$\log q(z) = -\frac{d}{2}\log(2\pi) - \frac{1}{2}\log|\Sigma_1| - \frac{1}{2}(z-\mu_1)^T\Sigma_1^{-1}(z-\mu_1)$$

Taking the difference and the expectation under $q$, using $\mathbb{E}_q[(z-\mu_1)(z-\mu_1)^T] = \Sigma_1$ and $\mathbb{E}_q[(z-\mu_2)(z-\mu_2)^T] = \Sigma_1 + (\mu_1-\mu_2)(\mu_1-\mu_2)^T$:

$$D_{\text{KL}} = \frac{1}{2}\left[\log\frac{|\Sigma_2|}{|\Sigma_1|} - d + \text{tr}(\Sigma_2^{-1}\Sigma_1) + (\mu_2-\mu_1)^T\Sigma_2^{-1}(\mu_2-\mu_1)\right]$$

Each term has a clear meaning: the log-determinant ratio measures the volume difference, the trace term measures the spread mismatch, and the quadratic form measures the mean displacement. When $\Sigma_1 = \Sigma_2$ and $\mu_1 = \mu_2$, all terms cancel giving zero.

🟡 Exercise 12: CAVI update for Gaussian mixture ▼

Derive the CAVI update equations for $q(c_n)$ and $q(\mu_k)$ in a Gaussian mixture model with known variance $\sigma^2$, uniform mixing weights, and Gaussian prior $\mu_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_0^2)$.

Update for $q(c_n)$: $\log q^*(c_n = k) = \mathbb{E}_{q(\mu)}[\log p(x_n | c_n = k, \mu_k)] + \text{const}$

$$= -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\mathbb{E}_q[(x_n - \mu_k)^2] + C = -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}(x_n^2 - 2x_n m_k + m_k^2 + s_k^2) + C$$

where $m_k = \mathbb{E}_q[\mu_k]$ and $s_k^2 = \text{Var}_q(\mu_k)$. Normalizing: $r_{nk} \propto \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}(x_n^2 - 2x_n m_k + m_k^2 + s_k^2)\right)$.

where $N_k = \sum_n r_{nk}$ is the effective number of points assigned to component $k$. This is a precision-weighted combination of the prior and the data, exactly like the Bayesian update for a Gaussian mean with known variance.

🟡 Exercise 13: EM as a special case of VI ▼

Show that the EM algorithm is a special case of variational inference where the E-step uses the exact posterior. Write both the E-step and M-step as ELBO operations.

The ELBO as a function of both $q$ and $\theta$ is: $\mathcal{L}(q, \theta) = \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(x, z | \theta)] - \mathbb{E}_q[\log q(z)]$.

E-step as VI: Maximize $\mathcal{L}(q, \theta^{\text{old}})$ over $q$ with $\theta$ fixed. The maximum is achieved when $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p(z|x, \theta^{\text{old}})) = 0$, i.e., $q^*(z) = p(z | x, \theta^{\text{old}})$. This sets the KL gap to zero, making the ELBO equal to $\log p(x | \theta^{\text{old}})$.

M-step as VI: Maximize $\mathcal{L}(q^*, \theta)$ over $\theta$ with $q$ fixed at $q^*$. Since $\mathbb{E}_{q^*}[\log q^*(z)]$ does not depend on $\theta$, this reduces to maximizing $\mathbb{E}_{p(z|x,\theta^{\text{old}})}[\log p(x, z | \theta)]$, which is exactly the EM M-step.

When the exact posterior is intractable, replacing the E-step with an approximate variational optimization gives variational EM. If we further restrict $q$ to a factorized family, we get mean-field variational EM.

🟡 Exercise 14: ELBO gradient with respect to variational parameters ▼

For $q_\phi(z|x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2 I)$ with $\phi = (\mu, \log\sigma)$, derive $\nabla_\mu \mathcal{L}$ and $\nabla_{\log\sigma} \mathcal{L}$ using the reparameterization trick $z = \mu + \sigma \epsilon$, $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$.

Using reparameterization, the ELBO becomes: $$\mathcal{L}(\phi) = \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)}[\log p(x, \mu + \sigma\epsilon) - \log q_\phi(\mu + \sigma\epsilon | x)]$$

The gradient with respect to $\mu$: $$\nabla_\mu \mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}_\epsilon\left[\nabla_z \log p(x, z)\big|_{z=\mu+\sigma\epsilon}\right] - \nabla_\mu D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p(z))$$

For the KL term with standard normal prior: $\nabla_\mu D_{\text{KL}} = \mu$ (pushing toward zero).

The gradient with respect to $\log\sigma$: $$\nabla_{\log\sigma} \mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}_\epsilon\left[\nabla_z \log p(x, z)\big|_{z=\mu+\sigma\epsilon} \cdot \sigma\epsilon\right] - \nabla_{\log\sigma} D_{\text{KL}}$$

For the KL term: $\nabla_{\log\sigma} D_{\text{KL}} = \sigma^2 - 1$ (per dimension), pushing $\sigma$ toward 1. The reparameterization trick makes both gradients low-variance because $\epsilon$ is independent of $\phi$.

🟡 Exercise 15: Mean-field for the Ising model ▼

Consider a 1D Ising model with $p(s_1, \ldots, s_N) \propto \exp\left(J\sum_{i} s_i s_{i+1} + h\sum_i s_i\right)$ where $s_i \in \{-1, +1\}$. Using mean-field $q(s) = \prod_i q_i(s_i)$ with $q_i(s_i = +1) = m_i$, derive the self-consistency equation for $m_i$.

🟡 Exercise 16: Forward KL vs reverse KL behavior ▼

Let $p(z) = 0.5\,\mathcal{N}(-2, 0.5^2) + 0.5\,\mathcal{N}(2, 0.5^2)$ be a mixture of two Gaussians. Using a unimodal Gaussian $q(z) = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, describe qualitatively what the optimal $q$ looks like under (a) $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p)$ and (b) $D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q)$.

(a) Reverse KL $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p)$ (mode-seeking): This objective penalizes $q$ for placing mass where $p$ is small. Since $p$ has near-zero density between the two modes, $q$ cannot bridge the gap without incurring enormous KL cost. The optimal $q$ concentrates on one of the two modes: either $q \approx \mathcal{N}(-2, 0.5^2)$ or $q \approx \mathcal{N}(2, 0.5^2)$. Both are local optima; which one is found depends on initialization.

(b) Forward KL $D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q)$ (mode-covering): This objective penalizes $q$ for having low density where $p$ has mass. Since $p$ has mass at both $z = -2$ and $z = 2$, $q$ must cover both regions. The optimal $q$ is centered at $\mu = 0$ (the mean of $p$) with $\sigma^2 \approx 4 + 0.25 = 4.25$ (the total variance of $p$). This $q$ covers both modes but also places substantial mass in the valley at $z = 0$ where $p$ has almost no density.

🟡 Exercise 17: Prove ELBO is a lower bound on log-evidence ▼

Give two independent proofs that $\mathcal{L}(\phi) \leq \log p(x)$: one using Jensen's inequality and one using the non-negativity of KL divergence.

Proof 1 (Jensen): $\log p(x) = \log \int \frac{p(x,z)}{q(z|x)} q(z|x)\,dz = \log \mathbb{E}_q\left[\frac{p(x,z)}{q(z|x)}\right] \geq \mathbb{E}_q\left[\log \frac{p(x,z)}{q(z|x)}\right] = \mathcal{L}(\phi)$, where the inequality is Jensen's applied to the concave $\log$.

Proof 2 (KL non-negativity): From the identity $\log p(x) = \mathcal{L}(\phi) + D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z|x))$ and the fact that $D_{\text{KL}} \geq 0$, we immediately get $\log p(x) \geq \mathcal{L}(\phi)$.

🟡 Exercise 18: Optimal q in the exponential family ▼

Suppose the complete-data log-likelihood is in the exponential family: $\log p(x, z | \theta) = \eta(\theta)^T t(x, z) - A(\theta) + \log h(x, z)$. Show that the optimal mean-field factor $q_j^*(z_j)$ is also in an exponential family, and identify its natural parameters.

The CAVI update gives $\log q_j^*(z_j) = \mathbb{E}_{q_{-j}}[\log p(x, z | \theta)] + C$.

Substituting the exponential family form and keeping terms that depend on $z_j$:

$$\log q_j^*(z_j) = \eta(\theta)^T \mathbb{E}_{q_{-j}}[t(x, z)]_{z_j} + \mathbb{E}_{q_{-j}}[\log h(x,z)]_{z_j} + C'$$

🟡 Exercise 19: REINFORCE gradient estimator for ELBO ▼

Derive the score function (REINFORCE) estimator for $\nabla_\phi \mathcal{L}$ when the reparameterization trick is not available (e.g., for discrete latent variables). Show that it is unbiased but discuss its variance.

Starting from $\mathcal{L}(\phi) = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[f(z)]$ where $f(z) = \log p(x,z) - \log q_\phi(z|x)$:

$$\nabla_\phi \mathcal{L} = \nabla_\phi \int q_\phi(z|x) f(z)\, dz = \int f(z) \nabla_\phi q_\phi(z|x)\, dz$$

Using the log-derivative trick $\nabla_\phi q_\phi = q_\phi \nabla_\phi \log q_\phi$:

$$\nabla_\phi \mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}\left[f(z) \nabla_\phi \log q_\phi(z|x)\right]$$

This can be estimated by sampling $z^{(s)} \sim q_\phi$ and computing $\frac{1}{S}\sum_s f(z^{(s)}) \nabla_\phi \log q_\phi(z^{(s)}|x)$. This estimator is unbiased but has notoriously high variance because $f(z)$ multiplies the score function, and both can be large. Variance reduction techniques (control variates, baselines) are essential. The reparameterization trick, when available, gives much lower variance by moving the gradient inside the expectation.

🟡 Exercise 20: Natural gradient for variational parameters ▼

The natural gradient update is $\phi \leftarrow \phi + \rho\, F^{-1} \nabla_\phi \mathcal{L}$ where $F$ is the Fisher information matrix of $q_\phi$. For $q_\phi = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with $\phi = (\mu, \sigma^2)$, compute $F$ and the natural gradient of the KL term in the ELBO.

The Fisher information matrix for $q_\phi = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ is:

$$F = \begin{pmatrix} 1/\sigma^2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1/(2\sigma^4) \end{pmatrix}$$

The Euclidean gradient of $-D_{\text{KL}}(q \| \mathcal{N}(0,1))$ is $\nabla_\mu(-D_{\text{KL}}) = -\mu$ and $\nabla_{\sigma^2}(-D_{\text{KL}}) = \frac{1}{2}(1/\sigma^2 - 1)$.

The natural gradient is $F^{-1}\nabla$:

$$\tilde{\nabla}_\mu = \sigma^2 \cdot (-\mu) = -\sigma^2 \mu$$ $$\tilde{\nabla}_{\sigma^2} = 2\sigma^4 \cdot \frac{1}{2}(1/\sigma^2 - 1) = \sigma^2(1 - \sigma^2)$$

The natural gradient automatically scales the update by the local curvature of the distribution: when $\sigma^2$ is small, parameter changes have large effects on the distribution, so the natural gradient scales them down. This adaptive scaling is why natural gradient methods converge faster than standard gradient descent for variational inference.

🔴 Exercise 21: ELBO gap equals KL(q || p(z|x)) ▼

Prove rigorously that $\log p(x) - \mathcal{L}(\phi) = D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z|x))$, starting from the definitions of each quantity. Do not use any intermediate results -- derive everything from scratch.

Left-hand side: $\log p(x) - \mathcal{L}(\phi) = \log p(x) - \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log p(x,z) - \log q_\phi(z|x)]$

$= \log p(x) - \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log p(x,z)] + \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log q_\phi(z|x)]$

Since $\log p(x)$ does not depend on $z$: $= \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log p(x)] - \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log p(x,z)] + \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log q_\phi(z|x)]$

$= \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log p(x) - \log p(x,z) + \log q_\phi(z|x)]$

Using $p(z|x) = p(x,z)/p(x)$, we get $\log p(x) - \log p(x,z) = -\log p(z|x)$:

$= \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log q_\phi(z|x) - \log p(z|x)]$

Right-hand side: $D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z|x)) = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log q_\phi(z|x) - \log p(z|x)]$

These are identical. The proof requires only the definition of the ELBO, the definition of KL divergence, the chain rule of probability $p(x,z) = p(z|x)p(x)$, and linearity of expectation. No inequalities are needed -- this is an exact identity.

🔴 Exercise 22: Laplace approximation as special case of VI ▼

The Laplace approximation sets $q(z) = \mathcal{N}(z^*, H^{-1})$ where $z^* = \arg\max_z \log p(z|x)$ and $H = -\nabla^2 \log p(z|x)\big|_{z^*}$. Show that this can be derived as a special case of variational inference by choosing the variational family to be all Gaussians and taking a second-order Taylor expansion of the ELBO.

Let $q(z) = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma)$ be our variational family. The ELBO is:

$$\mathcal{L}(\mu, \Sigma) = \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(x, z)] + H(q) = \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(x, z)] + \frac{1}{2}\log|2\pi e \Sigma|$$

Taylor-expand $\log p(x, z)$ around the MAP point $z^*$: $$\log p(x, z) \approx \log p(x, z^*) + \underbrace{\nabla \log p(x, z^*)}_{=0}{}^T(z - z^*) - \frac{1}{2}(z - z^*)^T H (z - z^*)$$

The gradient term vanishes because $z^*$ is the mode. Taking the expectation: $$\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(x, z)] \approx \log p(x, z^*) - \frac{1}{2}\text{tr}(H \Sigma) - \frac{1}{2}(\mu - z^*)^T H (\mu - z^*)$$

Optimizing over $\mu$: the quadratic is minimized at $\mu = z^*$. Optimizing over $\Sigma$: $\nabla_\Sigma \mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{2}H + \frac{1}{2}\Sigma^{-1} = 0$, giving $\Sigma = H^{-1}$. This recovers exactly the Laplace approximation. The Laplace approximation is thus variational inference with a Gaussian family and a second-order approximation to the log-joint.

🔴 Exercise 23: Mean-field underestimates posterior variance ▼

Consider $p(z_1, z_2) = \mathcal{N}\left(\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix}1 & \rho\\\rho & 1\end{pmatrix}\right)$ with $|\rho| < 1$. Using the mean-field family $q(z_1, z_2) = q_1(z_1)q_2(z_2)$, find the optimal $q$ and show that each marginal has variance strictly less than 1 when $\rho \neq 0$.

The log of the bivariate Gaussian is $\log p(z_1, z_2) = -\frac{1}{2(1-\rho^2)}(z_1^2 - 2\rho z_1 z_2 + z_2^2) + C$.

CAVI update for $q_1$: $\log q_1^*(z_1) = \mathbb{E}_{q_2}[\log p(z_1, z_2)] + C'$ $= -\frac{1}{2(1-\rho^2)}(z_1^2 - 2\rho z_1 \mathbb{E}[z_2]) + C'$

By symmetry and the zero-mean prior, $\mathbb{E}_{q_2}[z_2] = 0$. So $\log q_1^*(z_1) = -\frac{z_1^2}{2(1-\rho^2)} + C'$, giving $q_1^*(z_1) = \mathcal{N}(0, 1 - \rho^2)$.

By symmetry, $q_2^*(z_2) = \mathcal{N}(0, 1 - \rho^2)$ as well.

The true marginal variance is $\text{Var}_p(z_1) = 1$, but the mean-field gives $\text{Var}_{q_1}(z_1) = 1 - \rho^2 < 1$ whenever $\rho \neq 0$. The mean-field approximation underestimates the marginal variance by exactly $\rho^2$. This happens because the mean-field cannot represent the correlation between $z_1$ and $z_2$; to compensate and still minimize KL, it shrinks the marginal variances. This is a general phenomenon: mean-field VI systematically underestimates posterior uncertainty.

🔴 Exercise 24: Black-box variational inference ▼

Derive the black-box variational inference (BBVI) gradient estimator that works for any model and any variational family (without requiring reparameterization or conjugacy). Include at least one variance reduction technique and prove it does not introduce bias.

The ELBO gradient is $\nabla_\phi \mathcal{L} = \nabla_\phi \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log p(x,z) - \log q_\phi(z|x)]$.

Using the score function identity: $\nabla_\phi \mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}\left[(\log p(x,z) - \log q_\phi(z|x)) \nabla_\phi \log q_\phi(z|x)\right]$

(Note: the $\nabla_\phi$ acting on $-\log q_\phi$ inside the expectation contributes $-\mathbb{E}[\nabla_\phi \log q_\phi] = 0$ by the score function identity, so the full gradient simplifies to the above.)

Variance reduction via control variate: Replace $f(z) = \log p(x,z) - \log q_\phi(z|x)$ with $f(z) - b$ where $b$ is a baseline: $$\hat{g} = \frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^S (f(z^{(s)}) - b)\nabla_\phi \log q_\phi(z^{(s)}|x)$$

Proof of unbiasedness: $\mathbb{E}[b \cdot \nabla_\phi \log q_\phi(z|x)] = b \cdot \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\nabla_\phi \log q_\phi] = b \cdot \nabla_\phi \int q_\phi\, dz = b \cdot \nabla_\phi 1 = 0$. So subtracting any constant $b$ does not change the expected gradient. The optimal baseline that minimizes variance is $b^* = \frac{\mathbb{E}[f(z)^2 \|\nabla \log q\|^2]}{\mathbb{E}[\|\nabla \log q\|^2]}$, but in practice $b = \bar{f}$ (the running mean of $f$) works well.

🔴 Exercise 25: Convergence of CAVI ▼

Prove that CAVI monotonically increases the ELBO at each coordinate update, and that it converges to a stationary point of the ELBO. Under what conditions on the model is the ELBO concave in the variational parameters (guaranteeing a unique optimum)?

Monotonic increase: Each CAVI update sets $q_j^*(z_j) = \arg\max_{q_j} \mathcal{L}(q_1, \ldots, q_j, \ldots, q_d)$ while holding $q_{-j}$ fixed. Since this is an unconstrained maximization over $q_j$ (in the space of all distributions), the new ELBO is at least as large as the old one. Strict increase holds unless $q_j$ was already optimal.

Convergence: The ELBO is bounded above by $\log p(x)$. A bounded, monotonically increasing sequence converges. At a limit point, no single-coordinate update can improve the ELBO, which is exactly the definition of a coordinate-wise stationary point.

Concavity conditions: The ELBO is concave in the variational parameters when (1) the model is in the conjugate exponential family, (2) the mean-field family is the corresponding conjugate family, and (3) we parameterize by natural parameters. In this case, the ELBO is concave in each natural parameter vector (since the expected sufficient statistics are linear in the natural parameters and the log-partition function is convex). Global concavity is not guaranteed because the ELBO is generally not jointly concave in all variational parameters simultaneously -- the coupling between factors through the expected sufficient statistics can create non-convexity. However, for log-concave models with log-concave variational families, the ELBO is concave.

🔴 Exercise 26: Normalizing flows as flexible q ▼

A normalizing flow transforms a simple base distribution $z_0 \sim q_0(z_0)$ through a sequence of invertible maps $z_K = f_K \circ \cdots \circ f_1(z_0)$. Derive the density of $z_K$ using the change-of-variables formula, and show how this can be used as a flexible variational distribution. What is the ELBO with a normalizing flow posterior?

By the change-of-variables formula applied sequentially:

$$\log q_K(z_K) = \log q_0(z_0) - \sum_{k=1}^K \log\left|\det \frac{\partial f_k}{\partial z_{k-1}}\right|$$

where $z_0 = f_1^{-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_K^{-1}(z_K)$. Each $f_k$ must be invertible with a tractable Jacobian determinant.

The ELBO with a normalizing flow posterior:

$$\mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}_{q_0(z_0)}\left[\log p(x, z_K) - \log q_0(z_0) + \sum_{k=1}^K \log\left|\det \frac{\partial f_k}{\partial z_{k-1}}\right|\right]$$

This is computable as long as: (1) we can sample from $q_0$ (e.g., standard Gaussian), (2) each $f_k$ is differentiable and invertible, and (3) the Jacobian determinant is tractable. The flow adds expressiveness by allowing $q_K$ to be a highly non-Gaussian distribution, while maintaining exact density evaluation. Popular choices for $f_k$ include planar flows ($f(z) = z + u\, h(w^T z + b)$), radial flows, and autoregressive flows. As $K \to \infty$ with universal approximator flows, $q_K$ can approximate any distribution, closing the approximation gap of mean-field VI.

🔴 Exercise 27: Information geometry of VI ▼

Describe variational inference as a projection on a statistical manifold. What is the manifold? What is the metric? In what sense is the optimal $q^*$ a "projection" of $p(z|x)$? How does the choice of KL direction (forward vs reverse) correspond to different projections?

The manifold: The variational family $\mathcal{Q} = \{q_\phi : \phi \in \Phi\}$ is a submanifold of the space of all probability distributions $\mathcal{P}$. Each point on this manifold is a distribution, and the coordinates are the variational parameters $\phi$.

The metric: The Fisher information matrix $F_{ij}(\phi) = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}\left[\frac{\partial \log q_\phi}{\partial \phi_i} \frac{\partial \log q_\phi}{\partial \phi_j}\right]$ defines a Riemannian metric on $\mathcal{Q}$. This is the unique metric (up to scale) that is invariant under reparameterization of $\phi$, making it the natural geometry for probability distributions.

Projection interpretation: The optimal $q^* = \arg\min_{q \in \mathcal{Q}} D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p)$ is the information projection (I-projection) of $p(z|x)$ onto $\mathcal{Q}$. Locally around $q^*$, this is the closest point on the manifold $\mathcal{Q}$ to the target $p(z|x)$ in the KL "distance." For exponential families, the I-projection matches the expected sufficient statistics, which is the moment-matching property.

Forward vs reverse KL: Minimizing $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p)$ (reverse KL) gives the I-projection: $q^*$ is the closest member of $\mathcal{Q}$ that fits "inside" $p$. Minimizing $D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q)$ (forward KL) gives the M-projection (moment projection): $q^*$ is the member of $\mathcal{Q}$ whose moments match $p$'s. For exponential families, these correspond to matching natural parameters (I-projection) vs matching mean parameters (M-projection). The I-projection is mode-seeking; the M-projection is moment-matching and mode-covering.

🔴 Exercise 28: Tightness conditions for ELBO ▼

The ELBO is tight when $q_\phi(z|x) = p(z|x)$. But what if the true posterior is not in our variational family $\mathcal{Q}$? Characterize the ELBO gap for the mean-field family applied to a multivariate Gaussian posterior, and derive an expression for the gap in terms of the posterior's off-diagonal covariance entries.

Let $p(z|x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma)$ with $\Sigma$ having off-diagonal entries. The best mean-field approximation is $q^*(z) = \prod_i \mathcal{N}(z_i; \mu_i, \Sigma_{ii})$ (matching the marginals).

The ELBO gap is: $$D_{\text{KL}}(q^* \| p) = \frac{1}{2}\left[\log\frac{|\Sigma|}{\prod_i \Sigma_{ii}} - d + \text{tr}(\Sigma^{-1} \text{diag}(\Sigma)) + 0\right]$$

The mean term vanishes because $q^*$ and $p$ have the same mean. The trace term simplifies: $\text{tr}(\Sigma^{-1}\text{diag}(\Sigma)) = \sum_i (\Sigma^{-1})_{ii}\Sigma_{ii}$.

For a 2D case with $\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix}1 & \rho\\\rho & 1\end{pmatrix}$: $|\Sigma| = 1 - \rho^2$, $\prod_i \Sigma_{ii} = 1$, $\Sigma^{-1} = \frac{1}{1-\rho^2}\begin{pmatrix}1 & -\rho\\-\rho & 1\end{pmatrix}$, and $\text{tr}(\Sigma^{-1}\text{diag}(\Sigma)) = \frac{2}{1-\rho^2}$.

Gap $= \frac{1}{2}\left[\log(1-\rho^2) - 2 + \frac{2}{1-\rho^2}\right] = \frac{1}{2}\left[\log(1-\rho^2) + \frac{2\rho^2}{1-\rho^2}\right]$

For small $\rho$: gap $\approx \frac{1}{2}(-\rho^2 + 2\rho^2) = \frac{\rho^2}{2}$. The gap grows quadratically with the correlation strength, confirming that mean-field fails gracefully for weak correlations but poorly for strong ones.

🔴 Exercise 29: IWAE objective derivation ▼

The importance-weighted autoencoder (IWAE) uses $K$ samples to form a tighter bound: $\mathcal{L}_K = \mathbb{E}_{z^{(1)}, \ldots, z^{(K)} \sim q}\left[\log \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K \frac{p(x, z^{(k)})}{q(z^{(k)}|x)}\right]$. Prove that (a) $\mathcal{L}_1 = \text{ELBO}$, (b) $\mathcal{L}_K \leq \mathcal{L}_{K+1}$, and (c) $\lim_{K \to \infty} \mathcal{L}_K = \log p(x)$.

(a) $\mathcal{L}_1 = \text{ELBO}$: With $K=1$: $\mathcal{L}_1 = \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q}\left[\log \frac{p(x,z)}{q(z|x)}\right] = \text{ELBO}$. Direct substitution.

(b) $\mathcal{L}_K \leq \mathcal{L}_{K+1}$: Define $w_k = p(x, z^{(k)})/q(z^{(k)}|x)$. Then $\mathcal{L}_K = \mathbb{E}[\log \frac{1}{K}\sum_k w_k]$. We can write the $(K+1)$-sample estimator as: $$\frac{1}{K+1}\sum_{k=1}^{K+1} w_k = \frac{K}{K+1}\cdot\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K w_k + \frac{1}{K+1}w_{K+1}$$

By the concavity of $\log$ and Jensen's inequality applied conditionally on $z^{(1)}, \ldots, z^{(K)}$: $$\mathbb{E}_{z^{(K+1)}}\left[\log\left(\frac{K}{K+1}S_K + \frac{1}{K+1}w_{K+1}\right)\right] \geq \frac{K}{K+1}\log S_K + \frac{1}{K+1}\mathbb{E}[\log w_{K+1}]$$

where $S_K = \frac{1}{K}\sum_k w_k$. Taking full expectations and using symmetry of the samples, we get $\mathcal{L}_{K+1} \geq \frac{K}{K+1}\mathcal{L}_K + \frac{1}{K+1}\mathcal{L}_1 \geq \mathcal{L}_K$ (since $\mathcal{L}_K \geq \mathcal{L}_1$ by induction).

(c) $\lim_{K \to \infty} \mathcal{L}_K = \log p(x)$: By the law of large numbers, $\frac{1}{K}\sum_k w_k \to \mathbb{E}_q[w] = \mathbb{E}_q[p(x,z)/q(z|x)] = \int p(x,z)\,dz = p(x)$ almost surely. Therefore $\log \frac{1}{K}\sum_k w_k \to \log p(x)$, and by the dominated convergence theorem (or uniform integrability), $\mathcal{L}_K \to \log p(x)$.

🔴 Exercise 30: The amortization gap ▼

Define the amortization gap formally. Decompose the total inference gap into an approximation gap (due to the restricted variational family) and an amortization gap (due to sharing parameters across data points). Propose a method to measure each component empirically and discuss when the amortization gap dominates.

Definitions: For a given data point $x$, let:

- $\mathcal{L}^*_{\text{opt}}(x)$: the ELBO obtained by optimizing $\phi$ specifically for $x$ (per-example VI)

- $\mathcal{L}^*_{\text{amort}}(x)$: the ELBO obtained from the amortized encoder $f_\phi(x)$

- $\log p(x)$: the true log-evidence

The total inference gap decomposes as:

$$\underbrace{\log p(x) - \mathcal{L}^*_{\text{amort}}(x)}_{\text{total gap}} = \underbrace{\log p(x) - \mathcal{L}^*_{\text{opt}}(x)}_{\text{approximation gap}} + \underbrace{\mathcal{L}^*_{\text{opt}}(x) - \mathcal{L}^*_{\text{amort}}(x)}_{\text{amortization gap}}$$

The approximation gap is $D_{\text{KL}}(q^*_{\text{opt}}(z|x) \| p(z|x))$ -- the best the variational family can do. The amortization gap is the suboptimality of the shared encoder relative to per-example optimization within the same family.

Empirical measurement: (1) Train the amortized model normally to get $\mathcal{L}^*_{\text{amort}}$. (2) For a subset of test points, initialize from the encoder output and run additional gradient steps on $(\mu, \log\sigma)$ to convergence; the resulting ELBO is $\mathcal{L}^*_{\text{opt}}$. (3) The difference is the amortization gap.

When it dominates: The amortization gap tends to be large when: (a) the mapping from $x$ to optimal variational parameters is highly nonlinear or discontinuous (e.g., multimodal posteriors where different $x$ values require very different $q$), (b) the encoder is underparameterized, (c) training data is limited so the encoder generalizes poorly. For well-trained, expressive encoders on smooth problems, the amortization gap is typically small (a few nats) compared to the approximation gap.
