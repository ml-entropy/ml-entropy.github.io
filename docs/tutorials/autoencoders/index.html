Autoencoder Models

A dedicated seven-part track on autoencoders and variational methods, built from first principles. It begins with deterministic bottleneck compression, passes through variational inference and the VAE framework, explores rate-distortion theory and the information bottleneck, and ends with advanced architectures and real-world applications from learned image compression to drug discovery.

What is an Autoencoder?

An autoencoder is a neural network trained to copy its input to its output through a constrained bottleneck. The constraint forces the network to discover efficient representations — compressed codes that capture the most important structure in the data while discarding noise and redundancy. When the bottleneck is probabilistic, we arrive at variational autoencoders, which combine deep learning with Bayesian inference and connect directly to Shannon's rate-distortion theory. This track builds the full story: from the simplest deterministic bottleneck to the deepest information-theoretic foundations.

How to use this track

Read chapters 00–02 in order if you want the mathematical story to build naturally. Those chapters establish deterministic autoencoders, the variational inference framework, and the VAE that combines both — the foundation everything else rests on.

Chapters 03–04 are the information-theoretic core. Rate-distortion theory provides the fundamental limits of lossy compression, and the information bottleneck extends those ideas to supervised learning. These chapters can be read as focused references, but they reward sequential reading because each builds on the previous chapter's vocabulary.

Chapters 05–06 are the advanced toolkit. They assume the core ideas from the first five chapters, but they can also be read selectively: chapter 05 surveys the modern architecture zoo, while chapter 06 shows how these ideas power real applications from neural image codecs to molecular generation.

Prerequisites and reading strategies

You will get the most from this track if probability distributions, KL divergence, basic neural-network training, and simple linear algebra already feel comfortable. If any of that is rusty, the Probability, Machine Learning, and Information Theory sections of this site make the best warm-up before diving into autoencoders.

If your goal is intuition, read the theory tab of each chapter first and leave code and exercises for a second pass. If your goal is implementation, pair chapters 00, 02, 05, and 06: together they explain how a practical autoencoder system is built, extended to variational methods, scaled with modern architectures, and deployed in applications.

After this track

After completing this track, you'll have the theoretical and practical foundation to read cutting-edge papers on representation learning, neural compression, and generative modeling. You'll understand why VAEs, VQ-VAEs, and their descendants form the backbone of systems like Stable Diffusion (which uses a VAE encoder to project images into a latent space before applying diffusion). For related topics, explore the Diffusion Models track for the generative modeling perspective, or the ML track's coverage of information theory and deep learning foundations.
