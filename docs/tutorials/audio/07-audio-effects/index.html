Delay-Based Effects

The simplest audio effect is a delay line: store the signal in a buffer and play it back $D$ samples later. With no feedback, this is a single echo. With feedback, the delayed output is mixed back into the input, creating a repeating echo that decays over time.

The feedback delay equation is:

$$y[n] = x[n] + g \cdot y[n - D]$$

where $g$ is the feedback gain ($|g| < 1$ for stability) and $D$ is the delay in samples. The delay time in seconds maps to samples as:

$$D = t_{\text{delay}} \times f_s$$

For example, a 250 ms echo at 44,100 Hz requires $D = 0.25 \times 44100 = 11{,}025$ samples of buffer storage.

Feedback Delay Transfer Function

In the z-domain, $Y(z) = X(z) + g \cdot z^{-D} Y(z)$, so:

$$H(z) = \frac{Y(z)}{X(z)} = \frac{1}{1 - g \cdot z^{-D}}$$

This is an IIR filter — the denominator creates an infinite series of echoes at multiples of $D$ samples, each scaled by $g^k$.

Chorus, Flanger, Phaser

These three classic effects all modulate a delay line with a low-frequency oscillator (LFO), but differ in their delay times and the resulting sonic character.

Chorus uses a long delay (20–50 ms) with slow, gentle LFO modulation (0.1–1 Hz). The dry signal is mixed with the delayed, slightly detuned copy. The result resembles multiple performers playing in unison — the slight pitch and timing discrepancies add richness.

Flanger uses a very short delay (1–10 ms) swept by the LFO. At these short delays, the dry and delayed signals interfere constructively and destructively at harmonically related frequencies, creating a comb filter with evenly spaced peaks and notches. Sweeping the delay moves the comb in frequency, producing the characteristic "jet airplane" whoosh.

Phaser uses a chain of allpass filters (not a delay line per se) to create phase shifts that vary with frequency. At certain frequencies, the allpass output is 180° out of phase with the dry signal, producing cancellation notches. The LFO moves these notches, creating a "sweeping" sound similar to flanging but with fewer, unevenly spaced notches.

Reverb

Reverberation is the persistence of sound in a space after the source stops. It consists of two phases: early reflections (discrete echoes from walls, floor, ceiling, arriving within ~80 ms) and the late diffuse tail (a dense, exponentially decaying cloud of echoes).

The RT60 (reverberation time) is the time for the sound energy to decay by 60 dB. Concert halls have RT60 of 1.5–2.5 s; a bathroom might be 0.3–0.8 s; an anechoic chamber approaches 0 s.

The Schroeder reverberator (1962) models this with:

  • Four parallel comb filters (delay + feedback) with different delay lengths, generating the dense echo density of late reverberation.
  • Two series allpass filters to diffuse the output and suppress the metallic coloration of the comb filters.

Convolution reverb is the modern alternative: capture the impulse response (IR) of a real space by recording a starter pistol shot or a sine sweep, then convolve any audio signal with that IR. This produces a mathematically perfect simulation of the real room's acoustics, at the cost of heavy computation.

RT60 and Comb Filter Gain

For a comb filter with delay $D$ samples and sample rate $f_s$, the feedback gain needed for a target RT60 is:

$$g = 10^{-3D/(f_s \cdot \text{RT60})}$$

For RT60 = 1.0 s, $f_s$ = 44100, $D$ = 1557: $g = 10^{-3 \times 1557/(44100 \times 1.0)} = 10^{-0.1058} \approx 0.784$.

Dynamic Range Compression

A compressor reduces the dynamic range of audio: loud passages are attenuated while quiet passages may be boosted (via make-up gain). This is essential in mixing, mastering, broadcasting, and live sound.

The key parameters are:

  • Threshold $T$ (dB): level above which compression begins.
  • Ratio $R$:1 — for every $R$ dB above threshold, output rises only 1 dB.
  • Attack: how fast the compressor responds to a signal exceeding the threshold.
  • Release: how fast gain returns to normal after the signal drops below threshold.
  • Knee: soft knee rounds the transition at the threshold; hard knee is abrupt.

The gain-compute transfer function for a hard-knee compressor:

$$y_{\text{dB}} = \begin{cases} x_{\text{dB}} & x_{\text{dB}} \leq T \\ T + \dfrac{x_{\text{dB}} - T}{R} & x_{\text{dB}} > T \end{cases}$$

A limiter is simply a compressor with ratio $\infty$:1, so the output never exceeds the threshold regardless of input level.

Equalization (EQ)

An equalizer adjusts the frequency balance of audio by boosting or cutting specific frequency regions. All practical EQ bands are implemented using biquad (second-order IIR) filters with the transfer function:

$$H(z) = \frac{b_0 + b_1 z^{-1} + b_2 z^{-2}}{1 + a_1 z^{-1} + a_2 z^{-2}}$$

Common EQ filter types:

  • Low shelf — boosts or cuts all frequencies below a corner frequency $f_c$ by a fixed gain $G$ dB.
  • High shelf — same, but above $f_c$.
  • Peak/notch (parametric EQ band) — bell-shaped boost or cut centered at $f_0$, controlled by gain $G$ dB and quality factor $Q$. Higher $Q$ = narrower bandwidth.
  • Graphic EQ — fixed center frequencies (e.g., 31 Hz, 63 Hz, 125 Hz, …), each band a peak filter with fixed $Q$.

The bandwidth in octaves is related to $Q$ by: $\text{BW} = f_0/Q$ Hz, or equivalently $\text{BW}_{\text{oct}} = \log_2\!\left(\frac{1 + 1/(2Q^2) + \sqrt{1/Q^2 + 1/(4Q^4)}}{1}\right)$.

Distortion & Saturation

Distortion is nonlinear processing: the output is a nonlinear function of the input, $y = f(x)$ where $f$ is not a straight line. Nonlinearity generates harmonics — components at integer multiples of the input frequency — and intermodulation products when multiple frequencies are present.

Common waveshaping functions:

  • Hard clip: $f(x) = \text{clip}(x, -1, 1)$ — abrupt clipping, produces strong odd harmonics (transistor/digital style).
  • Soft clip: gradual rounding before the clip point, softer harmonic character.
  • Tanh saturation: $f(x) = \tanh(gx)$ — smooth asymptotic limit, rich in odd harmonics, resembles tube/transformer saturation. Gain $g$ controls drive level.
  • Asymmetric clipping — different waveshape on positive and negative halves; adds even harmonics (octaves), common in tube circuits.

A Taylor expansion of $\tanh(gx) = gx - \frac{(gx)^3}{3} + \frac{(gx)^5}{5} - \cdots$ shows that for a sinusoidal input $x = A\sin(\omega t)$, the cubic term produces $\sin(3\omega t)$ (third harmonic), the fifth-order term produces $\sin(5\omega t)$, and so on.

Pitch Shifting & Time Stretching

Pitch shifting changes the pitch without altering duration; time stretching changes duration without altering pitch. These seem contradictory — naive playback speed change does both simultaneously. Sophisticated algorithms decouple the two.

Phase vocoder is the classic algorithm:

  1. Compute the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) of the input signal, obtaining a sequence of complex spectra.
  2. To time-stretch by factor $\alpha$: output STFT frames at a rate $1/\alpha$ times slower, but carefully adjust the phases to maintain phase coherence between frames.
  3. To pitch-shift by factor $\beta$: first time-stretch by $1/\beta$, then resample the output by $\beta$ (restoring original duration but with shifted pitch).
  4. Recover time-domain audio via inverse STFT (ISTFT) using overlap-add synthesis.

The phase update rule ensures smooth sinusoidal tracks. For bin $k$ with analysis hop $H_a$ and synthesis hop $H_s$:

$$\Delta\phi_k = \text{angle}(X_k^{(m)}) - \text{angle}(X_k^{(m-1)}) - k \cdot \frac{2\pi H_a}{N}$$ $$\phi_k^{\text{out}} = \phi_k^{\text{prev,out}} + H_s \cdot \frac{\Delta\phi_k + k \cdot 2\pi H_a/N}{H_a}$$

Granular synthesis is an alternative: slice the audio into tiny "grains" (5–100 ms), then reassemble them with controlled overlap and spacing. Time-stretching = space the grains farther apart; pitch-shifting = replay each grain at a different rate.

PSOLA (Pitch-Synchronous Overlap-Add) is optimized for speech: marks pitch periods and rearranges them, exploiting the quasi-periodic nature of voiced speech for high-quality pitch and time modification.

Phase Vocoder Summary

Input signal $x[n]$ → STFT (frame with hop $H_a$, window $N$) → modify magnitudes/phases → ISTFT (hop $H_s$) → output $y[n]$. Time scale factor: $\alpha = H_s/H_a$. For pitch shift: time-stretch then resample.

2Echo Decay TimeEasy

A feedback delay has $g = 0.7$ and $D = 8820$ samples (200 ms at 44,100 Hz). After how many echoes has the level dropped below −20 dB of the original?

After $k$ echoes, amplitude = $g^k$. Need $20\log_{10}(g^k) \leq -20$ dB:

$k \log_{10}(0.7) \leq -1 \Rightarrow k \times (-0.1549) \leq -1 \Rightarrow k \geq 6.46$.

3Compressor Gain ReductionEasy

A compressor has threshold $T = -20$ dBFS and ratio 4:1. If the input is −4 dBFS, what is the output level?

$x_\text{dB} = -4$ dBFS, which is $-4 - (-20) = 16$ dB above threshold.

$y_\text{dB} = T + (x - T)/R = -20 + 16/4 = -20 + 4 = -16$ dBFS.

4Comb Filter Null FrequencyEasy

A flanger uses a delay of 4 ms (at 44,100 Hz) with no feedback. What is the lowest frequency at which a null (cancellation) occurs in the feedforward comb filter response?

Feedforward comb: $y[n] = x[n] - x[n-D]$. Nulls at frequencies where delay causes 180° phase shift: $f = \frac{1}{2D_t} = \frac{1}{2 \times 0.004} = 125$ Hz. Subsequent nulls at odd multiples: 375 Hz, 625 Hz, …

A parametric EQ peak filter has center frequency $f_0 = 1000$ Hz and $Q = 2$. What is the bandwidth in Hz?

Bandwidth $\approx f_0 / Q = 1000/2 = 500$ Hz (from roughly 750 Hz to 1250 Hz).

A room has RT60 = 2.5 s. If a sound source at −10 dBFS stops suddenly, after how long has the reverb tail decayed to −70 dBFS?

RT60 = time for 60 dB decay. Starting from −10 dBFS, reaching −70 dBFS is a 60 dB drop, which takes exactly RT60 = 2.5 s.

The input peaks at 0.8, which exceeds the clip level of 0.5. The output peak is clipped to 0.5.

8Make-Up GainEasy

A compressor with $T = -18$ dBFS and ratio 3:1 is fed a sustained 0 dBFS signal. How much make-up gain should be added to bring the output back to 0 dBFS?

Input at 0 dBFS is 18 dB above threshold. Output before make-up: $-18 + 18/3 = -18 + 6 = -12$ dBFS. To reach 0 dBFS: make-up gain = $+12$ dB.

9Chorus vs. FlangerEasy

Identify which effect uses the longer delay line: chorus or flanger? Explain in one sentence why this difference creates their distinct sounds.

Chorus uses the longer delay (20–50 ms vs. 1–10 ms for flanger). Because the delay is longer, the comb filter nulls fall at lower frequencies and are more widely spaced, giving a lush doubling effect rather than flanging's close-together metallic combing in the audible range.

10Delay Buffer SizeEasy

A stereo reverb plugin supports a maximum delay of 2 seconds. At 48,000 Hz with 32-bit floating point samples, how much RAM does each channel's delay buffer require?

$D = 2 \times 48000 = 96{,}000$ samples per channel. Memory: $96{,}000 \times 4 \text{ bytes} = 384{,}000$ bytes $\approx 375$ KB per channel. For stereo: $\approx 750$ KB total.

12Compressor Gain Reduction ScheduleMedium

A compressor has $T = -24$ dBFS, ratio 6:1, and a soft knee of width 12 dB (i.e., the knee spans from −30 to −18 dBFS). Compute the output for input levels −30, −24, −18, and −6 dBFS.

Knee region: $-30$ to $-18$ dBFS. Below −30: unity gain. Above −18: full 6:1 compression.

$x = -30$ dBFS: linear region, $y = -30$ dBFS.

$x = -24$ dBFS: center of knee — apply partial compression. With a quadratic soft knee, effective ratio is ~3.5:1 at knee center: $y \approx -24 + (0)/(3.5) = -24$ (at exact knee center gain is still essentially unity).

$x = -18$ dBFS: 6 dB above threshold, full compression: $y = -24 + 6/6 = -23$ dBFS.

$x = -6$ dBFS: 18 dB above threshold: $y = -24 + 18/6 = -24 + 3 = -21$ dBFS.

13Comb Filter AnalysisMedium

A feedback comb filter has $D = 2205$ samples (50 ms at 44,100 Hz) and $g = 0.9$. (a) What frequencies are boosted? (b) What is the peak magnitude response at those frequencies? (c) What is the RT60 of this filter?

(a) Boosted (peaks) at $f = k/D_t = k/0.05 = 20k$ Hz for $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ So 0, 20, 40, 60 Hz … (with $f_s = 44100$ Hz, up to Nyquist at 22,050 Hz).

(b) Peak magnitude: $H(z)|_{z=e^{j\omega_\text{peak}}} = 1/(1-g) = 1/(1-0.9) = 10$ (i.e., $+20$ dB).

(c) RT60: $g^{k_{60\text{dB}}} = 10^{-3}$, so $k = -3/\log_{10}(0.9) = -3/(-0.04576) = 65.6$ repetitions. Time: $65.6 \times 0.05 \text{ s} = 3.28$ s.

14Tanh Harmonic ContentMedium

For a sinusoidal input $x = A\sin(\theta)$ with $A = 0.5$ and gain $g = 2$, approximate the output $\tanh(2 \times 0.5\sin\theta)$ using the Taylor series up to the third-order term. What is the third harmonic amplitude relative to the fundamental?

$\tanh(u) \approx u - u^3/3$ for small $u$. Here $u = \sin\theta$ (after $g \times A = 1$):

$\tanh(\sin\theta) \approx \sin\theta - \sin^3\theta/3$.

Using $\sin^3\theta = \frac{3\sin\theta - \sin(3\theta)}{4}$:

$\approx \sin\theta - \frac{3\sin\theta - \sin(3\theta)}{12} = \left(1 - \frac{3}{12}\right)\sin\theta + \frac{\sin(3\theta)}{12} = \frac{3}{4}\sin\theta + \frac{1}{12}\sin(3\theta)$.

Third harmonic amplitude: $1/12$. Fundamental: $3/4$. Ratio: $(1/12)/(3/4) = \frac{1}{9} \approx 0.111$ or about $-19.1$ dB.

15Convolution Reverb CostMedium

An impulse response is 3 seconds long at 48,000 Hz ($L = 144{,}000$ samples). Using direct convolution, how many multiplications are needed to process one second of mono audio (48,000 output samples)?

Each output sample requires $L = 144{,}000$ multiplications (convolution sum). For 48,000 output samples:

$48000 \times 144000 = 6.912 \times 10^9$ multiplications per second — far too slow for real time on a CPU. This is why FFT-based overlap-add or overlap-save convolution (using $O(N\log N)$ per block) is used instead, reducing cost by roughly $\log_2(144000) \approx 17\times$.

17Pitch Shift by a SemitoneMedium

To pitch-shift a signal up by exactly 3 semitones using the phase-vocoder method (time-stretch then resample), what is the required time-stretch ratio $\alpha$ and resample factor $\beta$?

3 semitones up: pitch ratio $\beta = 2^{3/12} = 2^{0.25} \approx 1.18921$.

Phase-vocoder approach: first time-stretch by $1/\beta$ (make audio slower), then resample at $\beta \times f_s$ (speed it back up, raising pitch).

Time-stretch ratio: $\alpha = 1/\beta = 1/1.18921 \approx 0.84090$ (signal is $84.09\%$ of original duration after stretch).

Then resample: consume samples at $\beta = 1.18921$ times the original rate, restoring original duration with pitch raised by 3 semitones.

18Limiter BehaviorMedium

A look-ahead limiter has threshold $T = -3$ dBFS and look-ahead 5 ms (221 samples at 44,100 Hz). Explain why look-ahead is necessary, and calculate the additional latency introduced to the signal chain.

Without look-ahead, a limiter's attack must respond fast enough to catch transients before they exceed the threshold. A truly instantaneous (0 ms attack) limiter has no look-ahead but cannot apply gain reduction before the transient arrives — so very fast transients still clip for the first sample.

With look-ahead: the limiter reads 5 ms into the future (using a 221-sample delay on the output), sees the transient coming, and starts reducing gain before it arrives. The transient is caught perfectly with no overshoot.

Cost: the signal is delayed by exactly the look-ahead time = 5 ms = 221 samples. This is acceptable for recording/mastering but must be compensated in real-time live use.

19Phaser vs. FlangerMedium

A 4-stage phaser creates 2 notches in the frequency response; a flanger creates many evenly spaced notches. (a) Why does a phaser create fewer notches? (b) What determines the spacing of flanger notches?

(a) Each 2nd-order allpass stage contributes a 360° phase shift across the full frequency range. A notch occurs where the allpass output is 180° out of phase with the dry signal — each pair of allpass sections creates one such notch. With 4 stages (2 pairs), there are 2 notches. The notches are not evenly spaced (they are set by the allpass pole frequencies, tunable via the LFO).

(b) Flanger notch spacing: for a delay of $D_t$ seconds, nulls occur at odd multiples of $1/(2D_t)$. Spacing between adjacent nulls = $1/D_t$ Hz. A shorter delay (smaller $D_t$) spreads the notches farther apart.

20Distortion THD EstimateMedium

A waveshaper applies $y = \tanh(3x)$ to a full-scale sinusoidal input $x = \sin(\omega t)$. Using the expansion $\tanh(u) \approx u - u^3/3 + 2u^5/15$, estimate the THD (total harmonic distortion), approximating THD $\approx$ amplitude of 3rd harmonic / amplitude of fundamental.

$u = 3\sin\theta$. Up to 5th order: $\tanh(3\sin\theta) \approx 3\sin\theta - (3\sin\theta)^3/3 + 2(3\sin\theta)^5/15$.

$= 3\sin\theta - 9\sin^3\theta + \frac{2 \times 243}{15}\sin^5\theta$.

Using $\sin^3\theta = \frac{3\sin\theta - \sin(3\theta)}{4}$ and $\sin^5\theta = \frac{10\sin\theta - 5\sin(3\theta) + \sin(5\theta)}{16}$:

Coefficient of $\sin(3\theta)$: $9/4 - (32.4 \times 5/16) = 2.25 - 10.125 = -7.875$ (but note we are in approximation range — for a very driven tanh, harmonics are large).

A simpler approach: for $g=3$, the drive is strong and THD > 10%. In practice, $\tanh(3)$ clips almost like a hard clipper, producing THD ~ 20–30%. The approximation breaks down because the Taylor series converges slowly for $|u|$ near 3.

Hard (21–30)

21Convolution Reverb MathematicsHard

Given an impulse response $h[n]$ of a room, prove that applying convolution reverb $y[n] = (x * h)[n]$ is equivalent to passing the signal through a linear time-invariant (LTI) system, and explain why this means the room's response can be characterized completely by its impulse response.

Convolution is the defining operation of LTI systems. By definition, an LTI system is:

(1) Linear: if $x_1 \to y_1$ and $x_2 \to y_2$, then $ax_1 + bx_2 \to ay_1 + by_2$.
(2) Time-invariant: if $x[n] \to y[n]$, then $x[n-k] \to y[n-k]$.

Any such system is fully described by its response to a unit impulse $\delta[n]$, since any signal $x[n]$ can be written as a sum of shifted, scaled impulses: $x[n] = \sum_k x[k]\delta[n-k]$. By linearity and time-invariance:

$$y[n] = \sum_k x[k] h[n-k] = (x * h)[n]$$

For a room: the acoustic propagation is linear (sound pressure is additive) and time-invariant (the room geometry does not change during the recording). Therefore, firing an impulse (starter pistol, swept sine) and recording the response $h[n]$ completely characterizes how the room transforms any audio signal — convolution reproduces the exact acoustic effect of that space.

22Phase Vocoder Phase Update DerivationHard

Derive the phase vocoder phase update formula. Show why simply multiplying analysis phases by the time-scale ratio $\alpha = H_s/H_a$ fails, and why the instantaneous frequency estimate is needed.

The STFT at frame $m$ gives complex coefficient $X_k^{(m)} = |X_k^{(m)}| e^{j\phi_k^{(m)}}$. The phase advances by:

$$\Delta\phi_k^{(m)} = \phi_k^{(m)} - \phi_k^{(m-1)}$$

For a pure sinusoid at frequency $\omega_k = 2\pi k/N$, the expected phase advance over $H_a$ samples is $\omega_k H_a = 2\pi k H_a / N$. The deviation from expected:

$$\delta\phi_k = \Delta\phi_k - 2\pi k H_a/N$$

wrap $\delta\phi_k$ to $(-\pi, \pi]$. The instantaneous frequency (in radians/sample) is:

$$\hat{\omega}_k = \frac{\delta\phi_k + 2\pi k H_a/N}{H_a}$$

For synthesis with hop $H_s$, the output phase should advance by $\hat{\omega}_k \cdot H_s$:

$$\phi_k^{\text{out}} = \phi_k^{\text{prev,out}} + \hat{\omega}_k H_s$$

Why naive scaling fails: simply multiplying $\phi_k^{(m)}$ by $\alpha = H_s/H_a$ would change the absolute phases without respecting inter-frame continuity. The result is that neighboring frames are phase-incoherent — sinusoidal tracks across frames are phase-scrambled, producing the characteristic "phasiness" artifact. The instantaneous frequency method instead propagates phases consistently, maintaining coherence across frames.

23Multi-Band Compression DesignHard

Design a 3-band compressor for mastering. Specify: (a) crossover frequencies, (b) per-band threshold, ratio, attack, and release times, with engineering justification for each choice. Explain the advantage of multi-band over single-band compression.

Crossover frequencies: 200 Hz (sub-bass/bass split) and 5 kHz (bass-mid/treble split).

Band 1 (sub-bass <200 Hz): $T = -18$ dBFS, ratio 3:1, attack 80 ms (slow — punch preservation), release 300 ms. Low-frequency content is naturally peaky; moderate compression controls boomy kickdrums without killing impact. Slow attack preserves transient punch.

Band 2 (mid 200 Hz–5 kHz): $T = -20$ dBFS, ratio 2:1, attack 20 ms, release 150 ms. This is the most sensitivity-rich range. Gentle compression increases perceived loudness. Moderate attack to catch vocals and instruments without killing presence.

Band 3 (treble >5 kHz): $T = -22$ dBFS, ratio 2:1, attack 5 ms, release 80 ms. Sibilants and cymbals can become harsh; fast attack tames them. Relatively gentle ratio to preserve air and brightness.

Advantage: a single-band compressor responds to the loudest band. If the bass suddenly gets loud (kick drum), it compresses the entire mix — ducking the treble and mids. Multi-band is independent per band: the kick compresses only the bass band, leaving mid/treble dynamics unaffected. Result: more transparent processing, more consistent tonal balance.

24Allpass Filter in ReverbHard

Show that the Schroeder allpass filter $H(z) = \frac{-g + z^{-M}}{1 - g \cdot z^{-M}}$ has flat magnitude response ($|H(e^{j\omega})| = 1$ for all $\omega$) but nonlinear phase. Why is this useful in reverb despite not changing the energy?

Magnitude: Let $u = e^{-j\omega M}$ (with $|u|=1$). Then:

$$|H| = \left|\frac{-g + u}{1 - gu}\right| = \frac{|-g + u|}{|1 - gu|}$$

$$|-g+u|^2 = g^2 - 2g\cos(\omega M) + 1$$

$$|1-gu|^2 = 1 - 2g\cos(\omega M) + g^2$$

These are identical, so $|H| = 1$ for all $\omega$. The filter is allpass.

Phase response: The numerator and denominator are complex conjugates reflected (one has $-g$ where the other has $-g$ in the denominator with $g$), so the phase is generally nonlinear — different frequencies are delayed by different amounts.

Use in reverb: The allpass does not change spectral energy, so it cannot fix the comb filter's uneven frequency coloring directly. But it diffuses the echo pattern in time: an impulse fed through an allpass produces a smeared output with impulses at $M, 2M, 3M, \ldots$ samples — increasing temporal density without adding spectral coloration. This makes the reverb tail sound smoother and less metallic. Multiple allpass stages in series are extremely effective at achieving the desired diffuse, room-like character.

25Waveshaping Spectrum via ChebyshevHard

Chebyshev polynomials provide a way to map a sinusoidal input to a specific harmonic. $T_k(\cos\theta) = \cos(k\theta)$. If you want a waveshaper that produces only the 3rd harmonic (output $\propto \cos(3\omega t)$) from a cosine input $x = A\cos(\omega t)$, what waveshaping function $f(x)$ achieves this?

We want $f(A\cos\theta) = \cos(3\theta)$. Using $T_3(\cos\theta) = 4\cos^3\theta - 3\cos\theta$:

$$\cos(3\theta) = 4\cos^3\theta - 3\cos\theta$$

Substituting $\cos\theta = x/A$:

$$f(x) = 4(x/A)^3 - 3(x/A) = \frac{4x^3}{A^3} - \frac{3x}{A}$$

This is the 3rd-order Chebyshev polynomial applied to the normalized input. For a unit-amplitude input ($A=1$): $f(x) = 4x^3 - 3x$. This polynomial waveshaper produces only the 3rd harmonic — no fundamental leakthrough, no 5th harmonic — pure third harmonic synthesis. Chebyshev polynomials form an orthogonal basis for waveshaping, allowing spectral design of distortion effects.

26RT60 from Sabine's EquationHard

Sabine's equation for RT60 is: $\text{RT60} = \frac{0.161 \cdot V}{A}$ where $V$ is room volume (m³) and $A = \sum_i \alpha_i S_i$ is the total absorption ($\alpha_i$ = absorption coefficient, $S_i$ = surface area in m²). For a rectangular room 10 m × 8 m × 3 m with average absorption $\alpha = 0.2$ on all surfaces, (a) find RT60, (b) what $\alpha$ gives RT60 = 0.5 s?

(a) Volume: $V = 10 \times 8 \times 3 = 240$ m³.

Surfaces: floor/ceiling: $2 \times 80 = 160$ m². Walls: $2(10\times3 + 8\times3) = 2(30+24) = 108$ m². Total $S = 268$ m².

$A = 0.2 \times 268 = 53.6$ m² Sabins.

$\text{RT60} = 0.161 \times 240 / 53.6 = 38.64/53.6 = \mathbf{0.72}$ s.

(b) $0.5 = 0.161 \times 240 / A \Rightarrow A = 38.64/0.5 = 77.28$. $\alpha = A/S = 77.28/268 = \mathbf{0.288}$.

So about 29% absorption on all surfaces is needed — achievable with moderate acoustic treatment (carpet, acoustic panels).

27Sidechain CompressionHard

In "ducking" — used in radio broadcasting and modern pop music — the music track is compressed based on the level of the voiceover/vocals (the sidechain signal), not the music itself. Formally describe the signal flow and gain computation, and explain how this differs from standard compression.

Standard compression: the gain-control signal is derived from the same signal being compressed.

Sidechain compression signal flow:

1. Key signal (e.g., vocals) is fed into the sidechain input of the compressor — this signal is analyzed but NOT compressed.

2. The level detector (RMS or peak) measures the key signal level $x_k[n]$.

3. The gain-compute stage applies the threshold/ratio law to $x_k[n]$, producing a gain reduction $G[n]$.

4. The gain $G[n]$ (with attack/release smoothing) is applied multiplicatively to the program signal (the music), NOT the key signal: $y_{\text{music}}[n] = G[n] \cdot x_{\text{music}}[n]$.

Result: when the vocals are loud, the gain reduction is applied to the music, causing it to "duck" (decrease in level) and allowing the vocals to sit on top without the listener perceiving a level fight. The effect is automatic volume riding synchronized to the key signal's dynamics.

28Intermodulation DistortionHard

A nonlinear system has transfer function $y = x + 0.1 x^3$. The input is $x = \cos(\omega_1 t) + \cos(\omega_2 t)$ with $\omega_1/(2\pi) = 1000$ Hz and $\omega_2/(2\pi) = 1100$ Hz. What intermodulation (IM) products appear in the output, and at what frequencies?

$x^3 = (\cos\omega_1 t + \cos\omega_2 t)^3 = \cos^3\omega_1 t + 3\cos^2\omega_1 t\cos\omega_2 t + 3\cos\omega_1 t\cos^2\omega_2 t + \cos^3\omega_2 t$.

Expanding with product-to-sum identities:

$\cos^3\omega t = \frac{3}{4}\cos\omega t + \frac{1}{4}\cos(3\omega t)$ → harmonics at 3000 Hz, 3300 Hz.

$3\cos^2\omega_1 t\cos\omega_2 t = \frac{3}{2}(1 + \cos 2\omega_1 t)\cos\omega_2 t = \frac{3}{2}\cos\omega_2 t + \frac{3}{4}[\cos(2\omega_1+\omega_2)t + \cos(2\omega_1-\omega_2)t]$.

IM products at: $2f_1 - f_2 = 2000-1100 = \mathbf{900}$ Hz, $2f_1+f_2 = \mathbf{3100}$ Hz, $2f_2-f_1 = \mathbf{1200}$ Hz, $2f_2+f_1 = \mathbf{3200}$ Hz.

The most audibly disturbing are the lower-order IM products close to the originals: 900 Hz and 1200 Hz — they fall within the critical band and create beating/roughness. This is why IM distortion is more perceptually annoying than harmonic distortion alone.

29Granular Time Stretching AnalysisHard

A granular time-stretcher uses grain size $G = 50$ ms, overlap $50\%$, and hop $H = 25$ ms. To stretch by factor $\alpha = 1.5\times$ (50% longer), how should the synthesis hop $H_s$ be set? What is the maximum usable stretch factor before audible graininess appears, and why?

Analysis hop: $H_a = 25$ ms. For time stretch $\alpha = 1.5$: synthesis hop $H_s = \alpha \cdot H_a = 1.5 \times 25 = 37.5$ ms. Grains from the source are output $1.5\times$ further apart in time, stretching the total duration.

Maximum usable stretch: this depends on the grain size $G$ and the periodicity of the source material.

For voiced speech (periodic signals with period $T_0$): grains spaced by $H_s > G$ (no overlap) cause audible gaps — silence between grains. With 50% overlap ($H_s \leq G/2$), maximum stretch before gaps: $\alpha_{\max} = G / (2H_a) = 50/(2\times 25) = 1\times$ — but this is with full overlap already. For larger $\alpha$, the synthesis hop exceeds $G/2$, reducing overlap below 50%.

Practically: stretches beyond $\sim 2\times$ start to produce audible granularity for $G=50$ ms because grain boundaries become noticeable, and cross-grain continuity degrades. Longer grains (or pitch-synchronous placement) allow larger stretches. For music, $\alpha \leq 1.5\times$ gives clean results with typical grain sizes.

30Z-Transform of Allpass FilterHard

For the first-order allpass filter $H(z) = \frac{c + z^{-1}}{1 + c \cdot z^{-1}}$ with $|c| < 1$, (a) find the pole and zero locations, (b) show the magnitude response is flat, (c) derive the group delay $\tau(\omega) = -d\angle H(e^{j\omega})/d\omega$ and show it is always positive (i.e., the filter is causal and minimum phase in terms of delay).

(a) Zero: $z = -c$. Pole: $z = -1/c$. Since $|c| < 1$: the zero is inside the unit circle and the pole is outside — wait, pole at $z = -1/c$ has magnitude $1/|c| > 1$: this is outside. But then the system is unstable? Re-examine: $H(z) = \frac{c + z^{-1}}{1 + cz^{-1}} = \frac{cz + 1}{z + c}$ (multiplying by $z/z$). Pole at $z = -c$ (inside unit circle since $|c|<1$), zero at $z = -1/c$ (outside unit circle). System is stable.

(b) $|H(e^{j\omega})|^2 = H(e^{j\omega}) \overline{H(e^{j\omega})}$. Since zero is the complex conjugate reflection of the pole across the unit circle (the zero is at $-1/\bar{c}$ and pole at $-c$), the allpass property follows: the product $H(z)H(z^{-1}) = 1$ on the unit circle, giving $|H| = 1$.

Explicitly: $|c + e^{-j\omega}|^2 = (c+\cos\omega)^2 + \sin^2\omega = c^2 + 2c\cos\omega + 1$. Similarly $|1 + ce^{-j\omega}|^2 = 1 + 2c\cos\omega + c^2$. They are equal, so $|H|=1$.

(c) Phase: $\angle H = \arctan\!\left(\frac{-\sin\omega}{c+\cos\omega}\right) - \arctan\!\left(\frac{-\sin\omega}{1+c\cos\omega}\right)$. After differentiation (using the formula for allpass group delay):

$$\tau(\omega) = \frac{1 - c^2}{1 + 2c\cos\omega + c^2}$$

For $|c| < 1$: numerator $1-c^2 > 0$, denominator $= |1 + ce^{j\omega}|^2 > 0$, so $\tau(\omega) > 0$ for all $\omega$. The group delay is always positive — the filter delays all frequencies (by varying amounts), never advances them. The peak group delay is at $\omega = \pi$: $\tau(\pi) = (1-c^2)/(1-c)^2 = (1+c)/(1-c)$, which grows as $c \to 1$.
