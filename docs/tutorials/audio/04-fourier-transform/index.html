04. Fourier Transform & FFT

The Fourier Transform is one of the most powerful ideas in all of mathematics and engineering. It reveals the hidden frequency content of any signal—turning a waveform that stretches across time into a spectrum showing which pitches are present, at what amplitude, and with what phase. The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) makes this computation blazingly fast, enabling real-time spectrum analysis, audio compression, convolution-based reverb, and pitch detection. This tutorial builds the concept from intuition to rigorous definition, from Fourier series through the DFT to the Cooley-Tukey FFT algorithm.

1. The Big Idea

Press the keys C, E, and G on a piano simultaneously and you hear a C-major chord. Your auditory system does something remarkable: it decomposes that complex mixture of air pressure variations into three distinct pitches—261.6 Hz, 329.6 Hz, and 392 Hz—even though the physical signal arriving at your eardrum is a single undulating waveform. You don't hear a chord as one monolithic thing; you hear three individual notes simultaneously.

Joseph Fourier (1768–1830), investigating heat conduction, made the astonishing claim that any periodic signal can be decomposed into a (possibly infinite) sum of pure sinusoids. This is not merely an approximation—for a broad class of signals, the decomposition is exact. The sinusoids form a complete, orthogonal basis for the space of square-integrable functions, much like how $\hat{x}$, $\hat{y}$, $\hat{z}$ form a complete basis for 3-D space.

Core Insight

Every signal lives simultaneously in two equivalent worlds: the time domain (amplitude vs. time) and the frequency domain (amplitude and phase vs. frequency). The Fourier Transform is the dictionary that translates between them. No information is lost—the two representations are completely equivalent.

The analogy with light is illuminating (pun intended). White light looks featureless until a prism separates it into a rainbow of colors—each color corresponding to a distinct frequency of the electromagnetic wave. A microphone captures a single pressure waveform, but the FFT acts as an acoustic prism, revealing the spectrum of frequencies within.

2. Fourier Series

We begin with periodic signals. A signal $x(t)$ is periodic with period $T$ if $x(t + T) = x(t)$ for all $t$. The fundamental frequency is $f_0 = 1/T$ (in hertz) or $\omega_0 = 2\pi f_0$ (in radians per second).

Trigonometric Form

Fourier's claim is that any sufficiently well-behaved periodic signal can be written as:

$$x(t) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left[ a_n \cos(2\pi n f_0 t) + b_n \sin(2\pi n f_0 t) \right]$$

where the coefficients are computed by projecting $x(t)$ onto each basis function:

$$a_n = \frac{2}{T} \int_0^T x(t) \cos(2\pi n f_0 t)\, dt \qquad b_n = \frac{2}{T} \int_0^T x(t) \sin(2\pi n f_0 t)\, dt$$

These integrals work because cosines and sines at different frequencies are orthogonal: their product integrates to zero over one period. This orthogonality is the mathematical foundation that makes Fourier analysis possible.

Complex Exponential Form

Using Euler's formula $e^{j\theta} = \cos\theta + j\sin\theta$, we can combine the cosine and sine terms into a single, more elegant expression using complex exponentials:

$$\boxed{x(t) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n\, e^{j 2\pi n f_0 t}}$$

where the complex coefficients $c_n$ are:

$$c_n = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T x(t)\, e^{-j 2\pi n f_0 t}\, dt$$

The relationship between the real and complex forms is: $c_0 = a_0/2$, $c_n = (a_n - jb_n)/2$ for $n > 0$, and $c_{-n} = c_n^*$ (complex conjugate). For real signals, the spectrum is conjugate-symmetric: no information is lost by considering only $n \geq 0$.

Geometric Interpretation of $c_n$

The coefficient $c_n$ is a complex number. Its magnitude $|c_n|$ gives the amplitude of the $n$-th harmonic, and its angle $\angle c_n$ gives the phase. Think of winding the signal $x(t)$ around the complex unit circle at frequency $nf_0$: the center of mass of the resulting spiral is exactly $c_n$.

Convergence: The Dirichlet Conditions

The Fourier series converges to $x(t)$ at all points of continuity, and to the average of the left and right limits at jump discontinuities, provided $x(t)$:

  • Has a finite number of discontinuities per period
  • Has a finite number of maxima and minima per period (bounded variation)
  • Is absolutely integrable: $\int_0^T |x(t)|\, dt < \infty$

At a jump discontinuity (like the edge of a square wave), the Fourier series converges to the midpoint of the jump, and exhibits the Gibbs phenomenon: oscillations of about 9% overshoot that persist no matter how many terms are included.

3. Continuous Fourier Transform

What if the signal is not periodic? We can think of a non-periodic signal as a periodic signal whose period $T \to \infty$. As $T \to \infty$, the fundamental frequency $f_0 = 1/T \to 0$, so the discrete harmonics $\{nf_0\}$ become a continuous variable $f$, and the Fourier series sum becomes an integral.

The Continuous Fourier Transform (CFT) pair is:

$$\boxed{X(f) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)\, e^{-j 2\pi f t}\, dt} \qquad \text{(Analysis)}$$ $$x(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} X(f)\, e^{j 2\pi f t}\, df \qquad \text{(Synthesis)}$$

$X(f)$ is in general complex-valued. We decompose it as:

  • Magnitude spectrum: $|X(f)|$ — how much of each frequency is present
  • Phase spectrum: $\angle X(f)$ — the phase offset of each frequency component

Euler's Formula and the Unit Circle

The kernel $e^{-j2\pi ft}$ in the transform is a complex exponential. By Euler's formula:

$$e^{j\theta} = \cos\theta + j\sin\theta$$

This traces the unit circle in the complex plane as $\theta$ varies. When we compute $X(f)$, we multiply $x(t)$ by $e^{-j2\pi ft}$ and integrate: this is equivalent to "winding" the signal around the complex unit circle at frequency $f$ and measuring where the center of mass falls.

4. Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)

In practice, we deal with discrete-time signals: sequences of samples $x[0], x[1], \ldots, x[N-1]$ obtained by sampling at rate $f_s$. The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) is the exact analog of the CFT for finite-length discrete signals.

Definition

Let $W_N = e^{-j2\pi/N}$ be the primitive $N$-th root of unity. The DFT pair is:

$$\boxed{X[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n]\, W_N^{kn} = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n]\, e^{-j2\pi kn/N}} \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, N-1$$ $$x[n] = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} X[k]\, W_N^{-kn} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} X[k]\, e^{j2\pi kn/N} \quad n = 0, 1, \ldots, N-1$$

Frequency Bins and Resolution

The DFT computes $X[k]$ at exactly $N$ frequency bins. Each bin corresponds to a physical frequency:

$$f_k = k \cdot \frac{f_s}{N} \qquad k = 0, 1, \ldots, N-1$$

The frequency resolution (bin spacing) is:

$$\Delta f = \frac{f_s}{N} = \frac{1}{T_{record}}$$

where $T_{record} = N/f_s$ is the total recording duration. Finer frequency resolution requires longer recordings—this is a fundamental constraint, not a limitation of the FFT algorithm.

Interpretation of Bin Indices

For a real-valued signal with $N$ samples:
• Bin $k=0$: DC component (average value)
• Bins $k=1$ to $N/2-1$: positive frequencies $f_s/N$ to $f_s/2 - f_s/N$
• Bin $k=N/2$: Nyquist frequency $f_s/2$
• Bins $k=N/2+1$ to $N-1$: negative frequencies (complex conjugate mirror)
Only the first $N/2+1$ bins contain unique information for real signals.

Matrix Form of the DFT

The DFT is a linear transformation. In matrix form:

$$\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{W} \mathbf{x}$$

where $\mathbf{W}$ is the $N \times N$ DFT matrix with entries $W_{kn} = e^{-j2\pi kn/N}$. This matrix is symmetric, unitary (up to a scaling factor), and its inverse is $\mathbf{W}^* / N$. Direct matrix-vector multiplication costs $O(N^2)$ operations—the FFT exploits the structure of $\mathbf{W}$ to achieve $O(N \log N)$.

5. Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)

The DFT of $N$ points requires $N^2$ complex multiplications and additions—$O(N^2)$ complexity. For $N = 1{,}048{,}576$ (2^20, about one million), that's $10^{12}$ operations. At modern CPU speeds, this would take seconds or minutes per frame, making real-time audio processing impossible.

In 1965, James Cooley and John Tukey published an algorithm that computes the DFT in $O(N \log_2 N)$ operations. This was not entirely new (Gauss had a similar idea in 1805!), but the Cooley-Tukey publication triggered a revolution in digital signal processing. For $N = 2^{20}$, the speedup factor is roughly $N / \log_2 N \approx 50{,}000\times$.

The Divide-and-Conquer Idea

When $N$ is even, split the input into even-indexed and odd-indexed samples:

$$X[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n] W_N^{kn} = \underbrace{\sum_{m=0}^{N/2-1} x[2m] W_{N/2}^{km}}_{X_{\text{even}}[k]} + W_N^k \underbrace{\sum_{m=0}^{N/2-1} x[2m+1] W_{N/2}^{km}}_{X_{\text{odd}}[k]}$$

Crucially, $W_N^{2} = e^{-j4\pi/N} = e^{-j2\pi/(N/2)} = W_{N/2}$, so both sums are $(N/2)$-point DFTs. Using the periodicity of $W_{N/2}^k$ (period $N/2$), we need only compute the two half-size DFTs once and combine them for all $k$:

$$X[k] = X_{\text{even}}[k] + W_N^k \cdot X_{\text{odd}}[k]$$ $$X[k + N/2] = X_{\text{even}}[k] - W_N^k \cdot X_{\text{odd}}[k]$$

This 2-element combination—one complex multiply plus two adds—is called a butterfly. Apply this recursively ($\log_2 N$ stages, each with $N/2$ butterflies) and the total cost is $\frac{N}{2}\log_2 N$ complex multiplications.

Complexity Analysis

At each of the $\log_2 N$ stages, there are $N/2$ butterfly operations, each requiring 1 complex multiplication and 2 complex additions. Total:

$$\text{Multiplications:} \quad \frac{N}{2}\log_2 N \qquad \text{Additions:} \quad N\log_2 N$$
N DFT: $O(N^2)$ FFT: $O(N\log N)$ Speedup
64 4,096 192 21×
1,024 1,048,576 5,120 205×
16,384 268 million 114,688 2,341×
1,048,576 $10^{12}$ 10,485,760 ~100,000×

6. Inverse FFT

The Inverse DFT (IDFT) reconstructs the time-domain signal from its spectrum:

$$\boxed{x[n] = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} X[k]\, e^{j2\pi kn/N}}$$

Remarkably, the IDFT has nearly identical structure to the forward DFT—the only differences are the sign of the exponent (positive instead of negative) and the $1/N$ normalization factor. This means the same FFT butterfly code can compute the IDFT:

  1. Take the complex conjugate of the input spectrum: $X^*[k]$
  2. Apply the forward FFT to get $\widetilde{x}[n]$
  3. Take the complex conjugate again and divide by $N$: $x[n] = \widetilde{x}^*[n] / N$

This conjugate-FFT-conjugate trick is used in many efficient implementations. Alternatively, many libraries provide ifft() directly.

The Round-Trip

For any finite-energy signal: $\text{IDFT}(\text{DFT}(x)) = x$. This perfect reconstruction is what makes the FFT so powerful for audio processing: you can transform to the frequency domain, modify the spectrum (e.g., boost bass, remove noise), and transform back to get the modified audio with no artifacts (assuming the IDFT is exact, i.e., no quantization or truncation).

7. Properties of the Fourier Transform

The Fourier Transform satisfies a rich set of properties that make it enormously useful. We state them for the DFT (discrete case), but they hold analogously for the continuous transform.

Linearity

$\mathcal{F}\{ax + by\} = aX + bY$

The DFT is a linear map. Superposition holds in both domains.

Time Shift

$x[n-n_0] \longleftrightarrow W_N^{kn_0} X[k]$

A time delay of $n_0$ samples multiplies the spectrum by $e^{-j2\pi kn_0/N}$. Magnitudes unchanged; phases shift linearly.

Frequency Shift (Modulation)

$W_N^{-k_0 n} x[n] \longleftrightarrow X[k - k_0]$

Multiplying by a complex exponential in time shifts the spectrum in frequency. Basis of amplitude modulation.

Convolution Theorem

$x * h \longleftrightarrow X \cdot H$

Circular convolution in time = pointwise multiplication in frequency. This is why FFT-based filtering is $O(N\log N)$ instead of $O(N^2)$.

Parseval's Theorem

$\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}|x[n]|^2 = \dfrac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}|X[k]|^2$

Total signal energy is preserved across transform pairs. Power in time = power in frequency (up to scaling).

Conjugate Symmetry

$X[N-k] = X^*[k]$ for real $x[n]$

Spectrum of a real signal is conjugate-symmetric. Only $N/2+1$ bins are independent.

Time Reversal

$x[-n] \longleftrightarrow X^*[k]$ (real $x$: $X[N-k]$)

Reversing signal in time conjugates its spectrum. Phase negates; magnitude unchanged.

Duality

$X[n] \longleftrightarrow N \cdot x[-k]$

If $x[n]$ has DFT $X[k]$, then treating $X[n]$ as a signal gives $N\,x[-k]$. Time and frequency are interchangeable.

The Convolution Theorem: Deep Dive

The convolution theorem is perhaps the most practically important property. The circular convolution of two $N$-point sequences is:

$$(x \circledast h)[n] = \sum_{m=0}^{N-1} x[m]\, h[(n-m) \bmod N]$$

And the theorem states:

$$\mathcal{F}\{x \circledast h\} = X[k] \cdot H[k]$$

Parseval's Theorem

Parseval's theorem states that the DFT is an energy-preserving map (an isometry, up to scaling):

$$\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}|x[n]|^2 = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}|X[k]|^2$$

The left side is the signal energy in the time domain; the right side is the total energy summed over all frequency bins. This means the power spectrum $|X[k]|^2/N$ correctly represents how much energy is contributed by each frequency component. Parseval's theorem is crucial for power spectral density estimation and for proving that the DFT doesn't create or destroy energy.

8. Practical FFT Usage

Frequency Resolution and Zero-Padding

The frequency resolution of the DFT is $\Delta f = f_s / N$. To improve resolution, you can either:

  • Record longer: more samples at the same rate $\Rightarrow$ larger $N$ $\Rightarrow$ smaller $\Delta f$. This is the only way to get true resolution.
  • Zero-padding: append zeros to the signal before taking the FFT. This interpolates the spectrum, making it smoother, but does NOT add new information. It's like zooming in on an image—you get more pixels but not more detail.

Zero-padding is useful for: (1) making $N$ a power of 2 for efficiency, (2) smoother spectral displays, and (3) implementing linear (non-circular) convolution by padding each signal to length $\geq L + M - 1$.

Windowing

The DFT implicitly assumes the $N$-sample signal is one period of a periodic signal. If the signal doesn't start and end at the same value (which is almost always the case for a windowed segment of audio), the abrupt edges create artificial discontinuities that cause spectral leakage: energy from a single frequency "leaks" into neighboring bins.

The solution is to apply a window function $w[n]$ that smoothly tapers the signal to zero at both ends before taking the FFT:

$$X_{\text{windowed}}[k] = \text{FFT}\{x[n] \cdot w[n]\}$$

Common Window Functions

Window Formula Main Lobe Width Peak Sidelobe Use Case
Rectangular $w[n] = 1$ $4\pi/N$ −13 dB Rarely (worst leakage)
Hann $0.5(1-\cos(2\pi n/N))$ $8\pi/N$ −31 dB General audio analysis
Hamming $0.54 - 0.46\cos(2\pi n/N)$ $8\pi/N$ −41 dB Speech processing
Blackman $0.42 - 0.5\cos(\cdot) + 0.08\cos(2\cdot)$ $12\pi/N$ −58 dB High dynamic range audio
Kaiser Parameterized ($\beta$) Variable Variable Filter design, optimal tradeoff

Important: When using a window for a power spectrum estimate, you must compensate for the amplitude loss introduced by the window. The coherent gain of the Hann window is 0.5, so multiply $|X[k]|$ by $2/N$ (instead of $1/N$) to get correct amplitude scaling for single tones. Use $|X[k]|^2 \cdot (2/N^2) / \sum w[n]^2$ for power spectral density.

9. Applications in Audio

Spectrum Analyzers

A real-time spectrum analyzer takes successive overlapping frames of audio (typically using 50% overlap), applies a window, computes the FFT, and displays $|X[k]|^2$. The Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) is the mathematical framework:

$$\text{STFT}\{x\}(m, k) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n + mH] \cdot w[n] \cdot e^{-j2\pi kn/N}$$

where $m$ is the frame index and $H$ is the hop size (frame step). Setting $H = N/2$ gives 50% overlap.

Pitch Detection via FFT

For a quasi-periodic audio signal (speech, music), the FFT reveals the fundamental frequency $f_0$ as the peak with the lowest frequency (or as the GCD of visible harmonics). The Harmonic Product Spectrum (HPS) method downsamples the spectrum multiple times and multiplies, reinforcing the true fundamental while suppressing harmonics:

$$\text{HPS}[k] = \prod_{r=1}^{R} |X[r \cdot k]|$$

Equalization (EQ)

A graphic EQ boosts or cuts specific frequency bands. Using the convolution theorem, this is implemented as: FFT the audio, multiply by the desired frequency response $H[k]$ (e.g., 1.5 for +3 dB in the bass band, 0.7 for −3 dB in the midrange), then IFFT. This is called fast convolution and is how reverb plugins, noise cancellation, and many other effects work.

Audio Compression

MP3 and AAC use frequency-domain transforms (MDCT, a variant of the DCT) to convert audio frames to the frequency domain, then apply psychoacoustic masking: loud frequencies mask nearby quieter ones, so less precision is needed for masked components. This allows 10:1 compression while preserving perceptual quality.

Exercises: Fourier Transform & FFT

Easy (1–10)

1DFT of a ConstantEasy

Compute the 4-point DFT of $x[n] = [3, 3, 3, 3]$.

$X[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{3} 3 \cdot e^{-j2\pi kn/4}$. For $k=0$: $X[0] = 12$. For $k \neq 0$: the complex exponentials sum to zero.

$$X = [12, 0, 0, 0]$$

A constant signal has all its energy at DC (frequency 0).

2DFT of an ImpulseEasy

Compute the 4-point DFT of $x[n] = [1, 0, 0, 0]$.

$X[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{3} x[n] e^{-j2\pi kn/4} = 1 \cdot e^0 = 1$ for all $k$.

$$X = [1, 1, 1, 1]$$

An impulse contains all frequencies equally — flat spectrum.

3Frequency Bin SpacingEasy

With $f_s = 48000$ Hz and $N = 1024$, what is the frequency spacing between adjacent DFT bins? What frequency does bin $k = 100$ correspond to?

$\Delta f = f_s/N = 48000/1024 = 46.875$ Hz. Bin 100: $100 \times 46.875 = 4687.5$ Hz.

4FFT Size for Frequency ResolutionEasy

You need to distinguish two tones 5 Hz apart at $f_s = 44100$ Hz. What is the minimum FFT size (power of 2)?

Need $\Delta f \leq 5$ Hz. $N \geq 44100/5 = 8820$. Next power of 2: $N = 16384$.

5FFT Computational SavingsEasy

Compare the number of complex multiplications for a direct DFT vs. FFT with $N = 4096$.

DFT: $N^2 = 4096^2 = 16{,}777{,}216$. FFT: $\frac{N}{2}\log_2 N = 2048 \times 12 = 24{,}576$.

Speedup: $16{,}777{,}216 / 24{,}576 \approx 683\times$ faster!

6Magnitude and PhaseEasy

A DFT bin has value $X[k] = 3 + 4j$. What are the magnitude and phase?

$|X[k]| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{25} = 5$. Phase: $\angle X[k] = \arctan(4/3) \approx 53.1° \approx 0.927$ rad.

7Nyquist BinEasy

In a 512-point FFT at 44100 Hz, which bin index corresponds to the Nyquist frequency? What frequency is that?

Nyquist bin: $N/2 = 256$. Frequency: $f_s/2 = 22050$ Hz. Bins 0 through 256 are the unique frequency bins (257 total).

8Parseval's Theorem CheckEasy

Given $x = [1, 2, 3, 4]$, verify Parseval's theorem: $\sum|x[n]|^2 = \frac{1}{N}\sum|X[k]|^2$.

Left: $1 + 4 + 9 + 16 = 30$. DFT: $X = [10, -2+2j, -2, -2-2j]$.

$|X|^2 = [100, 8, 4, 8]$. Sum $= 120$. Right: $120/4 = 30$. ✔

9Real Signal SymmetryEasy

For a real-valued signal of length $N$, what is the relationship between $X[k]$ and $X[N-k]$?

$X[N-k] = X^*[k]$ (complex conjugate). This means $|X[N-k]| = |X[k]|$ (symmetric magnitude) and $\angle X[N-k] = -\angle X[k]$ (antisymmetric phase). This is why only bins 0 to $N/2$ contain unique information for real signals.

10Identify Frequency ComponentsEasy

A 1024-point FFT at 44100 Hz shows peaks at bins 10, 20, and 30. What are the corresponding frequencies? What musical relationship do they have?

$\Delta f = 44100/1024 = 43.07$ Hz. Frequencies: $430.7, 861.3, 1292.0$ Hz.

These are in ratio 1:2:3 — the fundamental and its 2nd and 3rd harmonics.

Medium (11–20)

11DFT by HandMedium

Compute the 4-point DFT of $x = [1, 0, -1, 0]$ by hand.

$W_4 = e^{-j\pi/2} = -j$. So $W_4^0=1, W_4^1=-j, W_4^2=-1, W_4^3=j$.

$X[0] = 1 + 0 + (-1) + 0 = 0$

$X[1] = 1 + 0\cdot(-j) + (-1)(-1) + 0\cdot(j) = 1 + 1 = 2$

$X[2] = 1 + 0\cdot(-1) + (-1)(1) + 0\cdot(-1) = 0$

$X[3] = 1 + 0\cdot(j) + (-1)(-1) + 0\cdot(-j) = 2$

$$X = [0, 2, 0, 2]$$

12Fourier Coefficients of Square WaveMedium

Derive the Fourier series coefficients for a square wave of amplitude $A$ and period $T$. Show that only odd harmonics are present.

Square wave: $f(t) = A$ for $0 < t < T/2$, $f(t) = -A$ for $T/2 < t < T$. The function has odd symmetry about $T/4$, so $a_n = 0$ for all $n$.

$$b_n = \frac{2}{T}\int_0^T f(t)\sin(n\omega_0 t)\,dt = \frac{4A}{n\pi}$$ for odd $n$, and $0$ for even $n$.

$$f(t) = \frac{4A}{\pi}\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{\sin((2k+1)\omega_0 t)}{2k+1}$$

Only odd harmonics ($f_0, 3f_0, 5f_0, \ldots$) with amplitudes decaying as $1/n$.

13Convolution Theorem ApplicationMedium

Signal $x = [1, 2, 3]$ is convolved with $h = [1, 1]$. Compute using (a) direct convolution and (b) the DFT convolution theorem. Verify they match.

(a) Direct: $y[0] = 1, y[1] = 2+1 = 3, y[2] = 3+2 = 5, y[3] = 3$. So $y = [1, 3, 5, 3]$.

(b) Zero-pad both to length 4: $x' = [1,2,3,0]$, $h' = [1,1,0,0]$. DFT: $X = [6, -2+2j, -2, -2-2j]$, $H = [2, 0, 0+0j, 0]$... Actually, let me compute properly:

$H[0] = 2, H[1] = 1+(-j) = 1-j, H[2] = 0, H[3] = 1+j$.

$Y = X \cdot H$: $Y[0] = 12, Y[1] = (-2+2j)(1-j) = -2+2j+2j-2j^2 = 0+4j, Y[2] = 0, Y[3] = (-2-2j)(1+j) = -2-2j-2j-2j^2 = 0-4j$.

IDFT of $[12, 4j, 0, -4j]$: $y = [1, 3, 5, 3]$ ✔

14Windowing EffectMedium

Explain why multiplying a signal by a window function in the time domain is equivalent to convolution in the frequency domain. What does this imply for spectral analysis?

By the convolution theorem: $\mathcal{F}\{x \cdot w\} = X * W$ (convolution in frequency). The window's spectrum $W(f)$ is convolved with the signal's spectrum.

A rectangular window has a sinc-like spectrum with wide side lobes, which smears (leaks) energy. A Hann or Hamming window has much lower side lobes, reducing leakage at the cost of a wider main lobe (reduced frequency resolution).

Implication: every spectral peak in the FFT output is actually the true spectrum convolved with the window's Fourier transform. No window is "transparent" — you always see the signal through the window's spectral shape.

15Zero-PaddingMedium

You have 100 samples of a signal. You compute a 128-point FFT and then a 1024-point FFT (with zero-padding). Does the 1024-point FFT reveal more frequencies? Explain.

No. Frequency resolution is determined by the signal duration (100 samples), not the FFT size. Zero-padding interpolates between the frequency bins (giving a smoother spectrum), but doesn't add new information. The minimum resolvable frequency difference remains $\Delta f = f_s/100$.

Zero-padding is useful for: (1) making peaks easier to locate, (2) achieving power-of-2 FFT sizes for efficiency, (3) visualizing the spectrum more smoothly.

16Time-Shift PropertyMedium

If $x[n]$ has DFT $X[k]$, show that delaying by $m$ samples gives $X[k] \cdot e^{-j2\pi km/N}$. What happens to the magnitude spectrum?

$\text{DFT}\{x[n-m]\} = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n-m] e^{-j2\pi kn/N}$. Let $p = n-m$:

$= \sum_{p} x[p] e^{-j2\pi k(p+m)/N} = e^{-j2\pi km/N} \sum_p x[p] e^{-j2\pi kp/N} = e^{-j2\pi km/N} X[k]$

The magnitude $|X[k]|$ is unchanged — a time shift only affects phase, not magnitude. This is why magnitude spectrograms are time-shift invariant.

17Power SpectrumMedium

A 1024-point FFT of white noise at 44100 Hz has an average $|X[k]|^2 = 500$ for all bins. What is the total signal power? What is the power spectral density?

By Parseval: total power $= \frac{1}{N}\sum|X[k]|^2 = \frac{1}{1024} \times 1024 \times 500 = 500$.

PSD: power per Hz $= |X[k]|^2 / (N \cdot \Delta f) = 500/(1024 \times 43.07) = 0.01133$ per Hz. Or equivalently: total power / bandwidth $= 500/22050 = 0.02268$ (single-sided).

18Fourier Transform of CosineMedium

A signal $x(t) = \cos(2\pi \cdot 1000t)$ is sampled at 8000 Hz for 0.5 seconds. At which DFT bin(s) will energy appear?

$N = 8000 \times 0.5 = 4000$ samples. $\Delta f = 8000/4000 = 2$ Hz. The 1000 Hz tone falls at bin $k = 1000/2 = 500$.

Since $\cos(\omega t) = \frac{1}{2}(e^{j\omega t} + e^{-j\omega t})$, energy appears at bins 500 and $N - 500 = 3500$ (the conjugate mirror). Each has magnitude $N/2 = 2000$.

19Butterfly OperationMedium

Draw the butterfly diagram for a radix-2 DIT FFT of length $N = 8$. How many stages and how many butterflies per stage?

Stages: $\log_2(8) = 3$. Butterflies per stage: $N/2 = 4$. Total butterflies: $3 \times 4 = 12$.

Each butterfly: inputs $a, b$; outputs $a + W_N^r b$ and $a - W_N^r b$ where $r$ is the twiddle factor index.

Stage 1: 4 butterflies with $W_8^0$. Stage 2: 2 groups of 2 butterflies with $W_8^0, W_8^2$. Stage 3: 1 group of 4 butterflies with $W_8^0, W_8^1, W_8^2, W_8^3$.

Input order (bit-reversed): $x[0], x[4], x[2], x[6], x[1], x[5], x[3], x[7]$.

20Circular vs. Linear ConvolutionMedium

Explain the difference between circular and linear convolution. How do you use the DFT (which performs circular convolution) to compute linear convolution correctly?

DFT multiplication performs circular convolution (the signals wrap around). Linear convolution of signals of length $M$ and $L$ produces output of length $M + L - 1$.

To get linear convolution from the DFT: (1) Zero-pad both signals to length $\geq M + L - 1$. (2) FFT both. (3) Multiply. (4) IFFT. The zero-padding prevents wrap-around aliasing.

Common choice: pad to the next power of 2 $\geq M + L - 1$ for FFT efficiency.

21Prove the Convolution TheoremHard

Prove that $\text{DFT}\{x \circledast h\} = X[k] \cdot H[k]$ where $\circledast$ denotes circular convolution.

Let $y[n] = \sum_{m=0}^{N-1} x[m] h[(n-m) \bmod N]$.

$Y[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} y[n] W_N^{kn} = \sum_n \sum_m x[m] h[(n-m) \bmod N] W_N^{kn}$

Substitute $p = (n-m) \bmod N$, so $n = (m+p) \bmod N$:

$= \sum_m x[m] W_N^{km} \sum_p h[p] W_N^{kp} = X[k] \cdot H[k]$ □

22FFT Complexity AnalysisHard

The radix-2 Cooley-Tukey FFT splits an $N$-point DFT into two $N/2$-point DFTs. Write the recurrence relation for the number of complex multiplications and solve it to show $T(N) = O(N\log N)$.

The FFT computes: $X[k] = E[k] + W_N^k O[k]$ and $X[k+N/2] = E[k] - W_N^k O[k]$, where $E$ and $O$ are the DFTs of even- and odd-indexed samples.

Multiplications: two FFTs of size $N/2$ plus $N/2$ twiddle multiplications:

$T(N) = 2T(N/2) + N/2$, with $T(1) = 0$.

By the Master Theorem (case 2): $T(N) = \frac{N}{2}\log_2 N = O(N\log N)$.

Explicitly: at each of $\log_2 N$ stages, there are $N/2$ butterflies, each requiring 1 complex multiplication. Total: $\frac{N}{2}\log_2 N$.

23Goertzel AlgorithmHard

The Goertzel algorithm computes a single DFT bin in $O(N)$ time. Derive the 2nd-order IIR filter form: $s[n] = x[n] + 2\cos(2\pi k/N)s[n-1] - s[n-2]$, with $X[k] = s[N] - W_N^k s[N-1]$. When is Goertzel more efficient than FFT?

$X[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n] W_N^{kn}$. Multiply by $W_N^{-kN} = 1$:

$X[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n] W_N^{-k(N-n)} = \left.y[n]\right|_{n=N}$ where $y[n] = \sum_{m=0}^{N-1} x[m] W_N^{-k(n-m)}$.

This is a convolution with filter $h[n] = W_N^{-kn} u[n]$, whose z-transform is $H(z) = \frac{1}{1 - W_N^{-k}z^{-1}}$.

To avoid complex arithmetic, combine with conjugate pole: $H(z) = \frac{1 - W_N^k z^{-1}}{1 - 2\cos(2\pi k/N)z^{-1} + z^{-2}}$.

The denominator gives the recurrence $s[n] = x[n] + 2\cos(2\pi k/N)s[n-1] - s[n-2]$, using only real multiplications. The final output uses one complex multiply.

Goertzel beats FFT when you need fewer than $\sim \log_2 N$ bins. For DTMF detection (8 bins from 205 samples), Goertzel is clearly better.

24Overlap-Save MethodHard

Describe the overlap-save method for efficiently convolving a very long signal with a short FIR filter of length $M$ using block FFTs of size $N$. How many output samples per block are valid? What is the computational advantage over direct convolution?

Method: (1) Divide input into overlapping blocks of $N$ samples, each overlapping by $M-1$ samples with the previous. (2) FFT each block and the zero-padded filter. (3) Multiply. (4) IFFT. (5) Discard the first $M-1$ samples of each output block (they're corrupted by circular wrap-around).

Valid samples per block: $N - M + 1$. Choose $N \gg M$ (e.g., $N = 4M$ or more).

Cost per output sample: FFT method $\approx \frac{N\log_2 N}{N - M + 1}$ multiplications. Direct: $M$ multiplications per sample. FFT wins when $\log_2 N \ll M$ (true for $M \gtrsim 64$).

25DFT of a Windowed SinusoidHard

Derive the DFT magnitude of a length-$N$ sinusoid $x[n] = \sin(2\pi f_0 n/f_s)$ when $f_0$ does not land exactly on a bin center. Express the result in terms of the Dirichlet kernel.

$x[n] = \frac{1}{2j}(e^{j2\pi f_0 n/f_s} - e^{-j2\pi f_0 n/f_s})$. Let $k_0 = Nf_0/f_s$ (not necessarily integer).

$X[k] = \frac{1}{2j}\left[D_N(k - k_0) - D_N(k + k_0)\right]$

where $D_N(\alpha) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} e^{-j2\pi(k - k_0)n/N} = e^{-j\pi(N-1)\alpha/N} \frac{\sin(\pi\alpha)}{\sin(\pi\alpha/N)}$ is the Dirichlet kernel.

$|D_N(\alpha)| = \left|\frac{\sin(\pi\alpha)}{\sin(\pi\alpha/N)}\right|$. When $k_0$ is integer, this is $N$ at $k = k_0$ and 0 elsewhere. When $k_0$ is non-integer, the sinc-like envelope creates spectral leakage — energy spreads to neighboring bins.

26Pitch Detection via AutocorrelationHard

The autocorrelation $r[l] = \sum_n x[n]x[n+l]$ can be computed efficiently via FFT using $r = \text{IFFT}(|X|^2)$. For pitch detection, we find the lag $l^*$ of the first strong peak after $l=0$. The fundamental frequency is $f_0 = f_s/l^*$. Design a pitch detector for the range 80–1000 Hz at $f_s = 16000$ Hz.

Lag search range: $l_{\min} = f_s/f_{\max} = 16000/1000 = 16$ samples. $l_{\max} = f_s/f_{\min} = 16000/80 = 200$ samples.

Algorithm: (1) Window frame (~25 ms = 400 samples). (2) FFT (zero-pad to 1024). (3) Compute $|X[k]|^2$. (4) IFFT to get autocorrelation. (5) Find the maximum of $r[l]$ for $16 \leq l \leq 200$. (6) $f_0 = 16000/l^*$.

Resolution: at $l = 16$ (1000 Hz), changing lag by 1 changes frequency by $16000/17 - 16000/16 = 941 - 1000 = 59$ Hz — poor resolution at high frequencies. At $l = 200$ (80 Hz): $16000/201 - 16000/200 = 0.4$ Hz — excellent at low frequencies. This asymmetry is inherent to time-domain pitch detection.

Enhancement: parabolic interpolation around the autocorrelation peak for sub-sample lag accuracy.

27Discrete Cosine TransformHard

The Type-II DCT is defined as $C[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n] \cos\!\left(\frac{\pi(2n+1)k}{2N}\right)$. Show that the DCT is equivalent to the DFT of a symmetrically extended signal and explain why it has better energy compaction than the DFT for typical audio signals.

Construct $y[n]$ of length $2N$ by: $y[n] = x[n]$ for $0 \leq n < N$ and $y[2N-1-n] = x[n]$ (mirror). The DFT of $y$ yields: $Y[k] = e^{-j\pi k/(2N)} \times 2\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n]\cos(\pi(2n+1)k/(2N))$.

So $C[k] = \text{Re}(Y[k] e^{j\pi k/(2N)})/2$ — the DCT is essentially the real part of a DFT of a symmetric extension.

Energy compaction: Audio signals have strong sample-to-sample correlation (smooth waveforms). The DCT's basis functions (cosines) closely match the eigenvectors of the autocorrelation matrix for first-order Markov processes. This means most energy concentrates in the first few DCT coefficients — far better than DFT, which spreads energy across both real and imaginary parts. This is why JPEG uses DCT, and why MDCT (a variant) is the core of MP3 and AAC.

28Chirp Z-TransformHard

The Chirp Z-Transform (CZT) evaluates the Z-transform on any contour $z_k = AW^{-k}$ for $k = 0, \ldots, M-1$, not just the unit circle. Explain how this can be used to "zoom in" on a narrow frequency band with high resolution, and describe the computational trick that keeps it $O(N\log N)$.

Set $A = e^{j2\pi f_1/f_s}$ (start frequency) and $W = e^{-j2\pi(f_2-f_1)/(Mf_s)}$ (frequency step). The CZT then evaluates the spectrum at $M$ equally spaced points between $f_1$ and $f_2$.

Key identity (Bluestein): $kn = \frac{1}{2}[k^2 + n^2 - (k-n)^2]$. This converts the sum $\sum x[n]W^{kn}$ into a convolution, computable via FFT.

$X_{\text{CZT}}[k] = W^{k^2/2} \sum_n [x[n] A^{-n} W^{n^2/2}] \cdot W^{-(k-n)^2/2}$

This is a convolution of two sequences, computable with 3 FFTs of size $\geq N + M - 1$. Total: $O((N+M)\log(N+M))$.

Use case: you want 1000 frequency points between 440 and 880 Hz. FFT would require $N > f_s/0.44 \approx 100{,}000$ points. CZT does it with any $N$ and $M = 1000$.

29Uncertainty Principle from DFTHard

Define the "spread" of a discrete signal in time as $\sigma_n^2 = \sum n^2 |x[n]|^2 / \sum |x[n]|^2$ (centered at 0) and similarly $\sigma_k^2$ in frequency. Prove that $\sigma_n \cdot \sigma_k \geq 1/(4\pi)$ cannot be achieved exactly in the discrete case but is approached by a sampled Gaussian.

The continuous uncertainty principle $\sigma_t \sigma_f \geq 1/(4\pi)$ is achieved exactly by the Gaussian $g(t) = e^{-\alpha t^2}$.

In the discrete case, the Gaussian $x[n] = e^{-\alpha n^2}$ has Fourier transform that is also approximately Gaussian (exact only for infinite length). The product $\sigma_n \sigma_k > 1/(4\pi)$ strictly, because:

(1) Truncation to finite length increases spread. (2) Aliasing from sampling in both domains adds uncertainty. (3) The discrete Fourier transform operates on a periodic (circular) domain, which fundamentally changes the geometry.

The minimum discrete uncertainty product approaches $1/(4\pi)$ as $N \to \infty$ with properly chosen $\alpha \propto 1/N$, but never exactly equals it. For practical window design, the discrete Gaussian remains the best choice for minimizing time-frequency spread.

30Spectral Analysis System DesignHard

Design a complete real-time spectrum analyzer for a mixing console: $f_s = 48000$ Hz, 31-band (1/3 octave from 20 Hz to 20 kHz), update rate 30 fps, latency < 50 ms. Choose between FFT, filterbank, or CQT approach and justify all parameters.

Approach: FFT with grouping into 1/3 octave bands.

For 20 Hz resolution at the low end: $N \geq 48000/20 = 2400$. Use $N = 4096$ ($\Delta f = 11.7$ Hz). Window: Hann. Hop: $48000/30 = 1600$ samples for 30 fps update.

Latency: window length = $4096/48000 = 85$ ms — exceeds 50 ms spec!

Better: Multi-rate approach. Split into octave bands with a tree of halfband filters (efficient!). Each octave halves the sample rate. At the lowest octave (20–40 Hz), $f_s = 375$ Hz, and a 256-point FFT gives $\Delta f = 1.46$ Hz — more than enough. Higher octaves use smaller FFTs at proportionally higher rates.

This gives logarithmic frequency resolution naturally, latency proportional to the period of the lowest band (~50 ms for 20 Hz), and computational cost of $O(N)$ total across all bands per update — far more efficient than a single giant FFT.
