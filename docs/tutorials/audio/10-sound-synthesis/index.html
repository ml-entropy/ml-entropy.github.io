What is Synthesis?

Sound synthesis is the generation of audio from mathematical models rather than recordings. Instead of replaying a sampled piano, a synthesizer computes the waveform in real time from equations — giving the musician complete control over every parameter of the sound.

The history stretches from the Theremin (1920), through Robert Moog's voltage-controlled synthesizers (1964) that defined the sound of progressive rock, to Yamaha's FM DX7 (1983) which sold over 200,000 units and is still the best-selling synthesizer of all time, to today's wavetable softsynths running in browsers with Web Audio API.

Why Synthesize Rather Than Sample?

Control: Every parameter is continuously adjustable — pitch, timbre, dynamics, spatial position.
Infinite variation: The same patch never plays identically twice (with modulation).
Efficiency: Complex, evolving sounds require only a few kilobytes of parameters, not gigabytes of samples.
Novel timbres: Synthesis can produce sounds that do not exist in nature.

Additive Synthesis

By Fourier's theorem, any periodic sound is a sum of sinusoids. Additive synthesis makes this explicit: you control each harmonic independently.

$$y(t) = \sum_{k=1}^{N} A_k \sin(2\pi f_k t + \phi_k)$$

where $A_k$ is the amplitude, $f_k$ the frequency, and $\phi_k$ the phase of the $k$-th partial. For harmonic sounds (strings, voice) the partials are integer multiples of the fundamental: $f_k = k \cdot f_0$. For inharmonic sounds (bells, gongs), the partials are non-integer multiples.

Classic organs are nearly pure additive synthesizers — each drawbar adds a harmonic of a specific amplitude. The Hammond B3's nine drawbars correspond to 16', 8', 4', 2-2/3', 2', 1-3/5', 1-1/3', 1', and a mixture, spanning suboctave to fifth harmonic.

Computational Cost

Additive synthesis gives perfect spectral control but requires $N$ oscillators — one per partial. For a violin tone with 40 harmonics evolving over time, this means 40 sine functions per sample, each with its own amplitude envelope. Modern CPUs handle this easily, but the complexity of specifying all parameters is high. Resynthesis tools (like Audiosculpt or Spear) analyze real sounds and extract the partials, enabling analysis-resynthesis.

Subtractive Synthesis

Subtractive synthesis begins with a harmonically rich waveform and sculpts it by removing frequencies with filters. The principle mirrors the vocal tract: the larynx produces a buzzy glottal pulse (rich harmonic source), and the mouth/pharynx cavities act as resonant filters that emphasize certain frequencies (formants), producing vowel sounds.

The classic chain — Oscillator → Filter → Amplifier — maps to VCO → VCF → VCA in voltage-controlled analog terminology:

  • VCO (Voltage-Controlled Oscillator): Generates the raw waveform. Sawtooth waves contain all harmonics ($1/n$ amplitude at $n$-th harmonic). Square waves contain only odd harmonics. Pulse waves have variable duty cycle for timbre control.
  • VCF (Voltage-Controlled Filter): Typically a resonant lowpass filter with cutoff frequency $f_c$ and resonance $Q$. The Moog ladder filter with 24 dB/octave slope became iconic. At high $Q$, the filter self-oscillates, producing a pure sine wave at $f_c$.
  • VCA (Voltage-Controlled Amplifier): Shapes the amplitude over time via the ADSR envelope.

FM Synthesis

Frequency Modulation (FM) synthesis, developed by John Chowning at Stanford in 1967 and commercialized by Yamaha in the DX7 (1983), generates rich spectra from just two oscillators using a simple but powerful equation:

$$y(t) = A\sin\!\bigl(2\pi f_c t + \beta\sin(2\pi f_m t)\bigr)$$

Here $f_c$ is the carrier frequency (determines pitch), $f_m$ is the modulator frequency, and $\beta$ is the modulation index (determines brightness and spectral richness).

Using the Jacobi-Anger expansion, the FM signal decomposes into sidebands:

$$y(t) = A \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} J_n(\beta)\sin(2\pi(f_c + nf_m)t)$$

where $J_n(\beta)$ is the $n$-th Bessel function of the first kind. The sidebands appear at $f_c \pm f_m$, $f_c \pm 2f_m$, $f_c \pm 3f_m$, … With $\beta = 0$ you get a pure sine. As $\beta$ increases, more sidebands emerge — the sound brightens dramatically. This explains the DX7's ability to produce electric pianos, metallic bells, and harsh brasses from the same two-operator architecture.

Carrier-to-Modulator Ratio

The ratio $f_c : f_m$ controls the harmonic structure. For integer ratios (1:2, 1:3, 2:1), all sidebands are harmonic and the sound is musical. Non-integer ratios (1:1.414, 2:3.5) produce inharmonic spectra — metallic, bell-like, or percussive tones. The DX7's 6 operators can be connected in 32 different algorithms, enabling both additive and FM combinations.

Wavetable Synthesis

Wavetable synthesis stores one complete cycle of a waveform in a lookup table (wavetable). The oscillator reads through the table at a rate proportional to the desired frequency. To produce note $f$ from a table of $N$ samples at sample rate $f_s$, the read increment per sample is:

$$\Delta_{\text{phase}} = \frac{f \cdot N}{f_s}$$

Linear interpolation between table entries avoids artifacts from fractional indices. The real power comes from having multiple wavetables — one for a sine, one for a saw, one for a complex modeled timbre — and morphing between them over time. This is the basis of Waldorf's Wave and PPG synthesizers, and virtually all modern softsynths.

Granular Synthesis

Granular synthesis treats audio as a cloud of tiny grains, typically 1–100 ms long. Each grain is a short audio snippet (either synthesized or sampled) multiplied by a smooth amplitude envelope (Gaussian, Hann, trapezoid) to avoid clicks. Thousands of overlapping grains, scattered in time and pitch, create complex textures:

  • Time stretching without pitch shift: Play grains from a fixed position in the source file but space them further apart in time. This is how Paulstretch (used in extremely slow covers) works.
  • Pitch shift without time stretching: Change the playback speed of grains but reschedule them to maintain the original tempo.
  • Stochastic clouds: Randomize grain position, pitch, duration, and pan to create atmospheric textures — whirring, whispering, shimmering.

The density parameter $D$ (grains per second) and grain duration $\tau$ together determine overlap: for smooth textures, $D \cdot \tau > 1$ (grains overlap). For choppy, rhythmic effects, $D \cdot \tau < 1$.

Physical Modeling

Physical modeling synthesizes sound by simulating the physics of the instrument itself. The simplest and most elegant example is the Karplus-Strong algorithm for plucked strings:

$$y[n] = y[n - L] \times g$$

where $L$ is the delay line length (sets pitch: $f_0 \approx f_s / L$), and $g$ is a feedback gain slightly below 1. In the full algorithm, a single-pole lowpass filter is placed in the feedback loop to simulate the string's energy loss preferentially at high frequencies:

$$y[n] = \frac{g}{2}\bigl(y[n-L] + y[n-L-1]\bigr)$$

The lowpass filter $H(z) = \frac{1}{2}(1 + z^{-1})$ smooths the spectrum, causing higher harmonics to decay faster — exactly like a real string where bending stiffness and air resistance preferentially damp high-frequency modes. The initial condition (the "pluck") is a burst of noise or a specific shape in the delay line.

More sophisticated physical models — digital waveguide synthesis (Julius Smith, CCRMA) — model acoustic transmission lines for wind and brass instruments. The bore of a clarinet, the bell flare of a trumpet, and the reed dynamics are all modeled as cascaded digital filters. The Yamaha VL1 (1994) used waveguide synthesis to achieve previously impossible realism in virtual wind instruments.

ADSR Envelope

The ADSR envelope is the universal amplitude shaping tool, applied as a multiplicative modulator to the oscillator output $x(t)$:

$$y(t) = A(t) \cdot x(t)$$

The four stages are:

  • Attack ($t_A$): Time to rise from 0 to peak amplitude after a key press. Short attack = percussive (drum). Long attack = slow swell (strings, pads).
  • Decay ($t_D$): Time to fall from peak to sustain level. Piano has moderate decay; organ has near-zero decay.
  • Sustain ($S$): Amplitude level held while the key is pressed. This is a level, not a time. Organ: sustain = 1.0. Piano: low sustain (the tone naturally decays).
  • Release ($t_R$): Time to fall from sustain level to silence after key release. Short release = staccato. Long release = reverberant/legato.

Envelopes are also applied to filter cutoff frequency (filter envelope) and pitch (pitch envelope), giving the characteristic "wah" opening of a classic synth brass patch.

Exercises: Sound Synthesis & Design

Easy (1–10)

1Waveform IdentificationEasy

Which synthesizer waveform contains only odd harmonics with amplitudes $1/n$? Which contains all harmonics?

Only odd harmonics (1/n): Square wave — harmonics at $f_0, 3f_0, 5f_0, 7f_0, \ldots$ with amplitudes $1, 1/3, 1/5, 1/7, \ldots$

All harmonics: Sawtooth wave — harmonics at $f_0, 2f_0, 3f_0, \ldots$ with amplitudes $1, 1/2, 1/3, \ldots$ The triangle wave also has only odd harmonics, but with amplitudes $1/n^2$ (much softer).

2ADSR: Piano vs. OrganEasy

Describe the ADSR settings (qualitatively fast/slow/high/low) for (a) a piano note and (b) a pipe organ note. Explain the differences.

(a) Piano: Attack = fast (immediate strike), Decay = moderate (0.5–2s), Sustain = low-to-zero (tone naturally dies away even while key is held — piano dampers only stop the string when key is released), Release = short-to-moderate.

(b) Pipe Organ: Attack = very fast (near-instant), Decay = zero (no decay phase), Sustain = full (1.0 — the pipe speaks at full volume as long as the key is held), Release = fast (pipe stops immediately when key is released).

The organ has no natural decay because the air column continuously excites the pipe — unlike a piano where the hammer strike imparts finite energy.

3Fundamental FrequencyEasy

A Karplus-Strong delay line has $L = 200$ samples at sample rate $f_s = 44{,}100$ Hz. What fundamental frequency does it produce?

$$f_0 = \frac{f_s}{L} = \frac{44100}{200} = 220.5 \text{ Hz}$$

This is approximately A3 (concert A below middle C is at 220 Hz). The small discrepancy (0.5 Hz) is inherent in integer delay lengths — fractional delay interpolation corrects this.

4Additive Synthesis — Harmonic CountEasy

You want to synthesize a sawtooth wave at 440 Hz using additive synthesis, including all harmonics up to 20 kHz. How many sinusoids do you need?

Harmonics of 440 Hz: $440, 880, 1320, \ldots$ up to $\leq 20{,}000$ Hz.

Highest harmonic number: $\lfloor 20000 / 440 \rfloor = \lfloor 45.45 \rfloor = 45$.

You need 45 sinusoids.

5FM Modulation IndexEasy

In FM synthesis, $\beta = 0$ gives a pure sine. What happens to the timbre as $\beta$ increases from 0 to 5?

As $\beta$ increases from 0:

  • $\beta \approx 0$: Pure sine (only carrier frequency, no sidebands).
  • $\beta \approx 0.5$: Slight brightness, first sideband pair becomes audible.
  • $\beta \approx 1$: Moderate brightness, several sideband pairs present.
  • $\beta \approx 2$: Rich, complex spectrum — resembles brass or electric piano.
  • $\beta \approx 5$: Very bright, almost harsh — many sideband pairs, some can alias below 0 Hz and fold back (spectral mirroring).

The energy redistributes from the carrier into sidebands — at certain $\beta$ values (e.g., $\beta \approx 2.4$), the carrier's amplitude reaches zero entirely (a Bessel zero).

6Subtractive Synthesis ChainEasy

In a VCO → VCF → VCA chain, what does each block control? What happens if you set the VCF cutoff very low?

VCO: Generates the raw waveform — controls pitch (frequency) and initial harmonic content (waveform shape).

VCF: Shapes the harmonic content by attenuating certain frequency ranges. Controls timbre — "bright" vs. "dark" sound.

VCA: Controls the overall amplitude envelope over time (ADSR).

If VCF cutoff is very low: All harmonics above the cutoff are attenuated. The output approaches a pure sine wave — only the fundamental survives. The sound becomes very muffled and dull.

7Wavetable Phase IncrementEasy

A wavetable has $N = 2048$ samples. At sample rate $f_s = 44{,}100$ Hz, what phase increment is needed to produce A4 (440 Hz)?

$$\Delta_{\text{phase}} = \frac{f \cdot N}{f_s} = \frac{440 \times 2048}{44100} \approx 20.42 \text{ samples per output sample}$$

The oscillator advances 20.42 table positions each sample. Since this is non-integer, linear (or higher-order) interpolation between adjacent table entries is used to avoid artifacts.

8Granular Grain DensityEasy

Granular synthesis uses grains of 30 ms duration at density $D = 40$ grains/second. Are the grains overlapping or separated?

Average spacing between grains = $1/D = 1/40 = 25$ ms.

Grain duration = 30 ms > 25 ms spacing → grains overlap.

Overlap factor: $D \cdot \tau = 40 \times 0.030 = 1.2$. On average, 1.2 grains are active at any time. This produces a relatively smooth, continuous texture rather than a choppy, gated effect.

9Synthesis Type MatchingEasy

Match each synthesizer to its primary synthesis method: (a) Hammond B3, (b) Yamaha DX7, (c) Waldorf Wave, (d) Kyma Capybara.

(a) Hammond B3 → Additive synthesis (tonewheel generators produce sinusoids at each harmonic; drawbars mix them).

(b) Yamaha DX7 → FM synthesis (6 operators in 32 algorithms).

(c) Waldorf Wave → Wavetable synthesis (interpolates through a bank of 300+ stored waveforms).

(d) Kyma Capybara → Granular synthesis (among other methods; Kyma is a general DSP environment).

10Sustain LevelEasy

An ADSR envelope has Attack = 10 ms, Decay = 200 ms, Sustain = 0.7, Release = 300 ms. Describe the amplitude at: (a) $t = 5$ ms, (b) $t = 100$ ms, (c) $t = 600$ ms (key still held), (d) 50 ms after key release.

(a) $t = 5$ ms: Still in Attack phase (0–10 ms). Amplitude $\approx 0.5$ (halfway up, assuming linear attack).

(b) $t = 100$ ms: In Decay phase (10–210 ms). 90 ms into 200 ms decay, amplitude $\approx 1 - (90/200)(1 - 0.7) = 1 - 0.135 = 0.865$. (Linear decay from peak 1.0 to sustain 0.7.)

(c) $t = 600$ ms (key held): In Sustain phase. Amplitude = 0.7.

(d) 50 ms after key release: In Release phase (300 ms total). Amplitude $\approx 0.7 \times (1 - 50/300) = 0.7 \times 0.833 = 0.583$.

12Filter Resonance DesignMedium

A resonant lowpass filter has cutoff $f_c = 1000$ Hz and $Q = 8$. What is the bandwidth of the resonant peak? At what frequency does the filter begin to roll off significantly, and what is the attenuation at $f = 4000$ Hz for a 24 dB/octave slope?

Bandwidth: $BW = f_c / Q = 1000/8 = 125$ Hz.

Rolloff: The filter attenuates frequencies above $f_c = 1000$ Hz at 24 dB/octave.

Attenuation at 4000 Hz: $4000/1000 = 4 = 2^2$, so this is 2 octaves above cutoff.

Attenuation = $24 \text{ dB/oct} \times 2 \text{ oct} = 48$ dB below the passband level.

High $Q = 8$ produces a sharp resonant peak at 1000 Hz (+18 to +20 dB above the passband, depending on implementation), giving the classic "wah" or "quack" character of a resonant filter sweep.

13Karplus-Strong TuningMedium

You want to play concert A (440 Hz) with the Karplus-Strong algorithm at 44,100 Hz sample rate. What integer delay line length $L$ gives the closest pitch? What is the actual frequency produced? How would you correct the remaining tuning error?

Ideal $L = f_s/f_0 = 44100/440 = 100.227$ samples.

Integer options: $L = 100$ or $L = 101$.

$L = 100$: $f = 44100/100 = 441$ Hz (+3.9 cents sharp).

$L = 101$: $f = 44100/101 \approx 436.6$ Hz (−13.4 cents flat).

$L = 100$ is closer. Tuning correction: Add a fractional delay element (e.g., an allpass filter with delay parameter $d = 0.227$ samples) in the feedback loop. An allpass filter $H(z) = (d + z^{-1})/(1 + dz^{-1})$ provides phase delay $\tau(\omega) \approx d$ at low frequencies without affecting magnitude, effectively adding the fractional 0.227 samples needed for exact pitch.

14Time Stretching with GranularMedium

You have a 4-second audio clip and want to stretch it to 12 seconds (3× stretch) using granular synthesis. If your grain size is 50 ms, describe the playback algorithm: how far does the read pointer advance per grain?

In the original file, grains are selected from sequential positions. In real-time playback:

At 1× speed, we would output one grain duration per grain duration of source: the source read pointer advances by grain size = 50 ms per grain.

At 3× stretch: to play 12 seconds of output from 4 seconds of source, we need the source pointer to advance at $1/3$ the normal rate.

Source advance per grain = grain size / stretch factor = $50 \text{ ms} / 3 \approx 16.7$ ms per grain.

So each output grain (50 ms long) reads from a source position that moves forward only 16.7 ms per grain. Multiple overlapping grains from nearby positions are played simultaneously, filling the stretched time.

15Additive Synthesis OrganMedium

A Hammond organ drawbar setting of [8, 8, 8, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0] means maximum level for 16', 8', and 4' pipes (harmonics 0.5, 1, 2 of the fundamental). Write the signal equation for concert A (f₀ = 440 Hz) with this setting, assuming unit amplitude per drawbar.

The three drawbars contribute:

  • 16' pipe: sub-octave = $0.5 \times 440 = 220$ Hz
  • 8' pipe: fundamental = $1 \times 440 = 440$ Hz
  • 4' pipe: octave = $2 \times 440 = 880$ Hz

$$y(t) = \sin(2\pi \cdot 220 \cdot t) + \sin(2\pi \cdot 440 \cdot t) + \sin(2\pi \cdot 880 \cdot t)$$

Adding equal-amplitude harmonics at octave intervals produces a richer, "fuller" sound with a suboctave "body" and an upper octave "brightness" — the classic full-register Hammond sound.

16FM Operator Ratio and TimbreMedium

An FM patch has carrier-to-modulator ratio $f_c:f_m = 1:1.5$. Is the resulting spectrum harmonic or inharmonic? Describe what type of sound this is likely to produce.

Sideband frequencies: $f_c + n \cdot f_m = f_c(1 + 1.5n)$ for integer $n$. These are not integer multiples of a common fundamental unless $f_c$ and $f_m$ share a rational relationship that resolves to integer harmonics.

$f_c:f_m = 1:1.5 = 2:3$ — this is a rational ratio! The GCD-based fundamental is $f_0 = f_c/2$. Sidebands at $f_c + n \cdot 1.5f_c = f_c(1 + 1.5n)$: for $n = -2, -1, 0, 1, 2$: $-2f_c, -0.5f_c, f_c, 2.5f_c, 4f_c$. In units of $f_0 = 0.5f_c$: $-4, -1, 2, 5, 8, \ldots$ — non-consecutive integers.

The spectrum is inharmonic with gaps. This produces a bell-like, metallic, or gong quality — the classic DX7 bell/marimba patch uses ratios near 1:1.414 (irrational) for true inharmonicity.

17Envelope Shaping a FilterMedium

A classic synth brass patch uses a filter envelope: the VCF cutoff sweeps from 200 Hz to 4000 Hz during the attack (50 ms), then decays back to 800 Hz over 300 ms. Write a piecewise formula for $f_c(t)$ and sketch the perceived brightness over time.

Assuming linear sweep during attack and exponential-style decay (common in analog synths):

Attack (0 to 50 ms):

$$f_c(t) = 200 + \frac{(4000 - 200)}{0.05}t = 200 + 76000t \text{ Hz}, \quad 0 \leq t \leq 0.05$$

Decay (50 ms to 350 ms):

$$f_c(t) = 800 + (4000 - 800)\,e^{-(t-0.05)/\tau} \text{ Hz}$$

where $\tau$ is chosen so the decay reaches near 800 Hz in about 3 time constants: $\tau = 0.300/3 = 100$ ms.

Brightness over time: The sound starts dull (200 Hz cutoff), swoops to maximum brightness (4 kHz) at 50 ms (the "bite" of the brass attack), then settles to a moderately bright sustained tone (800 Hz). This mimics a trumpet or trombone, where the initial air column pressure spike brightens the tone.

18Karplus-Strong Decay TimeMedium

In Karplus-Strong, the feedback gain is $g = 0.998$ and the delay length is $L = 100$ samples. After how many seconds does the amplitude decay to $1/e \approx 36.8\%$ of its initial value (the time constant $\tau$)?

Each pass around the loop (one period = $L$ samples) multiplies amplitude by $g$. After $n$ loops (samples $= nL$):

$$A(n) = g^n$$

At sample $m = nL$: $A = g^{m/L}$. We want $g^{m/L} = e^{-1}$, so:

$$\frac{m}{L}\ln g = -1 \Rightarrow m = \frac{-L}{\ln g} = \frac{-100}{\ln 0.998} = \frac{-100}{-0.002002} \approx 49{,}950 \text{ samples}$$

$$\tau = m / f_s = 49950 / 44100 \approx 1.133 \text{ s}$$

A decay constant of ~1.1 seconds is appropriate for a plucked guitar string.

19Wavetable AliasingMedium

A wavetable stores a sawtooth wave with harmonics up to the 64th partial. At $f_0 = 440$ Hz and $f_s = 44{,}100$ Hz, what is the highest harmonic frequency, and does it alias? At $f_0 = 1000$ Hz, does the 30th harmonic alias?

At 440 Hz: 64th harmonic = $64 \times 440 = 28{,}160$ Hz. Nyquist = 22,050 Hz. $28{,}160 > 22{,}050$ → aliases! The harmonics from the 51st ($51 \times 440 = 22{,}440$ Hz) onward would fold back.

At 1000 Hz: 30th harmonic = $30 \times 1000 = 30{,}000$ Hz > 22,050 Hz → aliases. The 22nd harmonic ($22{,}000$ Hz) is fine; from the 23rd ($23{,}000$ Hz) onward, aliasing occurs.

This is why professional wavetable synthesizers use multiple wavetables (band-limited for different pitch ranges) or BLEP/BLAMP (Band-Limited step/ramp) algorithms that truncate harmonics above Nyquist before playing.

20Physical Model — BellMedium

A bell's three lowest modes are at 440 Hz, 1100 Hz, and 1760 Hz. Write an additive synthesis model with exponential decay: each partial has initial amplitude $A_k$ and decay time constant $\tau_k$. If $\tau_1 = 5$ s, $\tau_2 = 2$ s, $\tau_3 = 0.8$ s, and $A_1 = A_2 = A_3 = 1$, write the complete signal.

$$y(t) = e^{-t/5}\sin(2\pi \cdot 440 \cdot t) + e^{-t/2}\sin(2\pi \cdot 1100 \cdot t) + e^{-t/0.8}\sin(2\pi \cdot 1760 \cdot t)$$

The inharmonic partials (440, 1100, 1760 Hz are not integer multiples of a single fundamental) give the bell its characteristic "metallic" quality. The high partials decay fastest, so the sound brightens slightly then slowly darkens as only the fundamental remains — exactly like a real bell. At $t = 2$ s, only partials 1 and 3 remain at appreciable level; by $t = 4$ s, essentially only the fundamental is audible.

Hard (21–30)

21FM Sideband Amplitudes via Bessel FunctionsHard

Derive the FM synthesis sideband expansion. Starting from $y(t) = \sin(2\pi f_c t + \beta\sin(2\pi f_m t))$, use the Jacobi-Anger identity to show the amplitudes of each sideband are Bessel functions $J_n(\beta)$.

Expand using the identity $e^{i\beta\sin\theta} = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} J_n(\beta) e^{in\theta}$:

$$y(t) = \text{Im}\!\left[e^{i(2\pi f_c t + \beta\sin(2\pi f_m t))}\right] = \text{Im}\!\left[e^{i2\pi f_c t} \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} J_n(\beta)e^{in \cdot 2\pi f_m t}\right]$$

$$= \text{Im}\!\left[\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} J_n(\beta) e^{i2\pi(f_c + nf_m)t}\right] = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} J_n(\beta)\sin(2\pi(f_c + nf_m)t)$$

using $\text{Im}[e^{i\theta}] = \sin\theta$. The $n$-th sideband has frequency $f_c + nf_m$ and amplitude $J_n(\beta)$.

Key Bessel function properties: $J_0(\beta) \approx 1 - \beta^2/4$ for small $\beta$ (carrier amplitude decreases as $\beta$ increases). $J_{-n}(\beta) = (-1)^n J_n(\beta)$. The bandwidth rule of thumb: significant sidebands exist for $|n| \lesssim \beta + 2$ (Carson's rule analog).

22Design a Complete Subtractive Synth PatchHard

Design a complete subtractive synthesis patch for a "classic analog lead" sound. Specify: oscillator waveforms and detuning, filter type/cutoff/resonance, filter envelope, amplitude ADSR, LFO routing, and any effects. Justify each choice.

Oscillators (2 VCOs): Both sawtooth (all harmonics → fat, edgy source). VCO1 at pitch, VCO2 detuned +7 cents. Slight detuning creates beating and chorus-like thickening without sounding out of tune.

Mixer: Equal parts VCO1 + VCO2. Optional sub-oscillator (square, −1 oct, 30%) for low-end weight.

VCF: 24 dB/oct resonant lowpass (Moog-style ladder). Cutoff = 1200 Hz, Resonance = 35% (mild coloring without self-oscillation). Filter keytrack = 50% (cutoff tracks pitch somewhat).

Filter Envelope: Attack = 5 ms, Decay = 120 ms, Sustain = 40%, Release = 80 ms. Envelope amount = +60% (sweeps filter from 1200 Hz up to ~3500 Hz on attack, back to ~1800 Hz sustained). Creates the characteristic "bite" at note onset.

Amplitude ADSR: Attack = 3 ms (near-instant — aggressive), Decay = 0 ms, Sustain = 100%, Release = 50 ms (clean cutoff).

LFO: Triangle, rate = 5 Hz, routed to pitch (±15 cents) → vibrato. Optional: rate = 0.2 Hz, routed to filter cutoff (±300 Hz) for slow "filter wobble".

Effects: Chorus (2 voices, depth 15 ms, rate 0.7 Hz) for width. Light compression (2:1, fast attack) for sustain. Short reverb (pre-delay 15 ms, RT60 0.8 s) for space.

This patch produces the lead sound heard on thousands of classic electronic tracks — bright, punchy, with vibrato and "analog warmth" from the detuned oscillators and resonant filter.

23Waveguide Synthesis — Clarinet ModelHard

A digital waveguide clarinet model uses a cylindrical bore (closed-open tube) with length $L_{\text{bore}}$ and a nonlinear reed reflection function at the mouthpiece. Explain why a clarinet produces only odd harmonics, and how the waveguide model captures this. What is the fundamental frequency of a clarinet with bore length 33 cm?

Why odd harmonics: A closed-open cylindrical tube (closed at the reed end, open at the bell) has standing wave modes where a pressure antinode exists at the closed end and a node at the open end. The boundary conditions require an odd number of quarter-wavelengths to fit in the tube:

$$L = \frac{(2n-1)\lambda}{4}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots \Rightarrow f_n = \frac{(2n-1)v}{4L}$$

Only odd multiples of the fundamental survive: $f_1, 3f_1, 5f_1, \ldots$ This gives the clarinet its characteristically "hollow" timbre (even harmonics are weak) and explains why it overblows a twelfth (to $3f_1$) rather than an octave.

Waveguide model: Two delay lines model the forward and backward traveling pressure waves. At the bell: a reflection filter (frequency-dependent reflection $r(f)$ < 1, modeling radiation losses). At the reed: a nonlinear function $r_{\text{reed}}(p) = r_0\tanh(k \cdot p_\Delta)$ where $p_\Delta$ is the pressure difference across the reed. This nonlinearity generates the harmonics and determines when the reed "blows open."

Fundamental frequency: $f_1 = v/(4L) = 343/(4 \times 0.33) \approx 260$ Hz $\approx$ C4 (middle C). This is approximately the lowest note of a B-flat clarinet when concert pitch is considered.

24FM Feedback OperatorHard

The Yamaha DX7 has a "feedback" mode where an operator modulates its own output: $y[n] = \sin(\omega n + \alpha \cdot y[n-1])$. For small feedback $\alpha$, show this produces odd harmonics. What happens as $\alpha \to \pi$?

For small $\alpha$, expand $\sin(\omega n + \alpha y[n-1])$ using $y \approx \sin(\omega n)$ as zeroth-order approximation:

$$y[n] \approx \sin(\omega n + \alpha\sin(\omega(n-1)))$$

This is identical to FM with $\beta = \alpha$ and $f_m = f_c$ (self-modulation with ratio 1:1). FM sidebands: $f_c + n \cdot f_c = (1+n)f_c$. For $n = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots$ → DC, $2f_c, 3f_c, \ldots$ Note: for self-FM with $n$ negative, frequencies fold to produce all integer harmonics.

More carefully: the Jacobi-Anger expansion with $f_c = f_m$ gives sidebands at $f_c + nf_c = (1+n)f_c$. The $n=0$ term is $f_c$ (fundamental), $n=1$ gives $2f_c$, etc. But due to the $J_{-n}$ symmetry and the discrete-time phase shift, odd harmonics dominate for small $\alpha$ because the even-order Bessel contributions partially cancel.

As $\alpha \to \pi$: The iterated map $y[n] = \sin(\omega n + \pi y[n-1])$ behaves chaotically — the trajectory of $y[n-1]$ acts as a highly nonlinear modulator. The spectrum broadens dramatically and becomes noise-like. The sound transitions from a musical tone to a harsh, buzzy, nearly white-noise character — useful for percussion and special effects on the DX7.

25Physical Modeling Math — String TensionHard

The frequency of a vibrating string is $f = \frac{1}{2L}\sqrt{T/\mu}$ where $T$ is tension and $\mu$ is linear mass density. A guitar string has $L = 0.65$ m, $\mu = 0.0004$ kg/m, and is tuned to E2 (82.4 Hz). Find the tension $T$. If you increase tension by 5%, by how many cents does the pitch rise?

Solving for T:

$$f = \frac{1}{2L}\sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}} \Rightarrow T = \mu(2Lf)^2 = 0.0004 \times (2 \times 0.65 \times 82.4)^2$$

$$T = 0.0004 \times (107.12)^2 = 0.0004 \times 11474.7 \approx 4.59 \text{ N}$$

(In practice, a low E guitar string has tension around 60–80 N for steel — this example uses a very thin/light string.)

Pitch change with 5% tension increase:

$f' = f\sqrt{T'/T} = f\sqrt{1.05} = 82.4 \times 1.0247 = 84.4$ Hz.

Cents: $1200\log_2(f'/f) = 1200\log_2(1.0247) = 1200 \times 0.03508 \approx 42.1$ cents.

Just under half a semitone — this shows why fine-tuning a string requires careful tension control; a 5% tension change corresponds to nearly a quarter-tone pitch shift.

27Feedback FM and ChaosHard

The logistic map $x[n+1] = rx[n](1-x[n])$ exhibits period-doubling bifurcations as $r$ increases, eventually becoming chaotic at $r \approx 3.57$. A feedback FM oscillator $y[n] = \sin(\omega n + \alpha y[n-1])$ shows similar behavior. At what value of $\alpha$ does the $n$-th period doubling occur? Compare to the Feigenbaum constant $\delta \approx 4.669$.

For the feedback FM oscillator, the dynamical behavior depends on the fixed point stability. The map has a fixed point when $y^* = \sin(\omega n + \alpha y^*)$ averaged over $n$, which is nontrivial due to the explicit $n$ dependence.

In practice, the first period-doubling occurs around $\alpha \approx \pi/2 \approx 1.57$ (where the linearized feedback gain exceeds 1 at some phase). The sequence of bifurcation values $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \alpha_3, \ldots$ satisfies:

$$\frac{\alpha_n - \alpha_{n-1}}{\alpha_{n+1} - \alpha_n} \to \delta \approx 4.669 \quad \text{(Feigenbaum constant)}$$

For the DX7's feedback operator (range 0 to $\pi$), values up to $\alpha \approx 1$ give progressively richer harmonic spectra. Values $\alpha > 2$ enter the chaotic regime, producing noise-like outputs. The Feigenbaum constant's universality means this cascade of period doublings is scale-invariant — the structure of the bifurcation diagram near the onset of chaos looks identical regardless of the specific nonlinearity, a deep result of dynamical systems theory. In practice, DX7 feedback settings 5–7 (out of 7) access both complex spectral and near-chaotic behaviors.

28Scanned Synthesis MathematicsHard

Scanned synthesis (Verplank, Mathews, Shaw) models a mass-spring system: each mass $m_i$ connected to neighbors and a central "hammer" potential. The 1D wave equation $\ddot{u} = c^2 \nabla^2 u - k u$ (adding a restoring term $-ku$) gives dispersion. Show that plane wave solutions $u = e^{i(\omega t - qx)}$ satisfy $\omega^2 = c^2 q^2 + k$, producing inharmonic modes. How does this differ from a standard string?

Substituting $u = e^{i(\omega t - qx)}$ into $\ddot{u} = c^2 \nabla^2 u - ku$:

$$-\omega^2 e^{i(\omega t-qx)} = c^2(-q^2)e^{i(\omega t-qx)} - k\,e^{i(\omega t-qx)}$$

Dividing through: $-\omega^2 = -c^2q^2 - k$, so:

$$\omega^2 = c^2 q^2 + k$$

For a string with boundary conditions $u(0) = u(L) = 0$: $q_n = n\pi/L$, giving:

$$\omega_n = \sqrt{c^2(n\pi/L)^2 + k} = \frac{n\pi c}{L}\sqrt{1 + \frac{kL^2}{n^2\pi^2c^2}}$$

Standard string: $k = 0$, so $\omega_n = n\pi c/L$ — perfectly harmonic (integer multiples of $\omega_1$).

Scanned synthesis string: $k > 0$ shifts all modes upward. Lower modes shift proportionally more (additive $k$ is larger relative to $c^2q_n^2$ for small $n$). This produces inharmonic modes where higher partials are closer to integer harmonics than lower ones — opposite to piano string inharmonicity. The resulting timbres are bell-like or exotic. Scanned synthesis lets composers explore a continuum from harmonic (string) to inharmonic (bell/gong) by varying the effective stiffness parameter $k$.

29Complete FM Patch: DX7 Electric PianoHard

The Yamaha DX7's famous "E. Piano 1" patch uses Algorithm 5: two operator pairs (Op1 modulates Op2; Op3 modulates Op4) summed at output. Op2 has ratio 1.0 ($f_c$); Op1 has ratio 14.0 ($14f_c$) with low modulation index. Op4 has ratio 1.0; Op3 has ratio 1.0 with feedback. Explain qualitatively how this produces the electric piano timbre, including the characteristic "bell-like" attack and warm sustain.

Pair 1 (Op1 → Op2): Op1 at $14f_c$ with a short, high-modulation-index envelope modulates Op2 (the carrier). At note onset, the high-ratio modulator creates a burst of inharmonic sidebands at $f_c \pm 14f_c = 15f_c, 13f_c, \pm27f_c, \ldots$ These are non-harmonic and create the characteristic "click" or "bell" transient of the electric piano. As the modulator envelope decays to near-zero, Op2 reverts to a nearly pure sine — the warm body of the note.

Pair 2 (Op3 → Op4 with feedback on Op3): Both at ratio 1.0 (same frequency as Op4). Op3's self-feedback creates additional harmonics (odd harmonics for small feedback). This pair contributes low-mid warmth and slight harmonic roughness, preventing the tone from being too pure/cold.

Combined timbre evolution:

  • Attack (0–50 ms): Bright, bell-like transient from Pair 1's high-ratio modulation. The inharmonic content triggers the ear's pitch-detection system in a "metallic" way.
  • Decay (50–500 ms): Modulation index on Op1 decays. Inharmonic sidebands fade. Tone becomes progressively warmer and more sine-like.
  • Sustain: Pair 2 (self-feedback) maintains a slightly harmonically complex but warm tone. This mimics the Fender Rhodes's tine resonance continuing to vibrate.

The genius of the patch is the separation of the "transient generator" (Pair 1) from the "body generator" (Pair 2), each with independent amplitude envelopes. This two-layer approach with FM is far more efficient than recording the transient and body separately as samples.

30Granular Synthesis Phase Vocoder ConnectionHard

The phase vocoder is an analysis-resynthesis system that time-stretches audio while preserving pitch. It computes STFT frames, scales them in time by stretching the phase, and resynthesizes. Show mathematically that for a pure sinusoid $x(t) = A\cos(2\pi f_0 t)$, stretching by factor $\alpha$ requires advancing the phase of each STFT bin by $\hat\omega_k \cdot \alpha \cdot H$ per hop $H$, where $\hat\omega_k$ is the instantaneous frequency estimate. Why does naive phase interpolation cause the "phasiness" artifact?

Setup: STFT hop size $H$ samples, window $w[n]$. Frame $m$ is computed at time $mH$. For a sinusoid at $f_0$, the STFT bin at frequency $\omega_k = 2\pi k/N$ has phase:

$$\phi_k[m] = 2\pi f_0 \cdot mH / f_s = \hat\omega_k \cdot mH$$

where $\hat\omega_k = 2\pi f_0 / f_s$ is the instantaneous frequency of bin $k$ (in radians/sample).

Phase advance per hop: Between frames $m$ and $m+1$, the expected phase advance is $\Delta\phi = \hat\omega_k \cdot H$. The instantaneous frequency estimate:

$$\hat\omega_k = \frac{\phi_k[m] - \phi_k[m-1]}{H} \pmod{2\pi}$$

Time stretching by $\alpha$: We want to output frames at rate $\alpha$ times slower, so output hop = $\alpha H$. Each synthesis frame must accumulate phase as if time moved at rate $1/\alpha$, but the oscillator must advance at the true frequency $\hat\omega_k$. The correct synthesis phase at output frame $m$ is:

$$\phi_k^{\text{syn}}[m] = \phi_k^{\text{syn}}[m-1] + \alpha \cdot \hat\omega_k \cdot H$$

This ensures the oscillator runs at the correct frequency ($\hat\omega_k$) even though frames are spaced further apart in time.

The "phasiness" artifact: Arises when bins from different frequency components overlap in the same analysis bin (spectral leakage). The phase vocoder assumes each bin has a single sinusoidal component with well-defined instantaneous frequency. When two nearby sinusoids fall in the same bin, their phases interact, and the instantaneous frequency estimate becomes meaningless. The synthesis then produces both components with incorrect phase relationships to one another, causing the characteristic "underwater" or "metallic swirling" phasiness. Solutions include: higher resolution FFT (reduce overlap), phase locking across channels (maintain relative phases of partials in harmonic sounds), or transient-preserving resynthesis (treat transients separately from tonal parts).
