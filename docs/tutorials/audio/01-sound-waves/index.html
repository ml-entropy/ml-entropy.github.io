What Is Sound?

Sound is a mechanical wave — a disturbance that travels through a medium (air, water, steel) by transferring energy from particle to particle without the particles themselves traveling. Unlike electromagnetic waves (light, radio), sound requires a medium. In the vacuum of space, no one can hear you scream.

When a guitar string vibrates, it pushes nearby air molecules together, creating a region of high pressure called a compression. As the string moves back, it creates a region of low pressure called a rarefaction. These alternating zones propagate outward like ripples on a pond — except in three dimensions.

Key Insight

Sound is a longitudinal wave: the particles oscillate parallel to the direction the wave travels, not perpendicular (as in ocean waves or guitar strings). Think of a Slinky being pushed and pulled from one end.

Properties of Sound Waves

Every sound wave is characterized by four fundamental properties:

Frequency ($f$) — the number of complete oscillations per second, measured in Hertz (Hz). A concert A note is 440 Hz, meaning air pressure at your eardrum oscillates 440 times per second. Human hearing spans roughly 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Below 20 Hz is infrasound (elephants communicate here); above 20 kHz is ultrasound (bats, medical imaging).

Amplitude ($A$) — the maximum displacement from the equilibrium position. Larger amplitude = louder sound. This is the "height" of the wave.

Wavelength ($\lambda$) — the spatial distance between two consecutive identical points on the wave (e.g., crest to crest). Related to frequency by the fundamental equation:

$$v = f \lambda$$

where $v$ is the speed of sound in the medium (~343 m/s in air at 20°C).

Period ($T$) — the time for one complete cycle: $T = 1/f$. A 440 Hz wave has period $T \approx 2.27$ ms.

The Wave Equation

A sinusoidal sound wave traveling in the positive $x$-direction is described by:

$$y(x, t) = A \sin(kx - \omega t + \phi)$$

where:

  • $A$ = amplitude (maximum displacement)
  • $k = 2\pi / \lambda$ = wave number (spatial frequency in radians per meter)
  • $\omega = 2\pi f$ = angular frequency (radians per second)
  • $\phi$ = phase offset (determines where in the cycle the wave starts at $x=0, t=0$)

The speed of the wave connects spatial and temporal periodicity:

$$v = \frac{\omega}{k} = f\lambda$$
The 1D Wave Equation

All well-behaved waves satisfy the second-order PDE:

$$\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} = v^2 \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2}$$

This says that the temporal curvature of the wave at any point is proportional to its spatial curvature. Verify: plug $y = A\sin(kx - \omega t)$ into both sides. The left gives $-\omega^2 A\sin(kx - \omega t)$, the right gives $-v^2 k^2 A\sin(kx - \omega t)$. These are equal when $\omega^2 = v^2 k^2$, i.e., $v = \omega/k$. ✔

In air, the speed depends on temperature: $v \approx 331.3 + 0.606\, T_C$ m/s, where $T_C$ is the Celsius temperature. At 20°C: $v \approx 331.3 + 12.1 = 343.4$ m/s.

Superposition Principle

When two or more waves pass through the same point in space, the resultant displacement is the sum of the individual displacements:

$$y_{\text{total}}(x, t) = y_1(x, t) + y_2(x, t) + \cdots$$

This leads to two critical phenomena:

Constructive interference occurs when waves are in phase (crests align with crests). The amplitudes add, producing a louder sound.

Destructive interference occurs when waves are out of phase by half a wavelength (crests align with troughs). The amplitudes cancel, producing silence.

When two waves have slightly different frequencies $f_1$ and $f_2$, you hear beats — periodic pulses in loudness at frequency $f_{\text{beat}} = |f_1 - f_2|$. Piano tuners use this: when two notes are in tune, beats disappear.

Standing Waves & Resonance

When a wave reflects back on itself (as in a guitar string fixed at both ends), the forward and reflected waves interfere to create a standing wave — a pattern that appears to vibrate in place rather than travel.

For a string of length $L$ fixed at both ends, only certain wavelengths "fit":

$$\lambda_n = \frac{2L}{n}, \quad f_n = \frac{nv}{2L}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

$n=1$ is the fundamental (first harmonic), $n=2$ is the second harmonic (first overtone), etc. These are the natural resonant frequencies of the string.

Why Instruments Sound Different

A piano, guitar, and clarinet playing the same note (same fundamental frequency) sound different because they produce different mixtures of harmonics. This mixture is called timbre (pronounced "TAM-ber"). A pure sine wave has only the fundamental; real instruments always have overtones. The relative strengths of these overtones are what give each instrument its characteristic color.

Sound Intensity & Decibels

Sound intensity $I$ is the power per unit area (W/m²) carried by the wave. For a point source radiating equally in all directions:

$$I = \frac{P}{4\pi r^2}$$

This is the inverse square law: doubling the distance from the source reduces intensity by a factor of 4.

Because human hearing spans an enormous range (~12 orders of magnitude), we use a logarithmic scale:

$$L = 10 \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{I}{I_0}\right) \text{ dB}$$

where $I_0 = 10^{-12}$ W/m² is the threshold of hearing. Some reference levels:

Decibel Rules of Thumb

+3 dB = double the intensity. +10 dB = 10× the intensity (sounds roughly "twice as loud" to humans). +20 dB = 100× the intensity.

The Doppler Effect

When a sound source or observer is moving, the observed frequency differs from the emitted frequency. An ambulance siren sounds higher-pitched as it approaches and lower-pitched as it recedes.

The general Doppler formula:

$$f' = f \cdot \frac{v + v_o}{v - v_s}$$

where $v$ is the speed of sound, $v_o$ is the observer's speed (positive if moving toward the source), and $v_s$ is the source's speed (positive if moving toward the observer).

Reflection, Refraction & Diffraction

Reflection: Sound bounces off surfaces. Hard, flat surfaces reflect strongly (think gymnasium echo). The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection. Echoes require a reflective surface at least ~17 m away (so the round-trip takes >0.1 s, the threshold for the brain to perceive a distinct echo).

Refraction: Sound bends when passing through regions of different temperature (and therefore different speed). On a cool evening, the ground layer is cooler (slower sound speed), bending sound waves downward — which is why you can hear distant sounds better at night.

Diffraction: Sound bends around obstacles. Low frequencies (long wavelengths) diffract more than high frequencies. This is why you can hear bass through walls but not treble — the bass wavelengths are comparable to or larger than typical obstacles, so they wrap around them.

Rule of Thumb for Diffraction

Significant diffraction occurs when the obstacle/opening size is comparable to or smaller than the wavelength. At 100 Hz in air, $\lambda \approx 3.4$ m — much larger than a doorway, so bass easily bends through. At 10 kHz, $\lambda \approx 3.4$ cm — much smaller than a doorway, so treble travels in straight lines and doesn't bend around corners well.

Easy (1–10)

1 Wavelength of Concert A Easy

A tuning fork vibrates at 440 Hz. If the speed of sound in air is 343 m/s, what is the wavelength?

Using $v = f\lambda$:

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{343}{440} \approx 0.780 \text{ m}$$

The wavelength is about 78 cm — roughly the length of your arm.

2 Period from Frequency Easy

Middle C on a piano has frequency 261.6 Hz. What is its period?

$$T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{261.6} \approx 3.82 \times 10^{-3} \text{ s} = 3.82 \text{ ms}$$

3 Speed of Sound at Temperature Easy

Calculate the speed of sound in air at 35°C using $v \approx 331.3 + 0.606\, T_C$.

$$v = 331.3 + 0.606 \times 35 = 331.3 + 21.21 = 352.5 \text{ m/s}$$

4 Decibel Calculation Easy

A sound has intensity $I = 10^{-5}$ W/m². What is the sound level in dB? ($I_0 = 10^{-12}$ W/m²$)

$$L = 10\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{10^{-5}}{10^{-12}}\right) = 10\log_{10}(10^7) = 10 \times 7 = 70 \text{ dB}$$

5 Frequency Range of Human Hearing Easy

Human hearing ranges from 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. What are the corresponding wavelengths in air (v = 343 m/s)?

At 20 Hz: $\lambda = 343/20 = 17.15$ m

At 20,000 Hz: $\lambda = 343/20000 = 0.01715$ m $= 1.715$ cm

Human hearing spans wavelengths from about 1.7 cm to 17 m!

6 Inverse Square Law Easy

You measure 80 dB at 2 m from a speaker. What is the sound level at 4 m (assuming free-field conditions)?

Doubling the distance reduces intensity by factor 4. In dB: $10\log_{10}(4) \approx 6$ dB reduction.

$$L_{4m} = 80 - 6 = 74 \text{ dB}$$

7 Wave Equation Parameters Easy

A wave is described by $y(x,t) = 0.02\sin(8\pi x - 2000\pi t)$ (SI units). Find the frequency, wavelength, and wave speed.

Comparing with $y = A\sin(kx - \omega t)$:

$k = 8\pi \Rightarrow \lambda = 2\pi/k = 2\pi/(8\pi) = 0.25$ m

$\omega = 2000\pi \Rightarrow f = \omega/(2\pi) = 1000$ Hz

$v = f\lambda = 1000 \times 0.25 = 250$ m/s

8 Beat Frequency Easy

Two tuning forks produce frequencies of 440 Hz and 444 Hz. What beat frequency do you hear?

$$f_{\text{beat}} = |f_1 - f_2| = |440 - 444| = 4 \text{ Hz}$$

You hear 4 beats per second — a slow pulsation in loudness.

9 Combining Decibels Easy

Two identical speakers each produce 70 dB. What is the combined sound level when both play?

Two identical sources double the intensity. $\Delta L = 10\log_{10}(2) \approx 3$ dB.

$$L_{\text{combined}} = 70 + 3 = 73 \text{ dB}$$

Note: 70 dB + 70 dB = 73 dB, not 140 dB! Decibels don't add linearly.

10 Harmonic Frequencies Easy

A guitar string has a fundamental frequency of 330 Hz (E4). What are the frequencies of the 2nd, 3rd, and 4th harmonics?

$f_n = n \cdot f_1$:

2nd harmonic: $2 \times 330 = 660$ Hz

3rd harmonic: $3 \times 330 = 990$ Hz

4th harmonic: $4 \times 330 = 1320$ Hz

Medium (11–20)

11 Doppler Shift — Approaching Siren Medium

An ambulance siren emits at 700 Hz and approaches you at 30 m/s. You are stationary. What frequency do you hear? ($v = 343$ m/s)

Source approaches observer: $v_s = 30$ m/s, $v_o = 0$.

$$f' = f \cdot \frac{v + v_o}{v - v_s} = 700 \cdot \frac{343 + 0}{343 - 30} = 700 \cdot \frac{343}{313} \approx 767.1 \text{ Hz}$$

12 Standing Wave on a String Medium

A violin string is 32.5 cm long and tuned to A4 (440 Hz). What is the wave speed on the string? What is the frequency of the third harmonic?

Fundamental: $f_1 = v/(2L)$, so $v = 2Lf_1 = 2 \times 0.325 \times 440 = 286$ m/s.

Third harmonic: $f_3 = 3f_1 = 3 \times 440 = 1320$ Hz.

13 Intensity from Distance Medium

A siren has acoustic power $P = 0.5$ W. (a) What is the intensity at 10 m? (b) What is the sound level in dB at 10 m?

(a) $I = P/(4\pi r^2) = 0.5/(4\pi \times 100) = 0.5/1256.6 \approx 3.98 \times 10^{-4}$ W/m²

(b) $L = 10\log_{10}(3.98 \times 10^{-4}/10^{-12}) = 10\log_{10}(3.98 \times 10^8) = 10 \times 8.6 = 86$ dB

14 Superposition of Two Waves Medium

Two waves are given by $y_1 = 3\sin(4x - 500t)$ and $y_2 = 3\sin(4x - 500t + \pi/3)$. Find the amplitude of the resultant wave.

Using the identity $A\sin\theta + A\sin(\theta + \phi) = 2A\cos(\phi/2)\sin(\theta + \phi/2)$:

$$A_{\text{result}} = 2 \times 3 \times \cos(\pi/6) = 6 \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = 3\sqrt{3} \approx 5.20$$

15 Organ Pipe Resonance (Open) Medium

An open organ pipe is 0.65 m long. What are its first three resonant frequencies? ($v = 343$ m/s)

Open pipe: both ends are antinodes, so $f_n = nv/(2L)$ for $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$

$f_1 = 343/(2 \times 0.65) = 263.8$ Hz

$f_2 = 2 \times 263.8 = 527.7$ Hz

$f_3 = 3 \times 263.8 = 791.5$ Hz

16 Organ Pipe (Closed One End) Medium

A pipe closed at one end is 0.65 m long. What are its first three resonant frequencies?

Closed pipe: node at closed end, antinode at open end. Only odd harmonics: $f_n = nv/(4L)$ for $n = 1, 3, 5, \ldots$

$f_1 = 343/(4 \times 0.65) = 131.9$ Hz

$f_3 = 3 \times 131.9 = 395.8$ Hz

$f_5 = 5 \times 131.9 = 659.6$ Hz

17 Doppler with Moving Observer Medium

A stationary whistle emits 1000 Hz. You run toward it at 5 m/s. What frequency do you hear?

$v_s = 0$, $v_o = 5$ m/s (toward source):

$$f' = 1000 \times \frac{343 + 5}{343 - 0} = 1000 \times \frac{348}{343} \approx 1014.6 \text{ Hz}$$

18 Echo Timing Medium

You shout toward a cliff and hear the echo 1.5 seconds later. How far away is the cliff? ($v = 343$ m/s)

The sound travels to the cliff and back (round trip):

$$d = \frac{v \times t}{2} = \frac{343 \times 1.5}{2} = 257.25 \text{ m}$$

19 Phase Difference and Path Difference Medium

Two speakers emit 686 Hz in phase. A listener is 3.0 m from one speaker and 3.5 m from the other. Is the interference constructive or destructive? ($v = 343$ m/s)

$\lambda = 343/686 = 0.5$ m. Path difference $= 3.5 - 3.0 = 0.5$ m $= \lambda$.

A path difference of exactly one wavelength means the waves arrive in phase: constructive interference.

20 Wavelength in Water Medium

A 500 Hz sound wave passes from air ($v = 343$ m/s) into water ($v = 1497$ m/s). What happens to (a) the frequency, (b) the wavelength, (c) the wave speed?

(a) Frequency stays the same: 500 Hz. Frequency is determined by the source and doesn't change when the medium changes.

(b) $\lambda_{\text{water}} = v_{\text{water}}/f = 1497/500 = 2.994$ m (vs. $\lambda_{\text{air}} = 343/500 = 0.686$ m). Wavelength increases by factor ~4.4.

(c) Speed increases from 343 to 1497 m/s.

Hard (21–30)

21 Derive the Wave Equation Hard

Starting from Newton's second law applied to a small element of a stretched string with tension $T$ and linear mass density $\mu$, derive the 1D wave equation and show that $v = \sqrt{T/\mu}$.

Consider an infinitesimal element of string from $x$ to $x + dx$, with small transverse displacement $y(x,t)$.

The net vertical force on the element: the tension pulls at angle $\theta_1$ at $x$ and $\theta_2$ at $x+dx$.

For small angles, $\sin\theta \approx \tan\theta = \partial y/\partial x$.

Net vertical force: $F_y = T\sin\theta_2 - T\sin\theta_1 \approx T\left[\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\Big|_{x+dx} - \frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\Big|_x\right] = T\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2}dx$

The mass of the element: $dm = \mu\, dx$. By Newton's 2nd law:

$$\mu\, dx \cdot \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} = T \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2} dx$$

$$\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} = \frac{T}{\mu} \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2}$$

Comparing with $\partial^2 y/\partial t^2 = v^2 \partial^2 y/\partial x^2$, we get $v = \sqrt{T/\mu}$. □

22 Acoustic Impedance Matching Hard

The acoustic impedance of a medium is $Z = \rho v$. When sound passes from medium 1 ($Z_1$) to medium 2 ($Z_2$), the reflection coefficient for intensity is $R = \left(\frac{Z_2 - Z_1}{Z_2 + Z_1}\right)^2$. (a) Calculate $R$ for air ($Z \approx 415$ Pa·s/m) to water ($Z \approx 1.48 \times 10^6$ Pa·s/m). (b) What fraction of energy is transmitted?

(a) $$R = \left(\frac{1.48 \times 10^6 - 415}{1.48 \times 10^6 + 415}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{1,479,585}{1,480,415}\right)^2 \approx (0.99944)^2 \approx 0.9989$$

(b) Transmission: $T_R = 1 - R \approx 0.0011 = 0.11\%$.

Only about 0.1% of sound energy passes from air into water — the massive impedance mismatch reflects almost everything. This is why your middle ear has three tiny bones: they act as an impedance-matching transformer between air and the fluid-filled cochlea.

23 Double Doppler Hard

A car moving at 25 m/s honks its horn (500 Hz) toward a building. The sound reflects off the building and returns to the driver. What frequency does the driver hear? ($v = 343$ m/s)

Step 1: Building "hears" the horn. Building is a stationary observer, car is moving source approaching:

$$f_{\text{wall}} = 500 \times \frac{343}{343 - 25} = 500 \times \frac{343}{318} = 539.3 \text{ Hz}$$

Step 2: Building reflects at $f_{\text{wall}}$. Now the building is a stationary source, and the driver is a moving observer approaching:

$$f_{\text{driver}} = 539.3 \times \frac{343 + 25}{343} = 539.3 \times \frac{368}{343} = 578.6 \text{ Hz}$$

The driver hears approximately 579 Hz.

24 Energy in a Standing Wave Hard

Show that the total energy of a standing wave $y(x,t) = 2A\sin(kx)\cos(\omega t)$ on a string of length $L$ (fixed at both ends) with linear density $\mu$ is $E = \frac{1}{2}\mu\omega^2 A^2 L$.

Kinetic energy: $dK = \frac{1}{2}\mu\left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}\right)^2 dx = \frac{1}{2}\mu(2A\omega\sin(kx)\sin(\omega t))^2 dx$

Potential energy: $dU = \frac{1}{2}T\left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\right)^2 dx = \frac{1}{2}T(2Ak\cos(kx)\cos(\omega t))^2 dx$

Using $T = \mu v^2 = \mu\omega^2/k^2$:

Total energy (time-averaged):

$$E = \int_0^L \left(\langle dK \rangle + \langle dU \rangle\right) = 2\mu\omega^2 A^2 \int_0^L \frac{1}{2}\sin^2(kx)\, dx$$

Since $\int_0^L \sin^2(kx)\,dx = L/2$ (for integer number of half-wavelengths):

$$E = 2\mu\omega^2 A^2 \times \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{L}{2} = \frac{1}{2}\mu\omega^2 A^2 L \quad \square$$

25 Decibel Addition of N Sources Hard

Derive a general formula for the combined sound level when $N$ identical incoherent sources each produce $L_1$ dB. Then compute the level for 100 violins each playing at 70 dB.

Each source has intensity $I_1$. Total intensity: $I_{\text{total}} = NI_1$.

$$L_{\text{total}} = 10\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{NI_1}{I_0}\right) = 10\log_{10}(N) + 10\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{I_1}{I_0}\right) = L_1 + 10\log_{10}(N)$$

For $N = 100, L_1 = 70$ dB:

$$L_{\text{total}} = 70 + 10\log_{10}(100) = 70 + 20 = 90 \text{ dB}$$

26 Resonance in a Tube with Temperature Gradient Hard

An open tube is 1.0 m long. The air at one end is at 20°C and at the other end at 40°C. Assuming a linear temperature gradient, estimate the fundamental frequency. Compare with the isothermal case at 30°C.

Speed of sound: $v(T) = 331.3 + 0.606T$.

Average temperature: $T_{\text{avg}} = 30°$C, so $v_{\text{avg}} = 331.3 + 18.18 = 349.5$ m/s.

For a linear gradient, the effective speed for the fundamental mode is approximately $v_{\text{avg}}$:

$$f_1 \approx \frac{v_{\text{avg}}}{2L} = \frac{349.5}{2.0} = 174.75 \text{ Hz}$$

Isothermal at 30°C gives the same: $f_1 = 349.5/2.0 = 174.75$ Hz.

For small temperature gradients, the first-order correction vanishes and the fundamental is well-approximated by the mean temperature. Second-order effects (wavefront bending inside the tube) are negligible for small gradients.

27 Shock Waves and Mach Number Hard

A jet flies at Mach 2.0 ($v = 343$ m/s). (a) What is the half-angle of the Mach cone? (b) The jet passes directly overhead at altitude 5000 m. How far past you (horizontally) is the jet when you first hear the sonic boom?

(a) $\sin\theta = v/v_s = 1/M = 1/2$, so $\theta = 30°$.

(b) The cone reaches you when the jet is at horizontal distance $d$ such that $\tan\theta = h/d$ (the cone slope). Actually, $\tan(90° - \theta) = d/h$.

$\tan(60°) = d/5000$, so $d = 5000\sqrt{3} \approx 8660$ m.

The jet is about 8.66 km past you when you hear the boom.

28 Fourier Decomposition of a Square Wave Hard

A square wave of amplitude $A$ and fundamental frequency $f_0$ can be written as a Fourier series. Show that only odd harmonics are present and find the first four non-zero terms.

A square wave with period $T$ (symmetric about origin) has odd symmetry, so only sine terms survive. For a square wave that is $+A$ for $0 < t < T/2$ and $-A$ for $T/2 < t < T$:

$$b_n = \frac{2}{T}\int_0^T f(t)\sin(2\pi nt/T)\,dt = \begin{cases} \frac{4A}{n\pi} & n \text{ odd} \\ 0 & n \text{ even}\end{cases}$$

Therefore:

$$f(t) = \frac{4A}{\pi}\left[\sin(2\pi f_0 t) + \frac{1}{3}\sin(6\pi f_0 t) + \frac{1}{5}\sin(10\pi f_0 t) + \frac{1}{7}\sin(14\pi f_0 t) + \cdots\right]$$

Only odd harmonics ($f_0, 3f_0, 5f_0, 7f_0, \ldots$) are present, with amplitudes decaying as $1/n$.

29 Room Modes Hard

A rectangular room has dimensions $L_x = 5$ m, $L_y = 4$ m, $L_z = 3$ m. The room modes (standing wave resonances) have frequencies $f_{n_x, n_y, n_z} = \frac{v}{2}\sqrt{(n_x/L_x)^2 + (n_y/L_y)^2 + (n_z/L_z)^2}$. Find the three lowest room mode frequencies ($v = 343$ m/s).

Axial modes (only one $n$ nonzero):

$(1,0,0)$: $f = 343/(2 \times 5) = 34.3$ Hz

$(0,1,0)$: $f = 343/(2 \times 4) = 42.9$ Hz

$(0,0,1)$: $f = 343/(2 \times 3) = 57.2$ Hz

The three lowest modes are 34.3 Hz, 42.9 Hz, and 57.2 Hz — all axial modes along each dimension.

30 Wave Equation with Damping Hard

The damped wave equation is $\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} + 2\beta\frac{\partial y}{\partial t} = v^2\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2}$ where $\beta$ is a damping coefficient. Show that $y(x,t) = Ae^{-\beta t}\sin(kx - \omega' t)$ is a solution and find $\omega'$ in terms of $\omega_0 = vk$ and $\beta$.

Let $y = Ae^{-\beta t}\sin(kx - \omega' t)$. Compute each partial derivative:

$\frac{\partial y}{\partial t} = A e^{-\beta t}[-\beta\sin(kx-\omega' t) - \omega'\cos(kx-\omega' t)]$

$\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} = Ae^{-\beta t}[(\beta^2 - \omega'^2)\sin(kx-\omega' t) + 2\beta\omega'\cos(kx-\omega' t)]$

$\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2} = -Ak^2 e^{-\beta t}\sin(kx - \omega' t)$

Substituting into the damped wave equation and equating sine and cosine coefficients:

Cosine: $2\beta\omega' - 2\beta\omega' = 0$ ✔ (automatically satisfied)

Sine: $\beta^2 - \omega'^2 - 2\beta^2 = -v^2k^2$

$$\omega'^2 = v^2 k^2 - \beta^2 = \omega_0^2 - \beta^2$$

$$\boxed{\omega' = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - \beta^2}}$$

Damping reduces the effective frequency. When $\beta > \omega_0$, the wave is overdamped and no longer oscillatory.
