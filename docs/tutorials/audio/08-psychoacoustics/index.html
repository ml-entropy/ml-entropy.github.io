The Human Ear

Psychoacoustics is the study of how humans perceive sound — the relationship between the physical properties of a sound wave and the subjective experience it produces. Understanding this perception is not merely academic: it is the engineering foundation of every modern audio codec (MP3, AAC, Opus) and audio processing algorithm.

Sound travels through three anatomical stages before becoming a nerve impulse:

  • Outer ear (pinna + ear canal) — Collects and funnels sound. The pinna's shape adds frequency-dependent reflections (Head-Related Transfer Function) that encode elevation cues.
  • Middle ear (ossicles) — Three tiny bones (malleus, incus, stapes) mechanically amplify and impedance-match air pressure waves to the fluid-filled inner ear.
  • Inner ear (cochlea) — A coiled, fluid-filled tube lined with the basilar membrane. Incoming vibrations travel as a wave along the membrane; each frequency causes maximum displacement at a specific location — a phenomenon called tonotopy.
Tonotopy: The Cochlea as a Spectrum Analyzer

The basilar membrane is stiff and narrow at the base (responds to high frequencies, ~20 kHz) and wide and floppy at the apex (responds to low frequencies, ~20 Hz). Hair cells sitting on the membrane at each location transduce mechanical motion into electrical nerve impulses. The brain receives a place-coded frequency map — the biological equivalent of a Fourier transform.

Loudness Perception

Sound pressure level (SPL) is measured in decibels: $L_p = 20\log_{10}(p/p_0)$ where $p_0 = 20\,\mu\text{Pa}$ is the reference threshold of hearing. But perceived loudness is not simply proportional to SPL — it depends strongly on frequency.

Fletcher and Munson (1933), later standardized as ISO 226, measured the SPL required at each frequency to produce equal perceived loudness. These equal-loudness contours reveal:

  • Human hearing is most sensitive around 2–5 kHz (the resonant frequency of the ear canal).
  • Low frequencies (<200 Hz) require much more SPL to be heard at the same loudness.
  • At very high listening levels, the frequency response flattens — the bass appears louder relative to midrange.
Phons and Sones

Phon — a unit of loudness level. A sound has $N$ phons if it sounds equally loud as a 1 kHz tone at $N$ dB SPL. The equal-loudness contour at $N$ phons passes through $(1000\,\text{Hz},\, N\,\text{dB})$.

Sone — a linear scale of perceived loudness. By definition, 1 sone = 40 phons. Each 10-phon increase doubles the sone value: $S = 2^{(P-40)/10}$ where $P$ is phons. A 60-phon sound is 4 sones.

A-weighting is a frequency-weighting curve applied to SPL measurements to approximate the ear's sensitivity. It attenuates low and very high frequencies, matching the 40-phon equal-loudness curve. Noise measurements are often quoted in dB(A) (decibels A-weighted) because this better predicts hearing damage risk and perceived annoyance.

$$A(f) \approx \frac{12200^2 f^4}{(f^2+20.6^2)\sqrt{(f^2+107.7^2)(f^2+737.9^2)}(f^2+12200^2)}$$

Frequency Perception

Pitch perception is logarithmic in frequency. An octave — the most fundamental musical interval — corresponds to a doubling of frequency. This is why we use a log frequency axis for spectrograms and equal-loudness plots. The musical note A4 is 440 Hz; A5 is 880 Hz; A3 is 220 Hz.

The frequency of any musical note $n$ semitones above A4 (440 Hz) is:

$$f = 440 \times 2^{n/12} \text{ Hz}$$

The just noticeable frequency difference (JND) for a pure tone is approximately 0.5% of the frequency (about 1/10th of a semitone) for trained listeners in the midrange. This corresponds roughly to $\Delta f/f \approx 0.005$, or in cents: $1200\log_2(1.005) \approx 8.6$ cents.

Critical Bands and the Bark Scale

The cochlea groups nearby frequencies into critical bands — regions where simultaneous tones interfere strongly (creating the sensation of roughness or masking). There are approximately 24 critical bands across the audible range, called Bark bands after Heinrich Barkhausen.

Below ~500 Hz each critical band spans about 100 Hz; above 500 Hz they are approximately 1/3 octave wide. The Bark frequency scale maps $f$ (Hz) to $z$ (Bark):

$$z = 13 \arctan(0.00076f) + 3.5 \arctan\!\left(\frac{f}{7500}\right)^2 \text{ Bark}$$

Temporal Masking

A loud sound can render nearby quiet sounds inaudible even if they do not overlap in time. This is temporal masking:

  • Forward masking — A loud masker makes a following soft sound inaudible for up to ~200 ms after the masker ends. The threshold decays roughly exponentially.
  • Backward masking — More surprising: a masker can mask sounds up to ~5 ms before it arrives, because neural processing has finite latency.
  • Simultaneous masking — Sounds overlapping in time that are below the masking threshold are inaudible.

Audio codecs exploit forward masking aggressively: quantization noise introduced during a loud transient is hidden by the masking effect, allowing coarser encoding of the few frames following a loud event.

Frequency Masking

Simultaneous masking in frequency is even more powerful. A loud tone raises the threshold of hearing for nearby frequencies — quiet sounds near the masker become inaudible. The region of elevated threshold is called the masking pattern.

Key properties of frequency masking:

  • Masking is asymmetric: a masker masks more effectively above its frequency than below.
  • The critical bandwidth (roughly 1/3 octave) defines the frequency resolution of masking. Tones within the same critical band interact most strongly.
  • Masking threshold rises approximately 25 dB/octave on the low-frequency side and falls more gradually (~10 dB/octave) on the high-frequency side.
The Foundation of Perceptual Audio Coding

MP3 and AAC exploit frequency masking directly. The encoder computes the masking threshold at each time frame, then allocates bits only to spectral components above the threshold. Quantization noise is shaped to stay below the masking threshold — the noise is present, but inaudible. This is why a 128 kbps MP3 sounds nearly identical to a 1,411 kbps WAV to most listeners.

Spatial Hearing

Humans localize sound using two primary cues, each dominant at different frequencies:

  • ITD — Interaural Time Difference: The time delay between sound arriving at the left vs. right ear. The maximum ITD (sound from directly to the side) is approximately 650 μs. Effective for frequencies below ~1.5 kHz where the phase difference is unambiguous.
  • ILD — Interaural Level Difference: The level difference between the ears, caused by the head's acoustic shadow. The head diffracts low-frequency sound around it (small ILD) but blocks high-frequency sound (large ILD). Effective above ~1.5 kHz.

The crossover at ~1.5 kHz between ITD and ILD dominance is known as the duplex theory of sound localization (Lord Rayleigh, 1907).

The Head-Related Transfer Function (HRTF) is the frequency response from a free-field sound source to the eardrum, encoding elevation and front/back cues via the pinna's shape-dependent reflections. Convolution with an HRTF creates the perception of a sound in 3D space — the principle behind binaural audio and VR spatialization.

Psychoacoustic Models

The MPEG-1 Audio (MP3) standard defines a psychoacoustic model that computes, for each time frame of audio, the maximum allowable quantization noise level at each frequency — the masking threshold. The encoder allocates bits such that the noise stays below this threshold.

The algorithm proceeds as follows:

  1. FFT analysis — Transform the audio frame to the frequency domain.
  2. Tonality estimation — Classify each spectral component as tonal (pure tone) or noise-like. Tonal maskers are more efficient maskers.
  3. Masking threshold computation — For each masker, spread its masking curve across frequency (accounting for the spread of excitation on the basilar membrane). Sum all masking contributions, add the absolute hearing threshold.
  4. SMR computation — Signal-to-Mask Ratio: $\text{SMR}[k] = 10\log_{10}(P[k]/T[k])$ where $P[k]$ is signal power and $T[k]$ is masking threshold at subband $k$. Higher SMR = needs more bits.
  5. Bit allocation — Iteratively assign bits to subbands in proportion to SMR until the bit budget is exhausted.
  6. Quantization — Quantize each subband with the allocated bits; noise stays below masking threshold.
$$\text{SMR}[k] = 10\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{P_{\text{signal}}[k]}{T_{\text{mask}}[k]}\right) \text{ dB}$$
Why 128 kbps MP3 Works

Uncompressed stereo CD audio requires 1,411 kbps. A 128 kbps MP3 achieves an 11:1 compression ratio. This is possible because: (1) much of the spectrum below the masking threshold can be discarded; (2) temporal masking allows coarser encoding after loud transients; (3) the encoder uses Huffman coding of the quantized coefficients. The result: the reconstructed audio is perceptually indistinguishable from the original for most listeners under typical listening conditions — despite containing significant numerical differences.

Exercises: Psychoacoustics & Human Hearing

Easy (1–10)

1Phon to Sone ConversionEasy

Convert 60 phons to sones using the formula $S = 2^{(P-40)/10}$.

$S = 2^{(60-40)/10} = 2^2 = 4$ sones.

A 60-phon sound is perceived as 4 times as loud as a 40-phon (1-sone) reference.

2Sone to Phon ConversionEasy

A sound is measured at 16 sones. What is its loudness level in phons?

$S = 2^{(P-40)/10} \Rightarrow 16 = 2^{(P-40)/10} \Rightarrow (P-40)/10 = \log_2 16 = 4 \Rightarrow P = 80$ phons.

3Frequency of a Musical NoteEasy

What is the frequency of C5 (middle C is C4 = 261.63 Hz)? Use $f = f_0 \times 2^{n/12}$ where C4 to C5 is 12 semitones.

$f = 261.63 \times 2^{12/12} = 261.63 \times 2 = 523.25$ Hz.

Each octave doubles the frequency.

4Octave CalculationEasy

How many octaves are there between 20 Hz and 20,000 Hz?

Number of octaves $= \log_2(20000/20) = \log_2(1000) = \log_2(1000)$.

$\log_2(1000) = \log_{10}(1000)/\log_{10}(2) = 3/0.301 \approx 9.97$ octaves.

The audible range spans approximately 10 octaves.

5JND in FrequencyEasy

A tone at 1000 Hz is presented. What is the smallest frequency change a trained listener can detect? (JND = 0.5%)

$\Delta f = 0.005 \times 1000 = 5$ Hz.

A change from 1000 Hz to 1005 Hz (or 995 Hz) is at the threshold of detectability.

6SPL at ThresholdEasy

The threshold of hearing at 1 kHz is 0 dB SPL. The threshold at 100 Hz is approximately 30 dB SPL. How many times greater is the sound pressure at 100 Hz threshold compared to 1 kHz threshold?

$\Delta L = 30$ dB $= 20\log_{10}(p_2/p_1) \Rightarrow p_2/p_1 = 10^{30/20} = 10^{1.5} \approx 31.6$.

The pressure at 100 Hz threshold is about 31.6 times greater than at 1 kHz threshold.

7Maximum ITDEasy

The distance between ears is approximately 17 cm and the speed of sound is 343 m/s. Estimate the maximum interaural time difference (ITD).

Maximum ITD occurs when sound arrives from directly to the side. The path difference is approximately the distance between ears: $d = 0.17$ m.

$\text{ITD}_{\max} = d/c = 0.17/343 \approx 495\ \mu\text{s} \approx 0.5\ \text{ms}$.

(The commonly cited value is ~650 μs because the path around the head is longer than the straight-line distance between ears.)

8Semitones to Frequency RatioEasy

What is the frequency ratio corresponding to a musical fifth (7 semitones)?

Ratio $= 2^{7/12} = 2^{0.5833} \approx 1.498 \approx 3:2$.

This is why the perfect fifth sounds consonant — the frequency ratio is close to the simple integer ratio 3:2, producing minimal beating between harmonics.

9Loudness DoublingEasy

A sound plays at 70 dB SPL. By how many dB must the level increase to sound twice as loud?

Perceived loudness doubles every 10 phons (at 1 kHz, phons = dB SPL).

An increase of 10 dB SPL doubles the perceived loudness.

New level = 70 + 10 = 80 dB SPL.

10Critical Band WidthEasy

Estimate the critical bandwidth at 1 kHz and at 4 kHz. (Above 500 Hz, critical bandwidths are approximately 1/3 octave.)

1/3 octave bandwidth: multiply center frequency by $2^{1/3} \approx 1.26$ and the ratio of upper to lower boundary is $2^{1/6} \approx 1.122$ either side.

At 1 kHz: $\Delta f \approx 1000 \times (2^{1/6} - 2^{-1/6}) \approx 1000 \times 0.231 \approx 231$ Hz.

At 4 kHz: $\Delta f \approx 4000 \times 0.231 \approx 925$ Hz.

Critical bandwidths grow with frequency — reflecting the logarithmic spacing of hair cells on the basilar membrane.

Medium (11–20)

11A-Weighting AttenuationMedium

A-weighting attenuates 100 Hz by approximately −19.1 dB relative to 1 kHz. If a noise source has equal energy at 100 Hz and 1 kHz (both at 60 dB SPL), what is the A-weighted level at 100 Hz?

A-weighted level at 100 Hz = $60 + (-19.1) = 40.9$ dB(A).

A-weighted level at 1 kHz = $60 + 0 = 60$ dB(A).

The 1 kHz component sounds over 6× louder (in sones) to a human listener than the 100 Hz component, even though both have identical SPL.

12Masking Threshold CalculationMedium

A 1 kHz tone at 80 dB SPL raises the masking threshold at 2 kHz by approximately 30 dB above the quiet threshold. If the quiet threshold at 2 kHz is 10 dB SPL, what is the masked threshold at 2 kHz? What is the maximum coding noise allowed at 2 kHz?

Masked threshold at 2 kHz = quiet threshold + masking elevation = $10 + 30 = 40$ dB SPL.

The maximum allowable coding noise at 2 kHz is 40 dB SPL — any noise below this level is inaudible.

Compare: without masking, any noise above 10 dB SPL would be audible. Masking raised the noise budget by 30 dB, allowing $10^{30/20} \approx 31.6\times$ more noise amplitude before it becomes perceptible.

13Bark Scale ConversionMedium

Using the simplified formula $z = 13\arctan(0.00076f) + 3.5\arctan(f/7500)^2$, calculate the Bark value for $f = 1000$ Hz and $f = 8000$ Hz.

At $f = 1000$ Hz: $z = 13\arctan(0.76) + 3.5\arctan(1000/7500)^2$.

$\arctan(0.76) \approx 0.6500$ rad; $13 \times 0.6500 = 8.45$. $\arctan(0.1333) \approx 0.1326$ rad; $3.5 \times (0.1326)^2 = 3.5 \times 0.01758 = 0.062$. Total: $\approx 8.51$ Bark.

At $f = 8000$ Hz: $z = 13\arctan(6.08) + 3.5\arctan(1.067)^2$. $\arctan(6.08) \approx 1.406$ rad; $13 \times 1.406 = 18.28$. $\arctan(1.067) \approx 0.820$ rad; $3.5 \times 0.672 = 2.35$. Total: $\approx 20.6$ Bark.

14Forward Masking DecayMedium

Forward masking decays by about 10 dB per decade of time. A masker at 80 dB SPL ends at $t = 0$. Estimate the masked threshold at $t = 10$ ms and $t = 100$ ms after the masker ends.

The masking threshold at $t = 0$ (just after masker) is at the masker level: ~80 dB SPL.

Decay of 10 dB per decade of time (i.e., per factor of 10 in time).

At $t = 10$ ms: one decade from 1 ms baseline → threshold $\approx 80 - 10 = 70$ dB SPL.

At $t = 100$ ms: two decades from 1 ms baseline → threshold $\approx 80 - 20 = 60$ dB SPL.

By $t \approx 200$ ms the threshold returns to the quiet threshold, so forward masking is effectively gone within ~200 ms of the masker.

15SMR ComputationMedium

In a particular subband, the signal power is $P = 10^{-3}$ W and the masking threshold power is $T = 10^{-6}$ W. Compute the Signal-to-Mask Ratio in dB.

$\text{SMR} = 10\log_{10}(P/T) = 10\log_{10}(10^{-3}/10^{-6}) = 10\log_{10}(10^3) = 30$ dB.

This subband needs bits allocated to it (SMR > 0 dB means signal is above the masking threshold).

16ITD Phase AmbiguityMedium

Show that for ITD-based localization, there is a phase ambiguity for frequencies above $f_{\max} = 1/(2 \times \text{ITD}_{\max})$. With $\text{ITD}_{\max} = 650\ \mu\text{s}$, find $f_{\max}$.

Phase ambiguity arises when the interaural phase difference exceeds $\pm\pi$ (half a cycle). At frequency $f$, the maximum phase difference is $2\pi f \times \text{ITD}_{\max}$.

Setting this to $\pi$: $2\pi f_{\max} \times \text{ITD}_{\max} = \pi \Rightarrow f_{\max} = 1/(2 \times \text{ITD}_{\max})$.

$f_{\max} = 1/(2 \times 650 \times 10^{-6}) = 1/0.0013 \approx 769$ Hz.

More conservatively with ITD up to the full 650 μs, unambiguous localization is limited to below ~770 Hz, broadly consistent with the ~1.5 kHz crossover (which accounts for envelope-based ITD cues allowing some higher-frequency localization).

17Equal Loudness at Bass FrequenciesMedium

A 100 Hz tone needs to be at 50 dB SPL to sound as loud as a 1 kHz tone at 40 dB SPL (i.e., the 40-phon contour passes through 50 dB at 100 Hz). If you reduce the playback level by 20 dB, why does the bass seem to disappear disproportionately?

The equal-loudness contours are not parallel — they converge at high SPLs and diverge at low SPLs.

Original: 1 kHz at 40 dB, 100 Hz at 50 dB → both at 40 phons. After −20 dB reduction: 1 kHz at 20 dB, 100 Hz at 30 dB.

At 20 phons, the 100 Hz component at 30 dB SPL is near or below the hearing threshold at 100 Hz (~35–45 dB SPL depending on the individual). The 1 kHz at 20 dB is still audible (threshold ~0 dB at 1 kHz).

This is why hi-fi amplifiers have a "loudness" button — it boosts bass (and sometimes treble) at low listening levels to compensate for the ear's reduced low-frequency sensitivity, restoring the perceived spectral balance.

18Compression Ratio in MP3Medium

A 1-minute 44.1 kHz / 16-bit / stereo audio file is compressed to 128 kbps MP3. Calculate the compression ratio and the file size of the MP3.

Uncompressed bit rate: $44100 \times 16 \times 2 = 1{,}411{,}200$ bps = 1411.2 kbps.

Compression ratio: $1411.2 / 128 \approx 11.0 : 1$.

MP3 file size: $128{,}000 \text{ bits/s} \times 60 \text{ s} = 7{,}680{,}000$ bits = 960,000 bytes $\approx 0.92$ MB.

Compare: uncompressed WAV would be $\approx 10.1$ MB per minute.

19Beats and Critical BandsMedium

Two pure tones at 1000 Hz and 1010 Hz are played simultaneously. Describe what you hear. What if the second tone is at 1200 Hz?

At 1000 Hz + 1010 Hz: frequency difference $= 10$ Hz, well within the critical band (~230 Hz wide at 1 kHz). The two tones cannot be resolved by the cochlea into separate pitches; they interfere, producing beating at 10 Hz — a rapid amplitude fluctuation perceived as roughness or tremolo.

At 1000 Hz + 1200 Hz: frequency difference $= 200$ Hz, still within the critical band (~230 Hz). This produces roughness/dissonance as the tones interact in the same critical band, but at a rate too fast for individual beats to be heard.

For tones separated by more than one critical bandwidth (e.g., 1000 + 1300 Hz), they occupy different critical bands and sound as two distinct, simultaneous pitches with consonance or mild dissonance depending on the interval.

20Hair Cell TuningMedium

The basilar membrane has approximately 3,500 inner hair cells spanning the 10-octave audible range. Assuming logarithmic spacing, estimate how many hair cells cover the octave from 1 kHz to 2 kHz. How does this relate to frequency resolution?

With logarithmic spacing over 10 octaves, each octave contains approximately $3500/10 = 350$ hair cells.

In the octave from 1 kHz to 2 kHz, there are ~350 hair cells spanning 1000 Hz, giving a spacing of $1000/350 \approx 2.9$ Hz per hair cell.

This is consistent with the JND of ~5 Hz at 1 kHz (about 2 hair-cell spacings) — the brain needs to compare activity across multiple hair cells to detect a pitch difference.

Note: outer hair cells (25,000) provide amplification and sharpening; inner hair cells (3,500) are the primary transducers sending signals to the auditory nerve.

Hard (21–30)

21Design a Perceptual Audio ModelHard

Outline a complete psychoacoustic model that computes the masking threshold from a PCM audio frame. Specify: (a) the frequency transform, (b) how to distinguish tonal vs. noise maskers, (c) how masking curves are spread, (d) how the global masking threshold is computed.

(a) Frequency transform: Apply a 1024-point FFT (with Hann window to reduce spectral leakage) to the audio frame at the analysis sample rate. Power spectrum: $P[k] = |X[k]|^2$. Convert to dB SPL using a calibration factor.

(b) Tonal vs. noise maskers: A spectral component at bin $k$ is classified as tonal if it is a local maximum and exceeds its neighbors by at least 7 dB: $P[k] > P[k\pm1]$ and $P[k] > P[k\pm2] + 7\,\text{dB}$. After extracting tonal maskers, the remaining power is aggregated into critical band-wide noise maskers. Tonal maskers are ~6 dB less effective than noise maskers at equal power (noise spreads energy; tones concentrate it).

(c) Spreading masking curves: For each masker at Bark position $z_m$ with level $L_m$ (dB), the masking contribution at Bark position $z$ (the spreadingfunction) is:

$$SF(z_m, z) = \begin{cases} 17(z-z_m) - 0.4 L_m + 11 & z_m \geq z \text{ (low side)} \\ (0.4 L_m + 6)(z - z_m) & z_m < z \text{ (high side)} \end{cases}$$

with appropriate limits. The masking threshold contribution from masker $m$ at Bark $z$ is $T_m(z) = L_m + SF(z_m, z)$.

(d) Global masking threshold: Sum all masker contributions and the absolute hearing threshold in the linear (power) domain:

$$T_{\text{global}}(z) = 10\log_{10}\!\left(10^{T_{\text{abs}}(z)/10} + \sum_m 10^{T_m(z)/10}\right) \text{ dB}$$

This is the minimum masking threshold — any component below $T_{\text{global}}$ is inaudible and can be discarded or coarsely quantized.

22Masking in MP3 Bit AllocationHard

An MP3 encoder has 32 subbands. In one frame, the SMR values (dB) are: subbands 1–8 each have SMR = 30 dB, subbands 9–16 have SMR = 0 dB (masked), subbands 17–32 have SMR = −10 dB (below masking threshold). Total bit budget = 384 bits. How should bits be allocated, and roughly how many bits per sample does subband 1 get if each subband contains 12 samples?

Subbands 9–32 have SMR ≤ 0 dB — their signal is at or below the masking threshold. They need 0 bits (their noise is already masked).

Only subbands 1–8 (SMR = 30 dB each) require bits. Total bits = 384, shared among 8 subbands.

Simple equal allocation: $384 / 8 = 48$ bits per subband. Each subband has 12 samples, so bits per sample $= 48 / 12 = 4$ bits per sample.

At 4 bits/sample, quantization noise is approximately $6.02 \times 4 + 1.76 \approx 25.8$ dB below full scale. For the signal to remain above the noise, SMR must be ≥ ~26 dB — roughly satisfied for our SMR = 30 dB subbands. In practice, the encoder iterates, assigning extra bits to subbands where quantization noise still exceeds the masking threshold.

23HRTF Convolution for Binaural RenderingHard

A binaural audio renderer convolves a mono signal $x[n]$ with HRTF impulse responses $h_L[n]$ and $h_R[n]$ (length $N = 512$ taps each) at 44.1 kHz. (a) What is the computational cost of direct convolution per second of audio? (b) How does overlap-add FFT convolution reduce this? (c) What perceptual artifacts occur if the HRTF is wrong for the listener?

(a) Direct convolution: Each output sample requires $N = 512$ multiplications and additions. For both ears: $2 \times 512 = 1024$ operations per sample. At 44,100 samples/s: $1024 \times 44100 \approx 45.2 \times 10^6$ multiply-accumulates per second.

(b) Overlap-add FFT: Process in blocks of $L$ samples. FFT size $M = L + N - 1 \approx 2N$ (next power of 2). Cost per block: $2 \times M\log_2 M$ for the two FFTs + $M$ complex multiplications + $M\log_2 M$ for the inverse FFT. For $L = 512$, $M = 1024$: $\approx 3 \times 1024 \times 10 = 30{,}720$ operations per $L$ samples = $30720/512 \approx 60$ operations/sample — versus 1024 for direct. Speedup $\approx 17\times$.

(c) Wrong HRTF artifacts: If the HRTF is from a different person's ears/head, the spectral coloration cues embedded in the HRTF will not match the listener's expectations from their own pinna. The result is: (1) in-head localization — sound appears inside the head rather than externalized; (2) front-back reversals — inability to distinguish whether sound is in front of or behind the listener; (3) elevation errors — incorrect perceived height. These artifacts are why personalized HRTFs provide much better spatialization than generic ones.

24Loudness Normalization (EBU R 128)Hard

EBU R 128 defines integrated loudness $L_I$ using K-weighting and the ITU-R BS.1770 algorithm. Explain (a) how K-weighting differs from A-weighting, (b) the gating mechanism (absolute gate −70 LUFS, relative gate −10 LU), and (c) why streaming platforms target −14 LUFS integrated loudness.

(a) K-weighting vs. A-weighting: A-weighting is derived from the 40-phon equal-loudness contour and attenuates both low and high frequencies relative to 1–4 kHz. K-weighting uses two stages: (1) a pre-filter (shelving boost above 1 kHz, modeling the acoustic effect of the head) and (2) a high-pass filter (RLB weighting, attenuating below ~60 Hz). K-weighting is calibrated to correlate with perceived loudness for broadcast content, not pure tones. Above ~4 kHz, K-weighting is less attenuating than A-weighting.

(b) Gating: The signal is analyzed in 400 ms blocks with 75% overlap. Block power (in LUFS) is computed. Absolute gate: discard blocks below −70 LUFS (silence/near-silence). Relative gate: compute the mean loudness of non-gated blocks; discard blocks more than 10 LU below this mean (very quiet passages in a dynamic program). Integrated loudness $L_I$ is the mean of the remaining blocks. This ensures that long silences in a broadcast don't artificially lower the perceived loudness measurement.

(c) −14 LUFS targeting: Streaming platforms (Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube) normalize to approximately −14 LUFS integrated. This value balances: (1) sufficient headroom (peak-to-loudness ratio) to avoid clipping after normalization; (2) a comfortable listening level that works across genres; (3) discouraging the "loudness war" — mastering louder than −14 LUFS causes the platform to turn your track down, eliminating any perceived loudness advantage while destroying dynamic range.

25Auditory Scene AnalysisHard

Bregman's Auditory Scene Analysis identifies principles by which the auditory system groups sound components into streams. For each principle, describe the physical cue and a real-world example: (a) harmonicity, (b) common onset, (c) spatial coherence, (d) frequency proximity.

(a) Harmonicity: Components whose frequencies are integer multiples of a common fundamental are grouped into a single auditory stream (perceived as one sound with a pitch corresponding to the fundamental). Physical cue: integer ratios in the spectrum. Example: the overtones of a violin string (440, 880, 1320, 1760 Hz...) fuse into the perception of a single violin tone rather than separate tones.

(b) Common onset: Components that start simultaneously tend to be grouped together. Physical cue: synchronized attack transient. Example: when a chord is struck on a piano, all notes start at the same instant — even though harmonics of different notes may overlap in frequency, the common onset helps segregate each note as a separate "object." This is why staggered attacks (as in a strummed guitar) make individual notes more separable.

(c) Spatial coherence: Components appearing to come from the same location (same ITD and ILD pattern) are grouped into one stream. Physical cue: matching HRTF cues. Example: at a cocktail party, you can follow one voice spatially distinct from others — the "cocktail party effect." This also underlies stereo channel separation.

(d) Frequency proximity: Spectral components close in frequency tend to be grouped together; large frequency jumps promote stream segregation. Physical cue: proximity on the basilar membrane. Example: a rapidly alternating sequence of tones (e.g., ABABAB) splits into two separate melodic streams when the frequency difference between A and B exceeds ~6 semitones — known as auditory streaming or Bregman's streaming paradigm.

26Nonlinear Distortion in the CochleaHard

The ear produces combination tones — phantom frequencies not present in the input — due to nonlinear mechanical processing in the cochlea. For two tones at $f_1 = 1000$ Hz and $f_2 = 1200$ Hz, predict the frequencies of the cubic difference tone (CDT) and the quadratic difference tone (QDT). Why are CDTs more audible than QDTs?

Quadratic difference tone (QDT): $f_{\text{QDT}} = f_2 - f_1 = 1200 - 1000 = 200$ Hz.

Cubic difference tone (CDT): $f_{\text{CDT}} = 2f_1 - f_2 = 2000 - 1200 = 800$ Hz.

Why CDTs are more audible: The QDT at 200 Hz falls outside the critical band of the two primary tones (both near 1 kHz). It is a low-frequency tone and thus subject to the ear's reduced sensitivity at low frequencies — and at moderate SPLs it may fall below the masking threshold or the absolute threshold. Additionally, headphones and speakers often have poor low-frequency response, further attenuating QDTs.

The CDT at 800 Hz is closer in frequency to the primaries and falls in a region of high sensitivity. Because it arises from the third-order (cubic) nonlinearity, it grows 3 dB per 1 dB increase in the primary level (rather than 2 dB for QDT), making it more prominent at typical listening levels. CDTs are exploited musically — on instruments like the theremin or electronic organs, combination tones reinforce the perceived bass pitch.

27Perceptual Entropy BoundHard

Johnston (1988) estimated the perceptual entropy of music at ~60–100 bits per frame (1024 samples at 44.1 kHz). (a) Convert this to a bit rate. (b) Compare with CD and with 128 kbps MP3. (c) What does this imply about the theoretical minimum transparent audio codec bit rate?

(a) Bit rate from perceptual entropy: Frame duration = $1024/44100 \approx 23.2$ ms. Frame rate = $44100/1024 \approx 43.1$ frames/s. Using 80 bits/frame (midpoint): $80 \times 43.1 \approx 3450$ bits/s per channel. Stereo: $\approx 6.9$ kbps.

(b) Comparison: CD = 1411.2 kbps (all information, including inaudible). 128 kbps MP3 = 128 kbps (11:1 compression). Perceptual entropy bound $\approx 7$ kbps (theoretical minimum for transparency). MP3 at 128 kbps is about 18× above the perceptual entropy bound.

(c) Implication: In theory, a perfect perceptual encoder could achieve transparency (indistinguishability from uncompressed audio) at ~7–15 kbps — a 100:1 compression ratio from CD. Modern codecs (Opus, AAC-LD) approach this bound for speech (~6 kbps), but music remains harder due to complex masking interactions. In practice, 32–64 kbps is considered near-transparent for music by most listeners, still 4–10× above the theoretical bound, suggesting further improvements are possible.

28Pre-echo Artifact in Transform CodecsHard

MP3 uses 1152-sample frames. Explain the pre-echo artifact that occurs on percussive transients (e.g., a snare drum hit), why it cannot occur in the human ear, and how the MP3 encoder mitigates it using window switching.

The pre-echo artifact: MP3 analyzes a 1152-sample block (~26 ms at 44.1 kHz) as a single transform unit. If a sharp transient (snare hit) occurs near the end of the block, the encoder must spread quantization noise over the entire block duration. This noise arrives ~20 ms before the transient — a "pre-echo" or "chirp" audible as a faint copy of the attack preceding it.

Why it doesn't occur in the cochlea: The basilar membrane has an asymmetric impulse response — it responds to rising edges very quickly (within 1–2 ms) via active amplification by outer hair cells. Forward masking then hides the transient's quantization noise in the ~200 ms after the event. The cochlea never puts noise before a sound. The codec violates temporal causality within its block.

MP3 mitigation — window switching: The encoder computes a transient detection metric (energy increase between successive blocks). When a transient is detected, the encoder switches from long blocks (1024-point MDCT) to short blocks (three 256-point MDCTs per long-block period). Shorter blocks = finer temporal resolution = noise is confined to a ~6 ms window around the transient. The snare's masking effect can then hide the noise (backward masking ~5 ms, forward masking ~200 ms). The tradeoff: short blocks have coarser frequency resolution, which reduces coding efficiency for steady-state content.

29Binaural Masking Level DifferenceHard

The Binaural Masking Level Difference (BMLD) is the improvement in signal detectability when the signal or masker has different interaural configurations. Explain the NoSo vs. NπSo condition and why BMLD can be up to 15 dB. What neural mechanism underlies this?

NoSo condition (baseline): Both noise (N) and signal (S) are diotic ("o" = in phase, identical in both ears). The signal is detected at the monaural threshold — no binaural advantage.

NπSo condition: The noise is presented with opposite phase in the two ears (π = antiphase: left ear gets $+n(t)$, right ear gets $-n(t)$), while the signal remains diotic (identical in both ears). Detection threshold improves by up to 15 dB for low-frequency signals (<1 kHz).

Why 15 dB improvement: In the NπSo condition, the noise is fully correlated between ears but with inverted polarity — a pure ITD of half a period. When the brain computes the interaural cross-correlation (IACC), the noise sums to zero (Nπ + N = 0 in the central processor), while the diotic signal remains (So + So = 2So). The effective SNR at the binaural processor is dramatically improved. The maximum BMLD (~15 dB) occurs around 500 Hz where ITD processing is most effective.

Neural mechanism: Neurons in the medial superior olive (MSO) are sensitive to ITD — they fire maximally when inputs from left and right cochleae are coincident (Jeffress coincidence detector model). The NπSo condition creates an ITD pattern that activates MSO neurons tuned to the wrong delay for the noise but the correct delay (0) for the signal, separating them in the neural representation. This is exploited in stereo audio — slightly decorrelated noise in stereo is easier to hear through than monophonic noise.

30Cochlear Amplifier and Otoacoustic EmissionsHard

The ear is not a passive receiver — outer hair cells actively amplify basilar membrane motion, providing ~40–50 dB of gain at low sound levels. This cochlear amplifier also causes the ear to emit sounds (otoacoustic emissions, OAEs). (a) Explain the mechanism of outer hair cell electromotility. (b) Why does the cochlear amplifier produce OAEs? (c) How does this relate to the nonlinearity discussed in Exercise 26, and why do OAEs disappear with hearing loss?

(a) Outer hair cell electromotility: Outer hair cells (OHCs) contain a protein called prestin in their lateral membrane. Prestin is a piezoelectric-like motor — it changes the cell's length in response to changes in membrane voltage. When a sound deflects the basilar membrane, it opens mechanosensory channels at the tips of OHC stereocilia, depolarizing the cell. Prestin contracts the OHC, amplifying the basilar membrane motion. This happens at acoustic frequencies up to ~80 kHz — much faster than any molecular motor. The gain is frequency-selective (each OHC amplifies only near its characteristic frequency) and level-dependent (high-level sounds saturate the amplification, compressing the dynamic range from ~120 dB of acoustic input to ~40 dB of neural dynamic range).

(b) OAEs from the cochlear amplifier: The OHC feedback amplification is a positive feedback loop (vibration → OHC contraction → more vibration). Like any feedback amplifier, if the loop gain approaches unity, the system can sustain oscillations. Even at gains below instability, the active process causes the basilar membrane to reflect acoustic energy back toward the oval window and into the ear canal, where it can be measured as a faint sound (OAE). Spontaneous OAEs occur in ~70% of normal-hearing adults and are always tonal — they represent the cochlea "ringing" at its natural resonant frequencies.

(c) Connection to nonlinearity and hearing loss: The prestin-based amplification is inherently nonlinear (it saturates). This nonlinearity is the source of the combination tones in Exercise 26 — the $2f_1 - f_2$ CDT arises directly from the cubic nonlinearity of OHC transduction. This is confirmed by the fact that CDTs can be measured as OAEs. When OHCs are damaged (by noise exposure, ototoxic drugs, aging), the amplifier is lost. Consequences: (1) OAEs disappear (used clinically to screen for OHC damage); (2) hearing threshold rises by 40–50 dB at the characteristic frequency; (3) frequency selectivity (tuning sharpness) degrades — damaged cochleae have broader critical bands; (4) the cochlear nonlinearity weakens, reducing combination tone generation. OAE screening is used universally in newborn hearing tests because it noninvasively assesses OHC function without requiring a behavioral response.
