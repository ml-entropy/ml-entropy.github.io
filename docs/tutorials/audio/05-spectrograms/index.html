The Time-Frequency Dilemma

The Fourier Transform tells you which frequencies are present — but not when they occur. A 3-second recording of a piano chord and the same notes played as an arpeggio have identical magnitude spectra. For audio analysis, we need both: what frequency, at what time.

But there's a fundamental limit. You can't know the exact frequency and exact time of an event simultaneously — this is the Gabor uncertainty principle (the acoustic analog of Heisenberg's):

$$\Delta t \cdot \Delta f \geq \frac{1}{4\pi}$$

Intuition: A short drum hit is precisely located in time but has no clear pitch (many frequencies). A long sustained violin note has a very clear pitch but occupies a large time window. You can't have both precision simultaneously.

Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT)

The STFT is the standard solution: apply the DFT to short, overlapping frames of the signal. For each frame, multiply the signal by a window function, then compute the FFT:

$$X(m, k) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n + mH] \cdot w[n] \cdot e^{-j2\pi kn/N}$$

where $m$ is the frame index, $k$ is the frequency bin, $N$ is the window/FFT size, $H$ is the hop size (how far the window slides between frames), and $w[n]$ is the window function.

Key parameters and their effects:

  • Time resolution: $\Delta t = N / f_s$ — determined by window size. Smaller $N$ = better time resolution.
  • Frequency resolution: $\Delta f = f_s / N$ — also determined by window size. Larger $N$ = better frequency resolution.
  • Hop size: $H$ samples between frames. Common: $H = N/4$ (75% overlap) or $H = N/2$ (50% overlap).
  • Overlap: $(N - H)/N \times 100\%$. More overlap = smoother time axis but more computation.
The Fundamental Tradeoff

$\Delta t \times \Delta f = 1$. A 1024-sample window at 44.1 kHz gives: $\Delta t = 23.2$ ms, $\Delta f = 43.1$ Hz. A 256-sample window: $\Delta t = 5.8$ ms, $\Delta f = 172.3$ Hz. You always trade one for the other. Choose based on your application.

Window Functions

Why not just use a rectangular window (cut a segment and FFT it)? Because sharp edges in time create spectral leakage — energy smears across frequency bins. Smooth window functions taper the edges to reduce leakage.

The Spectrogram

The spectrogram is the squared magnitude of the STFT, displayed as a 2D image:

$$S(m, k) = |X(m, k)|^2$$

Convention: horizontal axis = time (frame index $m$), vertical axis = frequency (bin $k$), color/brightness = magnitude (usually in dB). It's the workhorse of audio visualization.

Reading Spectrograms

Horizontal lines = sustained tones (harmonics). Vertical lines = transients (clicks, consonants). Diagonal lines = frequency sweeps (chirps, slides). Broadband fill = noise. The spacing between harmonics reveals the fundamental frequency.

Mel Spectrograms

Human pitch perception is roughly logarithmic: the perceptual difference between 100 Hz and 200 Hz (one octave) feels the same as between 1000 Hz and 2000 Hz. The mel scale captures this:

$$m = 2595 \log_{10}\!\left(1 + \frac{f}{700}\right)$$

Inverse: $f = 700\left(10^{m/2595} - 1\right)$

Constant-Q Transform

The Constant-Q Transform (CQT) uses windows whose length varies with frequency: long windows for low frequencies (good frequency resolution where you need it) and short windows for high frequencies (good time resolution for transients). The quality factor $Q = f/\Delta f$ is constant across all frequency bins.

This matches musical structure perfectly: each octave gets the same number of bins (typically 12 for semitones), and the frequency resolution is proportional to the frequency — just like human hearing.

STFT vs. Mel Spectrogram vs. CQT

STFT spectrogram: Uniform time-frequency resolution. Best for general analysis.
Mel spectrogram: Perceptually weighted. Best for ML and speech processing.
CQT: Logarithmic frequency axis. Best for music analysis (pitch tracking, chord detection).

Easy (1–10)

1Hz to Mel ConversionEasy

Convert 1000 Hz to the mel scale.

$$m = 2595\log_{10}(1 + 1000/700) = 2595\log_{10}(2.4286) = 2595 \times 0.3856 = 1000.5 \text{ mel}$$

Note: the mel scale was designed so that 1000 Hz ≈ 1000 mel.

2Mel to Hz ConversionEasy

Convert 2500 mel back to Hz.

$$f = 700(10^{2500/2595} - 1) = 700(10^{0.9634} - 1) = 700(9.19 - 1) = 700 \times 8.19 = 5733 \text{ Hz}$$

3STFT Frame CountEasy

A 3-second audio at 44,100 Hz is analyzed with window size $N = 2048$ and hop size $H = 512$. How many STFT frames are produced?

Total samples: $44100 \times 3 = 132{,}300$. Frames $\approx \lfloor(132300 - 2048)/512\rfloor + 1 = \lfloor 130252/512 \rfloor + 1 = 254 + 1 = 255$ frames.

4Frequency ResolutionEasy

With $f_s = 44100$ Hz and $N = 4096$, what is the frequency resolution $\Delta f$?

$\Delta f = f_s / N = 44100 / 4096 \approx 10.77$ Hz per bin.

5Time ResolutionEasy

Same setting: $f_s = 44100$, $N = 4096$. What is the time resolution (window duration)?

$\Delta t = N/f_s = 4096/44100 \approx 92.9$ ms.

6Overlap PercentageEasy

If $N = 1024$ and $H = 256$, what is the overlap percentage?

Overlap $= (N - H)/N = (1024 - 256)/1024 = 768/1024 = 75\%$.

7Number of Frequency BinsEasy

An FFT of size $N = 2048$ produces how many unique frequency bins? What is the highest frequency represented if $f_s = 48000$ Hz?

Unique bins: $N/2 + 1 = 1025$ (from DC to Nyquist). Highest frequency: $f_s/2 = 24{,}000$ Hz.

8Spectrogram DimensionsEasy

A 10-second mono audio at 16 kHz analyzed with $N=512$, $H=128$. What are the spectrogram dimensions (time frames × frequency bins)?

Samples: $160{,}000$. Frames: $\lfloor(160000-512)/128\rfloor + 1 = 1246$. Freq bins: $512/2 + 1 = 257$.

Spectrogram: $1246 \times 257$.

9Mel Filterbank SpacingEasy

If a mel filterbank covers 0–8000 Hz with 40 filters, what is the spacing in mel?

8000 Hz in mel: $2595\log_{10}(1 + 8000/700) = 2595\log_{10}(12.43) = 2595 \times 1.094 = 2840$ mel.

With 40 filters and 42 boundary points (including 0 and end): spacing = $2840/41 \approx 69.3$ mel.

10Window Length for MusicEasy

You want to distinguish notes a semitone apart near middle C (261.6 Hz vs 277.2 Hz). What minimum window length $N$ do you need at $f_s = 44100$ Hz?

Frequency difference: $277.2 - 261.6 = 15.6$ Hz. Need $\Delta f \leq 15.6$ Hz.

$N \geq f_s/\Delta f = 44100/15.6 \approx 2827$. Use $N = 4096$ (next power of 2).

Medium (11–20)

11STFT Time-Frequency GridMedium

Design STFT parameters for real-time speech processing: $f_s = 16000$ Hz, target frame rate = 100 frames/sec, want $\Delta f \leq 30$ Hz. Find $N$, $H$, overlap.

Frame rate = $f_s/H = 100 \Rightarrow H = 160$ samples (10 ms). Need $\Delta f = f_s/N \leq 30 \Rightarrow N \geq 533$. Use $N = 1024$.

Overlap: $(1024 - 160)/1024 = 84.4\%$. $\Delta f = 15.6$ Hz, $\Delta t = 64$ ms window, 10 ms hop.

12Window Function EffectMedium

A signal has two tones at 1000 Hz (0 dB) and 1050 Hz (−40 dB). Can a rectangular window resolve them with $N = 1024$, $f_s = 44100$? What about a Blackman window?

$\Delta f = 44100/1024 = 43.1$ Hz. The tones are 50 Hz apart > $\Delta f$, so they're in different bins.

Rectangular window: side lobes at −13 dB. The strong tone's leakage at 50 Hz offset is ∼−26 dB (falls off as $1/\Delta$), which overwhelms the −40 dB tone. Cannot resolve.

Blackman window: side lobes at −58 dB. Leakage at 50 Hz offset well below −40 dB. Can resolve, but at the cost of wider main lobe (3×).

13Mel Filterbank DesignMedium

Design a mel filterbank with 10 filters covering 300–8000 Hz. Calculate the center frequencies in Hz for each filter.

300 Hz → $m_{\text{low}} = 2595\log_{10}(1.429) = 401.3$ mel. 8000 Hz → $m_{\text{high}} = 2840.0$ mel.

12 equally spaced points (including endpoints): spacing = $(2840 - 401.3)/11 = 221.7$ mel.

Mel points: 401.3, 623.0, 844.7, 1066.4, 1288.1, 1509.8, 1731.5, 1953.2, 2174.9, 2396.6, 2618.3, 2840.0.

Convert back to Hz: 300, 462, 681, 977, 1376, 1912, 2627, 3581, 4858, 6571, 8000 Hz (approximate centers of the 10 filters).

14Choosing Window SizeMedium

For drum transcription (onset detection), would you use $N = 256$ or $N = 4096$ at $f_s = 44100$? Justify.

$N = 256$: $\Delta t = 5.8$ ms, $\Delta f = 172$ Hz. $N = 4096$: $\Delta t = 92.9$ ms, $\Delta f = 10.8$ Hz.

Use $N = 256$. Drum hits are short transients (~5–50 ms). With $N = 4096$, a 93 ms window would smear the onset across the frame and potentially merge closely spaced hits. We don't need precise frequency resolution for drums — we need precise timing.

15Constant-Q Bins per OctaveMedium

In a CQT with 24 bins per octave covering 55 Hz (A1) to 14080 Hz (A9), how many total bins are there? What is the frequency ratio between adjacent bins?

Octaves: $\log_2(14080/55) = \log_2(256) = 8$ octaves. Total bins: $8 \times 24 = 192$.

Frequency ratio: $2^{1/24} = 1.02930$ (quarter-tone resolution).

16Inverse STFT ReconstructionMedium

For perfect reconstruction via overlap-add ISTFT, what condition must the window satisfy? Verify that the Hann window with 75% overlap satisfies this.

The COLA (Constant Overlap-Add) condition: $\sum_m w[n - mH] = c$ for all $n$ (the overlapped windows sum to a constant).

Hann window: $w[n] = 0.5(1 - \cos(2\pi n/N))$. With $H = N/4$ (75% overlap), at any point 4 windows overlap. Their sum:

$w[n] + w[n-H] + w[n-2H] + w[n-3H]$. Due to the cosine structure of Hann, this sum equals exactly 1.5 for all $n$ (a constant). Dividing by 1.5 gives perfect reconstruction. ✔

17Spectrogram MemoryMedium

You compute a spectrogram of a 5-minute stereo song at 44.1 kHz with $N = 2048$, $H = 512$. How much memory (in MB) does the magnitude spectrogram require (32-bit floats, mono mix)?

Samples: $44100 \times 300 = 13{,}230{,}000$. Frames: $(13230000 - 2048)/512 + 1 \approx 25836$.

Freq bins: $1025$. Each value: 4 bytes. Total: $25836 \times 1025 \times 4 = 105{,}927{,}600$ bytes $\approx 101$ MB.

18Zero Padding EffectMedium

You have a 512-sample window but zero-pad the FFT to 2048. Does this improve frequency resolution? What does it actually do?

No — zero-padding does not improve the fundamental frequency resolution, which is determined by the window length: $\Delta f = f_s/512$ still applies.

What it does: it interpolates between the original frequency bins, giving a smoother-looking spectrum (4× more bins, spaced $f_s/2048$ apart). Think of it as upsampling in the frequency domain. Useful for more precise peak location but doesn't reveal frequencies that the window can't resolve.

19Mel Spectrogram for ASRMedium

The Whisper ASR model uses 80 mel bins, 25ms windows, 10ms hops at 16 kHz. Calculate $N$, $H$, and the mel spectrogram shape for 30 seconds of audio.

$N = 0.025 \times 16000 = 400$ samples. $H = 0.010 \times 16000 = 160$ samples.

Total samples: $16000 \times 30 = 480{,}000$. Frames: $(480000 - 400)/160 + 1 = 2998$.

Shape: $2998 \times 80$ (time frames × mel bins). This is a compact ~960 KB representation of 30 seconds of audio.

20Harmonic AnalysisMedium

On a spectrogram at 44.1 kHz with $N = 4096$, you see energy peaks at bins 30, 60, 90, and 120. What is the fundamental frequency? What note is this?

$\Delta f = 44100/4096 = 10.77$ Hz. Bin 30: $10.77 \times 30 = 323$ Hz. The pattern (30, 60, 90, 120) shows harmonics at 1:2:3:4 ratio.

Fundamental: $\approx 323$ Hz. This is close to E4 (329.6 Hz). The slight discrepancy is because real bin centers are approximate.

Hard (21–30)

21Uncertainty Principle ProofHard

The time-frequency uncertainty principle states $\sigma_t \cdot \sigma_f \geq 1/(4\pi)$, where $\sigma_t$ and $\sigma_f$ are the RMS durations in time and frequency. Prove that the Gaussian window $g(t) = e^{-\alpha t^2}$ achieves equality (it's the minimum-uncertainty window).

$g(t) = e^{-\alpha t^2}$. Its Fourier transform: $G(f) = \sqrt{\pi/\alpha}\, e^{-\pi^2 f^2/\alpha}$.

Time variance: $\sigma_t^2 = \frac{\int t^2 |g(t)|^2 dt}{\int |g(t)|^2 dt} = \frac{\int t^2 e^{-2\alpha t^2}dt}{\int e^{-2\alpha t^2}dt} = \frac{1}{4\alpha}$.

Frequency variance: $\sigma_f^2 = \frac{\int f^2 |G(f)|^2 df}{\int |G(f)|^2 df} = \frac{\alpha}{4\pi^2}$.

Product: $\sigma_t \sigma_f = \sqrt{\frac{1}{4\alpha} \cdot \frac{\alpha}{4\pi^2}} = \frac{1}{4\pi}$.

This is exactly $1/(4\pi)$, achieving the minimum. The Gaussian window is the unique minimum-uncertainty window. Any other window gives $\sigma_t \sigma_f > 1/(4\pi)$. □

22Phase Vocoder Time StretchHard

A phase vocoder stretches audio by factor 2 using STFT with $N=1024$, $H_a = 256$ (analysis hop), $H_s = 512$ (synthesis hop). Explain the phase adjustment needed for each bin and why simply using the STFT magnitudes with new hop causes artifacts.

At analysis hop $H_a$, bin $k$ expects phase advance $\Delta\phi_k = 2\pi k H_a/N$ between frames. At synthesis hop $H_s$, it should advance by $\Delta\phi_k' = 2\pi k H_s/N$.

For each bin: (1) Compute instantaneous frequency: $\omega_k = \angle X_m[k] - \angle X_{m-1}[k]$. (2) Unwrap to find true frequency deviation from bin center. (3) Propagate phase at synthesis hop: $\phi_s[k] += \omega_k \times H_s/H_a$.

Without phase adjustment: adjacent frames have inconsistent phases when overlap-added, causing destructive interference → "phasiness," metallic artifacts, and smeared transients. The phase vocoder maintains coherent phase evolution, but transients still suffer because the STFT time resolution is fixed.

23Multi-Resolution STFTHard

Design a multi-resolution analysis using three different window sizes for: (a) bass frequencies 20–300 Hz, (b) midrange 300–4000 Hz, (c) treble 4000–20000 Hz. Choose appropriate $N$ for each at $f_s = 44100$ Hz and justify.

(a) Bass: need to resolve down to ~20 Hz. $\Delta f \leq 10$ Hz → $N \geq 4410$. Use $N = 8192$ ($\Delta f = 5.4$ Hz, $\Delta t = 186$ ms). Bass changes slowly, so coarse time resolution is acceptable.

(b) Midrange: resolve ~50 Hz differences. $N \geq 882$. Use $N = 2048$ ($\Delta f = 21.5$ Hz, $\Delta t = 46$ ms). Good balance.

(c) Treble: need good time resolution for transients. Use $N = 512$ ($\Delta f = 86$ Hz, $\Delta t = 11.6$ ms). At 4 kHz+, 86 Hz resolution is ~2% — acceptable for a band that's 16 kHz wide.

This is essentially what the CQT achieves automatically with its frequency-dependent window length.

24Spectral Leakage AnalysisHard

A 1000 Hz tone is analyzed with $f_s = 44100$ and $N = 1000$ (non-standard). Show that because 1000 Hz doesn't land exactly on a bin center, spectral leakage occurs. Calculate the two nearest bins and the scalloping loss.

$\Delta f = 44100/1000 = 44.1$ Hz. The 1000 Hz tone falls at bin $k = 1000/44.1 = 22.676$ — between bins 22 (970.2 Hz) and 23 (1014.3 Hz).

With a rectangular window, the DFT of a tone at fractional bin $k_0 = 22.676$ has magnitude at bin $k$: $|X[k]| \propto |\text{sinc}(k - k_0)|$ (Dirichlet kernel, approximately). Energy leaks into all bins, not just one.

Scalloping loss (worst case, exactly between two bins): for rectangular window, the peak amplitude drops to $\text{sinc}(0.5) = 2/\pi \approx 0.637$ or −3.92 dB. For Hann window, scalloping loss is only −1.42 dB.

25CQT Kernel DesignHard

In a CQT with $B = 12$ bins per octave, derive the window length $N_k$ for bin $k$ with center frequency $f_k$, given that $Q = f_k / \Delta f_k$ is constant.

With $B$ bins per octave: $f_k = f_{\min} \cdot 2^{k/B}$. Adjacent bins: $f_{k+1}/f_k = 2^{1/B}$.

Bandwidth: $\Delta f_k = f_{k+1} - f_k = f_k(2^{1/B} - 1)$.

$Q = f_k/\Delta f_k = 1/(2^{1/B} - 1)$. For $B = 12$: $Q = 1/(2^{1/12} - 1) \approx 16.82$.

Window length for bin $k$: $N_k = f_s/\Delta f_k = f_s Q / f_k = \frac{f_s}{f_k(2^{1/B} - 1)}$.

At $f_k = 55$ Hz ($A1$), $N_k = 44100/(55 \times 0.0595) = 13{,}474$ samples. At $f_k = 7040$ Hz ($A8$), $N_k = 105$ samples. The window length varies by 128×!

26Griffin-Lim AlgorithmHard

The Griffin-Lim algorithm reconstructs audio from a magnitude-only spectrogram (no phase). Describe the iterative procedure and explain why it converges. What is the fundamental limitation?

Procedure: (1) Initialize with random phase. (2) Combine target magnitude with current phase → ISTFT → time-domain signal. (3) STFT the result → get new magnitude and phase. (4) Replace magnitude with target, keep new phase. (5) Repeat.

This is alternating projection between two constraint sets: (a) signals with the correct STFT magnitude, (b) signals that are consistent with overlap-add (valid STFT). Each projection reduces or maintains the distance to the intersection, so the error is non-increasing — it converges to a local minimum.

Limitation: the phase information encodes critical structure (edges, timing). Without it, the algorithm converges to a valid but often "mushy" reconstruction. Modern approaches (WaveGlow, HiFi-GAN) use neural networks trained to predict waveforms from mel spectrograms, producing far better quality.

27Reassignment MethodHard

The reassignment method sharpens spectrograms by moving each time-frequency point to its center of gravity. The reassigned time and frequency are: $\hat{t} = t - \text{Re}\{\frac{X_h(t,f) \overline{X_{Th}(t,f)}}{|X_h(t,f)|^2}\}$ and $\hat{f} = f + \frac{1}{2\pi}\text{Im}\{\frac{X_h(t,f)\overline{X_{Dh}(t,f)}}{|X_h(t,f)|^2}\}$ where $Th$ and $Dh$ are windowed with time-ramped and derivative windows. Why does this produce sharper spectrograms?

In a standard spectrogram, energy is spread across the Heisenberg box of each window position. The reassignment method computes where the energy actually "comes from" — the instantaneous frequency and group delay — and moves each point there.

For a pure sinusoid, standard STFT shows energy spread across multiple bins (window's main lobe width). Reassignment concentrates it at the true frequency and true onset time, approaching a delta function in the limit. The result is nearly "perfect" localization for tonal components, at the cost of requiring three STFTs per frame (one with the standard window, one with $tw(t)$, one with $w'(t)$). Noise gets scattered randomly, making it less visible — an additional benefit.

28Wavelet vs. STFTHard

The Continuous Wavelet Transform uses dilated/translated versions of a mother wavelet: $W(a,b) = \int x(t) \frac{1}{\sqrt{a}}\psi^*\!\left(\frac{t-b}{a}\right)dt$. Compare its time-frequency tiling with the STFT. For which audio analysis tasks is CWT superior?

STFT: uniform tiling — same $\Delta t$ and $\Delta f$ everywhere. CWT: dyadic tiling — at low frequencies, long windows (good $\Delta f$, poor $\Delta t$); at high frequencies, short windows (good $\Delta t$, poor $\Delta f$).

CWT is superior for: (1) Music where low notes need frequency precision and high notes need timing precision. (2) Analysis of signals with multi-scale structure (transients + sustains). (3) Onset detection across frequency ranges.

STFT is preferred for: (1) Speech processing where uniform resolution is fine. (2) When you need a simple, invertible representation. (3) Real-time applications (CWT is more computationally expensive).

29Log-Frequency SpectrogramHard

Show that on a log-frequency spectrogram, harmonic series appear as equally spaced horizontal lines, while on a linear spectrogram they have increasing spacing. Derive the relationship.

Harmonics: $f_n = nf_0$ for $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ On linear axis, spacing between adjacent harmonics = $f_0$ (constant) — they appear equally spaced.

On log axis: $\log(f_n) = \log(n) + \log(f_0)$. Spacing: $\log(f_{n+1}) - \log(f_n) = \log\frac{n+1}{n} = \log(1 + 1/n)$, which decreases as $n$ grows.

Wait — I inverted the claim. Let me reconsider: on a log-frequency axis, harmonics are NOT equally spaced (they get closer together). It's octaves that are equally spaced on a log axis. The statement should be: "On a log-frequency spectrogram, octaves have equal visual width, and pitch intervals are represented uniformly — useful for music. On a linear spectrogram, harmonics are equally spaced — useful for fundamental frequency estimation."

30Optimal Window for DetectionHard

You need to detect a 440 Hz tone buried in white noise at −20 dB SNR. You can choose any window length $N$ at $f_s = 44100$. Longer windows average more noise. If the detection SNR improves as $\sqrt{N}$, what window length gives a post-analysis SNR of +10 dB?

Need to gain $10 - (-20) = 30$ dB from averaging. Since SNR improves as $10\log_{10}(\sqrt{N}) = 5\log_{10}(N)$:

$5\log_{10}(N) = 30 \Rightarrow \log_{10}(N) = 6 \Rightarrow N = 10^6 = 1{,}000{,}000$ samples.

Duration: $10^6/44100 \approx 22.7$ seconds. This is the coherent integration time needed.

In practice, you'd use a shorter FFT and average multiple frames (non-coherent integration). With $K$ frames of $N$ points each, the improvement is $\approx 10\log_{10}(NK)/2$. For example, 100 frames of $N = 10000$: gain $= 5\log_{10}(10^6) = 30$ dB. Same total observation time but more practical.
