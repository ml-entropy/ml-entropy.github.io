Computing Has a Physical Cost

Every computation that has ever been performed — from an abacus bead sliding on a rod to a GPU training a billion-parameter neural network — obeys the laws of thermodynamics. Information is not abstract. It is always inscribed in physical degrees of freedom: voltages on a wire, magnetic domains on a disk, charges trapped in a floating gate. Manipulating information therefore means manipulating physical systems, and that manipulation is subject to the same constraints that govern steam engines and refrigerators.

This was not always obvious. For most of the 20th century, computer science developed as a branch of mathematics, with the implicit assumption that the energy cost of computation was a mere engineering detail. The theoretical breakthroughs of Turing, Church, and von Neumann said nothing about joules or watts. But starting with Rolf Landauer's work at IBM in 1961, a series of profound results established that there are fundamental physical limits on computation — limits that no amount of clever engineering can circumvent, because they arise from the laws of thermodynamics themselves.

The Central Question

What is the minimum energy required to perform a computation? The answer turns out to depend not on how many operations you perform, but on how much information you erase. This single insight connects thermodynamics, information theory, and computer science at the deepest level.

To appreciate the practical stakes, consider the numbers. A modern data center consumes roughly 10–50 MW of electrical power. The global IT infrastructure uses about 1% of the world's electricity. A single training run of a large language model can consume on the order of 1,000 MWh — comparable to the annual electricity consumption of over 100 American households. As AI models grow exponentially, the energy cost of computation is becoming a first-order concern, not just for the electric bill, but for the climate.

Yet current hardware operates at energy levels that are roughly six to seven orders of magnitude above the theoretical minimum set by thermodynamics. A modern CMOS transistor dissipates around $10^{-15}$ to $10^{-14}$ J per switching event, while the Landauer limit at room temperature is approximately $3 \times 10^{-21}$ J per bit erasure. This enormous gap tells us two things: there is vast room for improvement in hardware efficiency, and the thermodynamic limits represent a hard floor that we can approach but never breach.

Energy Scales in Computation

At room temperature ($T = 300$ K), the key energy scales are:

  • Thermal energy: $k_B T \approx 4.14 \times 10^{-21}$ J $\approx 0.026$ eV
  • Landauer limit: $k_B T \ln 2 \approx 2.87 \times 10^{-21}$ J per bit erasure
  • Modern CMOS switching: $\sim 10^{-15}$ J per transistor switch
  • Ratio: Current hardware uses $\sim 10^{5}$–$10^{6}$ times the Landauer limit

Landauer's Principle Revisited

Rolf Landauer (1927–1999)

Born in Stuttgart, Germany, Rolf Landauer emigrated to the United States in 1938. After earning his PhD in physics from Harvard in 1950, he spent his entire career at IBM's Thomas J. Watson Research Center. In his seminal 1961 paper Irreversibility and Heat Generation in the Computing Process, he established the fundamental link between logical irreversibility and thermodynamic dissipation. His work showed that the laws of physics impose inescapable constraints on computation — a result that has only grown in importance as we approach the physical limits of Moore's Law.

Landauer's principle states a deceptively simple claim: erasing one bit of information necessarily dissipates at least $k_B T \ln 2$ of energy as heat. The "at least" is not an engineering limitation — it is a consequence of the second law of thermodynamics.

To understand why, consider what it means to erase a bit. Before erasure, the bit is in one of two states: 0 or 1. We may not know which, but the physical system encoding the bit is in a definite microstate within one of two macrostates. After erasure, the bit is in a known state — say, 0. The erasure operation is a many-to-one mapping: both the "was 0" and "was 1" states map to the single "is 0" state.

Landauer's Principle

Any logically irreversible operation that maps $N$ distinct input states to fewer output states must increase the entropy of the environment by at least:

$$\Delta S_{\text{env}} \geq k_B \ln \frac{N_{\text{in}}}{N_{\text{out}}}$$

For erasing one bit ($N_{\text{in}} = 2$, $N_{\text{out}} = 1$), the minimum heat dissipated at temperature $T$ is:

$$W_{\text{min}} = k_B T \ln 2 \approx 2.87 \times 10^{-21} \text{ J at } T = 300 \text{ K}$$

Proof from Phase Space

The proof follows directly from statistical mechanics. Consider a one-bit memory modeled as a particle in a double-well potential, with the left well representing 0 and the right well representing 1. Before erasure, the particle is equally likely to be in either well. The phase space volume accessible to the system is $\Gamma_{\text{before}} = 2\Gamma_0$, where $\Gamma_0$ is the phase space volume of a single well.

After erasure (resetting to 0), the particle is known to be in the left well. The accessible phase space volume is $\Gamma_{\text{after}} = \Gamma_0$. The entropy change of the system is:

$$\Delta S_{\text{sys}} = k_B \ln \Gamma_{\text{after}} - k_B \ln \Gamma_{\text{before}} = k_B \ln \frac{\Gamma_0}{2\Gamma_0} = -k_B \ln 2$$

The system's entropy has decreased by $k_B \ln 2$. By the second law, the total entropy of the universe cannot decrease. Therefore the environment's entropy must increase by at least $k_B \ln 2$, which at temperature $T$ requires dissipating at least $Q = T \Delta S_{\text{env}} = k_B T \ln 2$ as heat.

Why AND/OR Gates Are Irreversible but NOT Is Reversible

A logic gate is logically reversible if you can uniquely determine its inputs from its outputs. Consider the standard gates:

  • NOT gate (1 bit in, 1 bit out): Input 0 → Output 1, Input 1 → Output 0. The mapping is one-to-one. Given the output, we can always recover the input. Reversible.
  • AND gate (2 bits in, 1 bit out): Three different inputs (00, 01, 10) all produce output 0. Only input 11 produces output 1. We map 4 states to 2 distinguishable outputs, but the output 0 corresponds to 3 distinct inputs. Irreversible — information is destroyed.
  • OR gate (2 bits in, 1 bit out): Three inputs (01, 10, 11) produce output 1. Only input 00 produces output 0. Again, information is destroyed. Irreversible.

Each AND or OR operation erases at least one bit of information (mapping 4 input states to 2 output states), so by Landauer's principle, each such gate must dissipate at least $k_B T \ln 2$ of energy. A complex circuit with $n$ irreversible gates must dissipate at least $n \cdot k_B T \ln 2$. This led to a natural question: can we compute without ever erasing information?

Reversible Computing

The answer, remarkably, is yes. In 1973, Charles Bennett at IBM proved that any computation can be performed using only logically reversible operations, meaning that in principle, computation can be done with arbitrarily small energy dissipation. The key insight is that irreversibility is not inherent to computation itself — it is an artifact of how we typically design circuits.

The Toffoli Gate

The Toffoli gate (also called the controlled-controlled-NOT or CCNOT gate), introduced by Tommaso Toffoli in 1980, is a universal reversible gate. It takes three input bits $(a, b, c)$ and produces three output bits:

$$(a, b, c) \mapsto (a, b, c \oplus (a \cdot b))$$

where $\oplus$ denotes XOR. The first two bits pass through unchanged, and the third bit is flipped if and only if both $a = 1$ and $b = 1$. This mapping is a bijection on $\{0,1\}^3$ — every input maps to a unique output, and vice versa. No information is ever destroyed. By setting the ancilla bit $c$ appropriately, the Toffoli gate can simulate AND, OR, NOT, and NAND, making it a universal gate for classical reversible computation.

The Fredkin Gate

The Fredkin gate (controlled-SWAP), proposed by Edward Fredkin in 1982, is another universal reversible gate. It takes three bits $(a, b, c)$:

$$(a, b, c) \mapsto \begin{cases} (a, b, c) & \text{if } a = 0 \\ (a, c, b) & \text{if } a = 1 \end{cases}$$

The first bit acts as a control: if it is 1, the second and third bits are swapped; otherwise, all bits pass through unchanged. Like the Toffoli gate, the Fredkin gate is a bijection and therefore logically reversible. It is also universal and conserves the number of 1s in the output (a property called being conservative).

Bennett's Trick: Compute → Copy → Uncompute

The main difficulty with reversible computing is the accumulation of garbage bits — ancilla bits that contain intermediate results. If we simply discard these bits, we perform an irreversible operation and incur the Landauer cost. Bennett's trick elegantly solves this problem using three stages:

  1. Compute: Run the reversible computation forward, producing the desired output along with garbage bits that record the entire computational history.
  2. Copy: Copy the final result to a fresh set of output bits (copying is reversible since it maps $(x, 0) \to (x, x)$, which is one-to-one).
  3. Uncompute: Run the computation in reverse, returning all garbage bits to their initial (blank) state. This is possible precisely because every step was reversible.

The net effect is: clean input bits are transformed into clean output bits plus the desired result, with no garbage left behind. The computation is fully reversible and, in principle, requires zero energy dissipation.

The Space-Time Tradeoff

Bennett's trick comes at a cost: it requires running the computation three times (forward, copy, reverse) and needs enough memory to store all intermediate results during the forward pass. A conventional irreversible computation that uses time $T$ and space $S$ can be made reversible using time $O(T)$ but space $O(T)$ (to store the full history). More sophisticated strategies by Bennett (1989) achieve a tradeoff: using $O(T^{1+\epsilon})$ time with $O(S \cdot \log T)$ space, for any $\epsilon > 0$.

Thermodynamic Cost of Logical Operations

How far are real computers from the Landauer limit? To answer this, we need to connect the abstract thermodynamic bound to the concrete physics of transistors.

CMOS Energy Dissipation

In CMOS (Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor) technology, the dominant source of energy dissipation during a switching event is dynamic power, given by:

$$E_{\text{switch}} = \frac{1}{2} C V_{dd}^2$$

where $C$ is the load capacitance of the gate (typically $\sim 1$ fF $= 10^{-15}$ F) and $V_{dd}$ is the supply voltage (typically $\sim 0.7$–$1.0$ V in modern processes). This gives:

$$E_{\text{switch}} \approx \frac{1}{2} \times 10^{-15} \times (0.8)^2 \approx 3.2 \times 10^{-16} \text{ J}$$

Compared to the Landauer limit of $k_B T \ln 2 \approx 2.87 \times 10^{-21}$ J, this is about $10^5$ times larger. The ratio has been steadily decreasing with each technology node (smaller transistors, lower voltages), but fundamental barriers including thermal noise, quantum tunneling, and interconnect resistance make it extremely difficult to approach the Landauer limit with conventional CMOS.

The Margolus-Levitin Bound

Beyond energy per operation, there is a fundamental limit on computational speed. The Margolus-Levitin theorem (1998) states that a quantum system with average energy $E$ above its ground state can transition between orthogonal states no faster than:

$$t_{\min} = \frac{\pi \hbar}{2E}$$

This sets an absolute speed limit on computation. For a system consuming 1 watt of power, the maximum number of elementary operations per second is:

$$\nu_{\max} = \frac{2E}{\pi \hbar} = \frac{2 \times 1}{\pi \times 1.055 \times 10^{-34}} \approx 6 \times 10^{33} \text{ ops/sec}$$

Current processors perform roughly $10^{11}$ operations per second per watt, which is approximately $10^{22}$ times below this ultimate limit. Combined with the Landauer bound, these results establish a "performance envelope" for computation: for a given amount of energy and time, there is a maximum number of bit operations that physics allows.

Thermodynamics of Machine Learning

The thermodynamic cost of computation takes on urgent practical significance in the context of modern machine learning, where training runs now consume megawatt-hours of electricity and the scale is growing exponentially.

Energy Per Floating Point Operation

A useful metric for comparing hardware efficiency is energy per floating-point operation (FLOP). For modern hardware:

  • NVIDIA A100 GPU: ~300 W TDP, ~312 TFLOPS (FP16 Tensor), so $\sim 10^{-12}$ J/FLOP
  • NVIDIA H100 GPU: ~700 W TDP, ~1979 TFLOPS (FP8 Tensor), so $\sim 3.5 \times 10^{-13}$ J/FLOP
  • Apple M2 Neural Engine: ~15 W, ~15.8 TOPS (INT8), so $\sim 10^{-12}$ J/op

Each floating-point operation involves many transistor switching events (hundreds to thousands), but the aggregate energy per useful FLOP is the relevant metric for estimating training costs.

Total Energy for Training Large Models

The total energy for training a model can be estimated as:

$$E_{\text{train}} = \frac{\text{Total FLOPs}}{(\text{Hardware FLOPS}) \times (\text{Utilization})} \times P_{\text{system}}$$

For a GPT-3 scale model ($\sim 3.14 \times 10^{23}$ FLOPs, trained on ~1000 A100 GPUs at ~40% utilization):

$$E_{\text{train}} \approx \frac{3.14 \times 10^{23}}{1000 \times 312 \times 10^{12} \times 0.4} \times (1000 \times 300) \approx 1{,}200 \text{ MWh}$$

At the Landauer limit, the minimum energy for $3.14 \times 10^{23}$ bit operations would be approximately $3 \times 10^{23} \times 3 \times 10^{-21} \approx 900$ J — less than the energy in a single AA battery. The actual energy consumption is roughly $10^{9}$ times the Landauer limit, reflecting the combined inefficiencies of transistor physics, memory access, interconnect losses, cooling overhead, and power conversion.

The Brain vs. Silicon

The human brain is often cited as an existence proof that dramatically more efficient computation is possible:

  • Power consumption: ~20 W (the brain runs on about the power of a dim light bulb)
  • Synaptic operations: ~$10^{15}$ synaptic operations per second (estimated)
  • Energy per synaptic operation: ~$10^{-14}$ J (about 10 fJ per synapse event)
  • Equivalent FLOPS: Difficult to define precisely, but by various estimates, the brain performs computation equivalent to $10^{15}$–$10^{18}$ FLOPS

Compared to a modern GPU at $\sim 10^{-12}$ J/FLOP, the brain is roughly 100 times more energy-efficient per operation (though comparisons are fraught because synaptic operations and FLOPs are fundamentally different). More remarkably, the brain is still about $10^{7}$ times above the Landauer limit per operation, suggesting that even biological computation has vast room for thermodynamic improvement — or alternatively, that the brain's computational power lies not in individual operations but in its massively parallel, event-driven architecture.

Thermodynamic Speed Limits for Learning

Beyond the cost of individual operations, we can ask a deeper question: what are the fundamental thermodynamic limits on learning itself? Can we derive bounds on how quickly a system can learn, analogous to the Carnot efficiency for heat engines?

Minimum Entropy Production for Parameter Updates

Consider a gradient descent update $\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla L(\theta_t)$, where $L$ is the loss function and $\eta$ is the learning rate. From a thermodynamic perspective, this update changes the state of the physical system encoding the parameters. The minimum entropy production for this state change is bounded by:

$$\Delta S_{\text{env}} \geq \frac{k_B}{2} \sum_i \frac{(\Delta \theta_i)^2}{\sigma_i^2}$$

where $\Delta \theta_i = -\eta \partial L / \partial \theta_i$ is the change in parameter $i$, and $\sigma_i^2$ is the thermal variance of the physical degree of freedom encoding that parameter. This resembles a Fisher information metric on parameter space, connecting information geometry to thermodynamic dissipation.

Loss Landscape and Free Energy Landscape

There is a striking mathematical analogy between optimization and statistical mechanics. Consider the Gibbs distribution over parameter space:

$$p(\theta) = \frac{1}{Z(\beta)} e^{-\beta L(\theta)}, \quad Z(\beta) = \int e^{-\beta L(\theta)} d\theta$$

where $\beta = 1/T$ plays the role of inverse temperature. At high $\beta$ (low temperature), this distribution concentrates on the global minimum of $L$. The associated free energy is:

$$F = -\frac{1}{\beta} \ln Z = \langle L \rangle - T \cdot S(\theta)$$

where $S(\theta) = -\int p(\theta) \ln p(\theta) d\theta$ is the entropy of the parameter distribution. Minimizing $F$ balances two competing objectives: minimizing the expected loss $\langle L \rangle$ (favoring sharp minima) and maximizing entropy $S$ (favoring flat regions with many near-optimal solutions). This provides a thermodynamic interpretation of the flat minima hypothesis in deep learning: solutions found by SGD tend to be in flat regions because those regions have higher entropy (more microstates) and thus lower free energy.

Thermodynamic Uncertainty Relations for SGD

The thermodynamic uncertainty relation (TUR), originally derived in the context of molecular motors and biochemical networks, has a computational analog. For any current-like observable $J$ (such as the rate of loss decrease) in a non-equilibrium steady state:

$$\frac{\text{Var}(J)}{\langle J \rangle^2} \geq \frac{2 k_B}{\dot{S}_{\text{env}}}$$

In the context of SGD, this implies a fundamental tradeoff: reducing the variance of learning progress (making training more predictable) requires increasing the rate of entropy production (dissipating more energy). Fast, precise learning is thermodynamically expensive. This provides a physics-based explanation for why large learning rates (which produce noisier but faster progress) can be more energy-efficient than small learning rates (which produce smoother but slower progress).

Stochastic Thermodynamics of Neural Networks

The connection between SGD and thermodynamics becomes precise when we view each training step as a non-equilibrium process in the space of network parameters.

Each Training Step as a Non-Equilibrium Process

In stochastic gradient descent, the parameter update is:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla L_B(\theta_t)$$

where $L_B$ is the loss computed on a random mini-batch $B$. We can decompose the stochastic gradient as:

$$\nabla L_B(\theta) = \nabla L(\theta) + \xi_t$$

where $\nabla L(\theta)$ is the true (full-batch) gradient and $\xi_t$ is a zero-mean noise term with covariance that depends on the batch size and the variance of per-sample gradients. The parameter dynamics then follow:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla L(\theta_t) - \eta \xi_t$$

This is precisely the discretized Langevin equation with "temperature" proportional to $\eta / B$ (learning rate divided by batch size). The analogy is not merely formal — SGD really does perform a random walk in parameter space, with the noise playing a role physically analogous to thermal fluctuations.

Temperature Analogy in Learning Rate

In the continuous-time limit, the SGD dynamics become:

$$d\theta = -\nabla L(\theta)\, dt + \sqrt{2D}\, dW_t$$

where $D = \eta \Sigma / (2B)$ is the diffusion coefficient, $\Sigma$ is the gradient noise covariance, and $dW_t$ is a Wiener process. Comparing with the overdamped Langevin equation from physics ($d\theta = -\nabla U \, dt + \sqrt{2k_BT/\gamma}\, dW_t$), we identify an effective temperature:

$$T_{\text{eff}} = \frac{\eta \, \text{tr}(\Sigma)}{2B \cdot d}$$

where $d$ is the number of parameters. This effective temperature controls the exploration-exploitation tradeoff: high $T_{\text{eff}}$ (large learning rate, small batch) promotes exploration of the loss landscape, while low $T_{\text{eff}}$ (small learning rate, large batch) promotes exploitation of nearby minima.

The Loss-Entropy Production Connection

The rate of entropy production along an SGD trajectory can be expressed as:

$$\dot{S}_{\text{prod}} = \frac{1}{T_{\text{eff}}} \left\langle \nabla L \cdot \frac{d\theta}{dt} \right\rangle + \frac{1}{T_{\text{eff}}} \left\langle \xi \cdot \frac{d\theta}{dt} \right\rangle$$

The first term is the "housekeeping" entropy production — the irreversible work done by the deterministic gradient force. The second is related to the correlation between noise and velocity. In steady state (when training has converged to fluctuating around a minimum), the total entropy production rate satisfies:

$$\dot{S}_{\text{prod}} \geq 0$$

with equality only at equilibrium (zero learning rate). This means any active training process necessarily produces entropy. The rate of loss decrease is directly connected to entropy production — faster learning requires greater thermodynamic dissipation.

The Future: Neuromorphic and Thermodynamic Computing

The enormous gap between current hardware efficiency and the Landauer limit has inspired several radically different approaches to computation that attempt to exploit, rather than fight against, the physics of dissipation.

Analog Computing

Analog computers represent information as continuous physical quantities (voltages, currents, charges) rather than discrete bits. In principle, analog computation can avoid the Landauer cost entirely, since it need not perform irreversible bit erasures. Modern analog AI accelerators use crossbar arrays of resistive memory elements to perform matrix-vector multiplications in $O(1)$ time and at a fraction of the energy cost of digital implementations. Companies like Mythic, Syntiant, and IBM's analog AI project have demonstrated $10\times$–$100\times$ energy efficiency improvements for inference workloads.

Equilibrium Propagation

Equilibrium propagation, proposed by Scellier and Bengio (2017), is a learning algorithm designed to be implementable in physical systems. Unlike backpropagation, which requires a separate backward pass with different computational machinery, equilibrium propagation uses only the natural relaxation dynamics of a physical system:

  1. Free phase: The system relaxes to equilibrium under its natural dynamics (minimizing an energy function).
  2. Nudged phase: A small perturbation proportional to the error signal is applied, and the system relaxes to a new equilibrium.
  3. Parameter update: The weight update is proportional to the difference between the two equilibrium states.

This algorithm can be implemented in any physical system that relaxes to equilibrium — electrical circuits, mechanical systems, or even chemical networks — and its energy cost is bounded by the dissipation that occurs during relaxation, potentially approaching the thermodynamic limits.

Boltzmann Machine Hardware

Boltzmann machines, originally proposed by Hinton and Sejnowski (1985), are neural networks that explicitly perform sampling from a thermal distribution. Modern hardware implementations use physical stochastic elements (magnetic tunnel junctions, noisy CMOS circuits) to generate the thermal fluctuations that Boltzmann machines require. These p-bits (probabilistic bits) harness, rather than suppress, thermal noise — turning a liability of conventional computing into an asset.

The Thermodynamic Computing Paradigm

The most radical vision is thermodynamic computing, which treats computation not as a sequence of deterministic logic operations but as a process of driving a physical system between carefully designed free-energy minima. Key ideas include:

  • Computing with physics: Instead of simulating physics on a digital computer, use the physics directly. Let natural relaxation, diffusion, and fluctuation do the computational work.
  • Energy-based models: Design hardware whose energy function encodes the desired computation. The answer is found at equilibrium.
  • Thermodynamic integration: Use controlled changes in external parameters to guide the system along a path in configuration space that "computes" the desired function, analogous to adiabatic quantum computation.
The Big Picture

The thermodynamics of computation reveals that information processing is fundamentally a physical process, subject to the same laws that govern engines, refrigerators, and living organisms. The Landauer limit sets a floor of $k_B T \ln 2$ per irreversible bit operation. Current hardware operates far above this floor, but the gap is closing. The next generation of computing architectures — analog, neuromorphic, thermodynamic — may achieve efficiencies far closer to the physical limits, not by fighting thermodynamics, but by embracing it.

1. Landauer Bound Calculations for Modern Hardware

Compute the Landauer limit and compare it to actual energy dissipation in modern processors across technology nodes.

python 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Physical constants
k_B = 1.380649e-23   # Boltzmann constant (J/K)
T = 300               # Room temperature (K)

# Landauer limit
E_landauer = k_B * T * np.log(2)
print(f"Landauer limit at T = {T} K:")
print(f"  E_min = k_B T ln 2 = {E_landauer:.3e} J")
print(f"  E_min = {E_landauer / 1.602e-19:.4f} eV")
print()

# CMOS technology nodes: (name, capacitance_fF, voltage_V, year)
tech_nodes = [
    ("250 nm", 50.0,  2.5, 1997),
    ("130 nm", 20.0,  1.5, 2001),
    ("65 nm",  10.0,  1.2, 2006),
    ("32 nm",  5.0,   0.9, 2010),
    ("14 nm",  2.0,   0.8, 2014),
    ("7 nm",   1.0,   0.7, 2018),
    ("5 nm",   0.7,   0.65, 2020),
    ("3 nm",   0.5,   0.6, 2023),
]

print(f"{'Node':<10} {'E_switch (J)':<15} {'Ratio to Landauer':<20} {'Year'}")
print("-" * 65)

years = []
ratios = []

for name, C_fF, V, year in tech_nodes:
    C = C_fF * 1e-15  # Convert fF to F
    E_switch = 0.5 * C * V**2
    ratio = E_switch / E_landauer
    print(f"{name:<10} {E_switch:<15.3e} {ratio:<20.1f} {year}")
    years.append(year)
    ratios.append(ratio)

# Plot the trend
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))
ax.semilogy(years, ratios, 'bo-', markersize=8, linewidth=2)
ax.axhline(y=1, color='red', linestyle='--', linewidth=2, label='Landauer limit')
ax.set_xlabel('Year', fontsize=12)
ax.set_ylabel('Energy / Landauer Limit', fontsize=12)
ax.set_title('CMOS Switching Energy vs. Landauer Limit', fontsize=14)
ax.legend(fontsize=12)
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

for i, name in enumerate([n[0] for n in tech_nodes]):
    ax.annotate(name, (years[i], ratios[i]),
                textcoords="offset points", xytext=(0, 12),
                ha='center', fontsize=8)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

2. Reversible Gate Simulation

Implement Toffoli and Fredkin gates, verify their reversibility, and build a reversible full adder.

python 
import numpy as np
from itertools import product

def toffoli(a, b, c):
    """Toffoli gate: (a, b, c) -> (a, b, c XOR (a AND b))"""
    return (a, b, c ^ (a & b))

def fredkin(a, b, c):
    """Fredkin gate: if a=1, swap b and c"""
    if a == 1:
        return (a, c, b)
    return (a, b, c)

def verify_reversibility(gate, name, n_bits=3):
    """Verify that a gate is a bijection on {0,1}^n"""
    inputs = list(product([0, 1], repeat=n_bits))
    outputs = [gate(*inp) for inp in inputs]
    is_bijection = len(set(outputs)) == len(inputs)
    print(f"{name} gate:")
    print(f"  Number of inputs:  {len(inputs)}")
    print(f"  Unique outputs:    {len(set(outputs))}")
    print(f"  Is bijection:      {is_bijection}")
    print(f"  Truth table:")
    for inp, out in zip(inputs, outputs):
        print(f"    {inp} -> {out}")
    print()
    return is_bijection

# Verify both gates
verify_reversibility(toffoli, "Toffoli")
verify_reversibility(fredkin, "Fredkin")

# Show that Toffoli can simulate AND
print("Toffoli as AND gate (set c=0):")
for a, b in product([0, 1], repeat=2):
    out = toffoli(a, b, 0)
    print(f"  AND({a}, {b}) = {out[2]}  (full output: {out})")

print()

# Show that Toffoli can simulate NOT (set a=1, b=1)
print("Toffoli as NOT gate (set a=1, b=1):")
for c in [0, 1]:
    out = toffoli(1, 1, c)
    print(f"  NOT({c}) = {out[2]}  (full output: {out})")

print()

# Bennett's trick: reversible computation of f(x) = AND(x1, x2)
print("=== Bennett's Trick Demo ===")
print("Computing f(x1, x2) = AND(x1, x2) reversibly")
print()
x1, x2 = 1, 1

# Step 1: Compute (using Toffoli with ancilla c=0)
a1, b1, result = toffoli(x1, x2, 0)
print(f"1. Compute: Toffoli({x1}, {x2}, 0) = ({a1}, {b1}, {result})")

# Step 2: Copy result to output register (using CNOT = Toffoli with a=1)
_, _, output = toffoli(1, result, 0)
print(f"2. Copy:    Toffoli(1, {result}, 0) = (1, {result}, {output})")

# Step 3: Uncompute (run Toffoli again to restore ancilla)
a3, b3, restored = toffoli(x1, x2, result)
print(f"3. Uncompute: Toffoli({x1}, {x2}, {result}) = ({a3}, {b3}, {restored})")
print(f"   Ancilla restored to: {restored}")
print(f"   Output: {output}")
print(f"   All garbage cleaned: ancilla = {restored} (should be 0)")

1. Landauer Bound at Room Temperature ↓

Compute the Landauer bound on energy dissipation per bit erasure at the following temperatures:

  1. Room temperature: $T = 300$ K
  2. Liquid nitrogen: $T = 77$ K
  3. Cosmic microwave background: $T = 2.725$ K
  4. Surface of the Sun: $T = 5778$ K

Express each answer in both joules and electron-volts. At which temperature would it be most energy-efficient to compute?

The Landauer limit is $E_{\min} = k_B T \ln 2$, where $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}$ J/K.

a) Room temperature ($T = 300$ K):

$$E_{\min} = 1.381 \times 10^{-23} \times 300 \times \ln 2 = 2.87 \times 10^{-21} \text{ J} = 0.0179 \text{ eV}$$

b) Liquid nitrogen ($T = 77$ K):

$$E_{\min} = 1.381 \times 10^{-23} \times 77 \times \ln 2 = 7.37 \times 10^{-22} \text{ J} = 4.60 \times 10^{-3} \text{ eV}$$

c) CMB ($T = 2.725$ K):

$$E_{\min} = 1.381 \times 10^{-23} \times 2.725 \times \ln 2 = 2.61 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J} = 1.63 \times 10^{-4} \text{ eV}$$

d) Sun surface ($T = 5778$ K):

$$E_{\min} = 1.381 \times 10^{-23} \times 5778 \times \ln 2 = 5.53 \times 10^{-20} \text{ J} = 0.345 \text{ eV}$$

Conclusion: It is most energy-efficient to compute at the lowest temperature (CMB, $T = 2.725$ K), where the Landauer bound is smallest. This is why cryogenic computing is of interest. However, the practical cost of maintaining cryogenic temperatures must be weighed against the energy savings.

3. Reversibility of Common Logic Gates ↓

For each of the following logic gates, determine whether it is logically reversible. If it is not, calculate the minimum energy dissipated per operation at $T = 300$ K using Landauer's principle.

  1. NOT gate (1 input, 1 output)
  2. AND gate (2 inputs, 1 output)
  3. XOR gate (2 inputs, 1 output)
  4. NAND gate (2 inputs, 1 output)
  5. CNOT gate (2 inputs, 2 outputs): $(a, b) \mapsto (a, a \oplus b)$

a) NOT gate: The mapping $0 \to 1$, $1 \to 0$ is one-to-one (bijection). Reversible. Minimum dissipation: $0$ J (beyond practical considerations).

b) AND gate: Maps 4 input states $\{00, 01, 10, 11\}$ to 2 output values $\{0, 1\}$. Three inputs map to 0, one maps to 1. This is a 4-to-2 mapping.

$$E_{\min} = k_B T \ln \frac{N_{\text{in}}}{N_{\text{out}}} = k_B T \ln \frac{4}{2} = k_B T \ln 2 = 2.87 \times 10^{-21} \text{ J}$$

One bit of information is erased. Irreversible.

c) XOR gate: Maps $\{00, 01, 10, 11\}$ to $\{0, 1\}$. Two inputs map to 0, two map to 1. This is a 4-to-2 mapping.

$$E_{\min} = k_B T \ln 2 = 2.87 \times 10^{-21} \text{ J}$$

Note: XOR is "balanced" (equal number of inputs map to each output) but still irreversible because you cannot recover the input from the output. Irreversible.

d) NAND gate: Maps $\{00, 01, 10, 11\}$ to $\{0, 1\}$. One input maps to 0, three map to 1. Same analysis as AND. $E_{\min} = k_B T \ln 2$. Irreversible.

e) CNOT gate: Maps $(0,0) \to (0,0)$, $(0,1) \to (0,1)$, $(1,0) \to (1,1)$, $(1,1) \to (1,0)$. This is a bijection on $\{0,1\}^2$ — every output is unique. Given the output, we can recover the input: $a$ is the first output bit, and $b = a \oplus (\text{second output bit})$. Reversible. Minimum dissipation: $0$ J.

4. Bennett's Reversible Computation Trick ↓

Consider computing $f(x_1, x_2) = x_1 \text{ AND } x_2$ using only reversible Toffoli gates.

  1. Show how a single Toffoli gate computes AND, identifying which bits are inputs, outputs, and ancillae.
  2. After computation, there are "garbage" bits. Describe Bennett's three-step procedure to clean them up.
  3. Write out the state of all bits at each step of Bennett's trick for input $(x_1, x_2) = (1, 0)$.
  4. How many Toffoli gates are used in total? How does this compare to a single irreversible AND gate in terms of gate count and energy cost?

a) The Toffoli gate: $(a, b, c) \mapsto (a, b, c \oplus (a \cdot b))$. Set $a = x_1$, $b = x_2$, $c = 0$ (ancilla). Then output is $(x_1, x_2, x_1 \text{ AND } x_2)$. The third bit is the desired AND output, and the first two bits are "garbage" (though they simply equal the inputs).

b) Bennett's trick:

  1. Compute: Apply Toffoli to $(x_1, x_2, 0)$ to get $(x_1, x_2, x_1 \wedge x_2)$.
  2. Copy: Use a CNOT gate to copy the result: apply CNOT to $(x_1 \wedge x_2, 0_{\text{out}})$ to get $(x_1 \wedge x_2, x_1 \wedge x_2)$. The output register now holds the result.
  3. Uncompute: Apply the Toffoli gate again to $(x_1, x_2, x_1 \wedge x_2)$, which restores it to $(x_1, x_2, 0)$ since XOR is self-inverse.

c) For $(x_1, x_2) = (1, 0)$:

Initial state: $(x_1, x_2, \text{anc}, \text{out}) = (1, 0, 0, 0)$

Step 1 (Compute): Toffoli on bits 1,2,3: $(1, 0, 0 \oplus (1 \cdot 0), 0) = (1, 0, 0, 0)$

Step 2 (Copy): CNOT on bits 3,4: $(1, 0, 0, 0 \oplus 0) = (1, 0, 0, 0)$

Step 3 (Uncompute): Toffoli on bits 1,2,3: $(1, 0, 0 \oplus 0, 0) = (1, 0, 0, 0)$

Final state: $(1, 0, 0, 0)$. The ancilla is clean (0), and the output register holds $f(1,0) = 0$. Correct.

d) Total gates: 2 Toffoli gates + 1 CNOT gate = 3 gates total. A single irreversible AND gate uses 1 gate but dissipates at least $k_B T \ln 2$. The reversible version uses 3 gates but dissipates zero energy in principle. The tradeoff: 3x more gates (and thus more time/space) in exchange for zero fundamental energy dissipation.

7. Toffoli Gate Universality ↓

The Toffoli gate maps $(a, b, c) \mapsto (a, b, c \oplus (a \wedge b))$.

  1. Show that setting $a = 1$ and $b = 1$ makes the Toffoli gate act as a NOT gate on $c$.
  2. Show that setting $c = 0$ makes the Toffoli gate compute AND($a$, $b$) in the third output bit.
  3. Using the fact that NAND is universal for classical computation, and that NOT and AND together can build NAND, argue that the Toffoli gate is universal for classical reversible computation.
  4. How many ancilla bits does a Toffoli-based reversible circuit for an $n$-input, $m$-output Boolean function require at minimum?

a) NOT gate: Set $a = 1$, $b = 1$:

$$(1, 1, c) \mapsto (1, 1, c \oplus (1 \wedge 1)) = (1, 1, c \oplus 1) = (1, 1, \overline{c})$$

The third output is $\overline{c}$, which is NOT($c$).

b) AND gate: Set $c = 0$:

$$(a, b, 0) \mapsto (a, b, 0 \oplus (a \wedge b)) = (a, b, a \wedge b)$$

The third output is AND($a$, $b$).

c) Universality: Since the Toffoli gate can compute NOT and AND (as shown above), it can compute NAND: first compute AND$(a,b)$ with a Toffoli, then apply NOT to the result with another Toffoli. Since NAND is universal for classical Boolean logic (any Boolean function can be built from NAND gates alone), the Toffoli gate is also universal. Moreover, since the Toffoli gate is reversible, any classical computation can be made reversible using only Toffoli gates (plus ancilla bits).

d) Ancilla bits: A reversible circuit must have the same number of input and output bits. An irreversible function $f: \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}^m$ maps $2^n$ input states to at most $2^m$ output states. To make this reversible, we need at least enough ancilla bits to ensure the total mapping is a bijection. The minimum number of ancilla bits is $n - m$ if $f$ is a bijection (unlikely for $m < n$), or more generally $\max(n, m)$ total output bits are needed. In practice, a function with $n$ inputs and $m$ outputs implemented reversibly needs at least $n$ ancilla bits (to store the input for uncomputation) and produces $m$ output bits, for a total of $n + m$ bits. After Bennett's trick, only $m$ output bits remain non-trivial.

8. Thermodynamic Speed Limit for Gradient Descent ↓

Consider gradient descent on a quadratic loss $L(\theta) = \frac{1}{2} \theta^T H \theta$ where $H$ is a positive-definite Hessian matrix with eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \leq \lambda_2 \leq \cdots \leq \lambda_d$.

  1. Write the gradient descent update and show that after $t$ steps, $\theta_t = (I - \eta H)^t \theta_0$.
  2. For convergence, we need $|1 - \eta \lambda_i| < 1$ for all $i$. What is the optimal learning rate, and what is the convergence rate?
  3. If each parameter is encoded as a physical degree of freedom at temperature $T$, argue that the minimum energy dissipated per gradient step is proportional to $\sum_i (\Delta \theta_i)^2 / \sigma_i^2$ where $\sigma_i^2 \propto k_B T / \lambda_i$.
  4. Derive a lower bound on the total energy required to reduce the loss by a factor of $\epsilon$, as a function of the condition number $\kappa = \lambda_d / \lambda_1$ and the temperature $T$.

a) The gradient is $\nabla L = H\theta$, so the update is:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta H \theta_t = (I - \eta H) \theta_t$$

By induction: $\theta_t = (I - \eta H)^t \theta_0$.

In the eigenbasis of $H$ (with eigenvalues $\lambda_i$), each component evolves independently:

$$\theta_t^{(i)} = (1 - \eta \lambda_i)^t \theta_0^{(i)}$$

b) For convergence: $|1 - \eta \lambda_i| < 1$ for all $i$, which requires $0 < \eta < 2/\lambda_d$. The convergence rate is determined by the slowest mode:

$$\rho = \max_i |1 - \eta \lambda_i|$$

The optimal learning rate minimizes $\rho$. Setting $|1 - \eta \lambda_1| = |1 - \eta \lambda_d|$ gives:

$$\eta^* = \frac{2}{\lambda_1 + \lambda_d}$$

$$\rho^* = \frac{\lambda_d - \lambda_1}{\lambda_d + \lambda_1} = \frac{\kappa - 1}{\kappa + 1}$$

where $\kappa = \lambda_d / \lambda_1$ is the condition number.

c) At thermal equilibrium at the minimum of $L$, each parameter mode has variance $\sigma_i^2 = k_B T / \lambda_i$ (equipartition theorem). A gradient step changes mode $i$ by:

$$\Delta \theta_i = -\eta \lambda_i \theta_i$$

The minimum entropy production for displacing a thermally fluctuating degree of freedom by $\Delta \theta_i$ is (from stochastic thermodynamics):

$$\Delta S_{\text{env}} \geq \frac{k_B}{2} \sum_i \frac{(\Delta \theta_i)^2}{\sigma_i^2} = \frac{k_B}{2} \sum_i \frac{\eta^2 \lambda_i^2 \theta_i^2}{k_B T / \lambda_i} = \frac{\eta^2}{2T} \sum_i \lambda_i^3 \theta_i^2$$

The minimum energy dissipated is $Q = T \cdot \Delta S_{\text{env}} \geq \frac{\eta^2}{2} \sum_i \lambda_i^3 \theta_i^2$.

d) To reduce the loss by a factor $\epsilon$, we need $\rho^t \leq \epsilon^{1/2}$ (since $L \propto \|\theta\|^2$), giving $t \geq \frac{\ln(1/\epsilon)}{2|\ln \rho|}$. For large $\kappa$: $|\ln \rho| \approx 2/\kappa$, so $t \geq \frac{\kappa \ln(1/\epsilon)}{4}$.

The total energy is at least $t$ times the per-step dissipation. Using the optimal learning rate and summing over steps:

$$E_{\text{total}} \geq \frac{\kappa \ln(1/\epsilon)}{4} \times \frac{k_B T}{2} \times d_{\text{eff}}$$

where $d_{\text{eff}}$ depends on the spectral structure. The key result is that the minimum dissipation scales as $O(\kappa \cdot d \cdot k_B T \cdot \ln(1/\epsilon))$ — ill-conditioned problems require fundamentally more energy to solve, even at the thermodynamic limit.

9. Minimum Dissipation for Logistic Regression ↓

Consider logistic regression with $n$ data points in $d$ dimensions, minimizing the cross-entropy loss:

$$L(\theta) = -\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \left[ y_i \ln \sigma(x_i^T \theta) + (1 - y_i) \ln(1 - \sigma(x_i^T \theta)) \right]$$

where $\sigma(z) = 1/(1 + e^{-z})$.

  1. Show that the Hessian of $L$ is $H = \frac{1}{n} X^T D X$ where $D = \text{diag}(\sigma_i (1 - \sigma_i))$ and $\sigma_i = \sigma(x_i^T \theta)$.
  2. If the data has unit variance features and the weights are near zero, approximate the eigenvalues of $H$ and the condition number.
  3. Using the result from Exercise 8, estimate the minimum thermodynamic energy required to train the model to loss $\epsilon$ at temperature $T = 300$ K, for $n = 10{,}000$ and $d = 100$.
  4. Compare this to the actual energy a modern GPU would use. What is the efficiency ratio?

a) The gradient of the logistic loss with respect to $\theta$ is:

$$\nabla L = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (\sigma_i - y_i) x_i = \frac{1}{n} X^T (\sigma - y)$$

Taking the derivative again:

$$\frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial \theta_j \partial \theta_k} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i(1 - \sigma_i) x_{ij} x_{ik}$$

In matrix form:

$$H = \frac{1}{n} X^T D X, \quad D = \text{diag}(\sigma_i(1-\sigma_i))$$

Since $0 < \sigma_i(1-\sigma_i) \leq 1/4$, $D$ is positive definite, and $H$ is positive semidefinite (positive definite if $X$ has rank $d$).

b) Near $\theta = 0$, $\sigma_i \approx 1/2$, so $D \approx \frac{1}{4}I$. Then:

$$H \approx \frac{1}{4n} X^T X$$

For unit-variance features, $\frac{1}{n}X^TX \approx I_d$ (sample covariance). So $H \approx \frac{1}{4}I_d$, meaning all eigenvalues are approximately $1/4$, and $\kappa \approx 1$. In practice, features are correlated, and the condition number of $X^TX$ might be $\kappa \sim 10$–$1000$ depending on the feature correlations.

c) Using the bound from Exercise 8 with $\kappa = 10$ (moderate conditioning), $d = 100$:

$$E_{\min} \geq \frac{\kappa \cdot d \cdot k_B T \cdot \ln(1/\epsilon)}{8}$$

For $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$: $\ln(1/\epsilon) \approx 13.8$

$$E_{\min} \geq \frac{10 \times 100 \times 4.14 \times 10^{-21} \times 13.8}{8} \approx 7.1 \times 10^{-18} \text{ J}$$

This is approximately $7 \times 10^{-18}$ J, or about 7 attojoules.

d) On a GPU, logistic regression with $n = 10{,}000$, $d = 100$ converges in roughly 100 epochs. Each epoch involves $\sim 2nd = 2 \times 10^6$ FLOPs, so total FLOPs $\sim 2 \times 10^8$. At $\sim 10^{-12}$ J/FLOP:

$$E_{\text{actual}} \approx 2 \times 10^8 \times 10^{-12} = 2 \times 10^{-4} \text{ J}$$

$$\text{Efficiency} = \frac{E_{\min}}{E_{\text{actual}}} = \frac{7 \times 10^{-18}}{2 \times 10^{-4}} \approx 3.5 \times 10^{-14}$$

The GPU operates at roughly $10^{13}$ times the thermodynamic minimum for this task. This enormous gap reflects the hierarchical inefficiencies: transistor physics, circuit design, memory hierarchy, software overhead, and the use of irreversible logic throughout.

10. Designing a Thermodynamically Efficient Learning Algorithm ↓

This exercise explores the fundamental limits of learning from a thermodynamic perspective.

  1. The thermodynamic uncertainty relation states that for any observable $J$ (e.g., rate of loss decrease): $\text{Var}(J) / \langle J \rangle^2 \geq 2k_B / \dot{S}_{\text{prod}}$. Explain in words what this means for the tradeoff between learning speed and energy dissipation.
  2. Consider SGD with learning rate $\eta$ and batch size $B$ on a loss with gradient noise variance $\sigma^2$. The effective temperature is $T_{\text{eff}} = \eta \sigma^2 / (2B)$. Derive the entropy production rate $\dot{S}_{\text{prod}} = \langle \|\nabla L\|^2 \rangle / T_{\text{eff}}$.
  3. Using parts (a) and (b), show that reducing the variance of the loss decrease by a factor of 2 requires at least doubling the entropy production rate. What does this imply for the energy cost of "smoother" training?
  4. Propose a learning schedule $\eta(t)$ that minimizes total entropy production while achieving a target final loss. Argue that it should start with a high learning rate and decrease over time (connecting to the empirical practice of learning rate schedules).

a) The TUR says: to make learning progress more predictable (low variance), you must dissipate more energy (high entropy production). If you want the loss to decrease steadily with small fluctuations, the physical system implementing the computation must produce entropy at a high rate. Conversely, if you tolerate large fluctuations in training progress, you can learn with less dissipation. This is a fundamental precision-cost tradeoff — not an engineering limitation but a law of physics.

b) In the Langevin picture, the SGD dynamics are $d\theta = -\nabla L \, dt + \sqrt{2D} \, dW$, where $D = \eta \sigma^2 / (2B)$ is the diffusion coefficient (related to $T_{\text{eff}}$).

The entropy production rate for overdamped Langevin dynamics in a potential $L$ is:

$$\dot{S}_{\text{prod}} = \frac{1}{T_{\text{eff}}} \langle v \cdot F \rangle = \frac{1}{T_{\text{eff}}} \langle \nabla L \cdot \nabla L \rangle \cdot \eta$$

More precisely, the average rate of dissipation per unit time is:

$$\dot{S}_{\text{prod}} = \frac{\eta}{T_{\text{eff}}} \langle \|\nabla L\|^2 \rangle = \frac{2B}{\sigma^2} \langle \|\nabla L\|^2 \rangle$$

This shows entropy production increases with batch size (more precise gradients cost more) and with gradient magnitude (steeper landscapes dissipate more).

c) From the TUR: $\text{Var}(J) \geq 2k_B \langle J \rangle^2 / \dot{S}_{\text{prod}}$. If we reduce $\text{Var}(J)$ by a factor of 2 while keeping $\langle J \rangle$ fixed, we need $\dot{S}_{\text{prod}}$ to at least double. Since entropy production is directly proportional to energy dissipation, halving the variance of training progress requires at least doubling the energy budget. This provides a physics-based explanation for the empirical observation that large-batch training (smoother loss curves) is less energy-efficient per training step than small-batch training (noisier but each step costs less energy).

d) The total entropy production is $S_{\text{total}} = \int_0^T \dot{S}_{\text{prod}}(t) \, dt$. To minimize this subject to reaching a target loss, we should use variational calculus (or the intuition from optimal transport).

Early in training, the gradient is large ($\|\nabla L\| \gg 0$), and the landscape is far from any minimum. A large learning rate (high $T_{\text{eff}}$) produces the same gradient step with less entropy per step (since $\dot{S} \propto 1/T_{\text{eff}}$ for fixed $\langle J \rangle$). Later in training, the gradient is small, and we are near a minimum. A smaller learning rate avoids overshooting (which wastes energy on "unproductive" entropy production from bouncing around the minimum).

The optimal schedule is therefore:

$$\eta(t) \propto \frac{\langle \|\nabla L(\theta_t)\|^2 \rangle}{\lambda_{\min}(H_t)}$$

which decreases as training progresses (since gradients shrink). This provides a thermodynamic justification for the universal practice of learning rate decay: decreasing the learning rate over time is not just an empirical trick — it is the thermodynamically optimal strategy.
