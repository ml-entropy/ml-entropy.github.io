02. The Carnot Cycle

The Carnot cycle is the most efficient heat engine possible—a theoretical ideal that sets fundamental limits on converting heat to work.

Heat Engines

A heat engine converts heat into work. It operates between a hot reservoir (temperature $T_H$) and a cold reservoir (temperature $T_C$):

  • Absorbs heat $Q_H$ from hot reservoir
  • Does work $W$
  • Rejects heat $Q_C$ to cold reservoir

By energy conservation:

$$Q_H = W + Q_C$$
Efficiency

$$\eta = \frac{W}{Q_H} = \frac{Q_H - Q_C}{Q_H} = 1 - \frac{Q_C}{Q_H}$$ Efficiency is always less than 1 (100%)—we can't convert all heat to work.

The Four Steps

The Carnot cycle consists of four reversible processes. Let's trace through them for an ideal gas:

Carnot Cycle Steps
1
Isothermal expansion at $T_H$: Gas expands slowly, absorbing heat $Q_H$ from hot reservoir while doing work. Temperature stays constant.
2
Adiabatic expansion: Gas expands without heat exchange, cooling from $T_H$ to $T_C$. Does work on surroundings.
3
Isothermal compression at $T_C$: Gas is compressed slowly, rejecting heat $Q_C$ to cold reservoir. Temperature stays constant.
4
Adiabatic compression: Gas is compressed without heat exchange, warming from $T_C$ back to $T_H$. Returns to starting state.

Carnot Efficiency

Since all steps are reversible, total entropy change is zero. The entropy changes occur only during isothermal steps:

$$\Delta S_{\text{total}} = \frac{Q_H}{T_H} - \frac{Q_C}{T_C} = 0$$

Therefore:

$$\frac{Q_C}{Q_H} = \frac{T_C}{T_H}$$
Carnot Efficiency

$$\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}$$ This is the maximum possible efficiency for any heat engine operating between temperatures $T_H$ and $T_C$ (in Kelvin).

Fundamental Limits

No real engine can exceed Carnot efficiency. To get 100% efficiency, you'd need $T_C = 0$ K (impossible) or $T_H = \infty$ (impossible). This is a fundamental law of nature, not an engineering limitation.

Numerical Examples

Power Plant

A steam power plant operates with steam at 550°C (823 K) and cooling water at 30°C (303 K):

$$\eta_{\text{max}} = 1 - \frac{303}{823} = 0.63 = 63\%$$

Real plants achieve about 40%—there's still room for improvement, but we can never exceed 63%.

Car Engine

Combustion at 1500°C (1773 K), exhaust at 100°C (373 K):

$$\eta_{\text{max}} = 1 - \frac{373}{1773} = 0.79 = 79\%$$

Real car engines achieve about 25-30% due to many irreversibilities.

Carnot's Theorem

Carnot proved two remarkable results:

  1. No engine operating between two temperatures can be more efficient than a Carnot engine.
  2. All reversible engines operating between the same temperatures have the same efficiency (independent of the working substance!).
Connection to Information

The Carnot cycle shows that there's a fundamental "cost" to extracting ordered energy (work) from disordered energy (heat). Similarly, Landauer's principle shows there's a fundamental energy cost to erasing information. Both arise from the Second Law of Thermodynamics.

Refrigeration: Carnot in Reverse

Run the Carnot cycle backwards and you get a refrigerator: work goes in, heat is pumped from cold to hot.

$$\text{COP}_{\text{cooling}} = \frac{Q_C}{W} = \frac{T_C}{T_H - T_C}$$

The coefficient of performance (COP) can exceed 1! A heat pump can deliver more heat than the electrical energy you put in, by extracting heat from the environment.
