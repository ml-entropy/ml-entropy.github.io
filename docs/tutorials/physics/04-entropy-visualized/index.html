Visualizing Entropy in Physics

Entropy is famously abstract. The equations are clean, but the intuition is slippery. This tutorial makes entropy visible. Every key concept is accompanied by diagrams, animated simulations, and interactive plots. By the end, you'll be able to see entropy increase, understand why it always does, and connect the physics to information theory and machine learning.

1. Microstates & Macrostates: The Counting Argument

The foundation of statistical mechanics is a single idea: a macrostate (what you can measure — temperature, pressure, volume) corresponds to many possible microstates (the exact positions and velocities of every particle). Entropy counts how many microstates match a given macrostate.

Boltzmann's Formula
$$S = k_B \ln \Omega$$

where $\Omega$ is the number of microstates and $k_B = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}$ J/K.

All Microstates of 4 Coins
With just 4 coins, the 2-Heads macrostate already dominates. With $10^{23}$ particles, the most probable macrostate is overwhelmingly dominant.

2. How the Dominant Macrostate Takes Over

With 4 coins, the most probable macrostate (2 Heads) has 6 out of 16 microstates — just 37.5%. But as the number of particles grows, the peak becomes overwhelmingly sharp. This is why the Second Law is not merely likely, but practically certain.

Probability of Each Macrostate as $N$ Grows
The binomial distribution $P(k) = \binom{N}{k} / 2^N$ for $N$ fair coins. As $N$ increases, virtually all microstates concentrate in a tiny band around $k/N = 0.5$. This is why the Second Law is practically a certainty.
Why the Second Law is Really a Probability Law

For $N = 10^{23}$ (Avogadro's number), the probability of being more than 0.001% away from the most probable macrostate is approximately $10^{-10^{17}}$. That's not "unlikely" — it's a number so small that the universe doesn't have enough time, space, or matter to ever witness such a fluctuation. The Second Law isn't a fundamental law — it's an overwhelmingly probable outcome of combinatorics.

3. Free Expansion: Watching Entropy Increase

The classic demonstration: a gas confined to one half of a box. Remove the partition, and the gas expands to fill the whole box. This is irreversible — you'll never see the gas spontaneously return to one side.

Free Expansion of a Gas
Before: Partition In Place
$\Omega_{\text{before}}$: particles in $V/2$
→
After: Partition Removed
$\Omega_{\text{after}}$: particles in $V$ — vastly more microstates
Each particle can now be in twice the volume. For $N$ particles, $\Omega_{\text{after}} / \Omega_{\text{before}} = 2^N$.
Entropy change: $\Delta S = k_B \ln(2^N) = N k_B \ln 2$. For 1 mole: $\Delta S \approx 5.76$ J/K.

Why is this irreversible? Each of the $N$ particles independently has a 1/2 chance of being in the left half at any given moment. The probability that all $N$ particles are simultaneously in the left half is $(1/2)^N$. For 1 mole of gas ($N \approx 6 \times 10^{23}$):

$$P(\text{all left}) = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{6 \times 10^{23}} \approx 10^{-1.8 \times 10^{23}}$$

This number is so unfathomably small that it will never happen in the lifetime of the universe. The gas doesn't "want" to expand — there are just overwhelmingly more ways to be spread out.

4. The Boltzmann Distribution: Energy Sharing

When a system is in thermal equilibrium at temperature $T$, the probability of finding it in a state with energy $E$ follows the Boltzmann distribution:

$$P(E) \propto e^{-E / k_B T}$$

This is the maximum entropy distribution given a fixed average energy — the same principle that underlies softmax in neural networks.

The Boltzmann Distribution at Different Temperatures
At low temperature, most particles occupy low-energy states (low entropy, ordered).
At high temperature, energy is spread more uniformly (high entropy, disordered).
Compare with softmax temperature in ML: low $T$ = peaked (confident), high $T$ = flat (uncertain).
The Physics-ML Connection

The softmax function $P(y_i) = e^{z_i/T} / \sum_j e^{z_j/T}$ used in every neural network classifier is exactly the Boltzmann distribution, where logits $z_i$ play the role of negative energies $-E_i$. When you tune "temperature" in GPT, you're adjusting the same thermodynamic parameter Boltzmann wrote about in 1877.

5. Entropy vs Temperature for Common Substances

Entropy increases with temperature, but not uniformly — it jumps sharply at phase transitions (melting and boiling). These jumps reveal the sudden increase in microscopic freedom as a substance changes phase.

Entropy of Water vs Temperature (at 1 atm)
Entropy increases gradually within each phase, but jumps at phase transitions. The boiling jump is ~5× larger than the melting jump because gas molecules have vastly more freedom (position & velocity) than liquid molecules.
Why Does Boiling Have a Bigger Entropy Jump?

When ice melts, molecules gain some freedom to move around each other, but they're still in close contact. When water boils, molecules fly apart into an enormous volume — their accessible phase space explodes. More accessible microstates = more entropy. The boiling entropy jump for water ($\Delta S_{vap} \approx 109$ J/mol·K) is so consistent across substances that it has a name: Trouton's Rule (most liquids have $\Delta S_{vap} \approx 85$–$88$ J/mol·K; water is higher due to hydrogen bonding).

6. Phase Space: Where Microstates Live

For a single particle in one dimension, the state is described by position $x$ and momentum $p$. The "phase space" is the 2D plane of all possible $(x, p)$ pairs. For $N$ particles in 3D, phase space has $6N$ dimensions. Entropy measures the volume of the accessible region in this space.

Phase Space of a Harmonic Oscillator
For a harmonic oscillator, constant-energy surfaces are ellipses in phase space. Higher energy means a larger accessible region, hence higher entropy. In $6N$ dimensions, these become hyper-ellipsoids.

7. Maxwell's Demon: Information Has a Physical Cost

Maxwell's 1867 thought experiment imagines a tiny intelligent being ("demon") controlling a frictionless trapdoor between two gas chambers. The demon measures each approaching molecule's speed and selectively opens the door to sort fast molecules into one chamber and slow ones into the other — creating a temperature difference from nothing.

Maxwell's Demon

The resolution came from Landauer's principle (1961) and Bennett's analysis: the demon must store information about each molecule it measures. When its memory fills up, it must erase bits to continue operating. Each bit erased dissipates at least $k_B T \ln 2$ of heat, and this dissipation produces at least as much entropy as the demon removed by sorting. The Second Law is saved.

Landauer's Principle
$$\Delta Q_{\text{erase}} \geq k_B T \ln 2 \text{ per bit erased}$$

At room temperature ($T = 300$ K), this is $\approx 2.87 \times 10^{-21}$ joules per bit. This is the fundamental minimum energy cost of computation.

8. Entropy of Mixing: Why Things Blend

When two different ideal gases at the same temperature and pressure are allowed to mix, the entropy increases — even though no energy is exchanged. This is purely a counting effect.

Entropy of Mixing Two Gases
$$\Delta S_{\text{mix}} = -N k_B \left[ x_A \ln x_A + x_B \ln x_B \right]$$
For equal amounts ($x_A = x_B = 0.5$): $\Delta S_{\text{mix}} = N k_B \ln 2$ per particle.
This is the exact same form as Shannon entropy $H = -\sum p_i \log p_i$!
The Deep Connection

The entropy of mixing formula $\Delta S = -Nk_B \sum x_i \ln x_i$ is identical in form to Shannon entropy $H = -\sum p_i \log p_i$. The mole fractions $x_i$ play the same role as probabilities $p_i$. The physical act of mixing gases and the mathematical act of increasing uncertainty are the same operation viewed from different angles.

10. The Entropy Budget of the Universe

Where does all the entropy in the universe reside? The answer is surprising.

Entropy Budget of the Observable Universe
Supermassive black holes contain 99.99...% of all entropy in the observable universe. All visible matter (stars, planets, life) is an insignificant fraction of the cosmic entropy budget.
Penrose's Puzzle: Why Did the Universe Start at Low Entropy?

The Second Law says entropy increases. So the early universe must have had very low entropy. But the Big Bang was a hot, uniform plasma — isn't that high entropy? Not gravitationally. Gravity wants to clump matter into stars and black holes. A smooth, uniform distribution of matter is actually a very special, low-entropy state when gravity is considered. The entire history of cosmic structure formation — stars igniting, galaxies forming, black holes growing — is the story of gravitational entropy increasing.

11. The Bridge to Information Theory

Let's make the physics-information connection precise.

From Boltzmann to Shannon: Two Views of the Same Idea
When all microstates are equally likely ($p_i = 1/\Omega$), Shannon's formula gives $H = \log \Omega$, which is Boltzmann's formula (up to the constant $k_B$). They are the same quantity.
