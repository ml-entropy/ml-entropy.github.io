The Zeroth Law: Temperature and Equilibrium

Thermodynamics is built upon four laws, numbered somewhat whimsically from zero to three. The Zeroth Law, added last historically but placed first logically, establishes the very concept of temperature as a well-defined physical quantity. Without it, thermodynamics has no foundation to stand on.

The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics

If system $A$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $B$, and system $B$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, then system $A$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $C$.

$$A \sim B \quad \text{and} \quad B \sim C \quad \implies \quad A \sim C$$

where $\sim$ denotes "is in thermal equilibrium with."

This statement seems almost trivially obvious — and that is precisely its power. It asserts that thermal equilibrium is a transitive relation. Combined with the fact that every system is in equilibrium with itself (reflexivity) and that if $A$ is in equilibrium with $B$ then $B$ is in equilibrium with $A$ (symmetry), thermal equilibrium forms an equivalence relation on the set of all thermodynamic systems.

Temperature as an Equivalence Class

An equivalence relation partitions a set into disjoint classes. Here, the equivalence classes are the sets of all systems that are mutually in thermal equilibrium with each other. We label each equivalence class with a number — and that number is what we call temperature.

The Deep Insight

Temperature is not a primitive concept that we measure directly. It is a derived quantity whose existence is guaranteed by the transitivity of thermal equilibrium. The Zeroth Law tells us that a function $\theta$ exists such that two systems are in thermal equilibrium if and only if $\theta(A) = \theta(B)$. This function $\theta$ is temperature. Without the Zeroth Law, there would be no guarantee that a single number could characterize whether two systems will exchange heat when brought into contact.

Mathematically, the Zeroth Law guarantees the existence of a function of state $\theta$ such that for any two systems $A$ and $B$:

$$A \sim B \iff \theta(A) = \theta(B)$$

Empirical vs. Absolute Temperature

The Zeroth Law guarantees that temperature exists, but it does not specify a unique scale. Any monotonic function of $\theta$ is an equally valid temperature. This leads to two classes of temperature scales:

  • Empirical temperature scales are defined by the properties of a particular substance — the expansion of mercury, the resistance of a platinum wire, the pressure of a gas at constant volume. Different thermometers may disagree except at calibration points. Examples: Celsius, Fahrenheit, Réaumur.
  • Absolute (thermodynamic) temperature is defined independently of any substance, using the efficiency of a Carnot engine operating between two reservoirs: $$\frac{T_C}{T_H} = \frac{Q_C}{Q_H}$$ This defines temperature ratios uniquely. Choosing one fixed point (the triple point of water at $273.16 \text{ K}$) then defines the Kelvin scale absolutely. The key insight, due to Lord Kelvin, is that this definition depends only on the laws of thermodynamics, not on the properties of any particular material.
Why the Zeroth Law Matters

Consider three cups of water: $A$, $B$, and $C$. You place a thermometer (system $B$) in cup $A$ and wait for equilibrium — the thermometer reads $50^\circ\text{C}$. You then place the same thermometer in cup $C$ and it also reads $50^\circ\text{C}$. The Zeroth Law guarantees that if you now pour cups $A$ and $C$ together, no net heat will flow — they are already in thermal equilibrium, even though they never touched each other.

Without the Zeroth Law, there would be no reason to believe that a thermometer reading tells you anything about whether two systems will exchange heat. The thermometer might equilibrate with $A$ at one reading and with $C$ at the same reading, yet $A$ and $C$ might still exchange heat when brought together. The Zeroth Law rules out this pathological possibility.

The Zeroth Law was formalized by Ralph H. Fowler in 1931, long after the First and Second Laws were established. It was numbered "zero" because it is logically prior to the other laws — they all presuppose a well-defined notion of temperature.

The First Law: Conservation of Energy

The First Law of Thermodynamics is the principle of energy conservation applied to thermodynamic systems. It states that energy can be converted between forms — heat, work, internal energy — but can never be created or destroyed.

The First Law of Thermodynamics

For an infinitesimal process, the change in internal energy $U$ of a system is:

$$dU = \delta Q - \delta W$$

Understanding the Variables:

  • $dU$ (Internal Energy Change): An exact differential — the change in the total microscopic energy (kinetic + potential) of all particles in the system. $U$ is a state function: it depends only on the current state, not on how the system got there.
  • $\delta Q$ (Heat): An inexact differential — the energy transferred due to a temperature difference between the system and its surroundings. Heat is a process quantity, not a state function. The $\delta$ reminds us that it is path-dependent.
  • $\delta W$ (Work): An inexact differential — the energy transferred by macroscopic mechanical means (e.g., expansion against a piston, $\delta W = P\,dV$ for pressure-volume work). Also path-dependent.

Sign convention: $\delta Q > 0$ means heat flows into the system; $\delta W > 0$ means work is done by the system on its surroundings. (Some textbooks use the opposite sign for work: $dU = \delta Q + \delta W$, where $\delta W$ is work done on the system.)

Internal Energy as a State Function

The deep content of the First Law is not merely "energy is conserved" — classical mechanics already tells us that. The First Law asserts something stronger: there exists a function of state $U$ (internal energy) such that for any process connecting two equilibrium states, the difference $Q - W$ is the same, even though $Q$ and $W$ individually depend on the path.

$$\Delta U = Q - W = \text{constant for fixed initial and final states}$$

This is remarkable. Heat and work are individually path-dependent — you can transfer wildly different amounts of heat and work depending on the process. But their difference always yields the same $\Delta U$. The First Law guarantees the existence of this state function.

Exact vs. Inexact Differentials Revisited

The distinction between $d$ and $\delta$ is not merely notational — it reflects deep mathematical structure. An exact differential $dU$ satisfies the property:

$$\oint dU = 0 \quad \text{(for any cyclic process)}$$

This means $U$ returns to its original value after any cycle. In contrast, for inexact differentials:

$$\oint \delta Q \neq 0, \qquad \oint \delta W \neq 0 \quad \text{(in general)}$$

A heat engine exploits exactly this: over one cycle, $\Delta U = 0$, but $Q_{\text{net}} = W_{\text{net}} \neq 0$. The engine absorbs more heat than it rejects, and the difference becomes useful work.

Example: Two Paths Between the Same States

Consider an ideal gas going from state $(P_1, V_1, T_1)$ to state $(P_2, V_2, T_2)$.

Path A — Isothermal expansion then isochoric heating:

  • Step 1: Expand at constant $T_1$ from $V_1$ to $V_2$. Work done: $W_1 = nRT_1 \ln(V_2/V_1)$. Heat absorbed: $Q_1 = W_1$ (since $\Delta U = 0$ for isothermal process of ideal gas).
  • Step 2: Heat at constant $V_2$ from $T_1$ to $T_2$. Work done: $W_2 = 0$. Heat absorbed: $Q_2 = nC_V(T_2 - T_1)$.

Path B — Isochoric heating then isothermal expansion:

  • Step 1: Heat at constant $V_1$ from $T_1$ to $T_2$. Work: $W_1' = 0$. Heat: $Q_1' = nC_V(T_2 - T_1)$.
  • Step 2: Expand at constant $T_2$ from $V_1$ to $V_2$. Work: $W_2' = nRT_2 \ln(V_2/V_1)$. Heat: $Q_2' = W_2'$.

The total work and total heat are different for the two paths ($W_A \neq W_B$ and $Q_A \neq Q_B$), but the change in internal energy is the same:

$$\Delta U = Q_A - W_A = Q_B - W_B = nC_V(T_2 - T_1)$$

This is the First Law in action: $U$ is a state function, while $Q$ and $W$ are not.

Common Pitfall

Never write "$dQ$" or "$dW$" with a regular $d$ — this would imply that $Q$ and $W$ are state functions, which they are not. Always use $\delta Q$ and $\delta W$ to indicate path dependence. The notation $dU = \delta Q - \delta W$ encodes the remarkable fact that the difference of two path-dependent quantities is path-independent. For the complete mathematical treatment, see Tutorial 03: Exact vs. Inexact Differentials.

The Second Law: Entropy and Irreversibility

The Second Law is the deepest and most consequential law of thermodynamics. While the First Law tells us that energy is conserved, it says nothing about direction: it would be perfectly consistent with energy conservation for heat to flow from cold to hot, or for a shattered glass to spontaneously reassemble. The Second Law introduces a fundamental asymmetry — an arrow of time — that the First Law alone cannot provide.

There are two classical statements of the Second Law, due to Clausius and Kelvin-Planck respectively. They appear very different but are logically equivalent.

Clausius Statement (1850)

It is impossible to construct a device that operates in a cycle and produces no effect other than the transfer of heat from a cooler body to a hotter body.

In plain language: heat does not spontaneously flow from cold to hot. A refrigerator can move heat from cold to hot, but only by consuming work — there is always "some other effect."

Kelvin-Planck Statement (1851)

It is impossible to construct a device that operates in a cycle and produces no effect other than the conversion of heat, extracted from a single thermal reservoir, entirely into work.

In plain language: you cannot build a perfect heat engine that converts heat to work with 100% efficiency. Some heat must always be rejected to a cold reservoir.

Equivalence of the Two Statements

The two statements are equivalent: violating either one implies violating the other. The proof proceeds by contradiction in both directions.

Clausius violation $\implies$ Kelvin-Planck violation: Suppose we have a device that transfers heat $Q_C$ from a cold reservoir to a hot reservoir with no other effect. Now couple it with a normal heat engine operating between the same reservoirs, absorbing $Q_H$ from the hot reservoir, doing work $W = Q_H - Q_C$, and rejecting $Q_C$ to the cold reservoir. The net effect of the combined system is: absorb $Q_H - Q_C$ from the hot reservoir, do work $W = Q_H - Q_C$, and reject zero net heat to the cold reservoir. This violates Kelvin-Planck.

Kelvin-Planck violation $\implies$ Clausius violation: Suppose we have an engine that absorbs heat $Q$ from a single hot reservoir and converts it entirely to work $W = Q$. Use this work to drive a heat pump that transfers heat from the cold reservoir to the hot reservoir. The net effect is the transfer of heat from cold to hot with no work input — violating Clausius.

Entropy as a State Function

The Second Law's most profound mathematical consequence is the existence of a new state function: entropy $S$. Clausius showed that for any reversible process:

$$dS = \frac{\delta Q_{\text{rev}}}{T}$$

The quantity $\delta Q_{\text{rev}} / T$ is an exact differential even though $\delta Q_{\text{rev}}$ alone is not. The temperature $T$ acts as an integrating factor that "tames" the path dependence of heat into a well-defined state variable.

For any process (reversible or not), the Clausius inequality holds:

$$dS \geq \frac{\delta Q}{T}$$

The equality holds for reversible processes; the strict inequality holds for all irreversible (real) processes. For an isolated system ($\delta Q = 0$), this reduces to:

$$dS \geq 0 \quad \text{(isolated system)}$$

This is the mathematical statement that entropy never decreases in an isolated system. Every irreversible process — friction, free expansion, heat conduction across a finite temperature difference, mixing — generates entropy. The universe, being the ultimate isolated system, marches inexorably toward higher entropy.

Irreversibility and Entropy Production

For any real process, we can decompose the entropy change into two parts:

$$dS = \frac{\delta Q}{T} + \delta S_{\text{irr}}, \qquad \delta S_{\text{irr}} \geq 0$$

The first term is entropy change due to heat exchange with the surroundings; the second is entropy production due to irreversibilities within the system. Entropy production $\delta S_{\text{irr}}$ is always non-negative and equals zero only for reversible processes. This quantity is the true measure of "wastefulness" in a process — it represents the useful work that was dissipated into thermal energy.

The Second Law: Statistical Interpretation

The classical Second Law, as stated by Clausius and Kelvin, is a macroscopic phenomenological statement. It tells us what happens but not why. The statistical interpretation, developed by Boltzmann, Maxwell, and Gibbs, reveals the Second Law to be a consequence of probability theory applied to systems with enormous numbers of particles.

Boltzmann's H-Theorem

In 1872, Boltzmann proved a remarkable result. He defined a quantity $H$ (related to entropy by $S = -k_B H$) for a gas described by a velocity distribution function $f(\mathbf{v}, t)$:

$$H(t) = \int f(\mathbf{v}, t) \ln f(\mathbf{v}, t) \, d^3v$$

Using the Boltzmann transport equation and the assumption of molecular chaos (Stosszahlansatz — that particle velocities are uncorrelated before collision), he proved that:

$$\frac{dH}{dt} \leq 0$$

Since $S = -k_B H$, this means $dS/dt \geq 0$ — entropy increases monotonically. The H-theorem was the first derivation of the Second Law from microscopic dynamics.

Loschmidt's Paradox and the Role of Assumptions

Josef Loschmidt raised a devastating objection: the microscopic equations of motion are time-reversible. If you reverse all particle velocities, the system retraces its path and $H$ increases. How can a time-irreversible result ($dH/dt \leq 0$) follow from time-reversible laws?

The resolution lies in the Stosszahlansatz. The assumption of molecular chaos is not time-symmetric — it applies to particles before collision but not after. This is where the arrow of time enters: it is smuggled in through a probabilistic assumption about initial conditions. The H-theorem tells us that given typical initial conditions, entropy overwhelmingly tends to increase. Atypical initial conditions (like all particles having precisely reversed velocities) are so fantastically improbable that they are never realized in practice.

The Fluctuation Theorem

Modern statistical mechanics has refined Boltzmann's insights through the Fluctuation Theorem (Evans, Cohen, Morriss, 1993; Gallavotti and Cohen, 1995). For a system driven out of equilibrium, the probability of observing an entropy decrease $-\sigma$ over a time interval $\tau$ is related to the probability of the corresponding entropy increase $+\sigma$ by:

$$\frac{P(\Delta S = +\sigma)}{P(\Delta S = -\sigma)} = e^{\sigma / k_B}$$

This is an exact result, valid for all system sizes. For macroscopic systems where $\sigma \gg k_B$, the ratio is astronomically large, making entropy decreases effectively impossible. But for nanoscale systems where $\sigma \sim k_B$, transient entropy decreases are observable — and have been experimentally confirmed. The Second Law is thus revealed to be a statement about overwhelming probability, not absolute impossibility.

The Bridge to Shannon Entropy

The Gibbs entropy formula provides the crucial bridge between physics and information theory:

$$S = -k_B \sum_i p_i \ln p_i$$

This is mathematically identical to Shannon's information entropy $H = -\sum p_i \log_2 p_i$, differing only in the constant ($k_B$ vs. $1/\ln 2$) and the logarithm base. The deep reason for this identity is that both quantities answer the same question: how much do we not know?

  • Gibbs entropy measures our ignorance about which microstate the physical system occupies, given macroscopic observations.
  • Shannon entropy measures our ignorance about which message will be received, given the probability distribution of possible messages.

The Second Law, in this light, says: our ignorance about the microscopic state of an isolated system never decreases. As the system evolves, information about its initial microstate is dispersed into correlations among more and more particles, becoming effectively inaccessible. This is not a loss of information in a fundamental sense (Liouville's theorem guarantees that phase space volume is preserved), but a practical loss: the information becomes so finely distributed that no realistic measurement can recover it.

E. T. Jaynes and Maximum Entropy

Edwin T. Jaynes (1957) pushed this connection further. He argued that statistical mechanics is not really a physical theory at all — it is a form of statistical inference. The Boltzmann distribution $p_i \propto e^{-\beta E_i}$ is not derived from the dynamics of particles; it is the unique probability distribution that maximizes Shannon entropy subject to the constraint of known average energy. In this view, the Second Law is a theorem of probability theory: if you update your beliefs rationally (maximizing entropy given constraints), your entropy estimate can never decrease. Jaynes's maximum entropy principle has become foundational in Bayesian statistics, machine learning, and information theory.

The Third Law: Absolute Zero

The Third Law completes the edifice of thermodynamics by establishing a fundamental limit on entropy as temperature approaches absolute zero.

The Third Law (Nernst's Theorem, 1906)

As the temperature of a system approaches absolute zero, its entropy approaches a constant value, independent of all other thermodynamic variables (pressure, magnetic field, etc.). For a perfect crystal, this constant is zero:

$$\lim_{T \to 0} S(T) = 0 \quad \text{(perfect crystal)}$$

In Boltzmann's statistical language, this means that at $T = 0$, the system is in its unique ground state: $\Omega = 1$, and $S = k_B \ln 1 = 0$. The system has been completely specified — there is no remaining uncertainty about its microstate.

The Unattainability of Absolute Zero

A striking consequence of the Third Law is that absolute zero can never be reached in a finite number of steps. Each cooling step reduces the entropy by a finite amount, but as $T \to 0$, the entropy changes associated with any finite process shrink to zero. You can asymptotically approach $T = 0$ but never reach it — like Zeno's paradox realized in thermodynamics.

More precisely, consider a cooling process that alternates between two thermodynamic paths (e.g., adiabatic demagnetization followed by isothermal magnetization). The Third Law requires that the entropy curves for different values of the control parameter (e.g., magnetic field) converge to the same value $S = 0$ as $T \to 0$. This convergence means that each successive cooling step produces a smaller temperature drop, and infinitely many steps would be required to reach $T = 0$.

Residual Entropy

Not all substances reach $S = 0$ at low temperatures. Some have residual entropy — a non-zero entropy that persists even as $T \to 0$. This arises when the ground state is degenerate: multiple distinct microstates share the same lowest energy.

Example: Residual Entropy of Ice

In ice, each oxygen atom is tetrahedrally bonded to four neighbors by hydrogen bonds, with two hydrogens close and two far (the "ice rules"). Linus Pauling (1935) calculated that the number of configurations satisfying these rules for $N$ water molecules is approximately:

$$\Omega \approx \left(\frac{3}{2}\right)^N$$

This gives a residual entropy per mole of $S_{\text{res}} = R \ln(3/2) \approx 3.37 \text{ J/(mol·K)}$, in excellent agreement with experiment ($3.41 \text{ J/(mol·K)}$). The ice rules create a macroscopic degeneracy that persists to $T = 0$.

Implications for Heat Capacities

The Third Law imposes a constraint on how heat capacities behave near absolute zero. Since entropy is related to heat capacity by:

$$S(T) = \int_0^T \frac{C(T')}{T'} \, dT'$$

For the integral to converge (i.e., for $S(T)$ to remain finite as $T \to 0$), the heat capacity $C(T)$ must vanish at least as fast as $T$ approaches zero:

$$\lim_{T \to 0} C(T) = 0$$

This rules out the classical Dulong-Petit law ($C = 3Nk_B = \text{const}$), which predicts a constant heat capacity at all temperatures. The resolution came from quantum mechanics: Einstein (1907) and Debye (1912) showed that $C \propto T^3$ at low temperatures for solids, exactly as the Third Law requires.

Thermodynamic Potentials and Maxwell Relations

With the four laws established, we can build a powerful mathematical framework using thermodynamic potentials. These are energy-like functions, each adapted to different experimental conditions (different "natural variables"), all related by Legendre transforms.

Internal Energy $U$

The fundamental thermodynamic relation combines the First and Second Laws:

$$dU = TdS - PdV$$

This tells us that the natural variables of $U$ are $S$ and $V$. From this differential, we read off:

$$T = \left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial S}\right)_V, \qquad P = -\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_S$$

Enthalpy $H = U + PV$

When pressure is held constant (common in chemistry and biology), it is convenient to Legendre-transform away the $V$ variable in favor of $P$:

$$H = U + PV \implies dH = TdS + VdP$$

Natural variables: $S$ and $P$. At constant pressure ($dP = 0$), the heat absorbed equals the enthalpy change: $\delta Q_P = dH$. This is why enthalpy is the natural quantity for calorimetry at constant pressure.

Helmholtz Free Energy $F = U - TS$

When temperature is held constant (e.g., a system in contact with a heat bath), we Legendre-transform $S$ to $T$:

$$F = U - TS \implies dF = -SdT - PdV$$

Natural variables: $T$ and $V$. At constant temperature, the maximum work extractable from a system is $-\Delta F$. This is why $F$ is called the "free" energy — it is the energy available to do useful work.

Helmholtz Free Energy in Statistical Mechanics

In the canonical ensemble (fixed $T$, $V$, $N$), the Helmholtz free energy is directly related to the partition function: $$F = -k_B T \ln Z, \qquad Z = \sum_i e^{-E_i / k_B T}$$ This single equation connects thermodynamics to statistical mechanics: once you know $Z$, you can derive all thermodynamic quantities by differentiation. The partition function is the Rosetta Stone of statistical physics.

Gibbs Free Energy $G = H - TS = U + PV - TS$

When both temperature and pressure are constant (the most common experimental conditions):

$$G = H - TS \implies dG = -SdT + VdP$$

Natural variables: $T$ and $P$. At constant $T$ and $P$, a process is spontaneous if and only if $\Delta G < 0$. Chemical equilibrium corresponds to $\Delta G = 0$. This is the most important potential for chemistry, biology, and engineering.

Legendre Transforms: The Unifying Principle

The four potentials are all related by Legendre transforms, which swap a variable with its conjugate:

$$\begin{aligned} U(S, V) &\quad \xrightarrow{S \to T} \quad F(T, V) = U - TS \\ U(S, V) &\quad \xrightarrow{V \to P} \quad H(S, P) = U + PV \\ U(S, V) &\quad \xrightarrow{S \to T, \, V \to P} \quad G(T, P) = U - TS + PV \end{aligned}$$

The conjugate pairs are $(T, S)$ and $(P, V)$. Each Legendre transform replaces an extensive variable (one that scales with system size: $S$, $V$) with its conjugate intensive variable (one that doesn't: $T$, $P$), or vice versa. The transform preserves all thermodynamic information — no data is lost.

Maxwell Relations

Since all thermodynamic potentials are state functions, their mixed second partial derivatives are equal (by the symmetry of second derivatives, also known as Schwarz's theorem). Applying this to each potential yields four Maxwell relations:

The Four Maxwell Relations

From $dU = TdS - PdV$:

$$\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_S = -\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial S}\right)_V$$

From $dH = TdS + VdP$:

$$\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial P}\right)_S = \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}\right)_P$$

From $dF = -SdT - PdV$:

$$\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T = \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V$$

From $dG = -SdT + VdP$:

$$\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial P}\right)_T = -\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P$$

These relations are extraordinarily useful because they connect quantities that are easy to measure (like $\partial V/\partial T$ at constant $P$ — just the thermal expansion coefficient) to quantities that are difficult to measure directly (like $\partial S/\partial P$ at constant $T$).

A Mnemonic: The Thermodynamic Square

Arrange the four potentials and four variables in a square:

$$\begin{array}{ccc} & S & \\ U & & H \\ & & \\ F & & G \\ & T & \end{array} \qquad \begin{array}{ccc} & S & \\ V & & P \\ & & \\ V & & P \\ & T & \end{array}$$

Each potential sits between its two natural variables. The differential can be read off: the coefficient of a natural variable is the opposite-corner variable, with a sign determined by the arrow direction. The Maxwell relations follow from the equality of mixed partials of each potential with respect to its natural variables.

Applications to Machine Learning

The laws of thermodynamics are not merely historical curiosities confined to steam engines. Their mathematical structures appear throughout modern machine learning, often providing the deepest available understanding of why certain algorithms work.

Energy-Based Models

An energy-based model (EBM) assigns an energy $E(\mathbf{x})$ to each possible configuration $\mathbf{x}$ of the data. The probability of observing $\mathbf{x}$ is given by the Boltzmann distribution:

$$p(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{e^{-E(\mathbf{x}) / T}}{Z}, \qquad Z = \sum_{\mathbf{x}} e^{-E(\mathbf{x}) / T}$$

This is identical in form to the canonical ensemble of statistical mechanics. Low-energy configurations are exponentially more probable than high-energy ones, with the "temperature" $T$ controlling the sharpness of the distribution.

Boltzmann Machines

A Boltzmann machine is a neural network whose units take binary values and interact through symmetric weights $w_{ij}$. The energy of a configuration $\mathbf{s} = (s_1, \ldots, s_n)$ is:

$$E(\mathbf{s}) = -\sum_{i < j} w_{ij} s_i s_j - \sum_i b_i s_i$$

This is exactly the Ising model of ferromagnetism. The network samples from the Boltzmann distribution at thermal equilibrium. Training adjusts the weights to minimize the free energy difference between the model distribution and the data distribution — a direct application of the variational principle from statistical mechanics.

Simulated Annealing as Thermodynamic Cooling

Simulated annealing exploits the thermodynamic analogy directly. To minimize a cost function $E(\mathbf{x})$:

  1. Start at a high "temperature" $T$, where the Boltzmann distribution is nearly uniform — the algorithm explores freely.
  2. Gradually decrease $T$ according to a cooling schedule.
  3. At each step, propose a random perturbation $\mathbf{x} \to \mathbf{x}'$. Accept it with probability $\min(1, e^{-\Delta E / T})$.
  4. As $T \to 0$, the distribution concentrates on the global minimum.

The analogy to physical annealing is precise: just as slowly cooling a metal allows its atoms to find the lowest-energy crystal structure (avoiding metastable defects), slowly decreasing the computational temperature allows the algorithm to find the global optimum (avoiding local minima). If the cooling schedule is logarithmically slow ($T(t) \propto 1/\ln t$), convergence to the global minimum is guaranteed — a result that follows directly from the theory of Markov chains and detailed balance.

Free Energy and Variational Inference

Perhaps the deepest connection between thermodynamics and machine learning is through variational inference. In Bayesian inference, we want the posterior $p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})$ but it is intractable. We approximate it with a simpler distribution $q(\mathbf{z})$ by minimizing the KL divergence:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) = \sum_{\mathbf{z}} q(\mathbf{z}) \ln \frac{q(\mathbf{z})}{p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})}$$

Rearranging, this is equivalent to maximizing the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO):

$$\text{ELBO} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_q[\ln p(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z})]}_{\text{energy term}} - \underbrace{(-\mathbb{E}_q[\ln q(\mathbf{z})])}_{\text{entropy term}} = \langle E \rangle - TS$$

The ELBO is exactly the negative variational free energy: $\text{ELBO} = -F_{\text{var}}$. Maximizing the ELBO is equivalent to minimizing the variational free energy, just as a physical system at constant temperature minimizes its Helmholtz free energy. The trade-off is identical:

  • The energy term $\langle E \rangle$ favors distributions concentrated on high-probability (low-energy) configurations — fitting the data well.
  • The entropy term $TS$ favors spread-out distributions — avoiding overconfidence and overfitting.

Temperature controls the balance. At low $T$ (high precision), the energy term dominates and the model fits the data tightly. At high $T$ (high entropy), the entropy term dominates and the model becomes more uncertain. This is the thermodynamic interpretation of the bias-variance trade-off.

Temperature in Softmax

The softmax function, ubiquitous in neural networks, is the Boltzmann distribution in disguise:

$$p_i = \frac{e^{z_i / T}}{\sum_j e^{z_j / T}}$$

where $z_i$ are the logits and $T$ is the temperature parameter. The thermodynamic analogy is exact:

  • $T \to 0$: The distribution becomes a delta function on the largest logit (ground state). This is the "greedy" or "argmax" limit — the system is "frozen."
  • $T = 1$: Standard softmax. A balance between exploitation and exploration.
  • $T \to \infty$: The distribution becomes uniform over all classes. Maximum entropy — the system is "infinitely hot." All states are equally likely regardless of their energy.

Temperature scaling in language models (e.g., GPT) exploits this: low temperature produces focused, deterministic text; high temperature produces diverse, creative (but potentially incoherent) text. This is precisely the thermodynamic trade-off between energy minimization and entropy maximization.

The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Thermodynamics in ML

The parallels between thermodynamics and machine learning are not superficial analogies. They arise because both fields deal with the same fundamental problem: making optimal inferences from incomplete information about high-dimensional systems. The laws of thermodynamics are, at their core, laws of large-number statistics and information processing. Boltzmann machines, variational autoencoders, diffusion models, and energy-based models all rest on the same mathematical foundation that Clausius, Boltzmann, and Gibbs built in the 19th century.

Computational Explorations

The following Python scripts illustrate key ideas from the laws of thermodynamics. Run them in a Jupyter Notebook with numpy and matplotlib installed.

1. Simulated Annealing Optimization

This script implements simulated annealing to find the global minimum of a multi-modal function. Watch how the algorithm explores broadly at high temperature and gradually freezes into the optimal solution.

python 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def cost_function(x):
    """A multi-modal function with one global minimum near x=0.5."""
    return np.sin(5 * np.pi * x)**2 + 0.5 * (x - 0.5)**2

def simulated_annealing(cost_fn, x0, T_init, T_final, n_steps, cooling='exponential'):
    """
    Simulated annealing optimizer.

    Parameters:
        cost_fn: function to minimize
        x0: initial guess
        T_init: starting temperature
        T_final: ending temperature
        n_steps: number of iterations
        cooling: 'exponential' or 'logarithmic'
    """
    x = x0
    E = cost_fn(x)
    best_x, best_E = x, E

    history = {'x': [x], 'E': [E], 'T': [T_init], 'accepted': []}

    for i in range(1, n_steps):
        # Cooling schedule
        if cooling == 'exponential':
            T = T_init * (T_final / T_init) ** (i / n_steps)
        else:  # logarithmic (guaranteed convergence)
            T = T_init / (1 + np.log(1 + i))

        # Propose a random perturbation
        x_new = x + np.random.normal(0, 0.1)
        x_new = np.clip(x_new, 0, 1)
        E_new = cost_fn(x_new)

        # Metropolis acceptance criterion
        dE = E_new - E
        if dE < 0 or np.random.random() < np.exp(-dE / T):
            x, E = x_new, E_new
            history['accepted'].append(True)
            if E < best_E:
                best_x, best_E = x, E
        else:
            history['accepted'].append(False)

        history['x'].append(x)
        history['E'].append(E)
        history['T'].append(T)

    return best_x, best_E, history

# Run simulated annealing
np.random.seed(42)
best_x, best_E, history = simulated_annealing(
    cost_function, x0=0.1, T_init=1.0, T_final=0.001, n_steps=2000
)

# Plot results
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(16, 4))

# Cost landscape with trajectory

Exercise 1: First Law for an Ideal Gas

An ideal monatomic gas ($C_V = \frac{3}{2}nR$) initially at temperature $T_1 = 300 \text{ K}$ and volume $V_1 = 10 \text{ L}$ undergoes a constant-pressure expansion to $V_2 = 20 \text{ L}$. The gas consists of $n = 1 \text{ mol}$.

  1. Find the final temperature $T_2$.
  2. Calculate the work done by the gas $W$.
  3. Calculate the heat absorbed $Q$.
  4. Calculate the change in internal energy $\Delta U$ and verify the First Law: $\Delta U = Q - W$.
Show Solution

a) At constant pressure, Charles's law gives:

$$\frac{V_1}{T_1} = \frac{V_2}{T_2} \implies T_2 = T_1 \frac{V_2}{V_1} = 300 \times \frac{20}{10} = 600 \text{ K}$$

b) Work at constant pressure:

$$W = P \Delta V = nR(T_2 - T_1) = 1 \times 8.314 \times (600 - 300) = 2{,}494 \text{ J}$$

(We used $PV = nRT$ to get $P\Delta V = nR\Delta T$.)

c) Heat at constant pressure uses $C_P = C_V + nR = \frac{5}{2}nR$:

$$Q = nC_P \Delta T = 1 \times \frac{5}{2} \times 8.314 \times 300 = 6{,}236 \text{ J}$$

d) Internal energy change:

$$\Delta U = nC_V \Delta T = 1 \times \frac{3}{2} \times 8.314 \times 300 = 3{,}741 \text{ J}$$

Verification:

$$Q - W = 6{,}236 - 2{,}494 = 3{,}742 \text{ J} \approx \Delta U \; \checkmark$$

(The small discrepancy is due to rounding.) The First Law is satisfied: all the heat that did not go into work was stored as internal energy.

Exercise 2: Entropy Change in an Irreversible Process

A $2 \text{ kg}$ block of iron at $T_H = 500 \text{ K}$ is dropped into a large lake at $T_C = 300 \text{ K}$. The specific heat of iron is $c = 450 \text{ J/(kg·K)}$. The lake is so large that its temperature remains essentially constant.

  1. What is the final equilibrium temperature?
  2. Calculate the entropy change of the iron block.
  3. Calculate the entropy change of the lake.
  4. Calculate the total entropy change of the universe. Is this process irreversible?
Show Solution

a) The lake is a thermal reservoir (infinite heat capacity), so the final temperature is $T_f = 300 \text{ K}$.

b) Entropy change of the iron block (temperature changes continuously):

$$\Delta S_{\text{iron}} = mc \int_{T_H}^{T_C} \frac{dT}{T} = mc \ln\frac{T_C}{T_H} = 2 \times 450 \times \ln\frac{300}{500}$$

$$= 900 \times \ln(0.6) = 900 \times (-0.5108) = -459.7 \text{ J/K}$$

The iron's entropy decreases because it lost heat.

c) The lake absorbs heat $Q = mc(T_H - T_C) = 2 \times 450 \times 200 = 180{,}000 \text{ J}$ at constant temperature $T_C = 300 \text{ K}$:

$$\Delta S_{\text{lake}} = \frac{Q}{T_C} = \frac{180{,}000}{300} = 600.0 \text{ J/K}$$

d) Total entropy change:

$$\Delta S_{\text{universe}} = \Delta S_{\text{iron}} + \Delta S_{\text{lake}} = -459.7 + 600.0 = +140.3 \text{ J/K}$$

Since $\Delta S_{\text{universe}} > 0$, the process is irreversible, as expected for heat transfer across a finite temperature difference ($200 \text{ K}$). The entropy produced measures the "lost work" — the work that could have been extracted by a Carnot engine operating between the iron and the lake.

Exercise 3: Clausius Inequality Proof

Consider a heat engine operating between a hot reservoir at $T_H = 800 \text{ K}$ and a cold reservoir at $T_C = 200 \text{ K}$. In one cycle, the engine absorbs $Q_H = 1{,}000 \text{ J}$ and rejects $Q_C = 600 \text{ J}$.

  1. Evaluate $\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T}$ for this cycle.
  2. Compare the engine's efficiency to the Carnot efficiency.
  3. Show that the Clausius inequality $\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T} \leq 0$ is equivalent to stating that no engine can be more efficient than a Carnot engine.
Show Solution

a) Heat is absorbed at $T_H$ and rejected at $T_C$:

$$\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T} = \frac{Q_H}{T_H} - \frac{Q_C}{T_C} = \frac{1000}{800} - \frac{600}{200} = 1.25 - 3.00 = -1.75 \text{ J/K}$$

Since $-1.75 < 0$, the Clausius inequality is satisfied with strict inequality, confirming irreversibility.

b) Engine efficiency: $\eta = \frac{W}{Q_H} = \frac{Q_H - Q_C}{Q_H} = \frac{1000 - 600}{1000} = 0.40 = 40\%$

Carnot efficiency: $\eta_C = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H} = 1 - \frac{200}{800} = 0.75 = 75\%$

The real engine is far less efficient than the Carnot limit.

c) For any engine absorbing $Q_H$ at $T_H$ and rejecting $Q_C$ at $T_C$:

$$\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T} = \frac{Q_H}{T_H} - \frac{Q_C}{T_C} \leq 0 \implies \frac{Q_C}{Q_H} \geq \frac{T_C}{T_H}$$

The efficiency is $\eta = 1 - \frac{Q_C}{Q_H}$, so:

$$\eta = 1 - \frac{Q_C}{Q_H} \leq 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H} = \eta_{\text{Carnot}}$$

The Clausius inequality directly implies $\eta \leq \eta_C$. Equality holds only for a reversible (Carnot) engine.

Exercise 4: Maxwell Relation Derivation

Starting from the Helmholtz free energy $F = U - TS$:

  1. Derive the differential $dF$ in terms of $dT$ and $dV$.
  2. Identify $S$ and $P$ in terms of partial derivatives of $F$.
  3. Use the equality of mixed partial derivatives to derive the Maxwell relation: $$\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T = \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V$$
  4. Verify this relation for an ideal gas where $PV = nRT$.
Show Solution

a) Starting from $F = U - TS$ and using $dU = TdS - PdV$:

$$dF = dU - TdS - SdT = (TdS - PdV) - TdS - SdT = -SdT - PdV$$

b) Since $dF = -SdT - PdV$, comparing with $dF = \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial T}\right)_V dT + \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial V}\right)_T dV$:

$$S = -\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial T}\right)_V, \qquad P = -\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial V}\right)_T$$

c) The equality of mixed partial derivatives (Schwarz's theorem) gives:

$$\frac{\partial^2 F}{\partial V \partial T} = \frac{\partial^2 F}{\partial T \partial V}$$

The left side: $\frac{\partial}{\partial V}\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial T}\right)_V = \frac{\partial}{\partial V}(-S) = -\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T$

The right side: $\frac{\partial}{\partial T}\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial V}\right)_T = \frac{\partial}{\partial T}(-P) = -\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V$

Setting them equal: $-\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T = -\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V$, hence:

$$\boxed{\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T = \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V}$$

d) For an ideal gas, $P = nRT/V$:

Right side: $\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V = \frac{nR}{V}$

Left side: From $S = nC_V \ln T + nR \ln V + \text{const}$ (ideal gas entropy), $\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T = \frac{nR}{V}$

Both sides equal $nR/V$. The Maxwell relation is verified.

Exercise 5: Legendre Transform for Gibbs Free Energy

The Gibbs free energy is obtained from the internal energy $U(S,V)$ by two successive Legendre transforms: first replacing $S$ with $T$, then $V$ with $P$.

  1. Starting from $dU = TdS - PdV$, perform the Legendre transform $S \to T$ to obtain the Helmholtz free energy $F(T,V)$. Write its differential.
  2. From $F(T,V)$, perform the Legendre transform $V \to P$ to obtain $G(T,P)$. Write its differential.
  3. Show that $G = U + PV - TS = H - TS$, confirming the standard definition.
  4. For a process at constant $T$ and $P$, show that $dG \leq 0$ for spontaneous processes (i.e., processes that increase total entropy).
Show Solution

a) To replace $S$ with its conjugate $T = (\partial U/\partial S)_V$, we define:

$$F = U - TS$$

$$dF = dU - TdS - SdT = (TdS - PdV) - TdS - SdT = -SdT - PdV$$

So $F = F(T, V)$ with natural variables $T$ and $V$.

b) To replace $V$ with its conjugate $-P = (\partial F/\partial V)_T$, we add $PV$:

$$G = F + PV$$

$$dG = dF + PdV + VdP = (-SdT - PdV) + PdV + VdP = -SdT + VdP$$

So $G = G(T, P)$ with natural variables $T$ and $P$.

c) Substituting $F = U - TS$:

$$G = F + PV = U - TS + PV = (U + PV) - TS = H - TS \; \checkmark$$

d) At constant $T$ and $P$, $dG = -SdT + VdP = 0$... but this is only for reversible changes of state. For a general (possibly irreversible) process at constant $T$ and $P$, we use the Clausius inequality. The entropy change satisfies $dS \geq \delta Q / T$, and at constant $P$, $\delta Q = dH$. Therefore:

$$dS \geq \frac{dH}{T} \implies TdS \geq dH \implies dH - TdS \leq 0 \implies dG \leq 0$$

Spontaneous processes at constant $T$ and $P$ decrease Gibbs free energy. Equilibrium is reached when $G$ is minimized ($dG = 0$).

Exercise 6: Equivalence of Clausius and Kelvin-Planck Statements

Prove rigorously that the Clausius and Kelvin-Planck statements of the Second Law are logically equivalent. That is, show:

  1. If the Clausius statement is violated, the Kelvin-Planck statement is also violated.
  2. If the Kelvin-Planck statement is violated, the Clausius statement is also violated.
  3. Draw energy flow diagrams for both parts to make the argument visual.
Show Solution

a) Clausius violation $\implies$ Kelvin-Planck violation:

Suppose there exists a "Clausius violator" $\mathcal{C}$ that transfers heat $Q_C$ from a cold reservoir at $T_C$ to a hot reservoir at $T_H$ with no other effect. Now run a standard Carnot engine $\mathcal{E}$ between the same reservoirs: it absorbs $Q_H$ from $T_H$, does work $W = Q_H - Q_C$, and rejects $Q_C$ to $T_C$.

Combine $\mathcal{C}$ and $\mathcal{E}$ into a single composite device:

  • Cold reservoir: $\mathcal{E}$ dumps $Q_C$, $\mathcal{C}$ removes $Q_C$. Net exchange: $0$.
  • Hot reservoir: $\mathcal{C}$ delivers $Q_C$, $\mathcal{E}$ absorbs $Q_H$. Net absorption: $Q_H - Q_C$.
  • Work output: $W = Q_H - Q_C$.

The composite device absorbs heat $Q_H - Q_C$ from a single reservoir ($T_H$) and converts it entirely to work, with no other effect. This violates Kelvin-Planck.

b) Kelvin-Planck violation $\implies$ Clausius violation:

Suppose there exists a "Kelvin-Planck violator" $\mathcal{K}$ that absorbs heat $Q$ from a single hot reservoir at $T_H$ and converts it entirely to work $W = Q$. Use this work to drive a heat pump $\mathcal{P}$ that absorbs $Q_C$ from the cold reservoir and delivers $Q_C + W$ to the hot reservoir.

Combine $\mathcal{K}$ and $\mathcal{P}$:

  • Hot reservoir: $\mathcal{K}$ absorbs $Q$, $\mathcal{P}$ delivers $Q_C + Q$. Net: $+Q_C$ delivered to hot.
  • Cold reservoir: $\mathcal{P}$ absorbs $Q_C$. Net: $-Q_C$ removed from cold.
  • Work: $\mathcal{K}$ produces $Q$, $\mathcal{P}$ consumes $Q$. Net: $0$.

The composite device transfers $Q_C$ from cold to hot with no work input — violating Clausius.

c) Since violating either statement implies violating the other, they are logically equivalent: $\neg \text{Clausius} \iff \neg \text{Kelvin-Planck}$, which means $\text{Clausius} \iff \text{Kelvin-Planck}$.

Exercise 7: Third Law and Heat Capacity

The Third Law requires that heat capacities vanish as $T \to 0$.

  1. Show that if $C(T) = \text{const} > 0$ (as the classical Dulong-Petit law predicts), then $S(T) \to -\infty$ as $T \to 0$, violating the Third Law.
  2. The Debye model gives $C(T) = \alpha T^3$ at low temperatures for a solid. Show that this is consistent with the Third Law by calculating $S(T)$ at low $T$.
  3. For a metal, the electronic contribution to heat capacity is $C_{\text{el}} = \gamma T$ at low temperatures. Show this is also consistent with the Third Law.
  4. What is the total low-temperature heat capacity of a metal, and how does $S(T) \to 0$ as $T \to 0$?
Show Solution

a) If $C = C_0 = \text{const}$:

$$S(T) = S(0) + \int_0^T \frac{C_0}{T'} \, dT' = S(0) + C_0 \ln T$$

As $T \to 0$, $\ln T \to -\infty$, so $S(T) \to -\infty$. This violates the Third Law, which requires $S \to 0$ (or at least a finite constant). The classical theory is inconsistent with the Third Law.

b) With $C(T) = \alpha T^3$ (Debye model):

$$S(T) = \int_0^T \frac{\alpha T'^3}{T'} \, dT' = \alpha \int_0^T T'^2 \, dT' = \frac{\alpha T^3}{3}$$

As $T \to 0$, $S(T) \to 0$. The integral converges because $C/T = \alpha T^2 \to 0$. The Debye model is consistent with the Third Law.

c) With $C_{\text{el}} = \gamma T$:

$$S_{\text{el}}(T) = \int_0^T \frac{\gamma T'}{T'} \, dT' = \gamma T$$

As $T \to 0$, $S_{\text{el}} \to 0$. Consistent with the Third Law.

d) Total low-temperature heat capacity for a metal:

$$C_{\text{total}} = \gamma T + \alpha T^3$$

Total entropy:

$$S(T) = \gamma T + \frac{\alpha T^3}{3} \to 0 \quad \text{as } T \to 0$$

Both contributions vanish, satisfying the Third Law. The linear term (electronic) dominates at very low $T$; the cubic term (phonon) dominates at higher temperatures. This crossover is observed experimentally and is used to determine both $\gamma$ and $\alpha$ from heat capacity measurements.

Exercise 8: H-Theorem Connection to the Second Law

Boltzmann's H-functional is defined as $H = \int f(\mathbf{v}) \ln f(\mathbf{v}) \, d^3v$, where $f(\mathbf{v})$ is the velocity distribution function.

  1. Show that for the Maxwell-Boltzmann equilibrium distribution $f_{\text{eq}}(\mathbf{v}) = n \left(\frac{m}{2\pi k_B T}\right)^{3/2} e^{-m v^2 / 2k_B T}$, the quantity $H$ is related to the entropy by $S = -k_B H + \text{const}$.
  2. The H-theorem states $dH/dt \leq 0$. Explain why this is equivalent to $dS/dt \geq 0$.
  3. Discuss Loschmidt's reversibility objection and how the assumption of molecular chaos (Stosszahlansatz) breaks time-reversal symmetry.
Show Solution

a) Substituting $f_{\text{eq}}$ into $H$:

$$H = \int f_{\text{eq}} \ln f_{\text{eq}} \, d^3v$$

$$\ln f_{\text{eq}} = \ln n + \frac{3}{2}\ln\left(\frac{m}{2\pi k_B T}\right) - \frac{mv^2}{2k_B T}$$

Taking the expectation:

$$H = n\left[\ln n + \frac{3}{2}\ln\left(\frac{m}{2\pi k_B T}\right)\right] - \frac{m}{2k_B T}\int f_{\text{eq}} v^2 \, d^3v$$

The last integral is $n \langle v^2 \rangle = n \cdot 3k_BT/m$. So:

$$H = n\left[\ln n + \frac{3}{2}\ln\left(\frac{m}{2\pi k_B T}\right) - \frac{3}{2}\right]$$

The Sackur-Tetrode entropy for an ideal gas includes terms $S \propto -N[\ln(n) - \frac{3}{2}\ln T + \text{const}]$. Comparing, we identify $S = -Nk_B H / n + \text{const} = -k_B H + \text{const}$ (per unit volume), confirming the relationship.

b) Since $S = -k_B H + \text{const}$:

$$\frac{dS}{dt} = -k_B \frac{dH}{dt}$$

If $dH/dt \leq 0$, then $dS/dt \geq 0$. The H-theorem is exactly the Second Law expressed in terms of the velocity distribution function.

c) Loschmidt's objection: the microscopic equations of motion (Newton's or Hamilton's equations) are time-reversible. If $\{\mathbf{v}_i(t)\}$ is a solution, so is $\{-\mathbf{v}_i(-t)\}$. For the time-reversed solution, $H$ would increase, contradicting the H-theorem.

The resolution: the Stosszahlansatz assumes that the velocities of two particles are uncorrelated before collision. This is a statement about initial conditions, not about the dynamics. After collision, velocities become correlated (they have just interacted). The assumption breaks time-reversal symmetry by treating pre-collision states differently from post-collision states. If you applied the reverse assumption (uncorrelated after collision), you would derive $dH/dt \geq 0$ — the anti-H-theorem.

The arrow of time in the H-theorem comes not from the dynamics but from the boundary condition: we assume a low-entropy (uncorrelated) initial state. This is ultimately traced back to the low-entropy initial condition of the universe (the "Past Hypothesis").

Exercise 9: Variational Free Energy and the ELBO

In variational inference, we approximate an intractable posterior $p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})$ with a tractable distribution $q(\mathbf{z})$. This exercise shows the thermodynamic structure underlying the ELBO.

  1. Starting from the log-evidence $\ln p(\mathbf{x})$, show that: $$\ln p(\mathbf{x}) = \text{ELBO}(q) + D_{\text{KL}}(q(\mathbf{z}) \| p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}))$$ where $\text{ELBO}(q) = \mathbb{E}_q[\ln p(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z})] - \mathbb{E}_q[\ln q(\mathbf{z})]$.
  2. Identify the "energy" and "entropy" terms in the ELBO and write it as $\text{ELBO} = -\langle E \rangle + S[q]$, analogous to $-F = -\langle E \rangle + TS$ in thermodynamics (at $T = 1$).
  3. Explain why maximizing the ELBO with respect to $q$ is equivalent to minimizing the variational free energy $F_{\text{var}} = \langle E \rangle_q - S[q]$.
  4. How does this connect to the physical principle that systems at constant temperature minimize their Helmholtz free energy?
Show Solution

a) Start with the KL divergence:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) = \mathbb{E}_q\left[\ln \frac{q(\mathbf{z})}{p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})}\right] = \mathbb{E}_q[\ln q(\mathbf{z})] - \mathbb{E}_q[\ln p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})]$$

Using Bayes' theorem, $p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}) = p(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z}) / p(\mathbf{x})$:

$$D_{\text{KL}} = \mathbb{E}_q[\ln q(\mathbf{z})] - \mathbb{E}_q[\ln p(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z})] + \ln p(\mathbf{x})$$

Rearranging:

$$\ln p(\mathbf{x}) = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_q[\ln p(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z})] - \mathbb{E}_q[\ln q(\mathbf{z})]}_{\text{ELBO}} + D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p)$$

Since $D_{\text{KL}} \geq 0$, the ELBO is a lower bound on $\ln p(\mathbf{x})$.

b) Define the "energy" as $E(\mathbf{z}) = -\ln p(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z})$ and the entropy as $S[q] = -\mathbb{E}_q[\ln q(\mathbf{z})] = H[q]$. Then:

$$\text{ELBO} = -\mathbb{E}_q[E(\mathbf{z})] + S[q] = -\langle E \rangle_q + S[q]$$

This is exactly $-F_{\text{var}}$ where $F_{\text{var}} = \langle E \rangle_q - S[q]$ is the variational free energy (at $T = 1$). Compare with thermodynamics: $F = \langle E \rangle - TS = U - TS$.

c) Since $\ln p(\mathbf{x})$ is fixed (it doesn't depend on $q$):

$$\max_q \text{ELBO}(q) \iff \min_q D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) \iff \min_q F_{\text{var}}(q)$$

Maximizing the ELBO, minimizing the KL divergence, and minimizing the variational free energy are all equivalent optimization problems.

d) In thermodynamics, a system in contact with a heat bath at temperature $T$ evolves toward the state that minimizes its Helmholtz free energy $F = U - TS$. This is because minimizing $F$ maximizes the total entropy of system + bath (the Second Law). In variational inference, the optimization $\min_q F_{\text{var}}$ plays the same role: the "system" is the variational distribution $q$, the "energy" is the negative log-joint, and the "entropy" prevents $q$ from collapsing to a delta function. The optimal $q$ balances fitting the data (low energy) against maintaining uncertainty (high entropy) — exactly the thermodynamic trade-off between energy and entropy.

Exercise 10: Temperature in Softmax as Thermodynamic Temperature

The softmax function with temperature $T$ is $p_i = e^{z_i/T} / \sum_j e^{z_j/T}$, where $z_i$ are logits (unnormalized log-probabilities).

  1. Show that $p_i$ is a Boltzmann distribution with energy $E_i = -z_i$ and temperature $T$ (in units where $k_B = 1$).
  2. Calculate the entropy $S(T) = -\sum_i p_i \ln p_i$ of the softmax distribution as a function of $T$. Show that $S \to 0$ as $T \to 0$ and $S \to \ln K$ as $T \to \infty$ (where $K$ is the number of classes).
  3. Compute the "internal energy" $U = \langle E \rangle = \sum_i p_i E_i$ and the "free energy" $F = U - TS$. Show that $F = -T \ln Z$, where $Z = \sum_i e^{z_i/T}$ is the partition function.
  4. In the context of language model sampling, explain the thermodynamic meaning of "temperature scaling." Why does high temperature produce more "creative" text and low temperature more "focused" text?
Show Solution

a) Setting $E_i = -z_i$ and $\beta = 1/T$:

$$p_i = \frac{e^{z_i/T}}{\sum_j e^{z_j/T}} = \frac{e^{-E_i/T}}{\sum_j e^{-E_j/T}} = \frac{e^{-\beta E_i}}{Z}$$

This is the canonical Boltzmann distribution with partition function $Z = \sum_j e^{-\beta E_j}$.

b) As $T \to 0$ ($\beta \to \infty$): the distribution concentrates on the state with the largest $z_i$ (lowest energy). If $z_{\max}$ is unique, $p_{\max} \to 1$ and all other $p_i \to 0$. Thus $S \to -1 \cdot \ln 1 = 0$ (pure state, zero entropy).

As $T \to \infty$ ($\beta \to 0$): all Boltzmann factors $e^{-\beta E_i} \to 1$, so $p_i \to 1/K$ for all $i$. The entropy becomes:

$$S = -\sum_{i=1}^K \frac{1}{K} \ln \frac{1}{K} = \ln K$$

This is the maximum entropy — uniform distribution over all states.

c) The internal energy: $U = \sum_i p_i E_i = -\sum_i p_i z_i$.

The free energy: $F = U - TS$. We can also compute directly:

$$F = -T \ln Z = -T \ln \sum_j e^{z_j/T}$$

To verify: $S = -\frac{\partial F}{\partial T} = \ln Z + \frac{1}{T}\sum_i p_i z_i = \ln Z - U/T$, hence $F = U - TS = -T\ln Z$. Consistent.

d) In language model sampling, each token in the vocabulary has a logit $z_i$ produced by the neural network. The softmax temperature controls the Boltzmann distribution over next tokens:

  • Low $T$ (freezing): The system is in its "ground state." Only the highest-logit token has significant probability. The model always picks the most likely next word. Output is deterministic, repetitive, and "focused" — like a crystal at low temperature.
  • $T = 1$ (room temperature): Standard softmax. A thermal equilibrium between exploitation (preferring likely tokens) and exploration (occasionally sampling unlikely tokens). Balanced, coherent text.
  • High $T$ (melting/boiling): The distribution approaches uniform. All tokens become roughly equally likely regardless of their logits. Output is random and "creative" but often incoherent — like a gas at high temperature where molecules move chaotically in all directions.

The thermodynamic interpretation is exact: temperature controls the trade-off between minimizing energy (picking the most probable token) and maximizing entropy (maintaining diversity). This is the same trade-off that governs every physical system in thermal equilibrium.
