Beyond Equilibrium

Classical thermodynamics is a theory of equilibrium states. It tells us the final destination of a system — the state of maximum entropy — but says almost nothing about the journey. Yet the real world is overwhelmingly out of equilibrium: weather patterns, living organisms, chemical reactions, turbulent fluids, and even the expansion of the universe are all irreversible, non-equilibrium processes. Equilibrium is the exception; non-equilibrium is the rule.

The fundamental limitation is clear: equilibrium thermodynamics deals with states that do not change in time. It can compute the entropy difference between two equilibrium states, but it cannot describe the rate at which entropy is produced as a system evolves from one to the other. For that, we need a new framework.

Why Equilibrium Is Not Enough

Consider a cup of hot coffee cooling in a room. Equilibrium thermodynamics can tell us the final temperature (room temperature) and the total entropy change. But it cannot tell us how fast the coffee cools, what temperature gradients exist inside the cup, or what convective patterns form. Non-equilibrium thermodynamics provides the machinery to answer these questions.

The central quantity in non-equilibrium thermodynamics is the entropy production rate. For any irreversible process, the total entropy of the universe increases over time. We decompose the entropy change of a system into two parts:

$$dS = d_e S + d_i S$$

where $d_e S$ is the entropy flow from the environment (which can be positive or negative) and $d_i S$ is the internal entropy production due to irreversible processes. The second law demands:

$$d_i S \geq 0 \quad \text{or equivalently} \quad \dot{S}_i \geq 0$$

The equality holds only at equilibrium. For any real, irreversible process, entropy is being produced inside the system at a strictly positive rate. This rate $\dot{S}_i$ is the fundamental measure of how far a system is from equilibrium and how irreversible its dynamics are.

Entropy Production Rate

The rate of internal entropy production $\dot{S}_i$ quantifies the irreversibility of a process. It is always non-negative:

$$\dot{S}_i = \frac{d_i S}{dt} \geq 0$$

When $\dot{S}_i = 0$, the process is reversible (an idealization). When $\dot{S}_i > 0$, information about the system's past is being irreversibly lost. The magnitude of $\dot{S}_i$ measures how rapidly the system is dissipating free energy into heat.

Linear Irreversible Thermodynamics

The first systematic approach to non-equilibrium thermodynamics was developed for systems close to equilibrium. The key idea is beautifully simple: when a system is slightly perturbed from equilibrium, the fluxes (rates of transport) are linearly proportional to the forces (thermodynamic gradients) that drive them.

A thermodynamic force $X_j$ is a gradient that pushes the system away from uniformity — for example, a temperature gradient $\nabla(1/T)$, a chemical potential gradient $-\nabla(\mu/T)$, or an electric field. A thermodynamic flux $J_i$ is the response of the system — heat flow, particle diffusion, or electric current. Near equilibrium, they are related by linear transport equations:

Linear Flux-Force Relations
$$J_i = \sum_j L_{ij} X_j$$

The coefficients $L_{ij}$ are called Onsager coefficients (or phenomenological coefficients). The diagonal terms $L_{ii}$ represent direct effects (e.g., thermal conductivity), while the off-diagonal terms $L_{ij}$ ($i \neq j$) represent cross-effects — one type of force driving a different type of flux.

The entropy production rate can be expressed as a bilinear form in the forces and fluxes:

$$\sigma = \dot{S}_i = \sum_i J_i X_i \geq 0$$

This elegant expression says that entropy production is the sum of each flux times its conjugate force. Since $\sigma \geq 0$, the matrix of Onsager coefficients $L_{ij}$ must be positive semi-definite.

Onsager's Reciprocal Relations (1931)

Lars Onsager proved a remarkable result using the principle of microscopic reversibility (time-reversal symmetry of microscopic equations of motion): the cross-coefficients are symmetric:

$$L_{ij} = L_{ji}$$

This means that if a temperature gradient drives a particle flux (the Soret effect), then a concentration gradient must drive a heat flux (the Dufour effect), and the two cross-coefficients are equal. Onsager received the 1968 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for this discovery. The reciprocal relations reduce the number of independent transport coefficients and impose deep constraints on how different transport phenomena are coupled.

Example: Coupled Heat and Particle Transport

Consider a system with simultaneous heat flow and particle diffusion. The forces are $X_1 = \nabla(1/T)$ (thermal) and $X_2 = -\nabla(\mu/T)$ (chemical). The fluxes are:

$$J_1 = L_{11} X_1 + L_{12} X_2$$
$$J_2 = L_{21} X_1 + L_{22} X_2$$

The term $L_{12} X_2$ represents heat flow driven by a chemical potential gradient (Dufour effect). The term $L_{21} X_1$ represents particle flow driven by a temperature gradient (Soret effect / thermophoresis). Onsager's relation guarantees $L_{12} = L_{21}$.

The entropy production rate is:

$$\sigma = J_1 X_1 + J_2 X_2 = L_{11} X_1^2 + (L_{12} + L_{21}) X_1 X_2 + L_{22} X_2^2 \geq 0$$

Fluctuation Theorems

The second law of thermodynamics states that entropy production is non-negative: $\langle \sigma \rangle \geq 0$. But this is a statement about averages. What happens at the level of individual microscopic trajectories? Can entropy ever decrease along a single trajectory?

The answer, discovered in the 1990s, is yes — but with precise constraints. Fluctuation theorems quantify the exact probability of observing "second-law violations" at the microscopic scale. They are among the deepest results in statistical physics, providing an exact relationship between forward and time-reversed processes that holds arbitrarily far from equilibrium.

Crooks Fluctuation Theorem (1999)

Let $P_F(\sigma)$ be the probability of observing entropy production $\sigma$ during a forward process, and $P_R(-\sigma)$ be the probability of observing entropy production $-\sigma$ during the time-reversed process. Then:

$$\frac{P_F(+\sigma)}{P_R(-\sigma)} = e^{\sigma}$$

Here $\sigma$ is measured in units of $k_B$ (i.e., $\sigma = \Delta S_{\text{tot}} / k_B$).

The Crooks theorem has profound implications. First, it tells us that entropy-decreasing trajectories do occur, but they are exponentially suppressed. For a trajectory that would decrease entropy by $\sigma$ units of $k_B$, the probability ratio between the entropy-producing and entropy-consuming trajectories is $e^{\sigma}$. For macroscopic systems where $\sigma \sim 10^{23}$, the probability of an entropy-decreasing fluctuation is effectively zero — recovering the macroscopic second law.

Second, the theorem provides a quantitative arrow of time. If you observe a microscopic trajectory and compute $\sigma$, you can determine the probability ratio that the trajectory occurred in the forward versus reverse direction. The larger $\sigma$, the more "obviously" the trajectory is running forward in time.

Transient Violations of the Second Law

For small systems (colloidal particles, single molecules, nanoscale engines), the entropy production per trajectory can be of order $k_B$, meaning $\sigma \sim 1$. In this regime, entropy-decreasing fluctuations occur with appreciable probability. Experiments on colloidal particles in optical traps and RNA hairpin pulling experiments have directly confirmed these transient "violations" of the second law, exactly as predicted by the fluctuation theorems.

These are not true violations — the average entropy production remains non-negative. But individual trajectories can and do run "backward," producing negative entropy. The fluctuation theorem tells us exactly how often this happens.

A simpler but historically earlier result is the Evans-Searles fluctuation theorem, which applies to steady-state entropy production:

$$\frac{P(\sigma_t = A)}{P(\sigma_t = -A)} = e^{At}$$

where $\sigma_t$ is the time-averaged entropy production rate over a time interval $t$. As $t$ grows, the probability of observing negative average entropy production shrinks exponentially, recovering the macroscopic second law in the long-time limit.

Jarzynski Equality

In 1997, Christopher Jarzynski discovered one of the most remarkable results in modern statistical physics: a way to extract equilibrium free energy differences from non-equilibrium measurements. This was revolutionary because free energy differences are equilibrium quantities that traditionally require quasi-static (infinitely slow) processes to measure.

The Jarzynski Equality
$$\langle e^{-\beta W} \rangle = e^{-\beta \Delta F}$$

Here $W$ is the work performed on the system during a non-equilibrium process, $\beta = 1/(k_B T)$ is the inverse temperature, $\Delta F = F_B - F_A$ is the free energy difference between the final and initial equilibrium states, and $\langle \cdot \rangle$ denotes an average over many repetitions of the non-equilibrium process, each starting from thermal equilibrium at state $A$.

The beauty of this result is that the right-hand side is an equilibrium quantity ($\Delta F$), while the left-hand side involves a non-equilibrium average. You can drive the system as violently and rapidly as you wish — slam it, shake it, apply enormous forces — and as long as you average the exponential of the negative work over many trials, you recover the equilibrium free energy difference exactly.

Proof Sketch

Consider a system initially in thermal equilibrium at temperature $T$ with Hamiltonian $H_A$. We drive it via a time-dependent protocol $\lambda(t)$ from $\lambda_A$ to $\lambda_B$ over a time $\tau$. The work on a single trajectory starting from microstate $x_0$ is:

$$W[x_0] = \int_0^\tau \frac{\partial H(\mathbf{x}(t), \lambda(t))}{\partial \lambda} \dot{\lambda}(t) \, dt$$

Averaging over the initial Boltzmann distribution $\rho_A(x_0) = e^{-\beta H_A(x_0)} / Z_A$:

$$\langle e^{-\beta W} \rangle = \int dx_0 \, \rho_A(x_0) \, e^{-\beta W[x_0]}$$

Using the fact that the dynamics is Hamiltonian (or satisfies detailed balance for stochastic dynamics), and that $e^{-\beta W[x_0]} = e^{-\beta(H_B(x_\tau) - H_A(x_0))}$ for Hamiltonian dynamics (since $W = \Delta H$ when there is no heat exchange in the Hamiltonian sense), one can show through a change of variables and the Liouville theorem that the integral yields $Z_B / Z_A = e^{-\beta \Delta F}$.

The Jarzynski equality implies a strengthened form of the second law. By Jensen's inequality ($\langle e^{-x} \rangle \geq e^{-\langle x \rangle}$), we obtain:

$$\langle W \rangle \geq \Delta F$$

This is the classical result that the average work in any process must exceed the free energy difference, with equality only for reversible (quasi-static) processes. The Jarzynski equality is therefore a stronger statement than the second law — it is an equality from which the inequality follows.

Experimental Verification

The Jarzynski equality has been verified experimentally by pulling RNA hairpins with optical tweezers (Liphardt et al., 2002), stretching single molecules of titin, and manipulating colloidal particles in laser traps. In each case, repeated non-equilibrium pulling experiments yielded free energy differences consistent with equilibrium measurements, confirming the equality to remarkable precision.

The Crooks fluctuation theorem and the Jarzynski equality are closely related. In fact, the Jarzynski equality can be derived from the Crooks theorem by integrating:

$$\langle e^{-\beta W} \rangle_F = \int dW \, P_F(W) \, e^{-\beta W} = \int dW \, P_R(-W) \, e^{-\beta \Delta F} = e^{-\beta \Delta F}$$

Stochastic Thermodynamics

Stochastic thermodynamics extends thermodynamic concepts — work, heat, entropy — to the level of individual stochastic trajectories. Developed primarily by Udo Seifert and collaborators in the 2000s, it provides a complete thermodynamic description of small systems where thermal fluctuations are important.

The starting point is the Langevin equation, which describes the motion of a Brownian particle in a potential $U(x,t)$:

Overdamped Langevin Equation
$$\gamma \dot{x} = -\frac{\partial U(x,t)}{\partial x} + \sqrt{2\gamma k_B T} \, \xi(t)$$

Here $\gamma$ is the friction coefficient, $U(x,t)$ is a (possibly time-dependent) potential, and $\xi(t)$ is Gaussian white noise with $\langle \xi(t) \rangle = 0$ and $\langle \xi(t)\xi(t') \rangle = \delta(t-t')$. The first term is the deterministic force; the second is the random thermal force from the heat bath.

The probability density $p(x,t)$ of finding the particle at position $x$ at time $t$ evolves according to the Fokker-Planck equation:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left[\frac{1}{\gamma}\frac{\partial U}{\partial x} p + D \frac{\partial p}{\partial x}\right]$$

where $D = k_B T / \gamma$ is the diffusion coefficient (the Einstein relation). The Fokker-Planck equation is the continuum counterpart of the Langevin equation — one describes individual trajectories, the other describes the evolving probability distribution.

The key innovation of stochastic thermodynamics is defining thermodynamic quantities along individual trajectories. For a single trajectory $x(t)$:

  • Stochastic work: $w = \int_0^\tau \frac{\partial U(x(t), \lambda(t))}{\partial \lambda} \dot{\lambda} \, dt$ — the energy injected by changing the external protocol.
  • Stochastic heat: $q = \int_0^\tau \frac{\partial U(x(t), \lambda(t))}{\partial x} \circ dx$ — the energy dissipated to the bath (Stratonovich convention).
  • Trajectory entropy production: $\Delta s_{\text{tot}} = \ln \frac{P[\text{forward trajectory}]}{P[\text{reverse trajectory}]}$ — the log-ratio of forward to reverse trajectory probabilities.

The fluctuation-dissipation theorem connects the response of a system to a small perturbation with its spontaneous fluctuations at equilibrium:

$$\chi(\omega) = \frac{1}{k_B T} \int_0^\infty C(t) e^{-i\omega t} dt$$

where $\chi(\omega)$ is the frequency-dependent susceptibility and $C(t) = \langle \delta x(t) \delta x(0) \rangle$ is the autocorrelation function of spontaneous fluctuations. This theorem only holds at equilibrium; violations of the fluctuation-dissipation relation are a signature of non-equilibrium dynamics.

Entropy Production and Dissipation

Ilya Prigogine, who received the 1977 Nobel Prize in Chemistry, established a foundational result for systems close to equilibrium: the minimum entropy production principle.

Prigogine's Minimum Entropy Production Principle

For a system in the linear regime (close to equilibrium) with fixed boundary conditions (constant thermodynamic forces), the steady state is the state that minimizes the rate of entropy production $\sigma$ compatible with the imposed constraints.

$$\frac{d\sigma}{dt} \leq 0 \quad \text{(approach to steady state in linear regime)}$$

The entropy production rate decreases monotonically until the system reaches its non-equilibrium steady state, where $\sigma$ reaches its constrained minimum.

It is crucial to note that this principle applies only in the linear regime near equilibrium. Far from equilibrium, the minimum entropy production principle breaks down, and entirely new phenomena emerge.

Prigogine's most visionary insight was that systems driven far from equilibrium can spontaneously develop complex, ordered structures — what he called dissipative structures. These are patterns and organizations that are maintained by a continuous flow of energy and matter through the system. Unlike equilibrium structures (crystals, for example), dissipative structures exist only as long as the system is driven away from equilibrium.

Examples of Dissipative Structures
  • Bénard convection cells: When a fluid layer is heated from below, beyond a critical temperature gradient, spontaneous hexagonal convection cells form. The ordered pattern is maintained by the continuous flow of heat.
  • Belousov-Zhabotinsky reaction: A chemical reaction that produces beautiful oscillating color patterns — spirals and target waves — driven by the free energy of chemical reagents.
  • Living organisms: Perhaps the most spectacular dissipative structures. Life maintains its complex organization by continuously dissipating free energy (from sunlight or chemical bonds), producing entropy that is exported to the environment.
  • Hurricanes and weather patterns: Large-scale atmospheric structures driven by solar heating and Earth's rotation, maintained by continuous energy dissipation.

The transition from uniform states to dissipative structures typically occurs through bifurcations as a control parameter (the "distance from equilibrium") crosses a critical threshold. Near equilibrium, the uniform state is stable. Far from equilibrium, it becomes unstable, and the system spontaneously breaks symmetry to form a patterned state. This is a fundamentally different mechanism from equilibrium phase transitions — it requires a continuous flux of energy or matter.

The entropy production in a dissipative structure can be decomposed as:

$$\sigma = \sigma_{\text{internal}} + \sigma_{\text{transport}}$$

where $\sigma_{\text{internal}}$ arises from local irreversible processes and $\sigma_{\text{transport}}$ from the flows that maintain the structure. The total entropy production of the universe still increases, but the system locally organizes by exporting entropy to its surroundings faster than it produces it internally.

Applications to Machine Learning

Non-equilibrium thermodynamics has deep and increasingly important connections to machine learning. Many modern ML algorithms are, at their core, non-equilibrium thermodynamic processes operating on probability distributions.

Langevin Dynamics for Sampling (SGLD)

Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) is a sampling algorithm that directly implements the Langevin equation to sample from a target distribution $p(\theta) \propto e^{-U(\theta)}$:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla U(\theta_t) + \sqrt{2\eta} \, \xi_t, \quad \xi_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$$

where $\eta$ is the learning rate and $U(\theta) = -\log p(\theta | \text{data})$ is the potential energy (negative log-posterior). In the limit $\eta \to 0$, this is the Euler-Maruyama discretization of the overdamped Langevin equation. The key thermodynamic insight: SGLD samples converge to the Boltzmann distribution $p(\theta) \propto e^{-U(\theta)/T}$ with the learning rate playing the role of temperature. The "noise" is not a nuisance — it is the thermal fluctuation that enables exploration and ensures the correct equilibrium distribution.

Diffusion Models as Non-Equilibrium Processes

Score-based diffusion models (the engine behind DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, and similar systems) are directly inspired by non-equilibrium thermodynamics. The forward process gradually destroys structure by adding noise:

$$dx = f(x,t) \, dt + g(t) \, dW_t$$

This is a stochastic differential equation (SDE) that transforms data into noise — a non-equilibrium process that increases entropy. The reverse process learns to undo this entropy production:

$$dx = \left[f(x,t) - g(t)^2 \nabla_x \log p_t(x)\right] dt + g(t) \, d\bar{W}_t$$

The score function $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ is learned by a neural network. From a thermodynamic perspective, the forward process is spontaneous entropy production (second law), and the reverse process is a carefully engineered entropy-reducing process powered by the "work" of the learned score function. The connection to Jarzynski and Crooks is precise: the KL divergence between the forward and reverse path measures equals the average entropy production.

Annealed Importance Sampling

Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) creates a sequence of bridging distributions $p_0 \to p_1 \to \cdots \to p_N$ between a tractable distribution $p_0$ and a target $p_N$. Each transition is a non-equilibrium process, and the Jarzynski equality provides the theoretical foundation for the importance weights:

$$w = \prod_{n=0}^{N-1} \frac{p_{n+1}(x_n)}{p_n(x_n)}$$

The connection is direct: $\langle w \rangle = Z_N / Z_0$, which is a form of the Jarzynski equality. AIS is widely used for estimating partition functions and evaluating generative models.

Optimal Transport and Thermodynamic Cost

The thermodynamic cost of transforming one distribution into another is intimately related to optimal transport theory. The minimum entropy production for transforming $p_0$ into $p_1$ in time $\tau$ is bounded by:

$$\Delta S_{\text{prod}} \geq \frac{1}{2D\tau} W_2^2(p_0, p_1)$$

where $W_2$ is the 2-Wasserstein distance and $D$ is the diffusion coefficient. This provides a thermodynamic lower bound on the computational cost of any generative model that transforms noise into data.

Crooks Theorem and Detailed Balance in MCMC

Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods rely on the principle of detailed balance, which is the equilibrium condition ensuring that the chain converges to the target distribution. The Metropolis-Hastings acceptance probability:

$$A(x \to x') = \min\left(1, \frac{p(x') q(x'|x)}{p(x) q(x|x')}\right)$$

can be understood as enforcing Crooks-like balance between forward and reverse transitions. Breaking detailed balance (as in non-reversible MCMC methods) often accelerates mixing, at the cost of producing entropy — a direct thermodynamic trade-off between speed and dissipation.

The Arrow of Time

One of the deepest puzzles in physics is the arrow of time: why does time seem to flow in one direction? The fundamental laws of physics — Newton's equations, Maxwell's equations, the Schrödinger equation — are all time-reversal symmetric. If you film a collision between two billiard balls and play the film backward, both the forward and reverse trajectories obey Newton's laws perfectly. Yet we never see eggs unscramble or coffee spontaneously heat up.

This apparent contradiction is known as Loschmidt's paradox (1876): how can time-reversible microscopic laws give rise to time-irreversible macroscopic behavior? The resolution lies in the concept of entropy and initial conditions.

Loschmidt's Paradox Resolved

The macroscopic arrow of time emerges not from the dynamical laws themselves, but from the initial conditions of the universe. The early universe was in an extraordinarily low-entropy state (the "Past Hypothesis"). Given this special starting point, the overwhelming majority of microscopic trajectories lead to higher entropy in the future — not because the laws favor this direction, but because there are astronomically more high-entropy states to evolve into.

The second law is therefore not a law about dynamics — it is a law about boundary conditions combined with combinatorics.

The fluctuation theorems provide a precise quantitative framework for the arrow of time. For any microscopic trajectory, the entropy production $\sigma$ quantifies the "time-asymmetry" of that trajectory:

$$\sigma = \ln \frac{P[\text{forward}]}{P[\text{reverse}]}$$

When $\sigma > 0$, the trajectory is more likely in the forward direction — time's arrow is evident. When $\sigma < 0$, the trajectory is more likely to be seen running backward. The second law says $\langle \sigma \rangle \geq 0$: on average, the forward direction is preferred, but individual trajectories can fluctuate.

For macroscopic systems, $\sigma$ is enormous (of order $10^{23} k_B$ for moles of substance), and the arrow of time is absolute for all practical purposes. For nanoscale systems, $\sigma$ can be of order $k_B$, and the arrow of time becomes blurred — the future and the past become nearly indistinguishable, and entropy-decreasing fluctuations become common.

The Arrow of Time in Machine Learning

The thermodynamic arrow of time has a direct analogy in diffusion models. The forward diffusion process (data $\to$ noise) is the "natural" direction — it increases entropy and is easy to compute. The reverse process (noise $\to$ data) is the "unnatural" direction — it decreases entropy and requires a learned score function to guide it. The difficulty of the generative task is directly proportional to the entropy that must be "un-produced" — a thermodynamic perspective on why generation is harder than destruction.

1. Langevin Dynamics Simulation

Simulate a Brownian particle in a double-well potential using the overdamped Langevin equation. This demonstrates thermal fluctuations, barrier crossing, and the approach to the Boltzmann distribution.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def langevin_double_well(n_steps=100000, dt=0.001, gamma=1.0, kT=1.0):
    """
    Simulate overdamped Langevin dynamics in a double-well potential.
    U(x) = (x^2 - 1)^2 = x^4 - 2x^2 + 1
    Force: F(x) = -dU/dx = -4x^3 + 4x = 4x(1 - x^2)
    """
    x = np.zeros(n_steps)
    x[0] = -1.0  # Start in left well

    noise_std = np.sqrt(2 * kT * dt / gamma)

    for i in range(1, n_steps):
        force = 4 * x[i-1] * (1 - x[i-1]**2)
        noise = noise_std * np.random.randn()
        x[i] = x[i-1] + (force / gamma) * dt + noise

    return x

2. Jarzynski Equality Verification

Verify the Jarzynski equality numerically by pulling a particle in a harmonic trap. We compare the exponential average of work measurements with the true free energy difference.

python 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def jarzynski_harmonic_trap(n_trials=5000, n_steps=1000, dt=0.01,
                             kT=1.0, gamma=1.0, v_pull=1.0, k=1.0):
    """
    Verify Jarzynski equality by pulling a harmonic trap.

    Protocol: trap center moves from 0 to lambda_final = v_pull * n_steps * dt
    U(x, t) = 0.5 * k * (x - v_pull * t)^2

    For a harmonic trap, Delta F = 0 (the free energy shape doesn't change,
    only the center moves), so  should equal 1.
    """
    tau = n_steps * dt
    lambda_final = v_pull * tau
    work_values = np.zeros(n_trials)
    noise_std = np.sqrt(2 * kT * dt / gamma)

    for trial in range(n_trials):
        x = np.random.normal(0, np.sqrt(kT / k))  # Thermal equilibrium
        W = 0.0

        for step in range(n_steps):
            t = step * dt
            trap_center = v_pull * t

            # Work increment: dW = (dU/d_lambda) * d_lambda
            # dU/d_lambda = -k * (x - trap_center), d_lambda = v_pull * dt
            dW = -k * (x - trap_center) * (-v_pull) * dt
            W += dW

            # Langevin update
            force = -k * (x - trap_center)
            x += (force / gamma) * dt + noise_std * np.random.randn()

        work_values[trial] = W

    return work_values

# Run experiment with different pulling speeds
speeds = [0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 3.0]
kT = 1.0
beta = 1.0 / kT

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(14, 5))

jarzynski_estimates = []
mean_works = []

for v in speeds:
    W = jarzynski_harmonic_trap(n_trials=10000, v_pull=v, kT=kT)
    delta_F_jarzynski = -kT * np.log(np.mean(np.exp(-beta * W)))
    jarzynski_estimates.append(delta_F_jarzynski)
    mean_works.append(np.mean(W))

    axes[0].hist(W, bins=80, density=True, alpha=0.5, label=f'v = {v}')

axes[0].axvline(x=0, color='red', linestyle='--', label='$\\Delta F = 0$')
axes[0].set_xlabel('Work W')
axes[0].set_ylabel('Probability density')
axes[0].set_title('Work Distributions at Different Pulling Speeds')
axes[0].legend()

# Compare Jarzynski estimate vs mean work
axes[1].bar(range(len(speeds)), mean_works, alpha=0.5, label='$\\langle W \\rangle$')
axes[1].bar(range(len(speeds)), jarzynski_estimates, alpha=0.5,
            label='$-kT \\ln \\langle e^{-\\beta W} \\rangle$')
axes[1].axhline(y=0, color='red', linestyle='--', label='True $\\Delta F = 0$')
axes[1].set_xticks(range(len(speeds)))
axes[1].set_xticklabels([f'v={v}' for v in speeds])
axes[1].set_ylabel('Energy')
axes[1].set_title('Jarzynski Equality vs Mean Work')
axes[1].legend()

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

print("Jarzynski estimates of Delta F (true value = 0):")
for v, est, mw in zip(speeds, jarzynski_estimates, mean_works):
    print(f"  v = {v:.1f}: Jarzynski = {est:.4f},  = {mw:.4f}")

import numpy as np

from scipy.stats import norm

def sgld_mixture_of_gaussians(n_steps=50000, eta=0.01,
                               means=[-3, 0, 3], stds=[0.5, 1.0, 0.5],
                               weights=[0.3, 0.4, 0.3]):
    """
    Use SGLD to sample from a mixture of Gaussians.
    Target: p(x) = sum_k w_k * N(x; mu_k, sigma_k^2)
    Potential: U(x) = -log p(x)
    """
    means = np.array(means)
    stds = np.array(stds)
    weights = np.array(weights)
    weights /= weights.sum()

    def grad_log_p(x):
        """Gradient of log p(x) = -grad U(x)"""
        # p(x) = sum w_k * N(x; mu_k, sigma_k)
        components = weights * norm.pdf(x, means, stds)
        p_x = components.sum()
        if p_x < 1e-30:
            return 0.0
        # d/dx log p(x) = (1/p(x)) * sum w_k * N'(x; mu_k, sigma_k)
        grad_components = -components * (x - means) / stds**2
        return grad_components.sum() / p_x

    samples = np.zeros(n_steps)
    samples[0] = np.random.randn()
    noise_std = np.sqrt(2 * eta)

    for i in range(1, n_steps):
        grad = grad_log_p(samples[i-1])
        samples[i] = samples[i-1] + eta * grad + noise_std * np.random.randn()

    return samples

# Run SGLD

4. Entropy Production Measurement

Measure the entropy production along individual Langevin trajectories in a non-equilibrium driven system. This demonstrates stochastic thermodynamics in action: entropy production fluctuates along individual trajectories but is positive on average.

def measure_entropy_production(n_trajectories=2000, n_steps=500,
                                dt=0.01, kT=1.0, gamma=1.0, f_ext=2.0):
    """
    Measure entropy production for a particle in a harmonic potential
    with a constant external driving force.

    U(x) = 0.5 * k * x^2, with external force f_ext.
    The system reaches a non-equilibrium steady state.

    Entropy production per trajectory:
    sigma = integral of (f_ext * dx) / kT  (dissipated work / temperature)
    """
    k = 1.0
    noise_std = np.sqrt(2 * kT * dt / gamma)
    entropy_productions = np.zeros(n_trajectories)

    for traj in range(n_trajectories):
        x = 0.0
        sigma = 0.0

        for step in range(n_steps):
            force = -k * x + f_ext
            dx = (force / gamma) * dt + noise_std * np.random.randn()
            # Entropy production: dissipated heat / temperature
            # Q_dissipated = (total force) * dx - change in kinetic energy
            # For overdamped: sigma += f_ext * dx / kT
            sigma += f_ext * dx / kT
            x += dx

        entropy_productions[traj] = sigma

    return entropy_productions

1. Entropy Production in Heat Conduction ↓

A metal rod connects a hot reservoir at $T_H = 600\text{ K}$ to a cold reservoir at $T_C = 300\text{ K}$. In steady state, heat flows at a rate $\dot{Q} = 50\text{ W}$ through the rod.

  1. Calculate the rate of entropy production $\dot{S}_i$ in the rod.
  2. If the temperature difference is halved (to $T_H = 450\text{ K}$, $T_C = 300\text{ K}$) while $\dot{Q}$ remains the same, how does $\dot{S}_i$ change?
  3. In the limit $T_H \to T_C$, what happens to $\dot{S}_i$? Relate this to the concept of a reversible process.

a) The entropy production rate is the difference between the entropy gained by the cold reservoir and the entropy lost by the hot reservoir:

$$\dot{S}_i = \frac{\dot{Q}}{T_C} - \frac{\dot{Q}}{T_H} = \dot{Q}\left(\frac{1}{T_C} - \frac{1}{T_H}\right)$$ $$\dot{S}_i = 50 \left(\frac{1}{300} - \frac{1}{600}\right) = 50 \times \frac{1}{600} = \frac{1}{12} \approx 0.0833 \text{ W/K}$$

b) With the new temperatures:

$$\dot{S}_i = 50 \left(\frac{1}{300} - \frac{1}{450}\right) = 50 \times \left(\frac{3 - 2}{900}\right) = \frac{50}{900} \approx 0.0556 \text{ W/K}$$

The entropy production rate decreased by about 33%. A smaller temperature gradient produces less entropy per unit of heat transferred.

c) As $T_H \to T_C = T$:

$$\dot{S}_i = \dot{Q}\left(\frac{1}{T} - \frac{1}{T}\right) = 0$$

In the limit of zero temperature difference, the entropy production vanishes. This is the hallmark of a reversible process: heat transfer between bodies at the same temperature produces no entropy. Real processes always have a finite $\Delta T$, so $\dot{S}_i > 0$ always in practice.

2. Onsager Coefficient Symmetry ↓

A system has two coupled transport processes with fluxes $J_1, J_2$ and forces $X_1, X_2$. The Onsager matrix is:

$$L = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$
  1. Verify that this matrix satisfies Onsager's reciprocal relations.
  2. Show that the entropy production $\sigma = \sum_i J_i X_i$ is non-negative for all $X_1, X_2$.
  3. Find the forces $(X_1, X_2)$ that produce the minimum entropy production rate $\sigma$ subject to the constraint $J_1 = 1$.

a) Onsager's reciprocal relations require $L_{12} = L_{21}$. Here $L_{12} = 1 = L_{21}$. Verified.

b) The entropy production is:

$$\sigma = \mathbf{X}^T L \mathbf{X} = 4X_1^2 + 2X_1 X_2 + 3X_2^2$$

We need to show this quadratic form is positive semi-definite. Check the eigenvalues of $L$:

$$\det(L - \lambda I) = (4-\lambda)(3-\lambda) - 1 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 11 = 0$$ $$\lambda = \frac{7 \pm \sqrt{49 - 44}}{2} = \frac{7 \pm \sqrt{5}}{2}$$

So $\lambda_1 = \frac{7 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 4.618$ and $\lambda_2 = \frac{7 - \sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 2.382$. Both eigenvalues are positive, so $L$ is positive definite and $\sigma \geq 0$ for all $(X_1, X_2)$, with equality only at $(0, 0)$.

c) We minimize $\sigma = 4X_1^2 + 2X_1 X_2 + 3X_2^2$ subject to $J_1 = 4X_1 + X_2 = 1$.

Using Lagrange multipliers with constraint $g = 4X_1 + X_2 - 1 = 0$:

$$\frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial X_1} = 8X_1 + 2X_2 = 4\mu, \quad \frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial X_2} = 2X_1 + 6X_2 = \mu$$

From the second equation: $\mu = 2X_1 + 6X_2$. Substituting into the first:

$$8X_1 + 2X_2 = 4(2X_1 + 6X_2) = 8X_1 + 24X_2$$ $$2X_2 = 24X_2 \implies 22X_2 = 0 \implies X_2 = 0$$

From the constraint: $4X_1 = 1 \implies X_1 = 1/4$. The minimum entropy production steady state has $X_2 = 0$ and $X_1 = 1/4$, giving $\sigma_{\min} = 4 \times (1/4)^2 = 1/4$.

This illustrates Prigogine's principle: the unconstrained force ($X_2$) adjusts to zero, minimizing entropy production.

3. Fluctuation Theorem Probability Ratio ↓

A nanoscale system undergoes a non-equilibrium process. The entropy production $\sigma$ (in units of $k_B$) follows a Gaussian distribution with mean $\langle \sigma \rangle = 5$ and standard deviation $\text{std}(\sigma) = 2$.

  1. What fraction of trajectories have $\sigma < 0$ (i.e., "violate" the second law)?
  2. Using the Crooks fluctuation theorem, compute $P(\sigma = +3) / P(\sigma = -3)$.
  3. How large must $\langle \sigma \rangle$ be (keeping $\text{std} = 2$) for fewer than 1 in $10^6$ trajectories to have $\sigma < 0$?

a) With $\sigma \sim \mathcal{N}(5, 4)$, the fraction with $\sigma < 0$ is:

$$P(\sigma < 0) = \Phi\left(\frac{0 - 5}{2}\right) = \Phi(-2.5) \approx 0.0062$$

About 0.62% of trajectories "violate" the second law — roughly 1 in 160.

b) The Crooks fluctuation theorem gives:

$$\frac{P(\sigma = +3)}{P(\sigma = -3)} = e^{3} \approx 20.09$$

Trajectories producing $+3 \, k_B$ of entropy are about 20 times more likely than those producing $-3 \, k_B$.

We can verify this directly from the Gaussian: $P(\sigma=3) \propto e^{-(3-5)^2/8} = e^{-1/2}$ and $P(\sigma=-3) \propto e^{-(-3-5)^2/8} = e^{-8}$. The ratio is $e^{-1/2}/e^{-8} = e^{7.5}$. Note: the simple Crooks ratio $e^\sigma$ applies to the ratio $P_F(+\sigma)/P_R(-\sigma)$; for a single-distribution ratio, the Gaussian calculation gives $e^{7.5} \approx 1808$.

c) We need $P(\sigma < 0) = \Phi(-\langle\sigma\rangle / 2) < 10^{-6}$. From standard normal tables, $\Phi(-4.75) \approx 10^{-6}$, so:

$$\frac{\langle\sigma\rangle}{2} \geq 4.75 \implies \langle\sigma\rangle \geq 9.5$$

The mean entropy production must be at least about $9.5 \, k_B$ for fewer than 1 in a million trajectories to show negative entropy production.

4. Jarzynski Equality for Stretching a Polymer ↓

A single polymer molecule is modeled as a spring with Hamiltonian $H(x, \lambda) = \frac{1}{2}k(x - \lambda)^2$, where $\lambda$ is the trap position controlled by the experimenter. The trap is moved from $\lambda = 0$ to $\lambda = d$ over time $\tau$.

  1. For a quasi-static (infinitely slow) process at temperature $T$, calculate the work done $W_{\text{rev}}$ and the free energy change $\Delta F$.
  2. Use the Jarzynski equality to show that $\Delta F = 0$ for this process (the shape of the potential doesn't change, only its position).
  3. If the process is performed rapidly (non-equilibrium), explain why $\langle W \rangle > \Delta F = 0$ and interpret the excess work physically.

a) The free energy of a harmonic oscillator at temperature $T$ is:

$$F(\lambda) = -k_B T \ln Z(\lambda) = -k_B T \ln \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\beta k(x-\lambda)^2/2} dx$$

The integral is a Gaussian: $\int e^{-\beta k(x-\lambda)^2/2} dx = \sqrt{2\pi / (\beta k)}$, which is independent of $\lambda$. Therefore:

$$F(\lambda) = -k_B T \ln \sqrt{\frac{2\pi k_B T}{k}} = \text{const (independent of } \lambda\text{)}$$ $$\Delta F = F(d) - F(0) = 0$$

For a quasi-static process, $W_{\text{rev}} = \Delta F = 0$. The average position of the particle follows the trap center, so no net work is done on average.

b) The Jarzynski equality states $\langle e^{-\beta W} \rangle = e^{-\beta \Delta F}$. Since $\Delta F = 0$:

$$\langle e^{-\beta W} \rangle = e^{0} = 1$$

This holds regardless of how fast the trap is moved. The exponential average of the work is always 1, even though individual work values can be positive or negative.

c) For a rapid process, the particle lags behind the trap center. The spring is stretched, and extra work is done against the friction of the surrounding medium. By Jensen's inequality applied to the Jarzynski equality:

$$\langle W \rangle \geq -k_B T \ln \langle e^{-\beta W} \rangle = \Delta F = 0$$

The excess work $W_{\text{diss}} = \langle W \rangle - \Delta F = \langle W \rangle > 0$ is the dissipated work. It is converted into heat that flows into the thermal bath, increasing the entropy of the universe by $\Delta S = W_{\text{diss}} / T$. The faster you pull, the more the particle lags behind, and the more work is dissipated as heat.

5. Langevin Equation and Diffusion Coefficient ↓

Consider the overdamped Langevin equation for a free particle (no potential, $U = 0$):

$$\gamma \dot{x} = \sqrt{2\gamma k_B T} \, \xi(t)$$
  1. Show that $\langle x(t) \rangle = x_0$ (no drift).
  2. Compute $\langle (x(t) - x_0)^2 \rangle$ and identify the diffusion coefficient $D$.
  3. Derive the Einstein relation $D = k_B T / \gamma$ from your result.
  4. A colloidal particle with radius $r = 1\,\mu\text{m}$ in water ($\eta = 10^{-3}\text{ Pa}\cdot\text{s}$) at $T = 300\text{ K}$. Using Stokes' law $\gamma = 6\pi\eta r$, calculate $D$ and the RMS displacement after 1 second.

a) Integrating the Langevin equation:

$$x(t) = x_0 + \frac{\sqrt{2\gamma k_B T}}{\gamma} \int_0^t \xi(t') \, dt'$$

Taking the expectation: $\langle x(t) \rangle = x_0 + \frac{\sqrt{2\gamma k_B T}}{\gamma} \int_0^t \langle \xi(t') \rangle \, dt' = x_0$, since $\langle \xi(t') \rangle = 0$.

b) The mean square displacement is:

$$\langle (x(t) - x_0)^2 \rangle = \frac{2\gamma k_B T}{\gamma^2} \int_0^t \int_0^t \langle \xi(t')\xi(t'') \rangle \, dt' \, dt''$$ $$= \frac{2 k_B T}{\gamma} \int_0^t \int_0^t \delta(t' - t'') \, dt' \, dt'' = \frac{2 k_B T}{\gamma} \cdot t$$

This is the diffusion equation result $\langle \Delta x^2 \rangle = 2Dt$.

c) Comparing with $\langle \Delta x^2 \rangle = 2Dt$, we identify:

$$\boxed{D = \frac{k_B T}{\gamma}}$$

This is the Einstein relation, connecting the diffusion coefficient (a measure of fluctuations) with the friction coefficient (a measure of dissipation) and temperature.

d) Using Stokes' law:

$$\gamma = 6\pi \eta r = 6\pi \times 10^{-3} \times 10^{-6} \approx 1.885 \times 10^{-8} \text{ kg/s}$$ $$D = \frac{k_B T}{\gamma} = \frac{1.381 \times 10^{-23} \times 300}{1.885 \times 10^{-8}} \approx 2.20 \times 10^{-13} \text{ m}^2/\text{s}$$ $$x_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{2Dt} = \sqrt{2 \times 2.20 \times 10^{-13} \times 1} \approx 6.6 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m} = 0.66\,\mu\text{m}$$

After 1 second, the colloidal particle diffuses about $0.66\,\mu\text{m}$ — comparable to its own radius. This is directly observable under a microscope, as Robert Brown first noted in 1827.

6. Crooks Relation from Microscopic Reversibility ↓

Consider a system driven by a protocol $\lambda(t)$ for $t \in [0, \tau]$. The forward process starts in equilibrium at $\lambda(0)$, and the reverse process starts in equilibrium at $\lambda(\tau)$ with the time-reversed protocol $\tilde{\lambda}(t) = \lambda(\tau - t)$.

  1. Write the probability of a forward trajectory $\Gamma = \{x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_N\}$ as a product of the initial distribution and transition probabilities.
  2. Write the probability of the time-reversed trajectory $\tilde{\Gamma}$ under the reverse protocol.
  3. Take the ratio $P_F[\Gamma] / P_R[\tilde{\Gamma}]$ and show it equals $e^{\beta(W - \Delta F)}$, where $W$ is the work done during the forward process.
  4. By summing over all trajectories with a given work value, derive the Crooks fluctuation theorem.

a) The forward trajectory probability is:

$$P_F[\Gamma] = \rho_A(x_0) \prod_{n=0}^{N-1} T_n(x_{n+1} | x_n)$$

where $\rho_A(x_0) = e^{-\beta H(x_0, \lambda_0)} / Z_A$ is the initial Boltzmann distribution and $T_n(x_{n+1}|x_n)$ is the transition probability at step $n$.

b) The reverse trajectory probability is:

$$P_R[\tilde{\Gamma}] = \rho_B(x_N) \prod_{n=0}^{N-1} \tilde{T}_{N-1-n}(x_n | x_{n+1})$$

where $\rho_B(x_N) = e^{-\beta H(x_N, \lambda_N)} / Z_B$ and $\tilde{T}$ are the transition probabilities under the reversed protocol.

c) Taking the ratio and using microscopic reversibility ($T_n(x_{n+1}|x_n) = \tilde{T}_n(x_n|x_{n+1})$ for each step):

$$\frac{P_F[\Gamma]}{P_R[\tilde{\Gamma}]} = \frac{\rho_A(x_0)}{\rho_B(x_N)} = \frac{Z_B}{Z_A} e^{-\beta[H(x_0, \lambda_0) - H(x_N, \lambda_N)]}$$

Since $Z_B/Z_A = e^{-\beta \Delta F}$ and the work is $W = \sum_n [H(x_n, \lambda_{n+1}) - H(x_n, \lambda_n)]$, which for Hamiltonian dynamics equals $H(x_N, \lambda_N) - H(x_0, \lambda_0) + Q$ (where $Q$ is heat), we can show:

$$\frac{P_F[\Gamma]}{P_R[\tilde{\Gamma}]} = e^{\beta(W - \Delta F)}$$

d) Define $P_F(W) = \sum_{\Gamma: W[\Gamma]=W} P_F[\Gamma]$. Then:

$$P_F(W) = \sum_{\Gamma: W[\Gamma]=W} P_R[\tilde{\Gamma}] \cdot e^{\beta(W - \Delta F)}$$ $$= e^{\beta(W - \Delta F)} \sum_{\Gamma: W[\Gamma]=W} P_R[\tilde{\Gamma}]$$

The reverse trajectory of a forward trajectory with work $W$ has work $-W$, so:

$$\boxed{\frac{P_F(W)}{P_R(-W)} = e^{\beta(W - \Delta F)}}$$

This is the Crooks fluctuation theorem. Setting $\sigma = \beta(W - \Delta F)$ gives the entropy production form $P_F(\sigma)/P_R(-\sigma) = e^\sigma$.

7. Minimum Entropy Production Principle ↓

Consider a system with three coupled fluxes and forces in the linear regime. The Onsager matrix is diagonal: $L_{ij} = L_i \delta_{ij}$ with $L_1 = 2$, $L_2 = 3$, $L_3 = 1$.

  1. Write the entropy production rate $\sigma$ as a function of the forces $X_1, X_2, X_3$.
  2. Force $X_1$ is fixed externally at $X_1 = 1$. Using Prigogine's minimum entropy production principle, find the steady-state values of $X_2$ and $X_3$.
  3. Calculate the entropy production rate at the steady state and compare it to the case where all forces are equal to 1.
  4. Prove in general that for a diagonal Onsager matrix with one fixed force, the minimum entropy production steady state has all unconstrained forces equal to zero.

a) With a diagonal Onsager matrix:

$$\sigma = \sum_i J_i X_i = \sum_i L_i X_i^2 = 2X_1^2 + 3X_2^2 + X_3^2$$

b) With $X_1 = 1$ fixed, minimize $\sigma = 2(1)^2 + 3X_2^2 + X_3^2 = 2 + 3X_2^2 + X_3^2$ over $X_2, X_3$:

$$\frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial X_2} = 6X_2 = 0 \implies X_2 = 0$$ $$\frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial X_3} = 2X_3 = 0 \implies X_3 = 0$$

The steady state has $X_2 = X_3 = 0$: the unconstrained forces vanish.

c) At steady state: $\sigma_{\min} = 2(1)^2 + 3(0)^2 + (0)^2 = 2$.

With all forces equal to 1: $\sigma = 2 + 3 + 1 = 6$. The minimum entropy production state produces one-third the entropy.

d) For a diagonal matrix, $\sigma = \sum_i L_i X_i^2$. Fix $X_1 = c$. For $i \geq 2$:

$$\frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial X_i} = 2 L_i X_i = 0$$

Since $L_i > 0$ (positive definite), $X_i = 0$ for all $i \geq 2$. The second derivative $\partial^2 \sigma / \partial X_i^2 = 2L_i > 0$ confirms this is a minimum. The unconstrained forces always vanish at the minimum entropy production steady state.

8. SGLD as a Thermodynamic Process ↓

Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics updates parameters as $\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla \tilde{U}(\theta_t) + \sqrt{2\eta\tau} \, \xi_t$, where $\tilde{U}$ is a noisy estimate of the potential and $\tau$ is a "temperature" parameter.

  1. Show that in the continuous-time limit ($\eta \to 0$), this becomes an overdamped Langevin equation. Identify the friction coefficient and temperature.
  2. The noisy gradient introduces an additional noise term: $\nabla \tilde{U} = \nabla U + \epsilon$ where $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma)$. Show that this is equivalent to Langevin dynamics at an effective temperature $T_{\text{eff}} = \tau + \eta \text{tr}(\Sigma) / (2d)$ (where $d$ is the dimension), at leading order in $\eta$.
  3. Discuss the thermodynamic implications: does SGLD sample from the correct Boltzmann distribution? Under what conditions?

a) Rewrite the SGLD update as:

$$\frac{\theta_{t+1} - \theta_t}{\eta} = -\nabla U(\theta_t) + \sqrt{\frac{2\tau}{\eta}} \xi_t$$

In the limit $\eta \to 0$ (with $\eta = dt$), this becomes:

$$\dot{\theta} = -\nabla U(\theta) + \sqrt{2\tau} \, \xi(t)$$

Comparing with the standard overdamped Langevin equation $\gamma \dot{\theta} = -\nabla U + \sqrt{2\gamma k_B T} \xi$, we identify $\gamma = 1$ and $k_B T = \tau$. The learning rate plays the role of a time step, and $\tau$ is the temperature.

b) With gradient noise, the update is:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta(\nabla U + \epsilon_t) + \sqrt{2\eta\tau} \xi_t$$ $$= \theta_t - \eta \nabla U + \underbrace{(-\eta \epsilon_t + \sqrt{2\eta\tau} \xi_t)}_{\text{total noise}}$$

The total noise has variance (per component, averaging over both sources):

$$\text{Var}[\text{noise}] = \eta^2 \frac{\text{tr}(\Sigma)}{d} + 2\eta\tau$$

For this to match Langevin dynamics at effective temperature $T_{\text{eff}}$, we need $\text{Var} = 2\eta T_{\text{eff}}$:

$$2\eta T_{\text{eff}} = 2\eta\tau + \eta^2 \frac{\text{tr}(\Sigma)}{d}$$ $$\boxed{T_{\text{eff}} = \tau + \frac{\eta \, \text{tr}(\Sigma)}{2d}}$$

c) SGLD samples from the Boltzmann distribution at temperature $\tau$ only in the limit $\eta \to 0$, where the gradient noise contribution vanishes. At finite learning rate, the effective temperature is higher than $\tau$ due to gradient noise, and the stationary distribution is biased. This is a non-equilibrium effect: the system is driven out of equilibrium by the stochastic gradient, producing entropy. To sample from the correct distribution, one must either (i) anneal $\eta \to 0$, (ii) use variance reduction to decrease $\Sigma$, or (iii) use Metropolis-Hastings corrections to restore detailed balance.

9. Diffusion Models and Stochastic Thermodynamics ↓

A score-based diffusion model uses the forward SDE $dx = -\frac{1}{2}\beta(t) x \, dt + \sqrt{\beta(t)} \, dW_t$ to transform data distribution $p_0$ into a Gaussian $p_T \approx \mathcal{N}(0, I)$.

  1. Show that if $p_0 = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma)$, the forward process preserves Gaussianity. Find $\mu(t)$ and $\Sigma(t)$.
  2. Compute the KL divergence $D_{\text{KL}}(p_t \| p_{\text{eq}})$ where $p_{\text{eq}} = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$, and show it decreases monotonically (an H-theorem for the forward process).
  3. The reverse SDE is $dx = \left[-\frac{1}{2}\beta(t) x - \beta(t) \nabla_x \log p_t(x)\right] dt + \sqrt{\beta(t)} \, d\bar{W}_t$. Interpret the term $-\beta(t) \nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ as a "thermodynamic force" that reduces entropy. What is the "thermodynamic cost" (in terms of entropy production) of running the reverse process?

a) The SDE $dx = -\frac{1}{2}\beta(t)x \, dt + \sqrt{\beta(t)} \, dW_t$ is an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process. For a Gaussian initial condition, the distribution remains Gaussian at all times. The mean and covariance satisfy:

$$\frac{d\mu}{dt} = -\frac{1}{2}\beta(t)\mu \implies \mu(t) = \mu_0 \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}\int_0^t \beta(s) ds\right)$$ $$\frac{d\Sigma}{dt} = -\beta(t)\Sigma + \beta(t)I \implies \Sigma(t) = I + (\Sigma_0 - I)\exp\left(-\int_0^t \beta(s) ds\right)$$

As $t \to \infty$: $\mu(t) \to 0$ and $\Sigma(t) \to I$, confirming convergence to $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$.

b) For two Gaussians, the KL divergence is:

$$D_{\text{KL}} = \frac{1}{2}\left[\text{tr}(\Sigma(t)) - d - \ln\det\Sigma(t) + \|\mu(t)\|^2\right]$$

Taking the time derivative and using the evolution equations, one can show $\frac{d}{dt}D_{\text{KL}} \leq 0$, with equality only when $\mu = 0$ and $\Sigma = I$. This is because the forward process is a contraction toward $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$ — it reduces the relative entropy monotonically, analogous to Boltzmann's H-theorem.

c) The score term $-\beta(t)\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ acts as an "anti-dissipative" force that pushes the distribution away from the equilibrium Gaussian and toward the structured data distribution. In equilibrium (when $p_t = \mathcal{N}(0,I)$), the score is $\nabla_x \log p_t = -x$, and the force cancels the drift, maintaining equilibrium.

The thermodynamic cost of running the reverse process is the KL divergence between the forward and reverse path measures:

$$\Delta S_{\text{prod}} = D_{\text{KL}}(P_{\text{reverse}} \| P_{\text{forward}}) = D_{\text{KL}}(p_0 \| p_T) + \int_0^T \beta(t) \mathbb{E}_{p_t}\left[\frac{1}{2}\|\nabla \log p_t - s_\theta\|^2\right] dt$$

where $s_\theta$ is the learned score. The first term is the "thermodynamic" cost (unavoidable), and the second is the "learning" cost (due to imperfect score estimation). A perfect score function ($s_\theta = \nabla \log p_t$) achieves the minimum entropy production.

10. Optimal Protocol for Minimum Dissipation ↓

A system is driven from equilibrium state $A$ to state $B$ by a protocol $\lambda(t)$ for $t \in [0, \tau]$ with $\lambda(0) = \lambda_A$ and $\lambda(\tau) = \lambda_B$. The dissipated work (excess over free energy change) is:

$$W_{\text{diss}} = \langle W \rangle - \Delta F = \int_0^\tau \frac{1}{2} \zeta(\lambda) \dot{\lambda}^2 \, dt$$

where $\zeta(\lambda)$ is a friction coefficient that depends on the protocol parameter.

  1. Using the calculus of variations, find the optimal protocol $\lambda^*(t)$ that minimizes $W_{\text{diss}}$ for constant $\zeta$.
  2. Show that the minimum dissipated work scales as $W_{\text{diss}}^* \propto 1/\tau$ — slower protocols dissipate less.
  3. For a non-constant $\zeta(\lambda)$, show that the optimal protocol satisfies $\zeta(\lambda) \dot{\lambda} = \text{const}$ (the system moves slowly where friction is high and fast where friction is low).
  4. Interpret this result in the context of diffusion models: the noise schedule $\beta(t)$ should vary to minimize the "thermodynamic cost" of generation.

a) For constant $\zeta$, minimize $W_{\text{diss}} = \frac{\zeta}{2}\int_0^\tau \dot{\lambda}^2 dt$ subject to $\lambda(0) = \lambda_A$, $\lambda(\tau) = \lambda_B$.

The Euler-Lagrange equation for $\mathcal{L} = \frac{\zeta}{2}\dot{\lambda}^2$ is:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{\lambda}} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \lambda} \implies \zeta \ddot{\lambda} = 0 \implies \ddot{\lambda} = 0$$

The solution is a linear protocol:

$$\boxed{\lambda^*(t) = \lambda_A + (\lambda_B - \lambda_A)\frac{t}{\tau}}$$

b) For the linear protocol, $\dot{\lambda} = (\lambda_B - \lambda_A)/\tau$, so:

$$W_{\text{diss}}^* = \frac{\zeta}{2} \int_0^\tau \frac{(\lambda_B - \lambda_A)^2}{\tau^2} dt = \frac{\zeta (\lambda_B - \lambda_A)^2}{2\tau}$$ $$\boxed{W_{\text{diss}}^* = \frac{\zeta (\Delta\lambda)^2}{2\tau} \propto \frac{1}{\tau}}$$

Slower protocols (larger $\tau$) dissipate less work, approaching the reversible limit $W_{\text{diss}} \to 0$ as $\tau \to \infty$.

c) For $\zeta(\lambda)$, minimize $W_{\text{diss}} = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^\tau \zeta(\lambda)\dot{\lambda}^2 dt$. The Euler-Lagrange equation is:

$$\frac{d}{dt}(\zeta \dot{\lambda}) = \frac{1}{2}\zeta'(\lambda)\dot{\lambda}^2$$

Expanding: $\zeta' \dot{\lambda}^2 + \zeta \ddot{\lambda} = \frac{1}{2}\zeta' \dot{\lambda}^2$, so $\zeta \ddot{\lambda} = -\frac{1}{2}\zeta' \dot{\lambda}^2$.

Alternatively, change variables to $u = \int_{\lambda_A}^{\lambda} \sqrt{\zeta(\lambda')} d\lambda'$ (the "thermodynamic length"). In this variable, the optimal protocol is uniform: $\dot{u} = \text{const}$, which means:

$$\sqrt{\zeta(\lambda)} \dot{\lambda} = \text{const} \implies \zeta(\lambda) \dot{\lambda}^2 = \text{const}$$

The system moves slowly ($\dot{\lambda}$ small) where friction $\zeta$ is large, and fast where $\zeta$ is small. This minimizes total dissipation by spending more time in "difficult" regions.

d) In diffusion models, $\lambda$ corresponds to the noise level and $\zeta(\lambda)$ to the Fisher information of $p_\lambda$ (which measures how "difficult" the score estimation is at noise level $\lambda$). The optimal noise schedule $\beta(t)$ should be chosen so that the model spends more steps at noise levels where the score is hardest to estimate (typically at intermediate noise levels, where the distribution transitions from structured data to Gaussian noise). This is the thermodynamic justification for non-uniform noise schedules in practice, and explains why cosine and other carefully designed schedules outperform linear ones.
