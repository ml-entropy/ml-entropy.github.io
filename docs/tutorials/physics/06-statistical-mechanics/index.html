From Macro to Micro

Thermodynamics tells us what happens: heat flows from hot to cold, entropy increases, engines have maximum efficiencies. But it says nothing about why. It treats matter as a continuous substance and entropy as an abstract state function. Statistical mechanics provides the why — it derives all of thermodynamics from the microscopic behavior of atoms and molecules.

The central idea is breathtakingly simple: macroscopic properties are averages over microscopic states. Temperature is the average kinetic energy of molecules. Pressure is the average force per unit area from molecular collisions. Entropy counts the number of microscopic arrangements compatible with what we observe. Every "law" of thermodynamics becomes a theorem of probability theory applied to $\sim 10^{23}$ particles.

Why Statistical Mechanics?

A mole of gas contains $N_A \approx 6 \times 10^{23}$ molecules. To track each one, we would need to solve $\sim 10^{23}$ coupled differential equations — a task that will never be computationally feasible. Statistical mechanics sidesteps this by asking: given our ignorance of microscopic details, what can we predict about macroscopic observables? The answer turns out to be: almost everything.

This is not a limitation but a feature. The laws of thermodynamics are more reliable than the laws governing individual particles, precisely because they emerge from the statistics of enormous numbers.

Phase Space

The state of a classical system of $N$ particles in three dimensions is fully specified by $3N$ positions $\mathbf{q} = (q_1, \ldots, q_{3N})$ and $3N$ momenta $\mathbf{p} = (p_1, \ldots, p_{3N})$. Together, these define a point in a $6N$-dimensional phase space $\Gamma$. As the system evolves in time, this point traces a trajectory through $\Gamma$ according to Hamilton's equations:

$$\dot{q}_i = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}, \qquad \dot{p}_i = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i}$$

where $H(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})$ is the Hamiltonian (total energy) of the system.

Liouville's Theorem

A fundamental result in classical mechanics constrains how probability distributions evolve in phase space. Let $\rho(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}, t)$ be the probability density of finding the system at a particular point in phase space at time $t$. Liouville's theorem states that this density is conserved along trajectories:

$$\frac{d\rho}{dt} = \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \sum_{i=1}^{3N}\left(\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial q_i}\dot{q}_i + \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial p_i}\dot{p}_i\right) = 0$$

In other words, phase space volumes are incompressible under Hamiltonian evolution — a cloud of initial conditions neither expands nor contracts, it merely deforms. This provides the rigorous foundation for the equal a priori probability postulate: if all microstates in an energy shell are initially equally likely, they remain so forever.

Microcanonical Ensemble

The simplest statistical ensemble describes an isolated system with fixed energy $E$, volume $V$, and particle number $N$. Since the system cannot exchange energy or particles with its surroundings, the total energy is exactly conserved.

The Fundamental Postulate

For an isolated system in equilibrium, all microstates compatible with the macroscopic constraints (same $E$, $V$, $N$) are equally probable. This is the equal a priori probability postulate — the single assumption from which all of statistical mechanics follows.

The number of accessible microstates is denoted $\Omega(E, V, N)$. For a classical system, this is the phase space volume of the energy shell:

$$\Omega(E) = \frac{1}{h^{3N} N!}\int_{E \leq H(\mathbf{q},\mathbf{p}) \leq E+\delta E} d^{3N}q \, d^{3N}p$$

where $h$ is Planck's constant (providing the correct quantum mechanical counting) and $N!$ accounts for the indistinguishability of identical particles (resolving the Gibbs paradox).

The entropy is then given by Boltzmann's formula:

$$S(E, V, N) = k_B \ln \Omega(E, V, N)$$

Temperature emerges as a derived quantity. It is defined through the relationship:

Temperature from Entropy
$$\frac{1}{T} = \frac{\partial S}{\partial E}\bigg|_{V, N}$$

Temperature is not fundamental — it is the rate at which entropy changes with energy. A system with many accessible states per unit energy (large $\partial S / \partial E$) has low temperature; one with few new states per unit energy has high temperature. Two systems in thermal contact reach equilibrium when their temperatures — and hence their $\partial S / \partial E$ — are equal.

Similarly, pressure and chemical potential emerge from entropy:

$$\frac{P}{T} = \frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\bigg|_{E,N}, \qquad -\frac{\mu}{T} = \frac{\partial S}{\partial N}\bigg|_{E,V}$$

Canonical Ensemble

Most systems are not isolated — they exchange energy with their surroundings. A system in thermal contact with a large heat reservoir at temperature $T$ is described by the canonical ensemble. Here, the energy fluctuates, but the temperature is fixed.

To derive the canonical distribution, consider a small system $A$ in contact with a much larger reservoir $R$. The total system $A + R$ is isolated with total energy $E_{\text{tot}}$. If system $A$ is in microstate $i$ with energy $E_i$, then the reservoir has energy $E_{\text{tot}} - E_i$ and multiplicity $\Omega_R(E_{\text{tot}} - E_i)$. Since $E_i \ll E_{\text{tot}}$, we can Taylor expand the logarithm:

$$\ln \Omega_R(E_{\text{tot}} - E_i) \approx \ln \Omega_R(E_{\text{tot}}) - \frac{\partial \ln \Omega_R}{\partial E} E_i = \text{const} - \beta E_i$$

where $\beta = 1/(k_B T)$. Since the probability of microstate $i$ is proportional to $\Omega_R$, we get:

The Partition Function

The central object of the canonical ensemble is the partition function:

$$Z = \sum_{i} e^{-\beta E_i}, \qquad \beta = \frac{1}{k_B T}$$

The probability of microstate $i$ is:

$$p_i = \frac{e^{-\beta E_i}}{Z}$$

The partition function is the Rosetta Stone of statistical mechanics — every thermodynamic quantity can be derived from it.

The key thermodynamic quantities follow from $Z$:

  • Average energy: $\langle E \rangle = -\dfrac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta}$
  • Helmholtz free energy: $F = -k_B T \ln Z$
  • Entropy: $S = -\dfrac{\partial F}{\partial T}\bigg|_V = k_B \ln Z + \dfrac{\langle E \rangle}{T}$
  • Heat capacity: $C_V = \dfrac{\partial \langle E \rangle}{\partial T}\bigg|_V = k_B \beta^2 \dfrac{\partial^2 \ln Z}{\partial \beta^2}$
Example: Two-Level System

A system has two energy levels: $E_0 = 0$ and $E_1 = \varepsilon$. The partition function is:

$$Z = e^{0} + e^{-\beta \varepsilon} = 1 + e^{-\beta \varepsilon}$$

The average energy is:

$$\langle E \rangle = -\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta} = \frac{\varepsilon \, e^{-\beta \varepsilon}}{1 + e^{-\beta \varepsilon}} = \frac{\varepsilon}{e^{\beta \varepsilon} + 1}$$

At low temperature ($\beta \varepsilon \gg 1$), $\langle E \rangle \to 0$ (system frozen in ground state). At high temperature ($\beta \varepsilon \ll 1$), $\langle E \rangle \to \varepsilon / 2$ (both states equally occupied).

The Boltzmann Distribution

The Boltzmann distribution $p_i = e^{-\beta E_i}/Z$ can be derived from a completely different direction: as the maximum entropy distribution subject to a constraint on the average energy. This derivation, due to Jaynes, reveals the deep connection between statistical mechanics and information theory.

We seek to maximize the Gibbs entropy:

$$S = -k_B \sum_i p_i \ln p_i$$

subject to the constraints:

$$\sum_i p_i = 1 \qquad \text{(normalization)}, \qquad \sum_i p_i E_i = \langle E \rangle \qquad \text{(fixed average energy)}$$

Using Lagrange multipliers $\alpha$ and $\lambda$, we form the functional:

$$\mathcal{L} = -k_B \sum_i p_i \ln p_i - \alpha\left(\sum_i p_i - 1\right) - \lambda\left(\sum_i p_i E_i - \langle E \rangle\right)$$

Setting $\partial \mathcal{L} / \partial p_j = 0$:

$$-k_B(\ln p_j + 1) - \alpha - \lambda E_j = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad p_j = \exp\!\left(-1 - \frac{\alpha}{k_B}\right) \exp\!\left(-\frac{\lambda}{k_B} E_j\right)$$

Identifying $\beta = \lambda / k_B$ and using normalization to fix the prefactor:

$$\boxed{p_j = \frac{e^{-\beta E_j}}{Z}, \qquad Z = \sum_i e^{-\beta E_i}}$$
MaxEnt and Statistical Mechanics

This derivation reveals a profound insight: the Boltzmann distribution is not an empirical fact about gases — it is a theorem of probability theory. It is the unique distribution that maximizes our uncertainty (entropy) given only the constraint that the average energy is known. The equilibrium state is simply the state of maximum ignorance consistent with macroscopic observations. As Jaynes put it: "Statistical mechanics is a form of statistical inference, not a physical theory."

Grand Canonical Ensemble

When a system can exchange both energy and particles with a reservoir, we need the grand canonical ensemble. The reservoir is characterized by temperature $T$ and chemical potential $\mu$, which controls the average number of particles.

Grand Partition Function
$$\Xi = \sum_{N=0}^{\infty} \sum_{i} e^{-\beta(E_i(N) - \mu N)} = \sum_{N=0}^{\infty} e^{\beta \mu N} Z_N$$

where $Z_N$ is the canonical partition function for $N$ particles. The probability of finding the system in a state with $N$ particles and energy $E_i$ is:

$$p(N, i) = \frac{e^{-\beta(E_i - \mu N)}}{\Xi}$$

The grand potential $\Phi = -k_B T \ln \Xi$ plays the role analogous to the Helmholtz free energy:

$$\Phi = -PV, \qquad \langle N \rangle = -\frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial \mu}\bigg|_{T,V}, \qquad \langle E \rangle - \mu \langle N \rangle = -\frac{\partial \ln \Xi}{\partial \beta}$$

Quantum Statistics

For systems of identical quantum particles, the grand canonical ensemble naturally gives rise to the two fundamental distribution functions:

Fermi-Dirac and Bose-Einstein Distributions

Fermions (electrons, protons, neutrons) — at most one particle per state:

$$\langle n_i \rangle_{\text{FD}} = \frac{1}{e^{\beta(\varepsilon_i - \mu)} + 1}$$

Bosons (photons, phonons, helium-4) — any number per state:

$$\langle n_i \rangle_{\text{BE}} = \frac{1}{e^{\beta(\varepsilon_i - \mu)} - 1}$$

In the limit $e^{\beta(\varepsilon_i - \mu)} \gg 1$ (high temperature or low density), both reduce to the classical Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution $\langle n_i \rangle \approx e^{-\beta(\varepsilon_i - \mu)}$.

The Ising Model

The Ising model is arguably the most important model in statistical physics. Despite its simplicity — binary spins on a lattice — it exhibits remarkably rich behavior, including phase transitions, spontaneous symmetry breaking, and critical phenomena.

Ising Hamiltonian

Consider $N$ spins $s_i \in \{-1, +1\}$ on a lattice. The energy is:

$$H = -J\sum_{\langle ij \rangle} s_i s_j - h\sum_{i} s_i$$

Understanding the terms:

  • $J$: Coupling constant. $J > 0$ favors alignment (ferromagnet); $J < 0$ favors anti-alignment (antiferromagnet).
  • $\langle ij \rangle$: Sum over nearest-neighbor pairs on the lattice.
  • $h$: External magnetic field. Biases spins to align with the field.
  • $s_i$: Spin variable at site $i$, taking values $\pm 1$.

The partition function is:

$$Z = \sum_{\{s_i\}} e^{-\beta H} = \sum_{s_1=\pm 1} \cdots \sum_{s_N=\pm 1} \exp\!\left(\beta J \sum_{\langle ij\rangle} s_i s_j + \beta h \sum_i s_i\right)$$

This sum has $2^N$ terms — for even a modest lattice of $100 \times 100$ spins, there are $2^{10{,}000}$ configurations, far more than atoms in the observable universe. Exact computation is intractable except in special cases (the 1D model was solved by Ising in 1925; the 2D model without external field was solved by Onsager in 1944).

Mean-Field Theory

A powerful approximation replaces the effect of all neighboring spins by their average. If each spin has $z$ nearest neighbors and the average magnetization is $m = \langle s_i \rangle$, each spin effectively sees a field:

$$h_{\text{eff}} = h + Jz \, m$$

The self-consistent equation for $m$ is:

$$m = \tanh(\beta h_{\text{eff}}) = \tanh\!\left(\beta(h + Jzm)\right)$$

For $h = 0$, this equation has a non-trivial solution $m \neq 0$ (spontaneous magnetization) when $\beta J z > 1$, i.e., below the critical temperature:

$$T_c = \frac{Jz}{k_B}$$
Connection to Boltzmann Machines

The Ising model is mathematically identical to a Boltzmann machine in machine learning. The spins correspond to binary neurons, $J$ becomes the weight matrix, and $h$ the bias vector. The partition function $Z$ plays the same normalizing role. Training a Boltzmann machine amounts to adjusting $J$ and $h$ so that the thermal distribution of the Ising model matches the data distribution — a direct application of statistical mechanics to learning.

Information-Theoretic Statistical Mechanics

In 1957, E.T. Jaynes published two landmark papers arguing that statistical mechanics is a form of statistical inference, not a physical theory about the dynamics of particles. His key insight: the probability distributions used in statistical mechanics are those that maximize entropy subject to the known macroscopic constraints — nothing more, nothing less.

"The probability assignment that is uniquely determined as the one which is maximally noncommittal with regard to missing information, and that agrees with what is known... is the one that maximizes the entropy."
— E.T. Jaynes, Information Theory and Statistical Mechanics, 1957
Jaynes' Maximum Entropy Principle

Given a set of constraints (expectation values of certain observables), the least biased probability distribution is the one that maximizes the Shannon/Gibbs entropy:

$$S = -k_B \sum_i p_i \ln p_i$$

subject to:

  • $\sum_i p_i = 1$ (normalization)
  • $\sum_i p_i f_k(i) = \langle f_k \rangle$ for each known observable $f_k$

The resulting distribution always takes the exponential family form: $p_i \propto \exp(-\sum_k \lambda_k f_k(i))$, where $\lambda_k$ are Lagrange multipliers determined by the constraints.

This framework unifies all the standard ensembles:

  • Microcanonical: No constraints beyond normalization $\Rightarrow$ uniform distribution over accessible states.
  • Canonical: Constrain $\langle E \rangle$ $\Rightarrow$ $p_i \propto e^{-\beta E_i}$ (Boltzmann distribution).
  • Grand canonical: Constrain $\langle E \rangle$ and $\langle N \rangle$ $\Rightarrow$ $p_i \propto e^{-\beta(E_i - \mu N_i)}$.

The Gibbs entropy $S = -k_B \sum p_i \ln p_i$ is not merely analogous to Shannon entropy — it is Shannon entropy (up to the constant $k_B$ and choice of logarithm base). The Boltzmann constant $k_B$ simply converts from the natural unit of information (nats) to the historical unit of thermal entropy (J/K). There is no conceptual difference: both measure the amount of uncertainty about the microstate of a system given macroscopic knowledge.

Why This Matters

Jaynes' insight dissolves many conceptual puzzles of statistical mechanics. The "equal a priori probability" postulate is not a physical law but a statement of maximum ignorance. Irreversibility does not require coarse-graining or ergodic hypotheses — it is simply the statement that our macroscopic observations constrain the distribution less than full microscopic knowledge would. The Second Law becomes a theorem of inference: if you know less, your entropy assignment is higher.

Monte Carlo Methods

Computing the partition function $Z = \sum_i e^{-\beta E_i}$ directly is usually impossible because the sum has astronomically many terms. Monte Carlo methods circumvent this by generating random samples from the Boltzmann distribution, allowing us to estimate thermodynamic averages without summing over all states.

The Metropolis Algorithm

The Metropolis-Hastings algorithm (1953) generates a Markov chain of states $\{s^{(1)}, s^{(2)}, \ldots\}$ whose stationary distribution is the Boltzmann distribution. The procedure for the Ising model is:

  1. Start from an arbitrary spin configuration $s$.
  2. Propose a move: flip a randomly chosen spin $s_k \to -s_k$.
  3. Compute the energy change $\Delta E = E_{\text{new}} - E_{\text{old}}$.
  4. Accept the move with probability $\min(1, e^{-\beta \Delta E})$:
    • If $\Delta E \leq 0$ (energy decreases), always accept.
    • If $\Delta E > 0$ (energy increases), accept with probability $e^{-\beta \Delta E}$.
  5. Repeat steps 2–4 many times.
Detailed Balance

The Metropolis acceptance rule satisfies detailed balance:

$$p_i \, T(i \to j) = p_j \, T(j \to i)$$

where $T(i \to j)$ is the transition probability from state $i$ to state $j$. This ensures that the Boltzmann distribution $p_i \propto e^{-\beta E_i}$ is the stationary distribution of the Markov chain.

Simulated Annealing

The Metropolis algorithm inspires a powerful optimization technique: simulated annealing. Instead of sampling at a fixed temperature, we slowly decrease $T$ (increase $\beta$) over time. At high temperature, the algorithm explores broadly; as the temperature drops, it increasingly favors low-energy states. In the limit of infinitely slow cooling, the algorithm converges to the global minimum of the energy function — the ground state.

This is a direct metaphor from metallurgy: real annealing slowly cools a metal to allow atoms to find their lowest-energy crystal configuration. The mathematical analogy makes precise the intuition that patience (slow cooling) is rewarded with better solutions (lower energy states).

1. Partition Function and Thermodynamic Quantities

Compute the partition function, average energy, free energy, and entropy for a discrete set of energy levels as a function of temperature.

python 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def partition_function(energies, beta):
    """Compute Z = sum_i exp(-beta * E_i)."""
    return np.sum(np.exp(-beta * energies))

def thermodynamic_quantities(energies, T_range):
    """Compute Z, , F, S, C_v as functions of temperature."""
    k_B = 1.0  # Natural units
    results = {'T': T_range, 'Z': [], 'E_avg': [], 'F': [], 'S': [], 'C_v': []}

    for T in T_range:
        beta = 1.0 / (k_B * T)
        boltz = np.exp(-beta * energies)
        Z = np.sum(boltz)
        probs = boltz / Z

        E_avg = np.sum(probs * energies)
        E2_avg = np.sum(probs * energies**2)
        F = -k_B * T * np.log(Z)
        S = (E_avg - F) / T
        C_v = (E2_avg - E_avg**2) / (k_B * T**2)

        results['Z'].append(Z)
        results['E_avg'].append(E_avg)
        results['F'].append(F)
        results['S'].append(S)
        results['C_v'].append(C_v)

    return {k: np.array(v) for k, v in results.items()}

# Two-level system: E = 0 and E = epsilon
epsilon = 1.0
energies = np.array([0.0, epsilon])
T_range = np.linspace(0.1, 5.0, 200)

results = thermodynamic_quantities(energies, T_range)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(12, 10))

axes[0, 0].plot(results['T'], results['E_avg'], 'b-', linewidth=2)
axes[0, 0].axhline(y=epsilon/2, color='r', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5, label=r'$\varepsilon/2$')
axes[0, 0].set_xlabel('Temperature T')
axes[0, 0].set_ylabel(r'$\langle E \rangle$')
axes[0, 0].set_title('Average Energy')
axes[0, 0].legend()

axes[0, 1].plot(results['T'], results['S'], 'g-', linewidth=2)
axes[0, 1].axhline(y=np.log(2), color='r', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5, label=r'$\ln 2$')
axes[0, 1].set_xlabel('Temperature T')
axes[0, 1].set_ylabel('Entropy S')
axes[0, 1].set_title('Entropy')
axes[0, 1].legend()

axes[1, 0].plot(results['T'], results['F'], 'm-', linewidth=2)
axes[1, 0].set_xlabel('Temperature T')
axes[1, 0].set_ylabel('Free Energy F')
axes[1, 0].set_title('Helmholtz Free Energy')

axes[1, 1].plot(results['T'], results['C_v'], 'r-', linewidth=2)
axes[1, 1].set_xlabel('Temperature T')
axes[1, 1].set_ylabel(r'$C_V$')
axes[1, 1].set_title('Heat Capacity (Schottky Anomaly)')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

2. Boltzmann Distribution Sampling

Visualize the Boltzmann distribution for a harmonic oscillator at different temperatures, and compare direct sampling with analytical probabilities.

python 
def boltzmann_distribution(energies, T):
    """Return Boltzmann probabilities for given energy levels."""
    beta = 1.0 / T  # k_B = 1
    boltz = np.exp(-beta * energies)
    return boltz / np.sum(boltz)

def sample_boltzmann(energies, T, n_samples=10000):
    """Sample from the Boltzmann distribution using direct sampling."""
    probs = boltzmann_distribution(energies, T)
    indices = np.random.choice(len(energies), size=n_samples, p=probs)
    return indices

# Quantum harmonic oscillator: E_n = (n + 1/2) * hbar * omega
n_levels = 20
energies = np.arange(n_levels) + 0.5  # Units of hbar*omega

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 5))
temperatures = [0.5, 2.0, 10.0]

for ax, T in zip(axes, temperatures):
    probs = boltzmann_distribution(energies, T)
    samples = sample_boltzmann(energies, T, n_samples=50000)

    ax.bar(np.arange(n_levels), probs, alpha=0.7, label='Analytical', color='steelblue')
    ax.hist(samples, bins=np.arange(n_levels + 1) - 0.5, density=True,
            alpha=0.5, label='Sampled', color='coral', edgecolor='black')
    ax.set_xlabel('Energy Level n')
    ax.set_ylabel('Probability')
    ax.set_title(f'T = {T}')
    ax.legend()
    ax.set_xlim(-0.5, 15)

plt.suptitle('Boltzmann Distribution: Quantum Harmonic Oscillator', fontsize=14)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

3. 2D Ising Model with Metropolis-Hastings

Simulate the 2D Ising model on an $L \times L$ square lattice using the Metropolis algorithm. This code computes magnetization, energy, and visualizes spin configurations at different temperatures.

python 
class IsingModel:
    """2D Ising model with Metropolis-Hastings Monte Carlo."""

    def __init__(self, L, T, J=1.0, h=0.0):
        self.L = L
        self.T = T
        self.J = J
        self.h = h
        self.beta = 1.0 / T
        # Random initial configuration
        self.spins = np.random.choice([-1, 1], size=(L, L))

    def energy(self):
        """Total energy of the configuration."""
        E = 0
        for i in range(self.L):
            for j in range(self.L):
                s = self.spins[i, j]
                # Nearest neighbors with periodic boundary conditions
                neighbors = (
                    self.spins[(i+1) % self.L, j] +
                    self.spins[(i-1) % self.L, j] +
                    self.spins[i, (j+1) % self.L] +
                    self.spins[i, (j-1) % self.L]
                )
                E += -self.J * s * neighbors - self.h * s
        return E / 2  # Correct double-counting of pairs

    def magnetization(self):
        """Average magnetization per spin."""
        return np.mean(self.spins)

    def metropolis_step(self):
        """One full sweep: attempt L^2 spin flips."""
        for _ in range(self.L ** 2):
            i = np.random.randint(self.L)
            j = np.random.randint(self.L)
            s = self.spins[i, j]

            neighbors = (
                self.spins[(i+1) % self.L, j] +
                self.spins[(i-1) % self.L, j] +
                self.spins[i, (j+1) % self.L] +
                self.spins[i, (j-1) % self.L]
            )
            dE = 2 * self.J * s * neighbors + 2 * self.h * s

            if dE <= 0 or np.random.random() < np.exp(-self.beta * dE):
                self.spins[i, j] *= -1

    def simulate(self, n_steps, n_warmup=1000):
        """Run simulation, return energy and magnetization history."""
        # Warmup phase
        for _ in range(n_warmup):
            self.metropolis_step()

        energies = []
        magnetizations = []
        for _ in range(n_steps):
            self.metropolis_step()
            energies.append(self.energy() / self.L**2)
            magnetizations.append(abs(self.magnetization()))

        return np.array(energies), np.array(magnetizations)


# Simulate at three temperatures
L = 32
temperatures_demo = [1.5, 2.269, 3.5]  # Below, at, above T_c
labels = ['T = 1.5 (ordered)', 'T = 2.269 (critical)', 'T = 3.5 (disordered)']

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 5))

for ax, T, label in zip(axes, temperatures_demo, labels):
    model = IsingModel(L, T)
    model.simulate(n_steps=500, n_warmup=2000)
    ax.imshow(model.spins, cmap='RdBu', vmin=-1, vmax=1, interpolation='nearest')
    ax.set_title(label)
    ax.axis('off')

plt.suptitle(f'2D Ising Model ({L}x{L} lattice)', fontsize=14)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

4. Phase Transition Visualization

Sweep over temperatures to observe the ferromagnetic phase transition. The critical temperature for the 2D Ising model on a square lattice is $T_c = 2J / (k_B \ln(1 + \sqrt{2})) \approx 2.269\, J/k_B$.

python 
def phase_transition_sweep(L=20, T_range=None, n_steps=2000, n_warmup=2000):
    """Compute average |m|, E, C_v, chi as functions of T."""
    if T_range is None:
        T_range = np.linspace(1.0, 4.0, 30)

    results = {'T': T_range, 'mag': [], 'energy': [],
               'heat_capacity': [], 'susceptibility': []}

    for T in T_range:
        model = IsingModel(L, T)
        E_hist, M_hist = model.simulate(n_steps, n_warmup)

        E_avg = np.mean(E_hist)
        M_avg = np.mean(M_hist)
        E2_avg = np.mean(E_hist**2)
        M2_avg = np.mean(M_hist**2)

        C_v = (E2_avg - E_avg**2) * L**2 / T**2
        chi = (M2_avg - M_avg**2) * L**2 / T

        results['mag'].append(M_avg)
        results['energy'].append(E_avg)
        results['heat_capacity'].append(C_v)
        results['susceptibility'].append(chi)

    return {k: np.array(v) for k, v in results.items()}

# Run the sweep (this may take a few minutes)
T_range = np.linspace(1.0, 4.0, 40)
results = phase_transition_sweep(L=20, T_range=T_range, n_steps=3000)

T_c = 2.0 / np.log(1 + np.sqrt(2))  # Exact critical temperature

fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(12, 10))

axes[0, 0].plot(results['T'], results['mag'], 'bo-', markersize=3)
axes[0, 0].axvline(x=T_c, color='r', linestyle='--', label=f'$T_c$ = {T_c:.3f}')
axes[0, 0].set_xlabel('Temperature T')
axes[0, 0].set_ylabel(r'$\langle |m| \rangle$')

Exercises

Test your understanding of statistical mechanics, from basic partition functions to deep connections with information theory. Each exercise has a full solution.

Easy

1. Partition Function for a Two-Level System ↓

A system has two energy levels: $E_0 = 0$ and $E_1 = \varepsilon$.

  1. Write down the partition function $Z(\beta)$.
  2. Compute the probability of occupying each level.
  3. What are the probabilities in the limits $T \to 0$ and $T \to \infty$?
  4. Compute the Helmholtz free energy $F = -k_BT \ln Z$.

a) The partition function sums over all states:

$$Z = e^{-\beta \cdot 0} + e^{-\beta \varepsilon} = 1 + e^{-\beta \varepsilon}$$

b) The probabilities are:

$$p_0 = \frac{e^{0}}{Z} = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\beta\varepsilon}}, \qquad p_1 = \frac{e^{-\beta\varepsilon}}{1 + e^{-\beta\varepsilon}}$$

c) Limits:

  • $T \to 0$ ($\beta \to \infty$): $e^{-\beta\varepsilon} \to 0$, so $p_0 \to 1$ and $p_1 \to 0$. The system freezes into the ground state.
  • $T \to \infty$ ($\beta \to 0$): $e^{-\beta\varepsilon} \to 1$, so $p_0 \to 1/2$ and $p_1 \to 1/2$. Both states are equally occupied.

d) The Helmholtz free energy is:

$$F = -k_BT \ln Z = -k_BT \ln(1 + e^{-\beta\varepsilon}) = -k_BT \ln(1 + e^{-\varepsilon/(k_BT)})$$

At low $T$: $F \to 0$ (only the ground state matters). At high $T$: $F \to -k_BT \ln 2$ (entropy $S = k_B \ln 2$ dominates).

2. Average Energy from the Partition Function ↓

For the two-level system from Exercise 1:

  1. Compute $\langle E \rangle$ using the formula $\langle E \rangle = -\partial \ln Z / \partial \beta$.
  2. Verify this by computing $\langle E \rangle = \sum_i p_i E_i$ directly.
  3. Compute the heat capacity $C_V = \partial \langle E \rangle / \partial T$ and sketch it. This is the famous Schottky anomaly.

a) From $Z = 1 + e^{-\beta\varepsilon}$:

$$\ln Z = \ln(1 + e^{-\beta\varepsilon})$$ $$\langle E \rangle = -\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta} = -\frac{-\varepsilon \, e^{-\beta\varepsilon}}{1 + e^{-\beta\varepsilon}} = \frac{\varepsilon}{e^{\beta\varepsilon} + 1}$$

b) Direct calculation:

$$\langle E \rangle = p_0 \cdot 0 + p_1 \cdot \varepsilon = \frac{\varepsilon \, e^{-\beta\varepsilon}}{1 + e^{-\beta\varepsilon}} = \frac{\varepsilon}{e^{\beta\varepsilon} + 1}$$

Both methods agree, confirming the general identity $\langle E \rangle = -\partial_\beta \ln Z$.

c) Using $x = \beta\varepsilon = \varepsilon / (k_BT)$:

$$C_V = \frac{\partial \langle E \rangle}{\partial T} = k_B \left(\frac{\varepsilon}{k_BT}\right)^2 \frac{e^{\varepsilon/(k_BT)}}{(e^{\varepsilon/(k_BT)} + 1)^2} = k_B x^2 \frac{e^x}{(e^x + 1)^2}$$

The heat capacity is zero at both $T = 0$ and $T = \infty$, with a peak near $T \approx 0.42\, \varepsilon/k_B$. This peak — the Schottky anomaly — arises because at intermediate temperatures the system is transitioning from the ground state to equal occupation of both levels, and the rate of energy absorption with temperature is maximized.

3. Entropy of an Ideal Gas (Sackur-Tetrode) ↓

The partition function for a single particle of mass $m$ in a box of volume $V$ at temperature $T$ is:

$$Z_1 = \frac{V}{\lambda^3}, \qquad \lambda = \sqrt{\frac{2\pi\hbar^2}{mk_BT}}$$

where $\lambda$ is the thermal de Broglie wavelength.

  1. For $N$ indistinguishable particles, the total partition function is $Z_N = Z_1^N / N!$. Using Stirling's approximation $\ln N! \approx N\ln N - N$, derive the Helmholtz free energy $F$.
  2. From $S = -\partial F / \partial T$, derive the Sackur-Tetrode equation: $$S = Nk_B\left[\frac{5}{2} + \ln\!\left(\frac{V}{N\lambda^3}\right)\right]$$

a) Starting from $Z_N = Z_1^N / N!$:

$$\ln Z_N = N \ln Z_1 - \ln N! \approx N \ln\!\left(\frac{V}{\lambda^3}\right) - N\ln N + N$$ $$= N\left[\ln\!\left(\frac{V}{N\lambda^3}\right) + 1\right]$$

The Helmholtz free energy is:

$$F = -k_BT \ln Z_N = -Nk_BT\left[\ln\!\left(\frac{V}{N\lambda^3}\right) + 1\right]$$

b) We need $\partial F / \partial T$. Since $\lambda \propto T^{-1/2}$, we have $\lambda^3 \propto T^{-3/2}$, so $\ln(V/(N\lambda^3)) = \ln(V/N) + \frac{3}{2}\ln T + \text{const}$. Therefore:

$$F = -Nk_BT\left[\ln\!\left(\frac{V}{N\lambda^3}\right) + 1\right]$$

Taking $-\partial F / \partial T$ (using the product rule and $\partial \ln \lambda^3 / \partial T = -3/(2T)$):

$$S = -\frac{\partial F}{\partial T} = Nk_B\left[\ln\!\left(\frac{V}{N\lambda^3}\right) + 1\right] + Nk_BT \cdot \frac{3}{2T}$$ $$= Nk_B\left[\ln\!\left(\frac{V}{N\lambda^3}\right) + 1 + \frac{3}{2}\right] = Nk_B\left[\frac{5}{2} + \ln\!\left(\frac{V}{N\lambda^3}\right)\right]$$

This is the Sackur-Tetrode equation. It gives the absolute entropy of an ideal gas and was one of the first results that required the $N!$ correction for identical particles. Note that $S$ is extensive (proportional to $N$) only because of the $N!$ factor — without it, entropy would grow super-linearly with $N$ (the Gibbs paradox).

4. MaxEnt Derivation of the Boltzmann Distribution ↓

A system has discrete energy levels $E_1, E_2, \ldots, E_k$. You know only two things: $\sum_i p_i = 1$ and $\sum_i p_i E_i = U$ (fixed average energy). Using Lagrange multipliers, maximize the entropy $S = -\sum_i p_i \ln p_i$ and show that the result is the Boltzmann distribution.

  1. Set up the Lagrangian with multipliers $\alpha$ (for normalization) and $\beta$ (for energy).
  2. Differentiate with respect to $p_j$ and solve for $p_j$.
  3. Show that $Z = \sum_i e^{-\beta E_i}$ and $p_j = e^{-\beta E_j}/Z$.
  4. Explain why this derivation shows that the canonical ensemble follows from inference, not dynamics.

a) The Lagrangian is:

$$\mathcal{L} = -\sum_i p_i \ln p_i - \alpha\!\left(\sum_i p_i - 1\right) - \beta\!\left(\sum_i p_i E_i - U\right)$$

b) Setting $\partial \mathcal{L} / \partial p_j = 0$:

$$-\ln p_j - 1 - \alpha - \beta E_j = 0$$ $$p_j = e^{-(1+\alpha)} e^{-\beta E_j}$$

c) Applying the normalization constraint:

$$\sum_j p_j = e^{-(1+\alpha)} \sum_j e^{-\beta E_j} = 1$$ $$\Longrightarrow \quad e^{-(1+\alpha)} = \frac{1}{\sum_j e^{-\beta E_j}} = \frac{1}{Z}$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{p_j = \frac{e^{-\beta E_j}}{Z}, \qquad Z = \sum_i e^{-\beta E_i}}$$

The multiplier $\beta$ is determined by the constraint $\langle E \rangle = U$, and comparison with thermodynamics identifies $\beta = 1/(k_BT)$.

d) This derivation uses only probability theory and information theory — no Hamiltonian dynamics, no ergodic hypothesis, no equal a priori probability postulate. The Boltzmann distribution emerges as the unique answer to the question: "Given that I know the average energy and nothing else, what is the least biased assignment of probabilities?" This is Jaynes' point: statistical mechanics is inference. The physical content is in specifying which constraints to impose (energy, particle number, etc.), not in the mathematical machinery of maximizing entropy.

5. Ising Model Mean-Field Approximation ↓

Consider the Ising model with $H = -J\sum_{\langle ij\rangle} s_i s_j$ (no external field) on a lattice where each spin has $z$ nearest neighbors.

  1. In the mean-field approximation, replace each neighbor $s_j$ by its average $\langle s_j \rangle = m$. Write the effective single-spin Hamiltonian.
  2. Compute the average of $s_i$ in this effective field and derive the self-consistency equation $m = \tanh(\beta J z m)$.
  3. Show that this equation has a non-trivial solution ($m \neq 0$) only for $T < T_c = Jz/k_B$.
  4. Near $T_c$, expand $\tanh$ to find $m \propto (T_c - T)^{1/2}$. What is the mean-field critical exponent $\beta_{\text{crit}}$?

a) Replacing each of the $z$ neighbors by $m$, spin $i$ sees an effective field $h_{\text{eff}} = Jzm$. The effective single-spin Hamiltonian is:

$$H_{\text{eff}} = -Jzm \, s_i$$

b) For a single spin in a field $h_{\text{eff}}$, the partition function is:

$$Z_1 = e^{\beta Jzm} + e^{-\beta Jzm} = 2\cosh(\beta Jzm)$$

The average magnetization is:

$$\langle s_i \rangle = \frac{e^{\beta Jzm} - e^{-\beta Jzm}}{e^{\beta Jzm} + e^{-\beta Jzm}} = \tanh(\beta Jzm)$$

Self-consistency requires $\langle s_i \rangle = m$:

$$\boxed{m = \tanh(\beta Jzm)}$$

c) Let $f(m) = \tanh(\beta Jzm)$. A non-trivial solution exists when the slope of $\tanh$ at the origin exceeds 1:

$$f'(0) = \beta Jz > 1 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad T < \frac{Jz}{k_B} = T_c$$

For $T > T_c$, the only solution is $m = 0$ (paramagnetic phase). For $T < T_c$, a spontaneous magnetization $m \neq 0$ appears (ferromagnetic phase).

d) Near $T_c$, $m$ is small, so $\tanh(x) \approx x - x^3/3$:

$$m \approx \beta Jzm - \frac{(\beta Jzm)^3}{3}$$ $$1 \approx \beta Jz - \frac{(\beta Jz)^2 m^2}{3}$$ $$m^2 \approx \frac{3(\beta Jz - 1)}{(\beta Jz)^2} \approx 3\left(\frac{T_c - T}{T_c}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{(\beta Jz)^2}$$

Near $T_c$ where $\beta Jz \approx 1$:

$$m \propto (T_c - T)^{1/2}$$

The mean-field critical exponent is $\beta_{\text{crit}} = 1/2$. (The exact 2D Ising value is $\beta_{\text{crit}} = 1/8$, showing that mean-field theory overestimates the exponent.)

6. Fluctuation-Dissipation Relation ↓

In the canonical ensemble, energy fluctuations are related to the heat capacity.

  1. Show that $\langle E^2 \rangle - \langle E \rangle^2 = \dfrac{\partial^2 \ln Z}{\partial \beta^2}$.
  2. Prove the fluctuation-dissipation relation: $\text{Var}(E) = k_BT^2 C_V$.
  3. For $N$ independent particles, $\langle E \rangle \propto N$ and $C_V \propto N$. Show that the relative fluctuation $\sigma_E / \langle E \rangle \propto 1/\sqrt{N}$.
  4. Explain why this result justifies using any ensemble in the thermodynamic limit.

a) From $Z = \sum_i e^{-\beta E_i}$:

$$\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta} = -\frac{\sum_i E_i e^{-\beta E_i}}{Z} = -\langle E \rangle$$ $$\frac{\partial^2 \ln Z}{\partial \beta^2} = \frac{\sum_i E_i^2 e^{-\beta E_i}}{Z} - \left(\frac{\sum_i E_i e^{-\beta E_i}}{Z}\right)^2 = \langle E^2 \rangle - \langle E \rangle^2$$

b) From $\langle E \rangle = -\partial \ln Z / \partial \beta$:

$$C_V = \frac{\partial \langle E \rangle}{\partial T} = \frac{\partial \langle E \rangle}{\partial \beta} \cdot \frac{\partial \beta}{\partial T} = -\frac{\partial^2 \ln Z}{\partial \beta^2} \cdot \left(-\frac{1}{k_BT^2}\right)$$ $$= \frac{\langle E^2 \rangle - \langle E \rangle^2}{k_BT^2}$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(E) = \langle E^2 \rangle - \langle E \rangle^2 = k_BT^2 C_V}$$

c) For $N$ independent particles: $\langle E \rangle = N\bar{\varepsilon}$ and $C_V = Nc_v$, where $\bar{\varepsilon}$ and $c_v$ are per-particle quantities. Thus:

$$\sigma_E = \sqrt{k_BT^2 \cdot Nc_v} \propto \sqrt{N}$$ $$\frac{\sigma_E}{\langle E \rangle} = \frac{\sqrt{k_BT^2 Nc_v}}{N\bar{\varepsilon}} \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}$$

d) For macroscopic systems ($N \sim 10^{23}$), the relative energy fluctuation is $\sim 10^{-12}$ — utterly negligible. This means the energy is essentially fixed even in the canonical ensemble, making it indistinguishable from the microcanonical ensemble. This is the equivalence of ensembles in the thermodynamic limit: all ensembles give the same predictions for macroscopic observables when $N \to \infty$.

7. Equivalence of Ensembles in the Thermodynamic Limit ↓

The canonical partition function $Z(\beta)$ and the microcanonical multiplicity $\Omega(E)$ are related by a Laplace transform:

$$Z(\beta) = \int_0^{\infty} \Omega(E) \, e^{-\beta E} \, dE$$
  1. Show that this integral can be evaluated by the saddle-point (Laplace) method for large $N$: $Z(\beta) \approx \Omega(E^*) e^{-\beta E^*}$, where $E^*$ maximizes $\Omega(E) e^{-\beta E}$.
  2. Show that the saddle-point condition is $\beta = \partial \ln \Omega / \partial E$, which is $1/(k_BT) = 1/T$ — the microcanonical definition of temperature.
  3. Show that in the thermodynamic limit, the entropy per particle is the same in both ensembles: $s_{\text{canon}} = s_{\text{micro}}$.

a) Write the integrand as $e^{f(E)}$ where $f(E) = \ln \Omega(E) - \beta E = S(E)/k_B - \beta E$. For large $N$, $S$ is extensive ($\propto N$), so $f$ has a sharp maximum. The saddle-point approximation gives:

$$Z(\beta) \approx \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{|f''(E^*)|}} \, e^{f(E^*)} \approx \Omega(E^*) e^{-\beta E^*}$$

(The prefactor contributes only $O(\ln N)$ terms, negligible compared to $O(N)$.)

b) The maximum of $f(E)$ satisfies:

$$f'(E^*) = \frac{1}{k_B}\frac{\partial S}{\partial E}\bigg|_{E^*} - \beta = 0$$ $$\Longrightarrow \quad \frac{\partial S}{\partial E}\bigg|_{E^*} = k_B \beta = \frac{1}{T}$$

This is precisely the microcanonical definition of temperature. The saddle-point energy $E^*$ is the most probable energy in the canonical ensemble, and it coincides with the microcanonical energy at the same temperature.

c) The canonical free energy per particle is:

$$\frac{F}{N} = -\frac{k_BT}{N} \ln Z \approx -\frac{k_BT}{N}\left[\ln \Omega(E^*) - \beta E^*\right] = \frac{E^*}{N} - T \frac{S(E^*)}{N}$$

The canonical entropy per particle is $s_{\text{canon}} = -\partial(F/N)/\partial T = S(E^*)/N = s_{\text{micro}}(E^*)$. In the thermodynamic limit, the ensembles are equivalent.

8. Restricted Boltzmann Machine as a Boltzmann Machine ↓

A Restricted Boltzmann Machine (RBM) has visible units $\mathbf{v} \in \{0,1\}^n$ and hidden units $\mathbf{h} \in \{0,1\}^m$ with energy function:

$$E(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{h}) = -\mathbf{a}^T\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{b}^T\mathbf{h} - \mathbf{v}^T W \mathbf{h}$$

where $\mathbf{a}$, $\mathbf{b}$ are bias vectors and $W$ is the weight matrix.

  1. Write down the partition function $Z$ and the joint probability $p(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{h})$.
  2. Show that the marginal over visible units is $p(\mathbf{v}) = \dfrac{1}{Z}\sum_{\mathbf{h}} e^{-E(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{h})}$ and derive the free energy $\mathcal{F}(\mathbf{v}) = -\ln \sum_{\mathbf{h}} e^{-E(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{h})}$ such that $p(\mathbf{v}) = e^{-\mathcal{F}(\mathbf{v})}/Z$.
  3. Show that because there are no hidden-hidden or visible-visible connections, the hidden units factorize: $p(\mathbf{h}|\mathbf{v}) = \prod_j p(h_j|\mathbf{v})$, and compute $p(h_j = 1|\mathbf{v})$.
  4. Explain how training the RBM (adjusting $W$, $\mathbf{a}$, $\mathbf{b}$ to match data) is equivalent to adjusting the parameters of an Ising-type model.

a) The partition function is:

$$Z = \sum_{\mathbf{v}} \sum_{\mathbf{h}} e^{-E(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{h})} = \sum_{\mathbf{v}} \sum_{\mathbf{h}} \exp\!\left(\mathbf{a}^T\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{b}^T\mathbf{h} + \mathbf{v}^TW\mathbf{h}\right)$$

The joint probability is $p(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{h}) = e^{-E(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{h})}/Z$.

b) Marginalizing over $\mathbf{h}$:

$$p(\mathbf{v}) = \frac{1}{Z}\sum_{\mathbf{h}} e^{-E(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{h})} = \frac{1}{Z}\sum_{\mathbf{h}} \exp\!\left(\mathbf{a}^T\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{b}^T\mathbf{h} + \mathbf{v}^TW\mathbf{h}\right)$$

Define the free energy:

$$\mathcal{F}(\mathbf{v}) = -\ln\sum_{\mathbf{h}} e^{-E(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{h})}$$

Since $\sum_{\mathbf{h}}$ factorizes over independent $h_j$:

$$\mathcal{F}(\mathbf{v}) = -\mathbf{a}^T\mathbf{v} - \sum_{j=1}^m \ln\!\left(1 + e^{b_j + W_{:,j}^T\mathbf{v}}\right)$$

Then $p(\mathbf{v}) = e^{-\mathcal{F}(\mathbf{v})}/Z$, where $Z = \sum_{\mathbf{v}} e^{-\mathcal{F}(\mathbf{v})}$.

c) Given $\mathbf{v}$, the energy is linear in each $h_j$ with no $h_j$-$h_k$ interactions. So:

$$p(\mathbf{h}|\mathbf{v}) = \prod_{j=1}^m p(h_j|\mathbf{v})$$

For each hidden unit:

$$p(h_j = 1|\mathbf{v}) = \sigma\!\left(b_j + \sum_i W_{ij} v_i\right)$$

where $\sigma(x) = 1/(1+e^{-x})$ is the sigmoid function. This is exactly a logistic regression — each hidden unit fires based on a weighted sum of visible inputs.

d) The RBM is precisely a Boltzmann machine (an Ising model with $\{0,1\}$ spins instead of $\{-1,+1\}$). The biases $\mathbf{a}$, $\mathbf{b}$ play the role of external fields $h$, and the weight matrix $W$ plays the role of coupling constants $J_{ij}$. Training adjusts these parameters to minimize the KL divergence between the model's Boltzmann distribution and the empirical data distribution. The gradient of the log-likelihood involves the difference between data-driven correlations $\langle v_i h_j \rangle_{\text{data}}$ and model-driven correlations $\langle v_i h_j \rangle_{\text{model}}$ — directly analogous to measuring correlation functions in a physical Ising system.

9. Phase Transition in the Ising Model: Critical Exponents ↓

Near the critical temperature $T_c$, physical quantities exhibit power-law behavior characterized by critical exponents. Define $t = (T - T_c)/T_c$ (reduced temperature).

  1. The magnetization vanishes as $m \sim |t|^{\beta}$ for $t \to 0^-$. From Exercise 5d, the mean-field value is $\beta = 1/2$. The exact 2D Ising value (Onsager) is $\beta = 1/8$. What does this discrepancy tell us about mean-field theory?
  2. The susceptibility $\chi = \partial m / \partial h |_{h=0}$ diverges as $\chi \sim |t|^{-\gamma}$. Using mean-field theory, show that $\gamma = 1$. (Hint: differentiate the mean-field equation with respect to $h$ near $T_c$.)
  3. The heat capacity diverges as $C \sim |t|^{-\alpha}$. The mean-field theory gives a discontinuity (jump) rather than a divergence. What is $\alpha$ for mean-field? For the exact 2D Ising model, $C \sim \ln|t|$ — what does this imply for $\alpha$?
  4. State the scaling relation $\alpha + 2\beta + \gamma = 2$ and verify it for both mean-field and exact 2D values.

a) Mean-field theory overestimates $\beta$ ($1/2$ vs $1/8$) because it neglects correlations between spins. In mean-field, each spin interacts with the average of its neighbors, missing the spatial structure of critical fluctuations. Near $T_c$, correlated spin clusters of all sizes appear (critical fluctuations), and the correlation length $\xi \to \infty$. Mean-field theory captures the qualitative physics (a continuous phase transition exists) but gets the quantitative exponents wrong, especially in low dimensions where fluctuations are strong.

b) The mean-field equation with external field is $m = \tanh(\beta(Jzm + h))$. Differentiating with respect to $h$ at $h = 0$:

$$\chi = \frac{\partial m}{\partial h} = \frac{\beta}{1 - \beta Jz(1 - m^2)}$$

For $T > T_c$ (where $m = 0$):

$$\chi = \frac{\beta}{1 - \beta Jz} = \frac{1/k_BT}{1 - T_c/T} = \frac{1}{k_B(T - T_c)}$$

Therefore $\chi \propto |T - T_c|^{-1}$, giving $\gamma = 1$.

c) In mean-field theory, the heat capacity has a finite jump discontinuity at $T_c$ (like a step function), not a power-law divergence. A jump corresponds to $\alpha = 0$ (with a discontinuity). For the exact 2D Ising model, $C \sim \ln|t|$. Since $\ln|t|$ diverges more slowly than any power law, this corresponds to $\alpha = 0$ (logarithmic divergence).

d) The Rushbrooke scaling relation is:

$$\alpha + 2\beta + \gamma = 2$$

Mean-field: $0 + 2(1/2) + 1 = 2$ ✓

Exact 2D Ising: $0 + 2(1/8) + 7/4 = 0 + 1/4 + 7/4 = 2$ ✓

(The exact 2D value is $\gamma = 7/4$.) The scaling relation holds in both cases. This is not a coincidence — it follows from the mathematical structure of the renormalization group, which also explains universality: systems with the same symmetry and dimensionality share the same critical exponents, regardless of microscopic details.

10. Jaynes' MaxEnt and Exponential Families ↓

Jaynes showed that the MaxEnt distribution subject to $k$ moment constraints $\langle f_r(\mathbf{x}) \rangle = F_r$ for $r = 1, \ldots, k$ is always an exponential family distribution.

  1. Set up the entropy maximization with Lagrange multipliers $\lambda_0$ (normalization) and $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k$ (moment constraints). Show that the solution is: $$p(\mathbf{x}) = \exp\!\left(-\lambda_0 - \sum_{r=1}^k \lambda_r f_r(\mathbf{x})\right)$$
  2. Define the log-partition function $\Psi(\boldsymbol{\lambda}) = \ln \sum_{\mathbf{x}} \exp(-\sum_r \lambda_r f_r(\mathbf{x}))$. Show that $\lambda_0 = \Psi(\boldsymbol{\lambda})$.
  3. Prove that $\langle f_r \rangle = -\partial \Psi / \partial \lambda_r$ and $\text{Var}(f_r) = \partial^2 \Psi / \partial \lambda_r^2$.
  4. Show that the maximum entropy as a function of the constraints $F_r$ is the Legendre transform of $\Psi$: $$S^*(F_1, \ldots, F_k) = \Psi(\boldsymbol{\lambda}) + \sum_r \lambda_r F_r$$ and explain the physical meaning: this is $S = (F - U)/(-T)$ in disguise.

a) Maximize $S[p] = -\sum_{\mathbf{x}} p(\mathbf{x}) \ln p(\mathbf{x})$ subject to $\sum_{\mathbf{x}} p(\mathbf{x}) = 1$ and $\sum_{\mathbf{x}} p(\mathbf{x}) f_r(\mathbf{x}) = F_r$. The Lagrangian is:

$$\mathcal{L} = -\sum_{\mathbf{x}} p(\mathbf{x})\ln p(\mathbf{x}) - \lambda_0\!\left(\sum_{\mathbf{x}} p(\mathbf{x}) - 1\right) - \sum_{r=1}^k \lambda_r\!\left(\sum_{\mathbf{x}} p(\mathbf{x}) f_r(\mathbf{x}) - F_r\right)$$

Setting $\partial \mathcal{L} / \partial p(\mathbf{x}) = 0$:

$$-\ln p(\mathbf{x}) - 1 - \lambda_0 - \sum_r \lambda_r f_r(\mathbf{x}) = 0$$ $$p(\mathbf{x}) = \exp\!\left(-(1 + \lambda_0) - \sum_r \lambda_r f_r(\mathbf{x})\right)$$

Absorbing the constant into a redefined $\lambda_0$:

$$\boxed{p(\mathbf{x}) = \exp\!\left(-\lambda_0 - \sum_{r=1}^k \lambda_r f_r(\mathbf{x})\right)}$$

b) The normalization condition $\sum_{\mathbf{x}} p(\mathbf{x}) = 1$ gives:

$$e^{-\lambda_0} \sum_{\mathbf{x}} \exp\!\left(-\sum_r \lambda_r f_r(\mathbf{x})\right) = 1$$ $$\lambda_0 = \ln \sum_{\mathbf{x}} \exp\!\left(-\sum_r \lambda_r f_r(\mathbf{x})\right) = \Psi(\boldsymbol{\lambda})$$

So $\lambda_0$ is the log-partition function, and $p(\mathbf{x}) = \exp(-\sum_r \lambda_r f_r(\mathbf{x})) / e^{\Psi}$.

c) From $e^{\Psi} = \sum_{\mathbf{x}} e^{-\sum_r \lambda_r f_r(\mathbf{x})}$:

$$\frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial \lambda_r} = \frac{-\sum_{\mathbf{x}} f_r(\mathbf{x}) e^{-\sum_s \lambda_s f_s(\mathbf{x})}}{e^{\Psi}} = -\sum_{\mathbf{x}} f_r(\mathbf{x}) p(\mathbf{x}) = -\langle f_r \rangle$$

Differentiating again:

$$\frac{\partial^2 \Psi}{\partial \lambda_r^2} = \langle f_r \rangle^2 - \langle f_r^2 \rangle = -\text{Var}(f_r)$$

Wait — more carefully: $\partial^2 \Psi / \partial \lambda_r^2 = \langle f_r^2 \rangle - \langle f_r \rangle^2 = \text{Var}(f_r)$. (The sign works out because the second derivative of a convex function is positive.)

d) The entropy at the maximum is:

$$S^* = -\sum_{\mathbf{x}} p(\mathbf{x})\ln p(\mathbf{x}) = -\sum_{\mathbf{x}} p(\mathbf{x})\!\left(-\lambda_0 - \sum_r \lambda_r f_r(\mathbf{x})\right)$$ $$= \lambda_0 + \sum_r \lambda_r \langle f_r \rangle = \Psi(\boldsymbol{\lambda}) + \sum_r \lambda_r F_r$$

This is the Legendre transform of $\Psi$ (switching from the "natural parameters" $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ to the "expectation parameters" $\mathbf{F}$).

Physical meaning: For the canonical ensemble with a single constraint $\langle E \rangle = U$, we have $\lambda_1 = \beta = 1/(k_BT)$ and $\Psi = \ln Z = -\beta F$, where $F$ is the Helmholtz free energy. Then:

$$S^* = -\beta F + \beta U = \frac{U - F}{T}$$

which is the standard thermodynamic identity $S = (U - F)/T$, or equivalently $F = U - TS$. The entire structure of thermodynamics — Legendre transforms between potentials, Maxwell relations, response functions as second derivatives — is a consequence of maximum entropy inference applied to exponential family distributions.
