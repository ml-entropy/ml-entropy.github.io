What Is Free Energy?

In thermodynamics, we learn that systems tend to maximize entropy. But in the real world, most systems are not isolated — they exchange heat with their surroundings. When a system is in thermal contact with a reservoir at temperature $T$, the correct criterion for equilibrium is not "maximize entropy of the system" but rather "minimize free energy."

Free energy answers a practical question: of all the energy stored in a system, how much is available to do useful work? The rest is "locked up" by entropy — it is the energy that nature uses to explore microstates, and you cannot extract it without violating the Second Law.

Free Energy: The Core Idea

Free energy = Total energy $-$ Energy "locked" by entropy

$$F = U - TS$$

The term $TS$ represents the energy that is thermally "spread out" among microstates and cannot be converted to work. Only the remainder $F = U - TS$ is free to do work.

The spontaneity criterion follows immediately. For a system at constant temperature and volume in contact with a heat bath, the combined entropy of the system and its surroundings increases if and only if the system's free energy decreases:

$$\Delta S_{\text{universe}} = -\frac{\Delta F}{T} \geq 0 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \Delta F \leq 0$$

A process is spontaneous when $\Delta F < 0$. At equilibrium, $F$ is at a minimum. This is far more useful than tracking the entropy of the entire universe: we only need to compute a property of the system itself.

Why "Free"?

The word "free" in free energy does not mean "without cost." It means available — the portion of energy that is free to be converted into work. Helmholtz originally called it freie Energie (free energy), while Gibbs called his version available energy. Both names point to the same idea: it is the energy that can actually do something.

Helmholtz Free Energy

The Helmholtz free energy $F$ (sometimes written $A$) is the appropriate thermodynamic potential for systems at constant temperature and volume. It is defined as:

Helmholtz Free Energy
$$F = U - TS$$

Understanding the Variables:

  • $F$ (Helmholtz Free Energy): The maximum work extractable from the system at constant $T$ and $V$. Measured in Joules ($\text{J}$).
  • $U$ (Internal Energy): The total microscopic energy of the system (kinetic + potential energy of all particles).
  • $T$ (Temperature): The absolute temperature, fixed by the heat reservoir.
  • $S$ (Entropy): The thermodynamic entropy of the system.

Natural variables: $(T, V, N)$ — temperature, volume, and particle number.

The Differential Form

Starting from $F = U - TS$ and using the fundamental relation $dU = TdS - PdV + \mu dN$, we obtain:

$$dF = dU - TdS - SdT = -SdT - PdV + \mu dN$$

This reveals the natural variables of $F$: if we know $F(T, V, N)$, we can extract all thermodynamic information:

  • Entropy: $S = -\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial T}\right)_{V,N}$
  • Pressure: $P = -\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial V}\right)_{T,N}$
  • Chemical potential: $\mu = \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial N}\right)_{T,V}$

Connection to Statistical Mechanics

The Helmholtz free energy has a remarkably elegant expression in terms of the partition function $Z$. Recall from statistical mechanics that the canonical partition function is:

$$Z = \sum_i e^{-\beta E_i}, \quad \beta = \frac{1}{k_B T}$$

The Helmholtz free energy is simply:

$$\boxed{F = -k_B T \ln Z}$$

This is one of the most important equations in statistical mechanics. It provides a direct bridge between the microscopic world (energy levels $E_i$ encoded in $Z$) and the macroscopic world (thermodynamic quantities derived from $F$).

Derivation: $F = -k_BT \ln Z$

In the canonical ensemble, the probability of microstate $i$ is $p_i = e^{-\beta E_i}/Z$. The average energy is:

$$\langle E \rangle = \sum_i p_i E_i = -\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta}$$

The Gibbs entropy is:

$$S = -k_B \sum_i p_i \ln p_i = -k_B \sum_i p_i (-\beta E_i - \ln Z) = \frac{\langle E \rangle}{T} + k_B \ln Z$$

Therefore:

$$F = \langle E \rangle - TS = \langle E \rangle - \langle E \rangle - k_B T \ln Z = -k_B T \ln Z$$

Minimization at Constant $T, V$

At constant temperature and volume, the Helmholtz free energy is minimized at equilibrium. This can be understood as a competition between two tendencies: the system wants to minimize energy $U$ (which favors ordered, low-energy states) and maximize entropy $S$ (which favors disordered, high-entropy states). The temperature $T$ controls the relative weight: at low $T$, energy dominates and the system orders; at high $T$, entropy dominates and the system disorders. Free energy $F = U - TS$ balances these two drives.

Gibbs Free Energy

Most laboratory experiments and biological processes occur at constant temperature and pressure (not constant volume). For these conditions, the appropriate thermodynamic potential is the Gibbs free energy:

Gibbs Free Energy
$$G = H - TS = U + PV - TS = F + PV$$

Understanding the Variables:

  • $G$ (Gibbs Free Energy): The maximum non-expansion work extractable at constant $T$ and $P$.
  • $H = U + PV$ (Enthalpy): The internal energy plus the work needed to make room for the system at pressure $P$.
  • $T$ (Temperature): Absolute temperature, fixed by the surroundings.
  • $S$ (Entropy): Thermodynamic entropy of the system.

Natural variables: $(T, P, N)$ — temperature, pressure, and particle number.

The differential form is:

$$dG = -SdT + VdP + \mu dN$$

At constant $T$ and $P$, the condition $\Delta G < 0$ determines spontaneity. This is the criterion used in chemistry, biology, and engineering:

  • $\Delta G < 0$: Process is spontaneous (exergonic)
  • $\Delta G = 0$: System is at equilibrium
  • $\Delta G > 0$: Process is non-spontaneous (endergonic); requires external work

Chemical Equilibrium and Phase Transitions

Gibbs free energy governs chemical reactions. For a reaction at equilibrium, $\Delta G = 0$, which leads to the relation between the standard Gibbs energy and the equilibrium constant:

$$\Delta G^\circ = -RT \ln K_{eq}$$

For phase transitions, two phases coexist when their molar Gibbs free energies are equal: $g_{\text{liquid}}(T,P) = g_{\text{gas}}(T,P)$. This condition defines the coexistence curve in the $(T, P)$ phase diagram and leads to the Clausius-Clapeyron equation:

$$\frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{\Delta S}{\Delta V} = \frac{L}{T \Delta V}$$

where $L$ is the latent heat and $\Delta V$ is the volume change during the transition.

Helmholtz vs. Gibbs: Which to Use?

Helmholtz $F$: Use when the system is at constant $T$ and $V$ (e.g., gas in a rigid container, statistical mechanics, simulations in the NVT ensemble).
Gibbs $G$: Use when the system is at constant $T$ and $P$ (e.g., chemical reactions in open containers, biological processes, phase transitions at atmospheric pressure).

The Variational Principle in Physics

Free energy minimization can be viewed as a variational problem. Instead of searching for the exact equilibrium state directly, we can optimize over a family of trial states and obtain rigorous bounds on the true free energy.

The key insight is this: the Gibbs entropy formula gives us the free energy as a functional of the probability distribution $q$ over microstates:

$$\mathcal{F}[q] = \sum_i q_i E_i + k_B T \sum_i q_i \ln q_i = \langle E \rangle_q - T S_q$$

This is the variational free energy. The true equilibrium free energy $F$ is obtained by minimizing $\mathcal{F}[q]$ over all valid probability distributions $q$:

$$F = \min_q \mathcal{F}[q]$$

The minimum is achieved when $q$ equals the Boltzmann distribution $p_i = e^{-\beta E_i}/Z$. For any other distribution $q \neq p$, the variational free energy is strictly higher: $\mathcal{F}[q] > F$. The gap is precisely the KL divergence:

$$\mathcal{F}[q] - F = k_B T \, D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) \geq 0$$

The Bogoliubov Inequality

The Bogoliubov inequality (also called the Gibbs-Bogoliubov inequality) provides a powerful variational bound. Suppose we have a complicated Hamiltonian $H$ whose partition function we cannot compute exactly, but we have a simpler reference Hamiltonian $H_0$ with known free energy $F_0$. Then:

Bogoliubov Inequality
$$F \leq F_0 + \langle H - H_0 \rangle_0$$

Understanding the Terms:

  • $F$: The true free energy of the system with Hamiltonian $H$.
  • $F_0 = -k_B T \ln Z_0$: The free energy of the reference system $H_0$.
  • $\langle H - H_0 \rangle_0$: The average of $(H - H_0)$ taken with respect to the reference distribution $p_0 \propto e^{-\beta H_0}$.

The inequality becomes an equality if and only if $H_0 = H + \text{const}$, i.e., when the reference Hamiltonian perfectly matches the true one.

Mean-Field Theory as Variational Approximation

The most important application of the Bogoliubov inequality is mean-field theory. The idea is simple: replace the complicated interacting system with a non-interacting (factorized) reference system, then optimize the reference parameters using the variational bound.

For example, consider the Ising model with $N$ spins $\sigma_i = \pm 1$ on a lattice:

$$H = -J \sum_{\langle i,j \rangle} \sigma_i \sigma_j - h \sum_i \sigma_i$$

The exact solution is intractable for general lattices. The mean-field approximation uses the factorized trial Hamiltonian:

$$H_0 = -h_{\text{eff}} \sum_i \sigma_i$$

where $h_{\text{eff}}$ is a variational parameter. Minimizing the Bogoliubov bound $F_0 + \langle H - H_0 \rangle_0$ with respect to $h_{\text{eff}}$ yields the famous mean-field self-consistency equation:

$$m = \tanh\!\Big(\beta(Jzm + h)\Big)$$

where $m = \langle \sigma_i \rangle$ is the magnetization and $z$ is the coordination number (number of nearest neighbors). This equation predicts a phase transition at $T_c = Jz/k_B$: below this temperature, spontaneous magnetization ($m \neq 0$) emerges even without an external field ($h = 0$).

From Physics to Machine Learning: The ELBO

One of the most profound connections in modern science is the exact parallel between variational free energy in physics and the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) in machine learning. This is not a loose analogy — it is a mathematical identity.

The Key Correspondence

Minimizing variational free energy in physics = Maximizing the ELBO in machine learning. They are the same optimization problem, viewed from opposite sides (minimizing a quantity vs. maximizing its negative).

Consider a latent variable model with observed data $x$ and latent variables $z$. The joint distribution is $p(x, z)$, and we introduce a variational approximation $q(z)$ to the true posterior $p(z|x)$. The variational free energy is:

$$\mathcal{F}[q] = \langle -\log p(x, z) \rangle_q + \langle \log q(z) \rangle_q$$

Let us unpack this by comparing term-by-term with physics:

Physics-ML Dictionary
Physics Machine Learning
Energy $E_i$ Negative log-joint $-\log p(x, z)$
Entropy $S_q = -k_B\sum q_i \ln q_i$ Entropy $H[q] = -\sum q(z) \log q(z)$
Variational free energy $\mathcal{F} = \langle E \rangle_q - TS_q$ Negative ELBO $= \langle -\log p(x,z) \rangle_q - H[q]$
True free energy $F = -k_BT \ln Z$ Negative log-evidence $-\log p(x)$
Partition function $Z$ Evidence (marginal likelihood) $p(x)$
Boltzmann distribution $p_i \propto e^{-\beta E_i}$ True posterior $p(z|x)$

The ELBO is simply the negative of the variational free energy (at $T = 1$, setting $k_B = 1$):

$$\text{ELBO}(q) = -\mathcal{F}[q] = \langle \log p(x, z) \rangle_q - \langle \log q(z) \rangle_q = \langle \log p(x, z) \rangle_q + H[q]$$

The gap between the ELBO and the true log-evidence is the KL divergence:

$$\log p(x) = \text{ELBO}(q) + D_{\text{KL}}(q(z) \| p(z|x)) \geq \text{ELBO}(q)$$

Since $D_{\text{KL}} \geq 0$, the ELBO is always a lower bound on the log-evidence. Maximizing the ELBO simultaneously:

  1. Makes $q(z)$ a better approximation to the true posterior $p(z|x)$ (reduces $D_{\text{KL}}$)
  2. Increases the log-evidence $\log p(x)$ (makes the model fit the data better)

This is exactly the physics story: minimizing the variational free energy simultaneously makes the trial distribution approach the Boltzmann distribution and tightens the bound on the true free energy.

Free Energy Principle (Friston)

Karl Friston extended the variational free energy framework far beyond physics and machine learning into neuroscience and biology. His Free Energy Principle (FEP) proposes that all adaptive systems — from single cells to human brains — can be understood as minimizing a quantity called variational free energy.

The core argument is elegant: any system that persists over time must resist the natural tendency toward thermodynamic equilibrium (dissolution). To persist, it must occupy a restricted set of states — it must have low entropy. Friston argues this is achieved by the system minimizing a bound on surprisal (self-information):

$$\text{Surprisal} = -\log p(o) \quad \text{where } o \text{ = observations}$$

Since surprisal is intractable to compute directly (it requires marginalizing over all hidden states), organisms instead minimize an upper bound — the variational free energy:

$$\mathcal{F}[q] = D_{\text{KL}}(q(s) \| p(s|o)) - \log p(o) \geq -\log p(o)$$

This leads to active inference: organisms do not just passively update their beliefs (perception), they also act on the world to make their predictions come true (action). Both perception and action serve the same objective — minimizing free energy.

A Unifying Framework?

The Free Energy Principle is ambitious and controversial. Supporters see it as a unifying principle for brain function, perception, action, learning, and even evolution. Critics argue it may be unfalsifiable or too general to make specific predictions. Regardless, it has been enormously productive in generating new models of brain function and connecting neuroscience to variational inference and information theory.

Thermodynamic Integration

Computing absolute free energies is hard. But computing free energy differences between two systems is often tractable, and this is usually what matters in practice (e.g., which drug binds more strongly, which phase is more stable).

Thermodynamic integration computes $\Delta F = F_1 - F_0$ by smoothly interpolating between two Hamiltonians:

$$H(\lambda) = (1 - \lambda) H_0 + \lambda H_1, \quad \lambda \in [0, 1]$$

The free energy difference is then given by the exact identity:

Thermodynamic Integration Formula
$$\Delta F = F_1 - F_0 = \int_0^1 \left\langle \frac{\partial H(\lambda)}{\partial \lambda} \right\rangle_\lambda d\lambda = \int_0^1 \langle H_1 - H_0 \rangle_\lambda \, d\lambda$$

Here $\langle \cdot \rangle_\lambda$ denotes an average over the equilibrium distribution at parameter $\lambda$, i.e., $p_\lambda \propto e^{-\beta H(\lambda)}$.

In practice, we evaluate the integrand at several values of $\lambda$ (e.g., $\lambda = 0, 0.1, 0.2, \ldots, 1$) using molecular dynamics or Monte Carlo simulation, then perform numerical integration (trapezoidal rule, Gauss-Legendre quadrature, etc.).

Connection to Annealed Importance Sampling

Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS), introduced by Radford Neal in 2001, is the machine learning counterpart of thermodynamic integration. It estimates the ratio of partition functions $Z_1/Z_0$ by constructing a sequence of intermediate distributions:

$$p_0 \to p_{\lambda_1} \to p_{\lambda_2} \to \cdots \to p_1$$

where each transition uses MCMC. The importance weight accumulated along the chain provides an unbiased estimate of $Z_1/Z_0$. This technique is widely used to evaluate generative models (e.g., estimating the log-likelihood of deep generative models like VAEs and energy-based models).

Bridge sampling is a closely related technique that uses a single intermediate "bridge" distribution to estimate $Z_1/Z_0$ with lower variance than either simple importance sampling or thermodynamic integration alone.

Applications

Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)

The VAE, introduced by Kingma and Welling (2014), is perhaps the purest embodiment of variational free energy minimization in machine learning. The VAE loss function is exactly the negative ELBO:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{VAE}} = -\text{ELBO} = -\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] + D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z))$$

The first term is the reconstruction error (analogous to the energy $\langle E \rangle_q$), and the second is the KL regularizer (analogous to the negative entropy $-TS_q$). Training a VAE is literally performing variational free energy minimization.

Energy-Based Models

Energy-based models (EBMs) define a probability distribution via a Boltzmann-like form:

$$p_\theta(x) = \frac{e^{-E_\theta(x)}}{Z_\theta}, \quad Z_\theta = \int e^{-E_\theta(x)} dx$$

The intractable partition function $Z_\theta$ makes maximum likelihood training difficult. Contrastive divergence (Hinton, 2002) approximates the gradient of the log-likelihood using short MCMC runs, effectively performing an approximate free energy computation at each training step.

Thermodynamic Computing

An emerging field explores using physical thermodynamic processes to perform computation. The idea is that a physical system naturally minimizes its free energy, so by engineering the Hamiltonian appropriately, the equilibrium state of the system encodes the solution to an optimization problem. This connects back to Hopfield networks, Boltzmann machines, and simulated annealing — all of which exploit the physics of free energy minimization for computation.

1. Variational Mean-Field Approximation for the Ising Model

This code solves the mean-field self-consistency equation $m = \tanh(\beta(Jzm + h))$ and plots the magnetization as a function of temperature, revealing the phase transition.

python 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def mean_field_magnetization(T, J=1.0, z=4, h=0.0, tol=1e-8, max_iter=10000):
    """
    Solve mean-field equation m = tanh(beta * (J*z*m + h))
    by fixed-point iteration.
    Returns both positive and negative solutions.
    """
    beta = 1.0 / T if T > 0 else np.inf
    solutions = []
    # Try multiple initial conditions to find all solutions
    for m0 in [0.99, 0.5, 0.01, -0.01, -0.5, -0.99]:
        m = m0
        for _ in range(max_iter):
            m_new = np.tanh(beta * (J * z * m + h))
            if abs(m_new - m) < tol:
                break
            m = m_new
        # Round to avoid near-duplicate solutions
        m_rounded = round(m, 6)
        if m_rounded not in [round(s, 6) for s in solutions]:
            solutions.append(m)
    return solutions

# Temperature range
T_values = np.linspace(0.01, 8.0, 500)
T_c = 4.0  # Critical temperature for J=1, z=4

# Compute magnetization vs temperature
m_positive = []
m_negative = []
m_zero = []

for T in T_values:
    sols = mean_field_magnetization(T)
    pos = [s for s in sols if s > 0.01]
    neg = [s for s in sols if s < -0.01]
    zero = [s for s in sols if abs(s) <= 0.01]
    m_positive.append(max(pos) if pos else np.nan)
    m_negative.append(min(neg) if neg else np.nan)
    m_zero.append(zero[0] if zero else np.nan)

# Plot
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))
ax.plot(T_values, m_positive, 'b-', linewidth=2, label='$m > 0$ (ordered)')
ax.plot(T_values, m_negative, 'r-', linewidth=2, label='$m < 0$ (ordered)')
ax.plot(T_values, m_zero, 'k--', linewidth=1, alpha=0.5, label='$m = 0$ (disordered)')
ax.axvline(x=T_c, color='gray', linestyle=':', label=f'$T_c = Jz/k_B = {T_c}$')
ax.set_xlabel('Temperature $T$ (units of $k_B$)', fontsize=14)
ax.set_ylabel('Magnetization $m$', fontsize=14)
ax.set_title('Mean-Field Phase Transition in the Ising Model', fontsize=16)
ax.legend(fontsize=12)
ax.set_ylim(-1.1, 1.1)
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

2. ELBO Computation for a Simple Gaussian Model

This example computes the ELBO for a Gaussian latent variable model, showing how the variational approximation $q(z)$ approaches the true posterior as we optimize.

python 
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def compute_elbo(mu_q, sigma_q, x_obs, sigma_prior=1.0, sigma_likelihood=1.0):
    """
    Compute ELBO for:
      Prior:      p(z) = N(0, sigma_prior^2)
      Likelihood: p(x|z) = N(z, sigma_likelihood^2)
      Variational: q(z) = N(mu_q, sigma_q^2)

    ELBO = E_q[log p(x,z)] - E_q[log q(z)]
         = E_q[log p(x|z)] + E_q[log p(z)] - E_q[log q(z)]
         = E_q[log p(x|z)] - KL(q || prior)
    """
    # E_q[log p(x|z)] = -0.5*log(2*pi*sigma_L^2) - 0.5*(x - mu_q)^2/sigma_L^2
    #                    - 0.5*sigma_q^2/sigma_L^2
    reconstruction = (-0.5 * np.log(2 * np.pi * sigma_likelihood**2)
                      - 0.5 * (x_obs - mu_q)**2 / sigma_likelihood**2
                      - 0.5 * sigma_q**2 / sigma_likelihood**2)

    # KL(q || prior) for two Gaussians
    kl = (np.log(sigma_prior / sigma_q)
          + (sigma_q**2 + mu_q**2) / (2 * sigma_prior**2) - 0.5)

    return reconstruction - kl

# Observed data point
x_obs = 2.0
sigma_prior = 1.0
sigma_lik = 0.5

# True posterior: p(z|x) is Gaussian with known mean and variance
sigma_post_sq = 1.0 / (1.0/sigma_prior**2 + 1.0/sigma_lik**2)
mu_post = sigma_post_sq * (x_obs / sigma_lik**2)
sigma_post = np.sqrt(sigma_post_sq)

# Scan ELBO over mu_q and sigma_q
mu_range = np.linspace(-1, 4, 200)
sigma_range = np.linspace(0.1, 2.0, 200)
MU, SIGMA = np.meshgrid(mu_range, sigma_range)
ELBO = np.vectorize(compute_elbo)(MU, SIGMA, x_obs, sigma_prior, sigma_lik)

# Plot
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(14, 5))

# ELBO landscape
cs = axes[0].contourf(MU, SIGMA, ELBO, levels=50, cmap='viridis')
axes[0].plot(mu_post, sigma_post, 'r*', markersize=15, label=f'Optimum ($\\mu$={mu_post:.2f}, $\\sigma$={sigma_post:.2f})')
axes[0].set_xlabel('$\\mu_q$')
axes[0].set_ylabel('$\\sigma_q$')
axes[0].set_title('ELBO Landscape')
axes[0].legend()
fig.colorbar(cs, ax=axes[0])

# ELBO vs mu_q at optimal sigma
elbos_mu = [compute_elbo(m, sigma_post, x_obs, sigma_prior, sigma_lik) for m in mu_range]
log_evidence = np.log(norm.pdf(x_obs, 0, np.sqrt(sigma_prior**2 + sigma_lik**2)))
axes[1].plot(mu_range, elbos_mu, 'b-', linewidth=2, label='ELBO')
axes[1].axhline(y=log_evidence, color='r', linestyle='--', label=f'log p(x) = {log_evidence:.3f}')
axes[1].axvline(x=mu_post, color='gray', linestyle=':', alpha=0.5)
axes[1].set_xlabel('$\\mu_q$')
axes[1].set_ylabel('ELBO')
axes[1].set_title('ELBO vs $\\mu_q$ (at optimal $\\sigma_q$)')
axes[1].legend()

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
print(f"True posterior: N({mu_post:.4f}, {sigma_post:.4f}^2)")
print(f"Log evidence: {log_evidence:.4f}")
print(f"Max ELBO: {max(elbos_mu):.4f}")

3. Thermodynamic Integration

This example uses thermodynamic integration to compute the free energy difference between two one-dimensional potentials (a harmonic oscillator and a double-well potential).

python 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def energy_harmonic(x, k=1.0):
    """Harmonic potential: E_0 = 0.5 * k * x^2"""
    return 0.5 * k * x**2

def energy_double_well(x, a=1.0, b=4.0):
    """Double-well potential: E_1 = a*(x^2 - b)^2"""
    return a * (x**2 - b)**2

def metropolis_sample(energy_func, beta, n_samples=50000, x_init=0.0, step=0.5):
    """Metropolis-Hastings sampling from p(x) ~ exp(-beta * E(x))"""
    samples = []
    x = x_init
    for i in range(n_samples + 5000):  # 5000 burn-in
        x_new = x + np.random.normal(0, step)
        dE = energy_func(x_new) - energy_func(x)
        if dE < 0 or np.random.random() < np.exp(-beta * dE):
            x = x_new
        if i >= 5000:
            samples.append(x)
    return np.array(samples)

def thermodynamic_integration(beta, n_lambda=21, n_samples=50000):
    """
    Compute Delta F = F_1 - F_0 via thermodynamic integration.
    H(lambda) = (1-lambda)*H_0 + lambda*H_1
    Delta F = integral_0^1 _lambda d_lambda
    """
    lambdas = np.linspace(0, 1, n_lambda)
    integrand = []

    for lam in lambdas:
        # Mixed energy function
        def energy_mixed(x, lam=lam):
            return (1 - lam) * energy_harmonic(x) + lam * energy_double_well(x)

        # Sample from p_lambda
        samples = metropolis_sample(energy_mixed, beta, n_samples)

        # Compute _lambda
        dE = energy_double_well(samples) - energy_harmonic(samples)
        integrand.append(np.mean(dE))

    # Trapezoidal integration
    delta_F = np.trapz(integrand, lambdas)
    return lambdas, integrand, delta_F

# Run thermodynamic integration
beta = 1.0  # inverse temperature
lambdas, integrand, delta_F = thermodynamic_integration(beta, n_lambda=21, n_samples=30000)

# Compute exact free energies for comparison (numerical integration)
from scipy.integrate import quad
x_range = np.linspace(-5, 5, 1000)
Z0, _ = quad(lambda x: np.exp(-beta * energy_harmonic(x)), -10, 10)
Z1, _ = quad(lambda x: np.exp(-beta * energy_double_well(x)), -10, 10)
delta_F_exact = -(1.0/beta) * np.log(Z1/Z0)

# Plot
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(14, 5))

# Plot integrand
axes[0].plot(lambdas, integrand, 'bo-', markersize=6, linewidth=2)
axes[0].fill_between(lambdas, integrand, alpha=0.2)
axes[0].set_xlabel('$\\lambda$', fontsize=14)
axes[0].set_ylabel('$\\langle H_1 - H_0 \\rangle_\\lambda$', fontsize=14)
axes[0].set_title('Thermodynamic Integration Integrand', fontsize=14)
axes[0].grid(True, alpha=0.3)

# Plot potentials
x = np.linspace(-4, 4, 500)
axes[1].plot(x, energy_harmonic(x), 'b-', linewidth=2, label='$H_0$ (harmonic)')
axes[1].plot(x, energy_double_well(x), 'r-', linewidth=2, label='$H_1$ (double well)')
axes[1].set_xlabel('$x$', fontsize=14)

4. Annealed Importance Sampling

This implementation uses AIS to estimate the partition function ratio between two distributions, demonstrating the connection between thermodynamic integration and importance sampling.

python 
import numpy as np
from scipy.integrate import quad

def ais_estimate(energy_0, energy_1, beta, n_chains=500,
                 n_intermediate=100, n_mcmc_steps=10, step_size=0.5):
    """
    Annealed Importance Sampling to estimate Z_1 / Z_0.

    Creates a sequence of distributions:
      p_k(x) ~ exp(-beta * [(1 - beta_k)*E_0(x) + beta_k*E_1(x)])
    where beta_k = k / n_intermediate, k = 0, 1, ..., n_intermediate.
    """
    betas = np.linspace(0, 1, n_intermediate + 1)
    log_weights = np.zeros(n_chains)

    # Initialize from p_0 (sample from energy_0 distribution)
    # For simplicity, sample from a Gaussian approximation
    x = np.random.normal(0, 1.0/np.sqrt(beta), size=n_chains)

    for k in range(1, len(betas)):
        # Log weight update: log(p_{k}(x) / p_{k-1}(x))
        # = -beta * [(betas[k] - betas[k-1]) * (E_1(x) - E_0(x))]
        dbeta = betas[k] - betas[k-1]
        log_weights += -beta * dbeta * (energy_1(x) - energy_0(x))

        # MCMC transition at current intermediate distribution
        def energy_k(x_val):
            return (1 - betas[k]) * energy_0(x_val) + betas[k] * energy_1(x_val)

        for _ in range(n_mcmc_steps):
            x_new = x + np.random.normal(0, step_size, size=n_chains)
            dE = beta * (energy_k(x_new) - energy_k(x))
            accept = np.log(np.random.random(size=n_chains)) < -dE
            x = np.where(accept, x_new, x)

    # Estimate log(Z_1/Z_0) using log-sum-exp for numerical stability
    max_lw = np.max(log_weights)
    log_ratio = max_lw + np.log(np.mean(np.exp(log_weights - max_lw)))

    return log_ratio, log_weights

# Define energy functions
energy_0 = lambda x: 0.5 * x**2              # Harmonic
energy_1 = lambda x: 1.0 * (x**2 - 4.0)**2  # Double well

beta = 1.0

# Run AIS
log_ratio_ais, log_weights = ais_estimate(
    energy_0, energy_1, beta,
    n_chains=1000, n_intermediate=200, n_mcmc_steps=5
)

# Exact computation
Z0, _ = quad(lambda x: np.exp(-beta * energy_0(x)), -10, 10)
Z1, _ = quad(lambda x: np.exp(-beta * energy_1(x)), -10, 10)
log_ratio_exact = np.log(Z1 / Z0)

print(f"AIS estimate:  log(Z1/Z0) = {log_ratio_ais:.4f}")
print(f"Exact result:  log(Z1/Z0) = {log_ratio_exact:.4f}")
print(f"Delta F (AIS):  {-log_ratio_ais/beta:.4f}")
print(f"Delta F (exact): {-log_ratio_exact/beta:.4f}")

# Plot histogram of log importance weights
plt.figure(figsize=(8, 5))
plt.hist(log_weights, bins=50, density=True, alpha=0.7, color='steelblue')
plt.axvline(x=log_ratio_exact, color='red', linestyle='--',
            linewidth=2, label=f'Exact log(Z1/Z0) = {log_ratio_exact:.3f}')
plt.xlabel('Log importance weight', fontsize=14)
plt.ylabel('Density', fontsize=14)
plt.title('Distribution of AIS Log Weights', fontsize=16)
plt.legend(fontsize=12)
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

1. Helmholtz Free Energy from the Partition Function ↓

A quantum system has three energy levels: $E_1 = 0$, $E_2 = \epsilon$, $E_3 = 2\epsilon$, each non-degenerate.

  1. Write down the canonical partition function $Z$ at temperature $T$.
  2. Compute the Helmholtz free energy $F = -k_BT \ln Z$.
  3. Find $F$ in the low-temperature limit ($k_BT \ll \epsilon$) and the high-temperature limit ($k_BT \gg \epsilon$). Interpret both physically.

a) The partition function is:

$$Z = e^{-\beta \cdot 0} + e^{-\beta \epsilon} + e^{-2\beta \epsilon} = 1 + e^{-\beta\epsilon} + e^{-2\beta\epsilon}$$

where $\beta = 1/(k_BT)$.

b) The Helmholtz free energy is:

$$F = -k_BT \ln\!\left(1 + e^{-\epsilon/(k_BT)} + e^{-2\epsilon/(k_BT)}\right)$$

c) Low-temperature limit ($k_BT \ll \epsilon$, so $\beta\epsilon \gg 1$):

$e^{-\beta\epsilon} \approx 0$, $e^{-2\beta\epsilon} \approx 0$, so $Z \approx 1$ and $F \approx -k_BT \ln 1 = 0$.

Interpretation: At low temperature, the system is almost certainly in the ground state ($E_1 = 0$). There is essentially one accessible state, so entropy is near zero and $F \approx U = 0$.

High-temperature limit ($k_BT \gg \epsilon$, so $\beta\epsilon \ll 1$):

All exponentials $\approx 1$, so $Z \approx 3$ and $F \approx -k_BT \ln 3$.

Interpretation: At high temperature, all three states are equally populated. The entropy is maximal: $S = k_B \ln 3$. The average energy is $\langle E \rangle \approx \epsilon$, and $F = U - TS \approx \epsilon - k_BT\ln 3$, which is dominated by the entropy term $-k_BT \ln 3$ at very high $T$.

2. Gibbs Free Energy for Ideal Gas Mixing ↓

Two ideal gases, A and B, are initially in separate containers at the same temperature $T$ and pressure $P$. Gas A has $n_A$ moles and gas B has $n_B$ moles. They are allowed to mix.

  1. Show that the entropy of mixing is $\Delta S_{\text{mix}} = -R(n_A \ln x_A + n_B \ln x_B)$, where $x_A = n_A/(n_A+n_B)$ and $x_B = n_B/(n_A+n_B)$ are mole fractions.
  2. Since the mixing is at constant $T$ and $P$ with no enthalpy change for ideal gases ($\Delta H_{\text{mix}} = 0$), compute $\Delta G_{\text{mix}}$.
  3. Show that $\Delta G_{\text{mix}} < 0$ for all non-trivial compositions, confirming mixing is always spontaneous.

a) Each gas undergoes a free expansion from its initial volume to the total volume. For an ideal gas expanding from $V_i$ to $V_f$ at constant $T$:

$$\Delta S_i = n_i R \ln \frac{V_f}{V_i}$$

Using $V_i/V_f = n_i/(n_A + n_B) = x_i$ (from the ideal gas law at constant $T, P$):

$$\Delta S_{\text{mix}} = n_A R \ln\frac{1}{x_A} + n_B R \ln\frac{1}{x_B} = -R(n_A \ln x_A + n_B \ln x_B)$$

Since $0 < x_A, x_B < 1$, the logarithms are negative, so $\Delta S_{\text{mix}} > 0$.

b) Since $\Delta H_{\text{mix}} = 0$ for ideal gases:

$$\Delta G_{\text{mix}} = \Delta H_{\text{mix}} - T\Delta S_{\text{mix}} = RT(n_A \ln x_A + n_B \ln x_B)$$

c) Since $0 < x_A < 1$ and $0 < x_B < 1$, both $\ln x_A < 0$ and $\ln x_B < 0$. Therefore $\Delta G_{\text{mix}} < 0$ for any non-trivial composition ($x_A \neq 0, 1$). Mixing is always spontaneous for ideal gases, driven entirely by the entropy of mixing.

3. Free Energy Minimization Criterion ↓

A system is in thermal contact with a heat bath at temperature $T$. Show that the condition "total entropy of system + reservoir is maximized" is equivalent to "Helmholtz free energy of the system is minimized."

The total entropy is $S_{\text{total}} = S_{\text{sys}} + S_{\text{res}}$.

When the system absorbs heat $\delta Q$ from the reservoir at constant temperature $T$:

$$\Delta S_{\text{res}} = -\frac{\delta Q}{T}$$

By the First Law (at constant $V$, no work), $\delta Q = \Delta U_{\text{sys}}$, so:

$$\Delta S_{\text{total}} = \Delta S_{\text{sys}} - \frac{\Delta U_{\text{sys}}}{T}$$

Multiply both sides by $-T$:

$$-T \Delta S_{\text{total}} = \Delta U_{\text{sys}} - T\Delta S_{\text{sys}} = \Delta F_{\text{sys}}$$

Therefore:

$$\Delta S_{\text{total}} = -\frac{\Delta F_{\text{sys}}}{T}$$

Maximizing $S_{\text{total}}$ requires $\Delta S_{\text{total}} \geq 0$, which is equivalent to $\Delta F_{\text{sys}} \leq 0$. At equilibrium, $S_{\text{total}}$ is at its maximum, so $F_{\text{sys}}$ is at its minimum. The two criteria are exactly equivalent.

4. Bogoliubov Inequality Proof ↓

Prove the Bogoliubov inequality: for any reference Hamiltonian $H_0$ with free energy $F_0 = -k_BT \ln Z_0$, the true free energy $F$ of a system with Hamiltonian $H$ satisfies:

$$F \leq F_0 + \langle H - H_0 \rangle_0$$

where $\langle \cdot \rangle_0$ denotes the thermal average with respect to $H_0$.

Hint: Use the convexity of the exponential function (Jensen's inequality).

Start from the definition of the free energy:

$$e^{-\beta F} = Z = \sum_i e^{-\beta E_i}$$

Write $H = H_0 + (H - H_0)$:

$$Z = \sum_i e^{-\beta E_i^{(0)}} e^{-\beta(E_i - E_i^{(0)})} = Z_0 \sum_i p_i^{(0)} e^{-\beta(E_i - E_i^{(0)})}$$

where $p_i^{(0)} = e^{-\beta E_i^{(0)}}/Z_0$ is the Boltzmann distribution for $H_0$. Therefore:

$$Z = Z_0 \left\langle e^{-\beta(H - H_0)} \right\rangle_0$$

Taking the logarithm:

$$-\beta F = \ln Z = \ln Z_0 + \ln\left\langle e^{-\beta(H - H_0)} \right\rangle_0$$

By Jensen's inequality ($\ln \langle e^X \rangle \geq \langle X \rangle$ since $e^x$ is convex):

$$\ln\left\langle e^{-\beta(H - H_0)} \right\rangle_0 \geq \left\langle -\beta(H - H_0) \right\rangle_0 = -\beta \langle H - H_0 \rangle_0$$

Therefore:

$$-\beta F \geq -\beta F_0 - \beta \langle H - H_0 \rangle_0$$

Dividing by $-\beta$ (which flips the inequality):

$$\boxed{F \leq F_0 + \langle H - H_0 \rangle_0}$$

Equality holds if and only if $H - H_0$ is a constant (i.e., the variance $\text{Var}_0(H - H_0) = 0$), which means the reference Hamiltonian is the true Hamiltonian up to an additive constant.

5. Mean-Field Variational Approximation for the Ising Model ↓

Consider the 2D Ising model on a square lattice ($z = 4$ nearest neighbors) with Hamiltonian $H = -J \sum_{\langle i,j \rangle} \sigma_i \sigma_j$ (no external field, $h = 0$).

  1. Using the factorized trial distribution $q(\{\sigma_i\}) = \prod_i q_i(\sigma_i)$ with $q_i(\sigma_i = +1) = \frac{1+m}{2}$, $q_i(\sigma_i = -1) = \frac{1-m}{2}$, compute the variational free energy $\mathcal{F}[q] = \langle H \rangle_q - TS_q$ as a function of $m$.
  2. Minimize $\mathcal{F}$ with respect to $m$ to derive the self-consistency equation.
  3. Determine the critical temperature $T_c$ within mean-field theory and compare to the exact Onsager result $T_c^{\text{exact}} = 2J/(k_B \ln(1 + \sqrt{2})) \approx 2.269 J/k_B$.

a) Under the factorized distribution, spins are independent, so $\langle \sigma_i \sigma_j \rangle_q = \langle \sigma_i \rangle_q \langle \sigma_j \rangle_q = m^2$. The number of nearest-neighbor bonds is $Nz/2$ (each bond counted once). Therefore:

$$\langle H \rangle_q = -J \cdot \frac{Nz}{2} \cdot m^2$$

The entropy of the factorized distribution is:

$$S_q = -Nk_B \left[\frac{1+m}{2}\ln\frac{1+m}{2} + \frac{1-m}{2}\ln\frac{1-m}{2}\right]$$

The variational free energy per spin is:

$$\frac{\mathcal{F}}{N} = -\frac{Jz}{2}m^2 + k_BT\left[\frac{1+m}{2}\ln\frac{1+m}{2} + \frac{1-m}{2}\ln\frac{1-m}{2}\right]$$

b) Setting $\frac{\partial \mathcal{F}/N}{\partial m} = 0$:

$$-Jzm + \frac{k_BT}{2}\ln\frac{1+m}{1-m} = 0$$

Using $\text{arctanh}(m) = \frac{1}{2}\ln\frac{1+m}{1-m}$:

$$\text{arctanh}(m) = \frac{Jz m}{k_BT} \quad \Rightarrow \quad m = \tanh\!\left(\frac{Jzm}{k_BT}\right)$$

This is the mean-field self-consistency equation.

c) The transition occurs when the slope of $\tanh(\beta Jzm)$ at $m = 0$ equals 1:

$$\left.\frac{d}{dm}\tanh(\beta Jzm)\right|_{m=0} = \beta_c Jz = 1 \implies T_c^{\text{MF}} = \frac{Jz}{k_B} = \frac{4J}{k_B}$$

Comparison: $T_c^{\text{MF}} = 4J/k_B \approx 4.0$, while $T_c^{\text{exact}} \approx 2.269 J/k_B$. Mean-field theory overestimates $T_c$ by about 76%. This is because the factorized approximation ignores correlations between spins, which in 2D are strong enough to significantly reduce the ordering temperature. The mean-field approximation becomes more accurate in higher dimensions (and is exact in $d = \infty$).

6. ELBO as Negative Variational Free Energy ↓

Consider a latent variable model with joint distribution $p(x, z) = p(x|z)p(z)$ and variational distribution $q(z)$.

  1. Show that the log-evidence decomposes as: $\log p(x) = \text{ELBO}(q) + D_{\text{KL}}(q(z) \| p(z|x))$.
  2. Show that the ELBO can be written in two equivalent forms:
    (i) $\text{ELBO} = \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(x, z)] - \mathbb{E}_q[\log q(z)]$
    (ii) $\text{ELBO} = \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(x|z)] - D_{\text{KL}}(q(z) \| p(z))$
  3. Identify the physics analogues of each term in form (ii).

a) Start from the definition of KL divergence:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(q(z) \| p(z|x)) = \mathbb{E}_q\left[\log \frac{q(z)}{p(z|x)}\right] = \mathbb{E}_q[\log q(z)] - \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(z|x)]$$

Using Bayes' rule, $p(z|x) = p(x,z)/p(x)$:

$$D_{\text{KL}} = \mathbb{E}_q[\log q(z)] - \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(x,z)] + \log p(x)$$

Rearranging:

$$\log p(x) = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(x,z)] - \mathbb{E}_q[\log q(z)]}_{\text{ELBO}} + D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p(z|x))$$

Since $D_{\text{KL}} \geq 0$, we have $\log p(x) \geq \text{ELBO}$.

b)(i) This is exactly the ELBO as defined above: $\text{ELBO} = \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(x,z)] - \mathbb{E}_q[\log q(z)]$.

(ii) Using $p(x,z) = p(x|z)p(z)$:

$$\text{ELBO} = \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(x|z)] + \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(z)] - \mathbb{E}_q[\log q(z)]$$

$$= \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(x|z)] - D_{\text{KL}}(q(z) \| p(z))$$

c) In the physics analogy (at $k_BT = 1$):

  • $-\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(x|z)]$ is the average energy $\langle E \rangle_q$ (reconstruction cost = energy of configuration)
  • $D_{\text{KL}}(q(z) \| p(z))$ is the negative entropy penalty: it measures how far $q$ deviates from the "equilibrium" prior, analogous to $-TS_q$ measuring how far the trial distribution is from the uniform (maximum entropy) state
  • The ELBO = $-\mathcal{F}$ (negative variational free energy): maximizing ELBO = minimizing free energy
7. Thermodynamic Integration Derivation ↓

Consider the interpolated Hamiltonian $H(\lambda) = (1-\lambda)H_0 + \lambda H_1$.

  1. Show that $\frac{\partial F(\lambda)}{\partial \lambda} = \left\langle \frac{\partial H(\lambda)}{\partial \lambda} \right\rangle_\lambda = \langle H_1 - H_0 \rangle_\lambda$.
  2. Derive the thermodynamic integration formula by integrating from $\lambda = 0$ to $\lambda = 1$.
  3. Explain why this formula is exact, not an approximation, even though we use it with finite sampling in practice.

a) The free energy at parameter $\lambda$ is $F(\lambda) = -k_BT \ln Z(\lambda)$ where $Z(\lambda) = \sum_i e^{-\beta H_i(\lambda)}$.

Differentiate:

$$\frac{\partial F}{\partial \lambda} = -k_BT \frac{1}{Z(\lambda)} \frac{\partial Z(\lambda)}{\partial \lambda}$$

$$= -k_BT \frac{1}{Z(\lambda)} \sum_i \left(-\beta \frac{\partial H_i(\lambda)}{\partial \lambda}\right) e^{-\beta H_i(\lambda)}$$

$$= \sum_i \frac{e^{-\beta H_i(\lambda)}}{Z(\lambda)} \frac{\partial H_i(\lambda)}{\partial \lambda} = \left\langle \frac{\partial H(\lambda)}{\partial \lambda} \right\rangle_\lambda$$

Since $\partial H(\lambda)/\partial \lambda = H_1 - H_0$:

$$\frac{\partial F}{\partial \lambda} = \langle H_1 - H_0 \rangle_\lambda$$

b) Integrating both sides from 0 to 1:

$$F(1) - F(0) = \int_0^1 \langle H_1 - H_0 \rangle_\lambda \, d\lambda$$

$$\boxed{\Delta F = F_1 - F_0 = \int_0^1 \langle H_1 - H_0 \rangle_\lambda \, d\lambda}$$

c) The formula is exact because it is derived from the identity $\partial F/\partial \lambda = \langle \partial H/\partial \lambda \rangle_\lambda$ without any approximation. The only approximations in practice are: (1) discretizing the integral over $\lambda$ (numerical quadrature), and (2) estimating each $\langle H_1 - H_0 \rangle_\lambda$ from a finite number of samples (statistical error). Both sources of error can be systematically reduced.

8. VAE Loss as Free Energy Minimization ↓

The Variational Autoencoder (VAE) trains an encoder $q_\phi(z|x)$ and decoder $p_\theta(x|z)$ by maximizing the ELBO:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta, \phi; x) = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z))$$

  1. Identify this as a variational free energy by mapping each term to its thermodynamic counterpart. What plays the role of energy? Temperature? Entropy?
  2. In a standard VAE with Gaussian prior $p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ and Gaussian encoder $q_\phi(z|x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu_\phi(x), \text{diag}(\sigma^2_\phi(x)))$, derive the closed-form expression for the KL term.
  3. Explain the "posterior collapse" phenomenon, where $q_\phi(z|x) \approx p(z)$ for all $x$, in terms of free energy. Which term dominates and why?

a) Rewrite the loss as:

$$-\mathcal{L} = \underbrace{-\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log p_\theta(x|z)]}_{\text{Energy } \langle E \rangle_q} + \underbrace{D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z))}_{-\text{Entropy term}}$$

The mapping is: $-\log p_\theta(x|z)$ plays the role of energy (it measures how poorly configuration $z$ explains observation $x$). The KL term acts as the entropic penalty $-TS_q$ (it penalizes $q$ for deviating from the "thermal equilibrium" prior $p(z)$). Temperature is implicitly $T = 1$. Minimizing $-\mathcal{L}$ is minimizing variational free energy.

b) For $d$-dimensional Gaussians $q = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \text{diag}(\sigma^2))$ and $p = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=1}^{d}\left(\mu_j^2 + \sigma_j^2 - \ln \sigma_j^2 - 1\right)$$

Derivation: Using the general formula for KL between two Gaussians:

$$D_{\text{KL}} = \frac{1}{2}\left[\text{tr}(\Sigma_2^{-1}\Sigma_1) + (\mu_2 - \mu_1)^T \Sigma_2^{-1}(\mu_2 - \mu_1) - d + \ln\frac{|\Sigma_2|}{|\Sigma_1|}\right]$$

With $\mu_1 = \mu$, $\Sigma_1 = \text{diag}(\sigma^2)$, $\mu_2 = 0$, $\Sigma_2 = I$:

$$D_{\text{KL}} = \frac{1}{2}\left[\sum_j \sigma_j^2 + \sum_j \mu_j^2 - d - \sum_j \ln\sigma_j^2\right] = \frac{1}{2}\sum_j(\mu_j^2 + \sigma_j^2 - \ln\sigma_j^2 - 1)$$

c) Posterior collapse occurs when the KL term dominates over the reconstruction term during early training. The decoder learns to model $p(x)$ without using $z$ (it ignores the latent code), so the optimal $q_\phi(z|x)$ is just the prior $p(z)$, giving $D_{\text{KL}} = 0$. In free energy language, the entropy penalty is so strong relative to the energy gain that the system is in a "high-temperature" phase where the trial distribution equals the thermal equilibrium (prior). The latent variables carry no information — analogous to a paramagnetic phase where thermal fluctuations destroy all order.

9. Jarzynski Equality and Free Energy ↓

The Jarzynski equality (1997) relates the free energy difference $\Delta F$ between two equilibrium states to the work $W$ done during non-equilibrium processes connecting them:

$$\langle e^{-\beta W} \rangle = e^{-\beta \Delta F}$$

where the average is over many repetitions of the (possibly irreversible) process.

  1. Show that Jensen's inequality applied to the Jarzynski equality recovers the Second Law: $\langle W \rangle \geq \Delta F$.
  2. In a hypothetical experiment, you rapidly stretch a polymer 1000 times, measuring the work $W_i$ each time. Given $\beta = 1/(k_BT)$, write the estimator for $\Delta F$.
  3. Why is this estimator problematic in practice? (Hint: consider the distribution of $e^{-\beta W}$ when $W$ has large variance.)
  4. Connect the Jarzynski equality to annealed importance sampling (AIS). What does $W$ correspond to in the AIS framework?

a) Jensen's inequality states that for a convex function $f$, $f(\langle X \rangle) \leq \langle f(X) \rangle$. The exponential is convex, so:

$$e^{-\beta \langle W \rangle} \leq \langle e^{-\beta W} \rangle = e^{-\beta \Delta F}$$

Taking the logarithm (monotonically increasing):

$$-\beta \langle W \rangle \leq -\beta \Delta F \implies \langle W \rangle \geq \Delta F$$

The average work done on a system is at least as large as the free energy difference. The excess $\langle W \rangle - \Delta F \geq 0$ is the dissipated work, which is converted to heat and increases the entropy of the universe. For reversible processes, $\langle W \rangle = \Delta F$ exactly.

b) The estimator is:

$$\Delta F = -k_BT \ln\left(\frac{1}{1000}\sum_{i=1}^{1000} e^{-\beta W_i}\right)$$

c) This estimator is dominated by rare low-work realizations. When the process is far from equilibrium, $W$ has large variance, and $e^{-\beta W}$ has extremely heavy tails. A few runs with anomalously low $W$ (corresponding to rare, nearly reversible trajectories) dominate the average, while most runs contribute negligibly. This makes the estimator have very high variance and converge extremely slowly — exponentially many samples may be needed for accurate estimation.

d) In AIS, the role of $W$ is played by the log importance weight: $-\beta W \leftrightarrow \log w$, where $w$ is the accumulated importance weight along an annealing chain. The Jarzynski equality becomes $\langle w \rangle = Z_1/Z_0$, and the AIS estimate of $\log(Z_1/Z_0)$ is exactly the free energy difference estimator. The practical challenges are identical: rare, high-weight samples can dominate the estimate.

10. Designing an Energy-Based Model Training Objective ↓

Consider an energy-based model $p_\theta(x) = e^{-E_\theta(x)}/Z_\theta$ where $E_\theta$ is a neural network.

  1. Write the gradient of the log-likelihood $\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x)$ and show it involves an intractable expectation under $p_\theta$.
  2. Contrastive Divergence (CD-$k$) approximates this expectation by running $k$ steps of MCMC starting from the data. Explain why CD-1 does not minimize the log-likelihood exactly, and what it minimizes instead.
  3. Noise Contrastive Estimation (NCE) avoids computing $Z_\theta$ entirely by training a classifier to distinguish data from noise. If the noise distribution is $p_n(x)$ and we define $\log p_\theta(x) = -E_\theta(x) - c$ (treating $c = \log Z_\theta$ as a learned parameter), write the NCE objective.
  4. Relate the NCE approach to the free energy framework: what corresponds to the partition function, and how does NCE avoid the intractable computation?

a) The log-likelihood is $\log p_\theta(x) = -E_\theta(x) - \log Z_\theta$. Taking the gradient:

$$\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x) = -\nabla_\theta E_\theta(x) - \nabla_\theta \log Z_\theta$$

The partition function gradient is:

$$\nabla_\theta \log Z_\theta = \frac{1}{Z_\theta}\sum_{x'} (-\nabla_\theta E_\theta(x')) e^{-E_\theta(x')} = -\mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}[\nabla_\theta E_\theta(x')]$$

Therefore:

$$\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x) = -\nabla_\theta E_\theta(x) + \mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}[\nabla_\theta E_\theta(x')]$$

The first term "pushes down" the energy of observed data. The second term "pushes up" the energy of samples from the model. The second expectation is intractable because sampling from $p_\theta$ requires MCMC.

b) CD-1 replaces the expectation $\mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}[\nabla_\theta E_\theta]$ with a single Gibbs step starting from the data point $x$. This biased estimator does not follow the gradient of the log-likelihood. Instead, Bengio and Delalleau (2009) showed that CD approximately minimizes a different objective related to the difference of two KL divergences. The bias decreases as $k$ increases (CD-$k$ with larger $k$ approaches the true gradient), but never vanishes for finite $k$.

c) NCE trains a binary classifier $D(x)$ to distinguish data ($y=1$) from noise ($y=0$). With $\nu$ noise samples per data sample, the NCE objective is:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{NCE}}(\theta, c) = \mathbb{E}_{p_{\text{data}}}[\log \sigma(-E_\theta(x) - c - \log(\nu \cdot p_n(x)))]$$

$$+ \nu \cdot \mathbb{E}_{p_n}[\log(1 - \sigma(-E_\theta(x) - c - \log(\nu \cdot p_n(x))))]$$

where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function and $c$ is a learned estimate of $\log Z_\theta$.

d) In the free energy framework, $Z_\theta$ is the partition function that normalizes the Boltzmann distribution $p_\theta$. Computing $Z_\theta$ requires summing $e^{-E_\theta(x)}$ over all possible $x$ — this is the intractable free energy computation. NCE sidesteps this by: (1) treating $\log Z_\theta$ as a free parameter $c$ to be learned, and (2) replacing the generative modeling problem with a discriminative one where the normalization constant is implicitly determined by the requirement that $p_\theta$ equals $p_{\text{data}}$ at the optimum. This is analogous to computing free energy differences (which are tractable) rather than absolute free energies (which are not).
