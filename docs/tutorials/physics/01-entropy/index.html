01. Thermodynamic Entropy

Entropy in physics measures disorder, energy dispersal, and the direction of time. Understanding it illuminates why Shannon chose this name for information entropy.

Theory Code Exercises

Clausius and Heat Engines

Rudolf Clausius introduced entropy in 1865 while studying heat engines. He noticed that heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold, never the reverse, and sought to quantify this asymmetry.

Thermodynamic Definition

For a reversible process at temperature $T$: $$dS = \frac{\delta Q_{\text{rev}}}{T}$$ where $\delta Q_{\text{rev}}$ is the heat absorbed reversibly.

The total entropy change in any process is:

$$\Delta S = \int \frac{\delta Q_{\text{rev}}}{T}$$

The Second Law

The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that the total entropy of an isolated system never decreases:

$$\Delta S_{\text{universe}} \geq 0$$

Equality holds only for reversible processes. All real processes are irreversible and increase total entropy.

The Arrow of Time

Entropy gives time a direction. The past has lower entropy than the future. This is why we remember the past but not the future, and why eggs break but don't unbreak.

Boltzmann's Statistical View

Ludwig Boltzmann connected entropy to microscopic states. If $\Omega$ is the number of microstates consistent with a macrostate:

Boltzmann Entropy

$$S = k_B \ln \Omega$$ where $k_B \approx 1.38 \times 10^{-23}$ J/K is Boltzmann's constant.

This formula is carved on Boltzmann's tombstone. It reveals entropy as a measure of how many ways a system can be arranged—our ignorance of its exact microstate.

Why Logarithm?
1
Two independent systems have $\Omega_{\text{total}} = \Omega_1 \cdot \Omega_2$
2
Entropy should be additive: $S_{\text{total}} = S_1 + S_2$
3
Only logarithm converts multiplication to addition!

Gibbs Entropy

When the system can be in various microstates with probabilities $p_i$:

$$S = -k_B \sum_i p_i \ln p_i$$

This is identical in form to Shannon entropy (with different constants)! When Shannon developed information theory, von Neumann reportedly told him to call it "entropy" because "no one really knows what entropy really is."

Connection to Information

Physical entropy and information entropy are deeply connected. Erasing information necessarily increases physical entropy (Landauer's principle), setting fundamental limits on computation.

Examples

Entropy of Mixing

When two gases mix, entropy increases even with no heat transfer. Initially, the system is "ordered" (gas A on left, gas B on right). After mixing, each molecule could be anywhere—more microstates, higher entropy.

$$\Delta S_{\text{mix}} = -nR(x_A \ln x_A + x_B \ln x_B)$$

where $x_A, x_B$ are mole fractions—again, the same form as Shannon entropy!

Heat Transfer

When heat $Q$ flows from hot (temperature $T_H$) to cold (temperature $T_C$):

$$\Delta S = -\frac{Q}{T_H} + \frac{Q}{T_C} = Q\left(\frac{1}{T_C} - \frac{1}{T_H}\right) > 0$$

Entropy increases because $T_C < T_H$. This is why heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold.
