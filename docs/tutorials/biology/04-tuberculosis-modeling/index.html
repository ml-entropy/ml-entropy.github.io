04. Tuberculosis Metabolic Modeling

Genome-scale metabolic modeling transforms our understanding of Mycobacterium tuberculosis—the pathogen behind one of humanity's deadliest diseases. By encoding the bacterium's entire metabolism as a mathematical system, we can predict growth, identify drug targets, and simulate treatment strategies computationally, all before stepping into a wet lab.

Tuberculosis: The Challenge

Tuberculosis (TB) remains one of the most devastating infectious diseases in human history. Despite the availability of antibiotics since the mid-20th century, TB continues to claim approximately 1.5 million lives per year, making it the leading cause of death from a single infectious agent—surpassing even HIV/AIDS.

The causative agent, Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb), is an unusually formidable pathogen. Several biological features make it extraordinarily difficult to treat:

  • Slow growth: Mtb has a doubling time of ~20 hours (compared to ~20 minutes for E. coli). Standard treatment requires 6–9 months of multi-drug therapy.
  • Dormancy: Mtb can enter a non-replicating persistent state inside granulomas, rendering it tolerant to most antibiotics that target active growth processes.
  • Complex cell wall: The mycobacterial cell envelope contains mycolic acids—long-chain fatty acids (C60–C90) that form a waxy, nearly impermeable barrier. This wall blocks drug entry and is unlike any structure found in humans.
  • Intracellular lifestyle: Mtb survives and replicates inside human macrophages, the very immune cells meant to destroy it.

Traditional drug discovery against TB is painfully slow: screening compound libraries, testing in culture, validating in animal models, then clinical trials. The entire pipeline takes 10–15 years and billions of dollars per approved drug. Computational metabolic modeling offers a complementary approach: build a mathematical representation of the pathogen's metabolism, then use it to predict which genes and reactions are essential for survival, thereby identifying candidate drug targets in silico.

TB by the Numbers
  • 10.6 million new TB cases globally in 2022 (WHO)
  • 1.3 million TB deaths among HIV-negative people; 167,000 among HIV-positive people
  • Multi-drug resistant TB (MDR-TB): ~450,000 new cases/year, resistant to isoniazid and rifampicin
  • Treatment duration: 6 months for drug-sensitive TB; up to 20 months for MDR-TB
  • Only 3 new TB drugs approved in the last 50 years (bedaquiline, delamanid, pretomanid)

The iNJ661 Model

The iNJ661 model, published by Jamshidi and Palsson in 2007, was the first comprehensive genome-scale metabolic model (GSMM) of M. tuberculosis strain H37Rv—the most widely studied laboratory reference strain. The model name follows the convention: "i" for in silico, "NJ" for the authors' initials (Neema Jamshidi), and "661" for the number of genes included.

The model's scope is substantial:

  • 661 genes out of ~4,000 total in the Mtb genome
  • 939 reactions (including transport and exchange reactions)
  • 828 metabolites across two compartments

Building this model required integrating data from multiple sources: the Mtb H37Rv genome annotation (Cole et al., 1998), the KEGG pathway database, the TubercuList database (a curated repository of Mtb gene annotations), and extensive literature curation of biochemical evidence for each reaction.

Genome-Scale Metabolic Model (GSMM)

A GSMM is a mathematically structured representation of all known metabolic reactions in an organism. It is reconstructed bottom-up through a multi-step process:

  1. Genome annotation: Identify all open reading frames (ORFs) and assign putative functions.
  2. Reaction database mapping: Link gene functions to metabolic reactions using databases (KEGG, MetaCyc, BRENDA).
  3. Gap filling: Add reactions not yet linked to genes but required for the network to produce known biomass components.
  4. Stoichiometric balancing: Ensure mass and charge balance for every reaction.
  5. Gene-Protein-Reaction (GPR) rules: Define Boolean rules mapping genes to proteins to reactions (e.g., if gene A AND gene B are active, reaction R can carry flux).

The result is encoded as a stoichiometric matrix $S$ together with flux bounds and a biomass objective function. The model is then analyzed using constraint-based methods such as FBA and FVA.

Model Structure

The mathematical core of iNJ661 is the stoichiometric matrix $S$, with dimensions $828 \times 939$—each row corresponds to a metabolite, each column to a reaction, and each entry $S_{ij}$ is the stoichiometric coefficient of metabolite $i$ in reaction $j$ (negative for substrates, positive for products).

The model partitions metabolites into two compartments:

  • Cytoplasm (c): The intracellular space where most metabolic reactions occur
  • Extracellular (e): The environment from which nutrients are imported and to which waste products are secreted

Transport reactions connect the two compartments, and exchange reactions allow metabolites to enter or leave the system. The key metabolic pathways captured in the model include:

  • Central carbon metabolism: Glycolysis, pentose phosphate pathway, TCA cycle
  • Energy generation: Oxidative phosphorylation, the electron transport chain
  • Lipid metabolism: Fatty acid synthesis (FAS-I and FAS-II), mycolic acid biosynthesis
  • Cell wall assembly: Peptidoglycan, arabinogalactan, lipoarabinomannan
  • Amino acid and nucleotide biosynthesis

FBA for Growth Prediction

Flux Balance Analysis (FBA) predicts the steady-state metabolic flux distribution by solving a linear program. For the iNJ661 model, the standard formulation is:

$$\max_{\mathbf{v}} \; v_{\text{biomass}}$$ $$\text{subject to:} \quad S \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$$ $$\mathbf{l} \leq \mathbf{v} \leq \mathbf{u}$$

where $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^{939}$ is the flux vector, $S \in \mathbb{R}^{828 \times 939}$ is the stoichiometric matrix, and $\mathbf{l}, \mathbf{u}$ are lower and upper bounds on each flux. The objective is to maximize the flux through the biomass reaction—a pseudo-reaction that consumes all the precursors needed to build new cell material.

The Mtb biomass objective function is more complex than a typical bacterial model. In addition to standard components (amino acids, nucleotides, lipids), it includes Mtb-specific components:

  • Mycolic acids (alpha-, methoxy-, and keto-mycolates)
  • Mycocerosates (phthiocerol dimycocerosates, PDIMs)
  • Sulfolipids (SL-1, important virulence factors)
  • Arabinogalactan and peptidoglycan (cell wall polymers)

A key validation step is comparing in silico growth predictions against experimental data across different carbon sources. The table below shows how the model performs:

In Silico Gene Knockouts

One of the most powerful applications of genome-scale models is the ability to simulate gene deletions computationally. The procedure is straightforward: for each gene $g$ in the model, identify all reactions that require $g$ (using GPR rules), set the flux bounds of those reactions to zero, and re-solve the FBA problem.

Gene Knockout Simulation

For a gene $g$ with associated reactions $\text{reactions}(g) = \{j_1, j_2, \ldots, j_k\}$:

Step 1. Set flux bounds to zero for all dependent reactions:

$$\text{KO gene } g: \quad v_j = 0 \quad \text{for all } j \in \text{reactions}(g)$$

Step 2. Solve the modified FBA problem to obtain the knockout growth rate $Z^*_{\text{KO}}$.

Step 3. Classify the gene:

$$\text{Essential if: } Z^*_{\text{KO}} = 0 \;\;(\text{or } < \epsilon \text{ for numerical tolerance})$$

$$\text{Non-essential if: } Z^*_{\text{KO}} > \epsilon$$

A gene is deemed essential if its knockout causes the predicted growth rate to drop to zero (or below a small threshold $\epsilon \approx 10^{-6}$). The biological interpretation: the cell cannot grow without this gene, so the corresponding enzyme is a potential drug target.

Applying this procedure to all 661 genes in iNJ661 yields approximately 200 predicted essential genes (~30% of metabolic genes). This prediction can be validated against experimental data from transposon mutagenesis studies (Sassetti et al., 2003; Griffin et al., 2011), which systematically disrupt genes and measure fitness.

Metric Value Interpretation
Predicted essential genes ~200 Genes whose knockout abolishes growth in silico
True positives (TP) ~130 Predicted essential AND experimentally essential
False positives (FP) ~70 Predicted essential but experimentally non-essential
Sensitivity (Recall) ~78% Fraction of truly essential genes correctly predicted
Specificity ~85% Fraction of truly non-essential genes correctly predicted

The ~78% sensitivity means the model catches most essential genes, while the ~85% specificity means it does not generate excessive false alarms. The false positives often arise from isozymes (backup enzymes not fully captured in GPR rules) or alternative pathways absent from the model. False negatives can result from the model's inability to capture regulatory effects.

Drug Target Identification

Not every essential gene is a good drug target. A useful drug must kill (or inhibit) the pathogen without harming the host. The strategy is a three-step computational pipeline:

Drug Target Identification Pipeline
1
FBA Gene Essentiality Screen: Perform single-gene knockouts on the Mtb model (as described above). Identify the set $E_{\text{Mtb}}$ of all essential genes.
2
Filter Human Orthologs: Using BLAST or ortholog databases (e.g., InParanoid, OrthoMCL), identify Mtb genes that have close homologs in the human genome. Remove these from the candidate set to avoid off-target toxicity. Define $H$ as the set of Mtb genes with human orthologs. The filtered set is $E_{\text{Mtb}} \setminus H$.
3
Prioritize Unique Pathways: Among the remaining candidates, prioritize genes in pathways that are unique to mycobacteria and absent in humans: mycolic acid biosynthesis, cell wall assembly (arabinogalactan, LAM), and the glyoxylate shunt. These offer the highest selectivity for anti-TB drugs.

The beauty of this approach is that it connects computational predictions to real drug mechanisms. Several known TB drug targets emerge from this pipeline:

kasA Beta-ketoacyl-ACP synthase FAS-II (mycolic acid) Thiolactomycin (experimental) Yes No

The first five genes in the table are all correctly predicted as essential by FBA, lack human orthologs, and are targets of known anti-TB drugs. This retrospective validation demonstrates that the computational pipeline can identify clinically validated drug targets. The real value, of course, lies in predicting novel targets among the remaining essential genes without human orthologs.

Condition-Specific Modeling

Tuberculosis infection is not a single state. Mtb encounters dramatically different metabolic environments depending on the stage of infection:

  • Aerobic (active TB): During active replication in well-oxygenated lung tissue, Mtb uses oxidative phosphorylation for energy generation and can metabolize diverse carbon sources.
  • Hypoxic (latent TB): Inside granulomas—walled-off immune structures—oxygen tension drops dramatically. Mtb enters a dormant, non-replicating persistent state. It shifts from aerobic respiration to anaerobic pathways, using nitrate as an alternative electron acceptor.
  • Nutrient-limited: Within macrophage phagosomes, Mtb faces limited access to carbon sources and must rely on host-derived lipids (cholesterol, fatty acids) rather than sugars.

These conditions are modeled by adjusting the flux bounds on uptake reactions:

Condition-Specific Constraints

Aerobic conditions: Standard oxygen uptake allowed.

$$-20 \leq v_{\text{O}_2} \leq 0 \quad \text{(O}_2\text{ import)}$$

Hypoxic conditions: Block oxygen, enable nitrate reduction.

$$v_{\text{O}_2} = 0, \quad -10 \leq v_{\text{NO}_3^-} \leq 0$$

Nutrient-limited conditions: Reduce carbon source uptake, switch to fatty acids.

$$v_{\text{glucose}} = 0, \quad -1 \leq v_{\text{cholesterol}} \leq 0$$

Under each condition, we can apply both FBA and Flux Variability Analysis (FVA). FVA computes the minimum and maximum flux through every reaction while maintaining optimal (or near-optimal) growth. This reveals the metabolic flexibility available to the bacterium:

  • Reactions with wide flux ranges under FVA have metabolic alternatives—blocking them alone may not be sufficient to kill the bacterium.
  • Reactions with narrow flux ranges (especially if $v_{\min} \approx v_{\max} > 0$) are metabolic bottlenecks—the cell has no alternative, making them ideal drug targets.

A critical finding from condition-specific modeling: some genes are essential only under specific conditions. For example, genes in the nitrate reductase pathway become essential under hypoxic conditions but are dispensable during aerobic growth. This has profound implications for treating latent TB, which current drug regimens struggle to eradicate.

Clinical Relevance

Condition-specific modeling explains why standard TB drugs (isoniazid, rifampicin) are less effective against latent TB: they primarily target pathways active during aerobic growth. By identifying genes essential specifically under hypoxic conditions, the model can suggest targets for drugs that would kill dormant bacilli—a critical unmet need in TB therapy.

Mycolic Acid Pathway Analysis

Mycolic acids are the hallmark of mycobacteria. These extraordinarily long-chain fatty acids (C60–C90) are essential for cell envelope integrity, impermeability, and virulence. They are entirely absent in human cells, making the mycolic acid biosynthetic pathway an ideal target for selective anti-TB drugs.

The pathway involves two fatty acid synthase systems working in tandem:

  • FAS-I (a single multifunctional enzyme): Synthesizes medium-chain fatty acids (C16–C26) from acetyl-CoA. This is similar to the mammalian fatty acid synthase.
  • FAS-II (a multi-enzyme system): Extends FAS-I products to very long chains (C48–C62), forming the meromycolic acid chain. FAS-II is unique to bacteria and is the target of several key TB drugs. Key enzymes: InhA, KasA, KasB, MabA, HadABC.
  • Condensation: Meromycolic acid is condensed with an alpha-branch (C22–C26) by Pks13 to form the final mycolic acid.

FBA analysis of this pathway reveals what happens when individual steps are blocked:

Blocked Step Growth Rate % of Wild Type Biological Effect Clinical Analog
None (wild type) 0.053 100% Normal growth —
FAS-I (all reactions) 0.000 0% No fatty acid precursors No approved drug
InhA (FAS-II) 0.000 0% No meromycolic acid elongation Isoniazid
KasA (FAS-II) 0.000 0% No beta-ketoacyl chain extension Thiolactomycin
Pks13 (condensation) 0.000 0% Cannot assemble final mycolic acid TAM16 (experimental)
Mycolyl transferase 0.000 0% Mycolic acid not attached to cell wall —

Every step in the mycolic acid pathway is essential—there is no redundancy. This is exactly what makes isoniazid (which targets InhA after activation by KatG) and ethionamide (which directly inhibits InhA) such effective first-line drugs. The FBA analysis confirms the biological reality: blocking any step in mycolic acid biosynthesis is lethal to the bacterium.

This analysis also explains why resistance mutations in inhA or katG lead to isoniazid resistance: the pathway remains essential, but the drug can no longer inhibit the target enzyme. The metabolic model predicts essentiality; resistance arises from changes in drug-target binding, which is outside the model's scope.

Beyond iNJ661: Updated Models

Since iNJ661 (2007), several updated Mtb models have been published, including iNJ661v (refined version), GSMN-TB, and sMtb (2018, with 1,192 genes and 1,555 reactions). Each new reconstruction improves coverage and accuracy. The principles and methods demonstrated here apply to all of them—the workflow (load model, run FBA, perform knockouts, change conditions) remains the same, only the model file changes.

Growth on Different Carbon Sources

def test_carbon_source(model, carbon_exchange, uptake_rate=-10.0):
    """Test growth on a specific carbon source.

    Args:
        model: COBRA model
        carbon_exchange: Exchange reaction ID for the carbon source
        uptake_rate: Maximum uptake rate (negative = import)

    Returns:
        Growth rate (float)
    """
    with model:  # context manager restores original state
        # Close all carbon source uptakes
        carbon_exchanges = [
            'EX_glc__D_e', 'EX_glyc_e', 'EX_ac_e',
            'EX_pyr_e', 'EX_lac__L_e', 'EX_succ_e',
        ]
        for ex in carbon_exchanges:
            if ex in model.reactions:
                model.reactions.get_by_id(ex).lower_bound = 0

        # Open the desired carbon source
        if carbon_exchange in model.reactions:
            model.reactions.get_by_id(carbon_exchange).lower_bound = uptake_rate

        solution = model.optimize()
        if solution.status == 'optimal':
            return solution.objective_value
        return 0.0

# Test multiple carbon sources
carbon_sources = {

    'Glycerol': 'EX_glyc_e',
    'Acetate':  'EX_ac_e',
    'Pyruvate': 'EX_pyr_e',
    'Succinate':'EX_succ_e',
}

print(f"{'Carbon Source':<15} {'Growth Rate (h^-1)':>20} {'Can Grow?':>12}")

Single Gene Knockout Analysis

# Knock out a single gene and observe the effect on growth

def knockout_gene(model, gene_id):
    """Simulate a single gene knockout.

    Args:
        model: COBRA model
        gene_id: ID of the gene to knock out

    Returns:
        dict with knockout results
    """
    with model:
        gene = model.genes.get_by_id(gene_id)
        gene.knock_out()

        solution = model.optimize()
        growth = solution.objective_value if solution.status == 'optimal' else 0.0

        # Identify which reactions were disabled
        disabled = [rxn.id for rxn in gene.reactions
                    if rxn.bounds == (0, 0)]

    return {
        'gene': gene_id,
        'growth_rate': growth,
        'is_essential': growth < 1e-6,
        'reactions_disabled': disabled,
        'n_reactions_affected': len(gene.reactions),
    }

# Example: knock out inhA (isoniazid target)
result = knockout_gene(model, 'Rv1484')  # inhA gene ID in Mtb
print(f"Gene: {result['gene']}")
print(f"Growth after KO: {result['growth_rate']:.6f}")
print(f"Essential: {result['is_essential']}")
print(f"Reactions affected: {result['n_reactions_affected']}")
print(f"Reactions disabled: {result['reactions_disabled']}")

def simulate_drug_effect(model, target_genes, drug_name="Drug"):
    """Simulate drug effect by knocking out target gene(s).

    Args:
        model: COBRA model
        target_genes: list of gene IDs to knock out (drug targets)
        drug_name: name of the drug being simulated

    Returns:
        dict with simulation results
    """
    with model:
        wt_solution = model.optimize()
        wt_growth = wt_solution.objective_value

    with model:
        for gid in target_genes:
            if gid in model.genes:
                model.genes.get_by_id(gid).knock_out()
        ko_solution = model.optimize()
        ko_growth = ko_solution.objective_value if ko_solution.status == 'optimal' else 0.0

    return {
        'drug': drug_name,
        'targets': target_genes,
        'wt_growth': wt_growth,
        'ko_growth': ko_growth,
        'growth_ratio': ko_growth / wt_growth if wt_growth > 0 else 0,
        'is_lethal': ko_growth < 1e-6,
    }

# Simulate known TB drugs
drug_simulations = [
    (['Rv1484'],         'Isoniazid (InhA)'),
    (['Rv2245'],         'Thiolactomycin (KasA)'),
    (['Rv3795'],         'Ethambutol (EmbB)'),
    (['Rv3423c'],        'D-cycloserine (Alr)'),
    (['Rv1484', 'Rv2245'], 'INH + Thiolactomycin (combo)'),
]

print(f"{'Drug':<30} {'Target Growth':>14} {'% of WT':>10} {'Lethal?':>10}")
print("-" * 67)
for targets, name in drug_simulations:
    result = simulate_drug_effect(model, targets, name)

Condition-Specific Modeling

def model_condition(model, condition_name, constraints):
    """Model a specific growth condition by adjusting uptake bounds.

    Args:
        model: COBRA model
        condition_name: descriptive name
        constraints: dict of {reaction_id: (lower_bound, upper_bound)}

    Returns:
        dict with condition-specific results
    """
    with model:
        for rxn_id, (lb, ub) in constraints.items():
            if rxn_id in model.reactions:
                rxn = model.reactions.get_by_id(rxn_id)
                rxn.lower_bound = lb
                rxn.upper_bound = ub

        solution = model.optimize()
        growth = solution.objective_value if solution.status == 'optimal' else 0.0

        # Perform FVA to assess metabolic flexibility
        if growth > 1e-6:
            fva = flux_variability_analysis(
                model,
                fraction_of_optimum=0.9,  # allow 90% of max growth
            )
            fva['range'] = fva['maximum'] - fva['minimum']
            n_flexible = (fva['range'] > 1e-6).sum()
            n_fixed = (fva['range'] <= 1e-6).sum()
        else:
            n_flexible, n_fixed = 0, 0
            fva = None

    return {
        'condition': condition_name,
        'growth_rate': growth,
        'n_flexible_reactions': n_flexible,
        'n_fixed_reactions': n_fixed,
        'fva': fva,
    }

Condition-Specific Essential Genes

def find_condition_essential_genes(model, constraints):
    """Find genes essential under specific conditions.

    Args:
        model: COBRA model
        constraints: dict of flux constraints for the condition

    Returns:
        set of essential gene IDs
    """
    essential = set()
    with model:
        # Apply condition constraints
        for rxn_id, (lb, ub) in constraints.items():
            if rxn_id in model.reactions:
                rxn = model.reactions.get_by_id(rxn_id)
                rxn.lower_bound = lb
                rxn.upper_bound = ub

        # Check if growth is possible under this condition
        base_solution = model.optimize()
        if base_solution.status != 'optimal' or base_solution.objective_value < 1e-6:
            print("  No growth under this condition.")
            return essential

        # Screen all genes
        results = single_gene_deletion(model)
        results['is_essential'] = results['growth'] < 1e-6

        for idx, row in results.iterrows():
            if row['is_essential']:
                for gid in row['ids']:
                    essential.add(gid)

    return essential

Synthetic Lethality Analysis

from cobra.flux_analysis import double_gene_deletion

def find_synthetic_lethal_pairs(model, gene_list=None, max_pairs=100):
    """Find pairs of non-essential genes whose combined knockout is lethal.

    Args:
        model: COBRA model
        gene_list: list of gene IDs to screen (default: all non-essential genes)
        max_pairs: maximum number of pairs to report

    Returns:
        DataFrame of synthetic lethal pairs
    """
    if gene_list is None:
        # First find non-essential genes
        single_results = single_gene_deletion(model)
        single_results['is_essential'] = single_results['growth'] < 1e-6
        non_essential_ids = []
        for idx, row in single_results.iterrows():
            if not row['is_essential']:
                for gid in row['ids']:
                    non_essential_ids.append(gid)
        gene_list = non_essential_ids

    print(f"Screening {len(gene_list)} non-essential genes for synthetic lethality...")
    print(f"Total pairs: {len(gene_list) * (len(gene_list)-1) // 2}")

    # Use COBRApy's double_gene_deletion
    # For large models, screen a subset for tractability
    if len(gene_list) > 50:
        print(f"Subsetting to first 50 genes for tractability...")
        gene_list = gene_list[:50]

    double_results = double_gene_deletion(model, gene_list1=gene_list)

    # Identify synthetic lethal pairs
    sl_pairs = []
    for idx, row in double_results.iterrows():
        if row['growth'] < 1e-6:
            genes = sorted(row['ids'])
            if len(genes) == 2:
                sl_pairs.append({
                    'gene_1': genes[0],
                    'gene_2': genes[1],
                    'double_ko_growth': row['growth'],
                })

    sl_df = pd.DataFrame(sl_pairs)
    print(f"\nSynthetic lethal pairs found: {len(sl_df)}")
    return sl_df.head(max_pairs)

Full Analysis Pipeline

def tb_drug_target_pipeline(model):
    """Complete drug target identification pipeline.

    Steps:
        1. Gene essentiality screen
        2. Filter human orthologs (simulated)
        3. Prioritize unique pathways

    Returns:
        DataFrame of prioritized drug targets
    """
    print("=" * 60)
    print("TB DRUG TARGET IDENTIFICATION PIPELINE")
    print("=" * 60)

    # Step 1: Gene essentiality
    print("\n[Step 1] Gene essentiality screen...")
    results = single_gene_deletion(model)
    results['is_essential'] = results['growth'] < 1e-6
    essential_ids = set()
    for idx, row in results.iterrows():
        if row['is_essential']:
            for gid in row['ids']:
                essential_ids.add(gid)
    print(f"  Essential genes: {len(essential_ids)}")

    # Step 2: Filter human orthologs
    # In practice, use BLAST or InParanoid. Here we simulate with
    # known conserved housekeeping genes
    print("\n[Step 2] Filtering human orthologs...")
    # This is a simplified simulation - real analysis uses BLAST
    human_orthologs = set()  # Would contain gene IDs with human homologs
    # For demonstration, assume ~30% of essential genes have human orthologs
    filtered = essential_ids - human_orthologs
    print(f"  After ortholog filter: {len(filtered)} candidates")

    # Step 3: Prioritize by pathway
    print("\n[Step 3] Prioritizing by pathway uniqueness...")
    targets = []
    for gid in filtered:
        gene = model.genes.get_by_id(gid)
        # Check which subsystems the gene's reactions belong to
        subsystems = set()
        for rxn in gene.reactions:
            if rxn.subsystem:
                subsystems.add(rxn.subsystem)

        # Score: higher if in mycolic acid / cell wall pathways
        priority = 0
        unique_pathway = False
        for sub in subsystems:
            sub_lower = sub.lower()
            if any(kw in sub_lower for kw in
                   ['mycol', 'cell wall', 'arabino', 'peptido', 'lipid']):
                priority += 3
                unique_pathway = True
            elif any(kw in sub_lower for kw in
                     ['folate', 'pantothenate', 'cofactor']):
                priority += 2
            else:
                priority += 1

        targets.append({
            'gene_id': gid,
            'name': gene.name if gene.name else gid,
            'n_reactions': len(gene.reactions),
            'subsystems': ', '.join(sorted(subsystems)),
            'priority_score': priority,
            'unique_pathway': unique_pathway,
        })

    target_df = pd.DataFrame(targets).sort_values(
        'priority_score', ascending=False
    )

    print(f"\n--- Top 20 Drug Target Candidates ---")
    print(target_df.head(20).to_string(index=False))

    return target_df

# Run the pipeline
candidates = tb_drug_target_pipeline(model)

Medium 4. Hypoxic Condition Modeling

Hard 5. Synthetic Lethality: Designing Combination Drug Therapy

Find pairs of individually non-essential genes whose simultaneous knockout is lethal. These synthetic lethal pairs represent targets for combination drug therapy—where neither drug alone kills the bacterium, but together they are lethal.

  1. Setup: First identify all non-essential genes from Exercise 3. Use double_gene_deletion from COBRApy to screen all pairs of non-essential genes. Due to the combinatorial explosion ($\binom{n}{2}$ pairs), you may need to restrict to a subset (e.g., 100 genes).
  2. Analysis: How many synthetic lethal pairs did you find? What fraction of all tested pairs are synthetic lethal? Plot the distribution of double-knockout growth rates.
  3. Pathway enrichment: For each synthetic lethal pair, identify which pathways the two genes belong to. Are synthetic lethal interactions more common between genes in the same pathway (redundancy) or different pathways (metabolic coupling)? Compute the enrichment and test for statistical significance.
  4. Drug target prioritization: Filter synthetic lethal pairs where:
    • Neither gene has a close human ortholog
    • At least one gene is in a druggable pathway (i.e., a known enzyme class with existing inhibitors)
    • The genes are in different pathways (to reduce cross-resistance risk)
    Rank the top 10 candidate drug pairs. For each, describe the biological rationale for why the combination might work.
  5. Robustness: Test whether your top synthetic lethal pairs remain lethal under different growth conditions (aerobic vs. hypoxic vs. nutrient-limited). A pair that is synthetic lethal under all conditions is a more robust drug target. Report which pairs pass this robustness filter.

Hint: Synthetic lethality often arises from isozymes (genes encoding enzymes that catalyze the same reaction) or from alternative metabolic routes (e.g., two pathways that can each independently supply the same essential metabolite). Understanding the mechanism behind each synthetic lethal interaction is key to assessing its therapeutic potential.
