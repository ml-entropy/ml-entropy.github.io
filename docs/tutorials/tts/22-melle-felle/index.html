22. MELLE And FELLE: Autoregressive Continuous Speech Generation

MELLE and FELLE answer a sharp question: can we keep the autoregressive planning ability of language models while dropping vector quantization entirely? Both systems generate continuous mel spectrogram frames one step at a time, preserving sequential coherence without discrete codebook constraints. This tutorial explains their architectures, training objectives, and the information-theoretic reasoning behind them.

Why Keep Autoregression If We Already Have Non-Autoregressive Flows?

Non-autoregressive flow-matching models like Voicebox and F5-TTS are excellent at generating high-quality speech. But they have a structural limitation: they generate all frames simultaneously, without explicit sequential planning. The model at position 500 does not "know" what was decided at position 1 — it infers coherence through the global conditioning and the shared transport dynamics.

Autoregressive models work differently. Each prediction is explicitly conditioned on all previous predictions:

$$p(\mathbf{z}_{1:T} \mid x) = \prod_{t=1}^T p(\mathbf{z}_t \mid \mathbf{z}_{This creates a natural planning mechanism: decisions accumulate, and each new decision must be consistent with everything that came before. For speech, this means the model maintains coherent prosody, consistent speaker identity, and logical content flow across the entire utterance.

The question MELLE and FELLE address is: can we have this planning benefit without the quantization cost of discrete tokens? If we replace the discrete codebook with a continuous output, do we keep the planning ability while gaining representational flexibility?

The answer turns out to be yes — but with important caveats about the training objective.

The AR Planning Advantage

Autoregressive models make sequential decisions where each choice constrains future choices. This creates implicit planning: the model at step $t$ has committed to specific content, timing, and style choices at steps $1, \ldots, t-1$, and must produce step $t$ consistently. Non-autoregressive models lack this constraint — they must infer consistency from global conditioning alone, which can fail for long or complex utterances.

What Replaces Next-Token Cross-Entropy?

In a discrete codec LM, the per-step loss is cross-entropy over a finite vocabulary:

$$\mathcal{L}_t^{\text{disc}} = -\log p_\theta(c_t \mid c_{This is clean and well-understood. The model outputs a probability distribution over $N$ classes, and the loss measures how surprised the model is by the correct class.

When the output is a continuous vector $\mathbf{z}_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$, cross-entropy over classes does not apply. You need a loss that measures how well the model predicts the correct continuous value. The three main options, which MELLE and FELLE explore, are:

Option 1: Simple Regression ($L_2$)

$$\mathcal{L}_t^{L_2} = \|\mathbf{z}_t - \hat{\mathbf{z}}_t\|_2^2.$$

This is the simplest option but the most dangerous. As discussed in earlier tutorials, $L_2$ regression predicts the conditional mean, which averages over valid variations and produces over-smoothed, robotic output. MELLE explicitly identifies this as insufficient and adds additional mechanisms to avoid it.

Option 2: Distributional Regression (Gaussian/GMM)

$$\mathcal{L}_t^{\text{dist}} = -\log p_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t \mid \mathbf{z}_{where $p_\theta$ is parameterized as a Gaussian or Gaussian mixture. This captures uncertainty (the model predicts both where to go and how uncertain it is), but choosing the right number of mixture components and maintaining numerical stability in high dimensions is challenging.

Option 3: Per-Step Transport (Flow/Diffusion)

At each autoregressive step, instead of predicting a point or a parameterized distribution, learn a small transport process that maps noise to the correct output. This is what FELLE does — each step includes a mini flow-matching process that models the full conditional distribution without explicit parameterization.

The Core Challenge

The fundamental challenge of continuous AR speech generation is: how do you predict a continuous vector at each step while (a) avoiding mean collapse, (b) maintaining temporal coherence, and (c) keeping inference fast enough for practical use? MELLE and FELLE offer different solutions to this trilemma.

MELLE: Autoregressive Mel Language Modeling Without VQ

MELLE (Mel spectrogram Language modEling) is a direct answer to the question: can we replace discrete codec tokens with continuous mel frames in an autoregressive language model? The answer is yes, but the training procedure must be significantly modified.

Architecture Overview

MELLE uses a standard Transformer decoder backbone (similar to GPT), but instead of predicting the next token from a finite vocabulary, it predicts the next mel spectrogram frame — a continuous vector of 100 dimensions (corresponding to 100 mel frequency bins).

The architecture has three key components:

  1. Text encoder: Processes the input text into conditioning embeddings. This is similar to text encoding in any TTS system.
  2. Autoregressive backbone: A causal Transformer that processes the sequence of previous mel frames and text conditioning. At each step, it produces a context vector $\mathbf{h}_t$ that summarizes all relevant information.
  3. Output head: Transforms the context vector $\mathbf{h}_t$ into a prediction for the next mel frame $\hat{\mathbf{z}}_t$. This is where MELLE differs most from discrete LMs — instead of a softmax over a vocabulary, it uses a multi-component prediction strategy.

MELLE's Multi-Component Training Objective

MELLE's training objective combines several components to avoid mean collapse while maintaining stable training:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{MELLE}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{reg}} + \lambda_{\text{var}} \mathcal{L}_{\text{var}} + \lambda_{\text{spec}} \mathcal{L}_{\text{spec}}.$$
  • Regression loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{reg}}$: An $L_1$ or $L_2$ loss between predicted and target mel frames. This provides the basic signal for prediction accuracy but would cause mean collapse if used alone.
  • Variational loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{var}}$: A KL-divergence term from a latent variable model. MELLE introduces a small per-step latent $\mathbf{e}_t$ that captures the residual uncertainty in the output. During training, $\mathbf{e}_t$ is inferred from the target; during inference, it is sampled from the prior. This is the key mechanism for avoiding mean collapse.
  • Spectrogram-level loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{spec}}$: A perceptual loss that compares predicted and target spectrograms at multiple resolutions. This helps capture both coarse and fine spectral structure.

The Latent Sampling Mechanism

MELLE's variational mechanism works as follows. At each step $t$, the model produces a deterministic prediction $\boldsymbol{\mu}_t = f_\theta(\mathbf{h}_t)$ and a latent code $\mathbf{e}_t$. The final output is:

$$\hat{\mathbf{z}}_t = \boldsymbol{\mu}_t + g_\theta(\mathbf{h}_t, \mathbf{e}_t),$$

where $g_\theta$ is a small network that modulates the deterministic prediction using the latent code.

During training, $\mathbf{e}_t$ is inferred from the actual target $\mathbf{z}_t$ (so the model knows the answer and can learn to use the latent code effectively). During inference, $\mathbf{e}_t$ is sampled from a prior distribution (typically Gaussian), introducing controlled randomness that prevents the model from always predicting the conditional mean.

Worked example. Consider predicting a mel frame for the word "hello" at a point where either rising or falling intonation would be valid. Without the latent code, MELLE would predict the average of both (flat intonation). With the latent code, different samples of $\mathbf{e}_t$ produce different valid intonation patterns — one sample might give rising, another falling. The latent code resolves the ambiguity that $L_2$ regression cannot.

Inference With Speculative Decoding

MELLE also introduces a speculative decoding strategy for inference. The idea is borrowed from text LLM inference optimization: instead of generating one frame at a time, the model proposes several frames in a batch, then verifies and corrects them. This can reduce the effective number of autoregressive steps without sacrificing quality.

The verification step checks whether each proposed frame is consistent with the model's own conditional distribution. Frames that are "too surprising" (low probability under the model) are rejected and regenerated. This creates a quality-speed tradeoff that is tunable at inference time.

MELLE's Key Insight

MELLE shows that continuous autoregressive generation requires more than just replacing the softmax with a regression head. The output distribution must be modeled explicitly — through a variational mechanism, a diffusion process, or some other approach — to avoid mean collapse. The deterministic component provides a baseline prediction, and the stochastic component provides the distributional richness needed for natural speech.

FELLE: Adding Token-Wise Flow Matching To Autoregression

FELLE (Flow-matching Enhanced Language modEling for speech synthesis) takes a different approach to the same problem. Instead of using a variational mechanism to inject stochasticity, FELLE uses a mini flow-matching process at each autoregressive step.

The Per-Step Flow Matching Idea

At each step $t$, instead of directly predicting $\mathbf{z}_t$, FELLE runs a small ODE that transforms noise into the predicted frame:

$$\hat{\mathbf{z}}_t = \text{ODE-solve}(\mathbf{v}_{\theta,t}, \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \tau: 0 \to 1),$$

where $\mathbf{v}_{\theta,t}$ is a velocity field conditioned on the context $\mathbf{h}_t$, and $\boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ is the noise starting point.

The training loss at each step is the standard flow-matching loss:

$$\mathcal{L}_t^{\text{FELLE}} = \mathbb{E}_{\tau, \boldsymbol{\epsilon}} \left[\|\mathbf{v}_{\theta,t}(\mathbf{x}_{t,\tau}, \tau) - (\mathbf{z}_t - \boldsymbol{\epsilon})\|_2^2\right],$$

where $\mathbf{x}_{t,\tau} = (1 - \tau)\boldsymbol{\epsilon} + \tau \mathbf{z}_t$ is the interpolated state at flow time $\tau$.

This is conceptually elegant: the autoregressive structure provides temporal planning, while the per-step flow matching provides distributional richness. Each step is a small generative process on its own, conditioned on the rich context from the AR backbone.

Computational Cost Analysis

The obvious concern with per-step flow matching is cost. If each AR step requires multiple ODE sub-steps, the total inference cost multiplies:

$$\text{Total steps} = T \times S,$$

where $T$ is the number of AR frames and $S$ is the number of ODE sub-steps per frame. For $T = 500$ frames (5 seconds at 100 Hz) and $S = 4$ sub-steps:

$$\text{Total steps} = 500 \times 4 = 2{,}000 \text{ forward passes}.$$

This is more expensive than MELLE (which needs only $T = 500$ forward passes) but still manageable on a GPU. FELLE mitigates this cost by using a very small flow-matching head (much smaller than the AR backbone) and by keeping $S$ small (typically 2-4 steps).

The key insight is that the per-step flow matching only needs to model the residual uncertainty after the AR context has already resolved most of the prediction. The context $\mathbf{h}_t$ already encodes what phoneme is being produced, the approximate pitch, and the speaker identity — the flow matching only needs to fill in the fine acoustic detail. This is a much simpler distribution than the full speech distribution, so fewer ODE steps suffice.

FELLE's Elegance

FELLE is conceptually the cleanest combination of AR and flow matching. The AR backbone provides the sequential planning that non-AR systems lack. The per-step flow matching provides the distributional modeling that discrete systems lack. Together, they address the two main limitations of the competing paradigms simultaneously.

The Information-Theory Lens

From an information-theory perspective, MELLE and FELLE solve the same problem: modeling the conditional distribution $p(\mathbf{z}_t \mid \mathbf{z}_{ $$h(\mathbf{z}_{1:T} \mid x) = \sum_{t=1}^T h(\mathbf{z}_t \mid \mathbf{z}_{Each conditional entropy $h(\mathbf{z}_t \mid \mathbf{z}_{

The difference between MELLE and FELLE is in how they model this residual uncertainty:

  • MELLE: Uses a learned latent code that captures the residual uncertainty in a low-dimensional space. The model learns to compress the unpredictable part of each frame into a small vector $\mathbf{e}_t$.
  • FELLE: Uses flow matching to model the residual uncertainty as a transport from Gaussian noise. The model learns a velocity field that maps noise to valid speech frames, conditioned on the AR context.

Both approaches ensure that the model can produce diverse, natural outputs rather than always predicting the conditional mean. The key insight is that the unpredictable part of speech (the part that varies between equally valid realizations) must be modeled explicitly, not collapsed.

Continuous AR vs Discrete AR: Information Content

A discrete AR model with codebook size $N$ can express at most $\log_2 N$ bits of information per step. A continuous AR model has no such ceiling — each frame can carry arbitrary precision, limited only by the model's ability to predict accurately. This means continuous AR models can potentially represent finer acoustic detail than discrete AR models at the same frame rate. The tradeoff is that continuous predictions are harder to train and evaluate than discrete token predictions.

When This Family Is Worth Using

Continuous AR models (MELLE/FELLE) occupy a specific niche in the TTS design space:

Use continuous AR when:

  • You want autoregressive planning (sequential coherence) with high acoustic fidelity (no quantization artifacts).
  • You need streaming output — continuous AR models can generate frames sequentially and feed them to a vocoder incrementally, unlike non-AR flow models.
  • You want to avoid the complexity of RVQ coordination and multi-stream generation that discrete codec LMs require.
  • You care about fine prosodic detail that might be lost in discrete quantization.

Consider alternatives when:

  • You need the fastest possible inference: non-AR flow models generate all frames in parallel (faster for long outputs), and discrete LMs have simpler per-step computation.
  • You need to integrate speech tokens into a text LLM: discrete tokens integrate naturally; continuous frames require architectural modifications.
  • You want the simplest possible training pipeline: cross-entropy over discrete tokens is simpler than MELLE's multi-component loss or FELLE's per-step flow matching.

Exercises

These exercises move from the basic idea of autoregressive continuous tokens to the modeling details of MELLE and FELLE. The hard section asks you to reason from an information-theory and system-design perspective.

Easy

1. What Stays Autoregressive?↓

In one sentence, what stays the same when we move from discrete-token LMs to continuous-token LMs?

Solution: The left-to-right factorization over time stays the same: the model still predicts the next step from previous steps.

2. What Changes?↓

What is the main thing that changes?

Solution: The output variable changes from a discrete symbol to a continuous acoustic vector such as a mel frame.

3. Cross-Entropy Replacement↓

What must replace next-token cross-entropy in a continuous-token model?

Solution: A continuous density or generative objective such as Gaussian likelihood, regression, variational modeling, or flow matching.

4. MELLE Target↓

What does MELLE predict directly?

Solution: It autoregressively predicts continuous mel-spectrogram frames directly.

5. Why No VQ?↓

Name one reason MELLE avoids vector quantization.

Solution: To avoid fidelity loss and robustness issues introduced by sampling discrete codec tokens.

6. Reduction Factor↓

What does the reduction factor $r$ control in MELLE?

Solution: It controls how many mel frames are predicted per autoregressive step.

7. Why Sample A Latent?↓

Why does MELLE sample a latent variable instead of always outputting a single deterministic frame?

Solution: Sampling adds diversity and robustness by letting the model represent uncertainty about the next frame.

8. Flux Loss Intuition↓

What problem is the spectrogram flux loss trying to reduce?

Solution: It tries to reduce overly static, repetitive, or silence-like frame sequences.

9. FELLE Key Addition↓

What is FELLE’s key modeling addition over a simpler continuous autoregressive model?

Solution: It adds token-wise flow matching, plus coarse-to-fine refinement, inside the autoregressive process.

10. Temporal Coherence↓

In plain language, what does temporal coherence mean here?

Solution: It means neighboring speech frames should evolve smoothly and consistently rather than jump unpredictably.

11. Gaussian Likelihood Output↓

What two quantities does MELLE’s latent sampling module predict from the hidden state?

Solution: It predicts a mean vector $\boldsymbol{\mu}_t$ and a log-variance vector $\log \boldsymbol{\sigma}_t^2$.

12. Post-Net Purpose↓

What is the purpose of the post-net in MELLE’s architecture?

Solution: The post-net refines the coarse mel prediction into a higher-fidelity output by adding residual spectral detail.

13. Stop Token In Continuous AR↓

Why does MELLE still need a stop prediction even though it generates continuous frames?

Solution: Because autoregressive generation needs an explicit signal to know when the utterance is finished and to stop producing frames.

14. Flow Matching In One Sentence↓

Describe flow matching in one sentence.

Solution: Flow matching learns a vector field that transports samples from a simple prior distribution to the target data distribution.

15. Coarse-To-Fine Stages↓

In FELLE, does the coarse stage or the fine stage run first?

Solution: The coarse stage runs first, producing a rough prediction that the fine stage then refines.

16. Mel Frames Versus Codec Tokens↓

Name one concrete difference between a mel frame and a codec token as a model output.

Solution: A mel frame is a continuous real-valued vector, while a codec token is a discrete integer index into a learned codebook.

17. Reparameterization Noise↓

What distribution does the noise $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ follow in MELLE’s reparameterization trick?

Solution: It follows a standard normal distribution $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$.

18. KL Prior Center↓

In MELLE’s KL term, what is the prior distribution centered on?

Solution: The prior is centered on the ground-truth target frame $\mathbf{y}_t$.

19. FELLE’s AR Structure↓

Does FELLE replace the autoregressive factorization with a fully parallel one?

Solution: No. FELLE keeps the left-to-right autoregressive structure but uses flow matching inside each step.

20. Vocoder Requirement↓

After MELLE generates mel-spectrogram frames, what additional component is needed to produce a waveform?

Solution: A vocoder such as HiFi-GAN is needed to convert the mel spectrogram into an audible waveform.

Explain the role of $\mathbf{z}_t = \boldsymbol{\mu}_t + \boldsymbol{\sigma}_t \odot \boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ in one or two sentences.

Solution: This expresses sampling as a differentiable transformation of noise. It lets the model learn a distribution over next-step latents while still training with backpropagation.

5. Why KL?↓

What does the KL term regularize in MELLE?

Solution: It regularizes the predicted latent distribution so it stays close to a simple target-centered prior instead of becoming unstable or badly calibrated.

6. Why Previous-Step Priors Help FELLE↓

Why is a prior informed by the previous frame a natural idea for FELLE?

Solution: Because adjacent speech frames are strongly correlated, so the previous frame is already a good guess about the local neighborhood of the next frame.

7. Coarse-To-Fine Reasoning↓

Why can coarse-to-fine generation improve quality?

Solution: It lets the model first solve the large-scale structure problem and then spend separate capacity on local detail, which is usually easier than solving everything in one shot.

8. Differential Entropy Direction↓

If the conditional covariance $\Sigma_t$ gets smaller, does the conditional differential entropy increase or decrease?

Solution: It decreases, because the next-frame distribution becomes more concentrated and less uncertain.

9. Single-Stage Advantage↓

Why is MELLE called more streamlined than a typical codec LM pipeline?

Solution: Because it directly predicts mel-spectrograms in one autoregressive model instead of predicting multiple layers of codec tokens and then decoding them through a separate tokenizer/decoder stack.

10. Conditional Uncertainty↓

Why is $h(\mathbf{y}_t \mid \mathbf{y}_{t-1},x)$ usually smaller than $h(\mathbf{y}_t \mid x)$ in speech?

Solution: Because the previous speech frame carries strong local information about the next frame’s energy, formants, and timing, reducing uncertainty.

Solution: $$-\log p(\mathbf{y}_t)=\sum_{i=1}^{d}\left[\frac{(y_{t,i}-\mu_{t,i})^2}{2\sigma_i^2}+\frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma_i^2)\right].$$

13. Reduction Factor And Latency↓

If MELLE runs at 50 ms per AR step and an utterance has 400 mel frames, compare the wall-clock latency for $r{=}1$ versus $r{=}4$.

Solution: With $r{=}1$: $400 \times 50\text{ms}=20\text{s}$. With $r{=}4$: $100 \times 50\text{ms}=5\text{s}$. The reduction factor cuts latency by $4{\times}$.

14. Variance Collapse Risk↓

What happens if MELLE’s predicted variance $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_t^2$ collapses toward zero during training?

Solution: The model degenerates into deterministic mean prediction, losing the ability to represent uncertainty and produce diverse samples.

15. Flow Matching Versus Diffusion↓

State one practical difference between flow matching and diffusion as used inside an AR step.

Solution: Flow matching uses a deterministic ODE with linear interpolation paths, while diffusion uses a stochastic SDE with noise schedules, making flow matching simpler to train and faster to sample.

16. Mutual Information And AR Conditioning↓

Express the mutual information $I(\mathbf{y}_t; \mathbf{y}_{t-1} \mid x)$ in terms of conditional differential entropies.

Solution: $$I(\mathbf{y}_t;\mathbf{y}_{t-1}\mid x)=h(\mathbf{y}_t\mid x)-h(\mathbf{y}_t\mid \mathbf{y}_{t-1},x).$$ High mutual information means the previous frame greatly reduces next-frame uncertainty.

17. L1 Versus L2 Regression↓

Why might MELLE use both $L_1$ and $L_2$ regression losses rather than just one?

Solution: $L_2$ penalizes large errors heavily but can over-smooth; $L_1$ is more robust to outliers and preserves sharper spectral detail. Combining them balances fidelity and stability.

18. Coarse-To-Fine As Residual Modeling↓

Explain why FELLE’s coarse-to-fine mechanism is analogous to residual VQ but without quantization.

Solution: Both decompose the prediction into a base approximation plus successive refinements. The difference is that FELLE keeps all stages in continuous space instead of quantizing each residual into a discrete codebook.

19. Prompt Conditioning In Continuous AR↓

How does a continuous-token AR model use a speech prompt at inference time?

Solution: The prompt’s mel frames are fed as the initial context sequence, and the model continues autoregressively from where the prompt ends, naturally matching speaker identity and style.

20. Comparing Entropy Types↓

A discrete codec LM with vocabulary size 1024 has maximum entropy $\log_2 1024 = 10$ bits. What analogous bound exists for a continuous-token model?

Solution: There is no finite upper bound on differential entropy; it depends on the support and variance of the distribution. This is a fundamental difference: discrete entropy is bounded but differential entropy is not.

Hard

1. Why Discrete Tokens Are Not Required For LMs↓

Give a careful argument for why the language-model perspective does not require discrete tokens.

Solution: A language model is fundamentally a sequential factorization of a joint distribution. The variables being factorized can be categorical, continuous, or mixed. Discrete tokens are convenient because cross-entropy is easy, but the autoregressive idea itself only requires ordered conditional prediction.

2. Mean Regression Failure Mode↓

Why can plain mean regression be too weak for speech generation?

Solution: Because when the conditional future is multimodal, predicting only the conditional mean can average over plausible acoustic outcomes, producing oversmoothed or dull speech.

3. Flux Loss Tradeoff↓

What is one benefit and one risk of adding a flux-style loss?

Solution: The benefit is reduced repetition and more dynamic frame evolution. The risk is encouraging too much frame-to-frame change, which could hurt smoothness if over-weighted.

4. FELLE Versus MELLE↓

Why might token-wise flow matching improve over a Gaussian latent sampler for the next frame?

Solution: A flow-matching generator can model richer, more flexible next-frame distributions and nonlinear transport paths, while a simple Gaussian sampler may underfit complicated local acoustic uncertainty.

5. Information-Theory Comparison↓

Contrast the uncertainty handled by a discrete codec LM and a continuous-token LM in one paragraph.

Solution: A discrete codec LM handles uncertainty over a finite set of symbols and measures it with categorical entropy. A continuous-token LM handles uncertainty over regions of acoustic space and measures it through densities, variances, or flow trajectories. The first asks which symbol is next; the second asks how tightly the next acoustic state is localized after conditioning on past frames and text.

6. When Continuous AR Is Better Than Non-AR Flow Matching↓

Give one situation where you might prefer MELLE or FELLE over a fully non-autoregressive continuous-latent model.

Solution: I might prefer them when I want strong prompt continuation behavior or explicit left-to-right control, since the autoregressive factorization naturally matches continuation and incremental generation.

7. Reduction Factor Tradeoff↓

Why does increasing the reduction factor improve speed but potentially hurt quality?

Solution: Because the model takes fewer autoregressive steps, but each step must predict a larger block of future frames, which is a harder conditional problem and can weaken local accuracy.

8. Sampling And Robustness↓

Why can learned continuous sampling be more robust than top-p sampling over discrete codec tokens?

Solution: Because it avoids arbitrary jumps among similar discrete codes and instead samples from a local continuous distribution matched to the acoustic context, which can reduce brittle repetition and noise loops.

9. Strongest Remaining Weakness↓

What is the strongest structural weakness continuous autoregressive mel generation still has?

Solution: It is still autoregressive, so long utterances remain sequential at inference and cannot match the full parallelism of strong non-autoregressive continuous generators.

10. Your Rule Of Thumb↓

Write a one-sentence rule of thumb for this model family.

Solution: If you want LM-style speech continuation but do not want vector quantization, autoregressive continuous-token models are the most direct replacement for codec language models.

11. Designing A Hybrid MELLE-FELLE System↓

Propose a concrete architecture that uses MELLE’s simplicity for coarse prediction and FELLE’s flow matching for refinement within each AR step. What would the loss function look like?

Solution: Use the AR transformer to predict a Gaussian coarse frame (MELLE-style regression loss), then run a small conditional flow-matching network to refine that coarse frame into the final output (flow-matching loss on the residual). The total loss combines regression on the coarse output, flow-matching on the refinement, and a KL term on the latent.

12. Variance Scheduling For Latent Sampling↓

In MELLE, the predicted variance $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_t^2$ is learned freely. Argue whether a scheduled variance that decreases over AR steps would help or hurt generation quality.

Solution: A decreasing schedule could help late in generation when the utterance context is rich and uncertainty is genuinely low, but it would hurt early steps where the model legitimately needs more variance to explore. Learned per-step variance is preferable because the model can adapt to context-dependent uncertainty rather than following a rigid schedule.

13. Information-Theoretic Cost Of Removing VQ↓

From an information-theory standpoint, what capacity advantage does a continuous-token model have over a VQ model, and what does it sacrifice?

Solution: A continuous-token model avoids the quantization distortion that lower-bounds reconstruction error by $D \geq D_{\text{VQ}}$, giving it access to the full acoustic manifold. However, it sacrifices the implicit regularization and compression that discrete tokens provide, meaning it must learn to regularize uncertainty through other means like KL penalties or flow priors.

14. Failure Modes Of Continuous AR At Scale↓

Identify two failure modes unique to continuous autoregressive generation that do not affect discrete-token AR models, and suggest mitigations.

Solution: First, error accumulation through continuous drift: small prediction errors compound smoothly across steps, causing gradual pitch or energy drift. Mitigation: add an explicit drift-correction loss or periodic re-anchoring. Second, mode averaging in multimodal futures: the Gaussian assumption can blend distinct acoustic futures into a blurry mean. Mitigation: use mixture-of-Gaussians or flow-matching to capture multimodality.

15. Flow Matching ODE Steps Versus AR Steps↓

In FELLE, each AR step internally runs multiple ODE integration steps for flow matching. Analyze how the total compute scales with utterance length $T$, reduction factor $r$, and ODE steps $K$.

Solution: Total compute scales as $O((T/r) \times K \times C)$ where $C$ is the cost of one ODE function evaluation. Increasing $r$ reduces AR steps but makes each ODE problem harder; increasing $K$ improves flow-matching quality but linearly increases cost per AR step. The practitioner must balance all three for a given latency budget.

16. Why Not Use A Full Diffusion Model Per Step?↓

Argue why FELLE chose flow matching over full diffusion for the per-step generator, considering both theoretical and practical factors.

Solution: Flow matching uses straight-line ODE paths that are easier to learn and faster to integrate than the curved SDE paths of diffusion. Practically, since each AR step must be fast, the fewer ODE steps needed by flow matching are critical. Theoretically, flow matching achieves comparable log-likelihood bounds with simpler training objectives, making it a better fit for the inner loop of an AR system.

17. Continuous Tokens And Streaming Inference↓

Design a streaming inference protocol for MELLE that generates audio with bounded latency. What is the minimum lookahead required?

Solution: Buffer $r$ frames (one AR step), run the vocoder on each completed block, and output audio. Minimum lookahead is $r$ frames times the hop size, plus vocoder latency. For $r{=}4$ at 10ms hop, that is 40ms of acoustic lookahead plus vocoder processing, giving sub-100ms latency for real-time streaming.

18. Entropy Rate Of Continuous Speech Models↓

Define the entropy rate for a continuous-token AR speech model and explain why it is a more meaningful metric than per-frame differential entropy.

Solution: The entropy rate $\bar{h} = \lim_{T\to\infty}\frac{1}{T}h(\mathbf{y}_1,\dots,\mathbf{y}_T\mid x)$ captures the average residual uncertainty per frame after exploiting all temporal dependencies. Per-frame differential entropy ignores conditioning and overestimates true uncertainty. The entropy rate reveals how much irreducible acoustic variation remains after the model exploits temporal structure.

19. Bridging Continuous AR And Non-AR Models↓

Propose a training curriculum that starts with a non-autoregressive flow model and gradually transitions to autoregressive continuous-token generation. Justify each stage.

Solution: Stage 1: train a non-AR flow model on full utterances to learn the acoustic manifold without sequential dependencies. Stage 2: introduce teacher-forced AR conditioning by masking future frames and fine-tuning. Stage 3: switch to fully autoregressive inference with scheduled sampling to close the train-test gap. This curriculum avoids the cold-start problem of AR training and leverages the NAR model’s global acoustic knowledge.

20. Ultimate Comparison: VQ-AR vs Continuous-AR vs NAR-Flow↓

Write a structured comparison of three paradigms—VQ autoregressive (VALL-E style), continuous autoregressive (MELLE/FELLE), and non-autoregressive flow (Voicebox/F5-TTS)—along the axes of fidelity, latency, controllability, and information-theoretic efficiency.

Solution: VQ-AR offers strong LM prompting but introduces quantization error and multi-stage complexity. Continuous-AR (MELLE/FELLE) removes quantization loss and simplifies the pipeline but remains sequential. NAR-flow achieves parallel generation and high fidelity but loses natural left-to-right prompting. In information-theoretic terms, VQ-AR compresses into a finite codebook (bounded entropy), continuous-AR models the full acoustic density (unbounded differential entropy), and NAR-flow models the joint distribution directly without temporal factorization.
