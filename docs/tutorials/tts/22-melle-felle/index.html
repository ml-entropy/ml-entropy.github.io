22. MELLE, FELLE, And Continuous-Token LMs

Discrete codec tokens made speech look like language modeling. MELLE and FELLE ask a sharper question: can we keep the autoregressive language-model factorization, but make the token itself continuous instead of discrete? The answer is yes, but cross-entropy must be replaced by continuous probabilistic objectives.

Why Keep Autoregression If We Already Have Non-Autoregressive Flows?

Autoregression is not only about discrete vocabularies. It is really about a factorization over time. If a speech sequence is $\mathbf{y} = (\mathbf{y}_1, \mathbf{y}_2, \dots, \mathbf{y}_T)$, then an autoregressive model says:

$$p(\mathbf{y}\mid x) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} p(\mathbf{y}_t \mid \mathbf{y}_{This equation does not require $\mathbf{y}_t$ to be a symbol. It only requires there to be an ordered next step.

That is the key conceptual shift behind MELLE and FELLE. The model still predicts the future from the past, but the future unit is a continuous mel-spectrogram frame or block rather than a codebook index.

Mental Model

An ordinary language model says, "given the previous words, what is the next word?" A continuous-token LM says, "given the previous frames, what is the next point in acoustic space?" The sequencing stays. The output alphabet disappears.

What Replaces Next-Token Cross-Entropy?

In a discrete token model, you predict a categorical distribution and train with cross-entropy:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{disc}} = -\log p(c_t \mid c_{When the target is continuous, there is no softmax over a vocabulary. So we need a density. The simplest choice is a Gaussian:

$$p(\mathbf{y}_t \mid \mathbf{y}_{The model now predicts the mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}_t$ and possibly a covariance $\Sigma_t$.

Why does regression show up? Because maximizing Gaussian likelihood often reduces to a squared-error objective. If $\Sigma_t = \sigma^2 I$, then

$$-\log p(\mathbf{y}_t \mid \mathbf{y}_{where $C$ is a constant independent of the prediction. So minimizing negative log-likelihood becomes minimizing squared error, up to scaling.

This step matters because it explains why continuous-token LMs often look like "autoregressive regression models" at first sight. Under the hood, that regression is a density model in continuous space.

Information-Theory View

Cross-entropy measures uncertainty over symbols. Gaussian likelihood measures uncertainty over regions of Euclidean space. One asks, "which symbol?" The other asks, "how concentrated is the next acoustic state?"

MELLE: Autoregressive Mel Language Modeling Without VQ

MELLE takes the continuous-token idea seriously and keeps the pipeline simple. Instead of predicting codec indices and then decoding them, it directly autoregressively predicts mel-spectrogram frames.

The paper writes the target factorization as:

$$p(\mathbf{y}\mid x;\theta)=\prod_{t=0}^{T-1}p(\mathbf{y}_t\mid \mathbf{y}_{Step by step, this means:

  1. read text prompt and speech prompt,
  2. read all previously generated mel frames,
  3. predict a distribution for the next mel frame,
  4. sample or decode that frame,
  5. repeat.

MELLE also introduces a reduction factor $r$. Instead of predicting one frame per step, it can predict a group of $r$ frames per step:

$$p(\mathbf{y}\mid x;\theta)=\prod_{t=0}^{T/r-1} p(\mathbf{y}_{tr:(t+1)r}\mid \mathbf{y}_{This is just a speed-quality tradeoff. Larger $r$ means fewer steps, but each step becomes harder because it must predict a longer block.

Latent Sampling Module, Slowly Explained

MELLE does not only output a deterministic frame. It predicts a Gaussian latent distribution and samples from it. If the LM hidden state at step $t$ is $\mathbf{e}_t$, the model predicts:

$$[\boldsymbol{\mu}_t, \log \boldsymbol{\sigma}_t^2] = W\mathbf{e}_t + b.$$

Then it samples:

$$\mathbf{z}_t = \boldsymbol{\mu}_t + \boldsymbol{\sigma}_t \odot \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\epsilon}\sim \mathcal{N}(0,I).$$

Read this left to right:

  1. the decoder hidden state summarizes text, prompt, and previous acoustics,
  2. that summary becomes a mean and variance,
  3. a random standard-normal vector adds controlled variation,
  4. the sampled latent is mapped into mel space.

This is variational sampling, but the intuition is simple: the model does not commit to one exact next frame. It defines a cloud of plausible next frames.

The Loss, Derived In Pieces

MELLE trains with four terms:

$$\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{reg}} + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{\text{KL}} + \beta \mathcal{L}_{\text{flux}} + \gamma \mathcal{L}_{\text{stop}}.$$

Here is what each term is doing.

1. Regression loss. The model predicts a coarse mel output and then a refined post-net output. Both are pulled toward the true mel target with $L_1$ and $L_2$ penalties. This is the fidelity term: "be near the right frame."

2. KL loss. MELLE compares the predicted latent distribution to a simpler target-centered prior. A stylized version of the KL term is:

$$D_{\text{KL}}\big(\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_t,\mathrm{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_t^2)) \,\|\, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{y}_t, I)\big).$$

Why this matters: KL does not force one exact sample. It regularizes the uncertainty itself. The mean should stay near the target, but the distribution should also remain well-behaved.

3. Spectrogram flux loss. MELLE adds a term that rewards change across time rather than allowing the model to stay too flat. The paper defines a loss based on the distance between the predicted mean at time $t$ and the previous ground-truth frame. Intuitively, if the model keeps repeating nearly the same frame, it risks monotone speech, silence loops, or frozen acoustics.

4. Stop loss. The model needs to know when to terminate generation.

Why Flux Loss Makes Sense

Imagine the true mel sequence changes like 0.20, 0.38, 0.51, 0.62 on one frequency band, but the model keeps outputting 0.31, 0.31, 0.31, 0.31. Ordinary regression may not punish this strongly enough if averages look acceptable globally. A flux-style term explicitly says, "successive frames should not become unnaturally static."

FELLE: Adding Token-Wise Flow Matching To Autoregression

FELLE keeps the same broad ambition as MELLE: autoregressive continuous-token speech modeling without vector quantization. But it changes the local generation mechanism.

Instead of saying, "the next frame comes from a Gaussian around a mean," FELLE says, "the next frame should be generated by a small flow-matching process conditioned on the AR context."

The official description highlights two ideas:

  • token-wise flow matching, where each next continuous token is produced with a flow-matching generator,
  • a coarse-to-fine mechanism, where a coarse prediction is refined hierarchically.

The token-wise part is important. FELLE does not replace sequence factorization with a fully non-autoregressive generator. It keeps the left-to-right language-model structure, but each step uses a richer continuous generative process than plain Gaussian regression.

Why Previous-Step Information Matters

The FELLE project page says it modifies the prior in flow matching using information from the previous step. The intuition is strong even before writing equations.

Speech is temporally coherent. Consecutive mel frames are usually close. If the model begins the next-frame generation from a prior informed by the last frame, it does not have to reinvent the local acoustic neighborhood at every step. It starts near a plausible part of the space.

That is an information-theory argument as much as an engineering one. The conditional uncertainty of the next frame is much smaller than the unconditional uncertainty:

$$h(\mathbf{y}_t \mid \mathbf{y}_{t-1}, x) \ll h(\mathbf{y}_t \mid x).$$

So a prior informed by $\mathbf{y}_{t-1}$ is better matched to the real problem.

Why Coarse-To-Fine Helps

Coarse-to-fine generation is a way to spend modeling capacity where it matters. First get broad structure right: energy, formant placement, rough timing. Then refine local details. This resembles residual coding, but without forcing the representation into discrete residual codebooks.

The mental model is:

  1. predict the next frame or block roughly,
  2. condition a second generator on that rough answer,
  3. add detail without disturbing large-scale coherence.

That is attractive because it separates easy uncertainty from fine-grained uncertainty.

MELLE Versus FELLE

ModelLocal next-step modelMain motivation
MELLERegression plus variational latent samplingKeep AR modeling but remove VQ and two-stage codec decoding

MELLE is easier to explain as "continuous autoregressive mel LM." FELLE is better read as "continuous autoregressive mel LM plus a stronger local generator." Both reject the idea that speech LMs must operate on discrete codec vocabularies.

The Information-Theory Lens

Discrete codec LMs spend model capacity on predicting the next symbol from a finite alphabet. Continuous-token LMs spend model capacity on reducing conditional uncertainty in a continuous space.

If the next frame is modeled as Gaussian with covariance $\Sigma_t$, its differential entropy is:

$$h(\mathbf{y}_t \mid \mathbf{y}_{This formula is worth reading slowly:

  1. if $\Sigma_t$ is large, the model is uncertain,
  2. if $\Sigma_t$ shrinks, the model has concentrated belief about the next frame,
  3. autoregression helps because past frames often reduce uncertainty sharply.

So the continuous-token question is not "which of 1024 symbols comes next?" It is "how much uncertainty remains about the next acoustic state after conditioning on all past context?"

This is why temporal coherence matters so much. Adjacent speech frames share a lot of mutual information. A good continuous-token LM should exploit that directly instead of flattening it into a symbolic code inventory.

When This Family Is Worth Using

Autoregressive continuous-token models are attractive when:

  • you want LM-style prompting and continuation behavior,
  • you do not want codec quantization errors,
  • you prefer a single-stage mel-generation pipeline over AR plus NAR codec decoding,
  • you care about temporal coherence and acoustic fidelity more than compact storage.

They are less attractive when:

  • you need audio represented as portable discrete tokens for a multimodal LLM,
  • you want entropy coding and compression,
  • you want fully parallel generation instead of left-to-right decoding.

Exercises

These exercises move from the basic idea of autoregressive continuous tokens to the modeling details of MELLE and FELLE. The hard section asks you to reason from an information-theory and system-design perspective.

Easy

1. What Stays Autoregressive?↓

In one sentence, what stays the same when we move from discrete-token LMs to continuous-token LMs?

Solution: The left-to-right factorization over time stays the same: the model still predicts the next step from previous steps.

2. What Changes?↓

What is the main thing that changes?

Solution: The output variable changes from a discrete symbol to a continuous acoustic vector such as a mel frame.

3. Cross-Entropy Replacement↓

What must replace next-token cross-entropy in a continuous-token model?

Solution: A continuous density or generative objective such as Gaussian likelihood, regression, variational modeling, or flow matching.

4. MELLE Target↓

What does MELLE predict directly?

Solution: It autoregressively predicts continuous mel-spectrogram frames directly.

5. Why No VQ?↓

Name one reason MELLE avoids vector quantization.

Solution: To avoid fidelity loss and robustness issues introduced by sampling discrete codec tokens.

6. Reduction Factor↓

What does the reduction factor $r$ control in MELLE?

Solution: It controls how many mel frames are predicted per autoregressive step.

7. Why Sample A Latent?↓

Why does MELLE sample a latent variable instead of always outputting a single deterministic frame?

Solution: Sampling adds diversity and robustness by letting the model represent uncertainty about the next frame.

8. Flux Loss Intuition↓

What problem is the spectrogram flux loss trying to reduce?

Solution: It tries to reduce overly static, repetitive, or silence-like frame sequences.

9. FELLE Key Addition↓

What is FELLE’s key modeling addition over a simpler continuous autoregressive model?

Solution: It adds token-wise flow matching, plus coarse-to-fine refinement, inside the autoregressive process.

10. Temporal Coherence↓

In plain language, what does temporal coherence mean here?

Solution: It means neighboring speech frames should evolve smoothly and consistently rather than jump unpredictably.

Medium

1. Factorization↓

Write the autoregressive factorization for a continuous sequence $\mathbf{y}_1,\dots,\mathbf{y}_T$ conditioned on text $x$.

Solution: $$p(\mathbf{y}\mid x)=\prod_{t=1}^{T}p(\mathbf{y}_t\mid \mathbf{y}_{

2. Gaussian To Regression↓

If $p(\mathbf{y}_t \mid \cdot)=\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_t,\sigma^2 I)$, what error term appears in the negative log-likelihood?

Solution: A squared-error term proportional to $$\|\mathbf{y}_t-\boldsymbol{\mu}_t\|_2^2.$$

3. Reduction Factor Arithmetic↓

If an utterance has 240 mel frames and MELLE uses $r=4$, how many autoregressive steps are needed?

Solution: $$240/4=60$$ autoregressive steps.

4. Reparameterization↓

Explain the role of $\mathbf{z}_t = \boldsymbol{\mu}_t + \boldsymbol{\sigma}_t \odot \boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ in one or two sentences.

Solution: This expresses sampling as a differentiable transformation of noise. It lets the model learn a distribution over next-step latents while still training with backpropagation.

5. Why KL?↓

What does the KL term regularize in MELLE?

Solution: It regularizes the predicted latent distribution so it stays close to a simple target-centered prior instead of becoming unstable or badly calibrated.

6. Why Previous-Step Priors Help FELLE↓

Why is a prior informed by the previous frame a natural idea for FELLE?

Solution: Because adjacent speech frames are strongly correlated, so the previous frame is already a good guess about the local neighborhood of the next frame.

7. Coarse-To-Fine Reasoning↓

Why can coarse-to-fine generation improve quality?

Solution: It lets the model first solve the large-scale structure problem and then spend separate capacity on local detail, which is usually easier than solving everything in one shot.

8. Differential Entropy Direction↓

If the conditional covariance $\Sigma_t$ gets smaller, does the conditional differential entropy increase or decrease?

Solution: It decreases, because the next-frame distribution becomes more concentrated and less uncertain.

9. Single-Stage Advantage↓

Why is MELLE called more streamlined than a typical codec LM pipeline?

Solution: Because it directly predicts mel-spectrograms in one autoregressive model instead of predicting multiple layers of codec tokens and then decoding them through a separate tokenizer/decoder stack.

10. Conditional Uncertainty↓

Why is $h(\mathbf{y}_t \mid \mathbf{y}_{t-1},x)$ usually smaller than $h(\mathbf{y}_t \mid x)$ in speech?

Solution: Because the previous speech frame carries strong local information about the next frame’s energy, formants, and timing, reducing uncertainty.

Hard

1. Why Discrete Tokens Are Not Required For LMs↓

Give a careful argument for why the language-model perspective does not require discrete tokens.

Solution: A language model is fundamentally a sequential factorization of a joint distribution. The variables being factorized can be categorical, continuous, or mixed. Discrete tokens are convenient because cross-entropy is easy, but the autoregressive idea itself only requires ordered conditional prediction.

2. Mean Regression Failure Mode↓

Why can plain mean regression be too weak for speech generation?

Solution: Because when the conditional future is multimodal, predicting only the conditional mean can average over plausible acoustic outcomes, producing oversmoothed or dull speech.

3. Flux Loss Tradeoff↓

What is one benefit and one risk of adding a flux-style loss?

Solution: The benefit is reduced repetition and more dynamic frame evolution. The risk is encouraging too much frame-to-frame change, which could hurt smoothness if over-weighted.

4. FELLE Versus MELLE↓

Why might token-wise flow matching improve over a Gaussian latent sampler for the next frame?

Solution: A flow-matching generator can model richer, more flexible next-frame distributions and nonlinear transport paths, while a simple Gaussian sampler may underfit complicated local acoustic uncertainty.

5. Information-Theory Comparison↓

Contrast the uncertainty handled by a discrete codec LM and a continuous-token LM in one paragraph.

Solution: A discrete codec LM handles uncertainty over a finite set of symbols and measures it with categorical entropy. A continuous-token LM handles uncertainty over regions of acoustic space and measures it through densities, variances, or flow trajectories. The first asks which symbol is next; the second asks how tightly the next acoustic state is localized after conditioning on past frames and text.

6. When Continuous AR Is Better Than Non-AR Flow Matching↓

Give one situation where you might prefer MELLE or FELLE over a fully non-autoregressive continuous-latent model.

Solution: I might prefer them when I want strong prompt continuation behavior or explicit left-to-right control, since the autoregressive factorization naturally matches continuation and incremental generation.

7. Reduction Factor Tradeoff↓

Why does increasing the reduction factor improve speed but potentially hurt quality?

Solution: Because the model takes fewer autoregressive steps, but each step must predict a larger block of future frames, which is a harder conditional problem and can weaken local accuracy.

8. Sampling And Robustness↓

Why can learned continuous sampling be more robust than top-p sampling over discrete codec tokens?

Solution: Because it avoids arbitrary jumps among similar discrete codes and instead samples from a local continuous distribution matched to the acoustic context, which can reduce brittle repetition and noise loops.

9. Strongest Remaining Weakness↓

What is the strongest structural weakness continuous autoregressive mel generation still has?

Solution: It is still autoregressive, so long utterances remain sequential at inference and cannot match the full parallelism of strong non-autoregressive continuous generators.

10. Your Rule Of Thumb↓

Write a one-sentence rule of thumb for this model family.

Solution: If you want LM-style speech continuation but do not want vector quantization, autoregressive continuous-token models are the most direct replacement for codec language models.
