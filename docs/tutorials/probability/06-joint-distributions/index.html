06. Joint Distributions

Real-world phenomena rarely involve just one random variable. Heights and weights are correlated; stock prices move together; features in a dataset interact. Joint distributions give us the mathematical language to describe how multiple random variables behave together—their simultaneous probabilities, their dependencies, and the rich structure that emerges when we move beyond one dimension.

Why Joint Distributions?

Up to now we have studied random variables in isolation: a single coin flip, a single measurement, a single waiting time. But the interesting questions in probability and machine learning almost always involve multiple random variables at once:

  • A medical study records blood pressure $X$ and cholesterol level $Y$ for each patient. Are they related?
  • A neural network produces a distribution over the next word and its part-of-speech tag simultaneously.
  • A sensor array gives readings $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ from $n$ microphones. How do they co-vary?
  • In Bayesian inference, we need the joint distribution $p(\theta, \mathbf{x})$ of parameters and data.

The joint distribution of random variables $X$ and $Y$ captures everything about their simultaneous behavior. From the joint, we can recover:

  • The marginal distributions of $X$ and $Y$ individually
  • The conditional distribution of $Y$ given $X = x$
  • Whether $X$ and $Y$ are independent
  • The covariance and correlation between $X$ and $Y$
  • The distribution of any function $g(X, Y)$

If the marginal distributions are the "shadows" of a higher-dimensional object, the joint distribution is the object itself. Understanding joints is the gateway to multivariate statistics, graphical models, and modern machine learning.

Joint PMF

For discrete random variables $X$ and $Y$, the joint probability mass function (PMF) gives the probability of each pair of outcomes:

$$\boxed{p_{X,Y}(x, y) = P(X = x \text{ and } Y = y)}$$

The joint PMF must satisfy two properties:

  1. Non-negativity: $p_{X,Y}(x, y) \geq 0$ for all $x, y$
  2. Normalization: $\displaystyle\sum_{x}\sum_{y} p_{X,Y}(x, y) = 1$

A joint PMF is most naturally displayed as a probability table. Consider two dice: let $X$ be the minimum of the two rolls and $Y$ be the maximum.

Example. Roll two fair dice. Let $X = \min(D_1, D_2)$ and $Y = \max(D_1, D_2)$. Since $X \leq Y$ always, $p_{X,Y}(x,y) = 0$ when $x > y$. For $x < y$, $p_{X,Y}(x,y) = 2/36$ (two orderings), and for $x = y$, $p_{X,Y}(x,y) = 1/36$ (only one way).

Figure 1. Joint PMF of $X = \min(D_1, D_2)$ and $Y = \max(D_1, D_2)$. Non-zero entries appear only for $x \leq y$. Darker cells have higher probability.

For any event $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$, the joint PMF lets us compute:

$$P\bigl((X, Y) \in A\bigr) = \sum_{(x,y) \in A} p_{X,Y}(x, y)$$

This extends naturally to more than two variables. For $n$ discrete random variables $X_1, \ldots, X_n$, the joint PMF is $p(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = P(X_1 = x_1, \ldots, X_n = x_n)$.

Joint PDF

For continuous random variables $X$ and $Y$, probabilities are specified by a joint probability density function (PDF) $f_{X,Y}(x, y)$, defined by the requirement that for any region $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$:

$$\boxed{P\bigl((X, Y) \in A\bigr) = \iint_A f_{X,Y}(x, y) \, dx \, dy}$$

The joint PDF must satisfy:

  1. Non-negativity: $f_{X,Y}(x, y) \geq 0$ for all $(x, y)$
  2. Normalization: $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x, y) \, dx \, dy = 1$

Important: Unlike the PMF, $f_{X,Y}(x, y)$ is not a probability. It is a density—the probability per unit area. The value $f_{X,Y}(x, y)$ can exceed 1. Only when integrated over a region does it yield a probability.

Example: Uniform distribution on a triangle. Let $(X, Y)$ be uniformly distributed on the triangle $T = \{(x, y) : 0 \leq x \leq 1, \; 0 \leq y \leq x\}$. The area of $T$ is $1/2$, so:

$$f_{X,Y}(x, y) = \begin{cases} 2 & \text{if } 0 \leq y \leq x \leq 1 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Verification: $\int_0^1 \int_0^x 2 \, dy \, dx = \int_0^1 2x \, dx = [x^2]_0^1 = 1$. The total probability is 1, as required.

The bivariate normal distribution is the most important continuous joint distribution. For random variables $X$ and $Y$ with means $\mu_X, \mu_Y$, standard deviations $\sigma_X, \sigma_Y$, and correlation $\rho$:

$$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_X\sigma_Y\sqrt{1-\rho^2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2(1-\rho^2)}\left[\frac{(x-\mu_X)^2}{\sigma_X^2} - 2\rho\frac{(x-\mu_X)(y-\mu_Y)}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y} + \frac{(y-\mu_Y)^2}{\sigma_Y^2}\right]\right)$$

Figure 2. Contour plots of the bivariate normal for different correlation coefficients $\rho$. When $\rho = 0$, contours are circular; positive $\rho$ tilts ellipses toward the $y = x$ line; negative $\rho$ tilts toward $y = -x$.

Marginal Distributions

Given a joint distribution, we often want to recover the distribution of just one variable—ignoring the other. This is called the marginal distribution. We "marginalize out" (sum or integrate over) the unwanted variable.

Discrete case (PMF):

$$\boxed{p_X(x) = \sum_{y} p_{X,Y}(x, y), \qquad p_Y(y) = \sum_{x} p_{X,Y}(x, y)}$$

Continuous case (PDF):

$$\boxed{f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x, y) \, dy, \qquad f_Y(y) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x, y) \, dx}$$

Geometric intuition: Think of the joint density as a surface in 3D. The marginal $f_X(x)$ is obtained by "collapsing" the surface onto the $x$-axis—summing up all the density along the $y$-direction. This is exactly the shadow (projection) of the surface onto the $x$-axis.

Figure 3. Marginal distributions are projections of the joint density. Integrating $f(x,y)$ over $y$ yields the marginal $f_X(x)$ (bottom); integrating over $x$ yields $f_Y(y)$ (right).

Example. Returning to the uniform distribution on the triangle $\{0 \leq y \leq x \leq 1\}$ with $f(x,y) = 2$:

$$f_X(x) = \int_0^x 2 \, dy = 2x, \quad 0 \leq x \leq 1$$
$$f_Y(y) = \int_y^1 2 \, dx = 2(1 - y), \quad 0 \leq y \leq 1$$

Note that $X \sim \text{Triangular}$ with linearly increasing density and $Y \sim \text{Triangular}$ with linearly decreasing density.

Key warning: You cannot recover the joint distribution from the marginals alone, unless you also know the dependence structure. Many different joints can produce the same marginals. This is precisely the insight behind copulas in statistics.

Conditional Distributions

The conditional distribution of $Y$ given $X = x$ describes the behavior of $Y$ once we know the value of $X$. It is the "slice" of the joint distribution at a fixed $x$, renormalized to integrate to 1.

Discrete case:

$$\boxed{p_{Y|X}(y \mid x) = \frac{p_{X,Y}(x, y)}{p_X(x)}, \quad \text{provided } p_X(x) > 0}$$

Continuous case:

$$\boxed{f_{Y|X}(y \mid x) = \frac{f_{X,Y}(x, y)}{f_X(x)}, \quad \text{provided } f_X(x) > 0}$$

This is precisely the definition of conditional probability applied to densities. The denominator $f_X(x)$ normalizes the "slice" so that it integrates to 1 over $y$.

Connection to Bayes' theorem. Rearranging the conditional density formula:

$$f_{X,Y}(x, y) = f_{Y|X}(y \mid x) \cdot f_X(x) = f_{X|Y}(x \mid y) \cdot f_Y(y)$$

Setting these equal and solving for $f_{X|Y}(x \mid y)$:

$$f_{X|Y}(x \mid y) = \frac{f_{Y|X}(y \mid x) \, f_X(x)}{f_Y(y)}$$

This is Bayes' theorem for densities. The conditional distribution $f_{X|Y}$ reverses the conditioning direction from $f_{Y|X}$.

Example. For the triangle distribution with $f(x,y) = 2$ on $\{0 \leq y \leq x \leq 1\}$:

$$f_{Y|X}(y \mid x) = \frac{f_{X,Y}(x,y)}{f_X(x)} = \frac{2}{2x} = \frac{1}{x}, \quad 0 \leq y \leq x$$

So $Y \mid X = x \sim \text{Uniform}(0, x)$. Given that $X = x$, $Y$ is uniformly distributed between 0 and $x$. This makes geometric sense: every horizontal slice of the triangle is a uniform strip.

Conditional expectation follows naturally:

$$E[Y \mid X = x] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} y \, f_{Y|X}(y \mid x) \, dy$$

For the triangle example: $E[Y \mid X = x] = x/2$, the midpoint of the uniform distribution on $[0, x]$.

The Law of Total Expectation (also called the tower property or Adam's Law) connects conditional and unconditional expectations:

$$E[Y] = E\bigl[E[Y \mid X]\bigr]$$

In words: to find the overall expected value of $Y$, first compute the conditional expectation $E[Y \mid X]$ (a function of $X$), then average it over all values of $X$.

Independence of Random Variables

Random variables $X$ and $Y$ are independent if knowing the value of one gives no information about the other. Formally:

$$\boxed{X \perp Y \iff f_{X,Y}(x, y) = f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y) \quad \text{for all } x, y}$$

Equivalently, $X \perp Y$ if and only if $f_{Y|X}(y \mid x) = f_Y(y)$—conditioning on $X$ does not change the distribution of $Y$.

How to test independence:

  1. Factorization criterion: Check whether $f_{X,Y}(x, y) = g(x) \cdot h(y)$ for some functions $g$ and $h$. If so, $X \perp Y$ (the functions need not be normalized—normalization follows automatically).
  2. Support criterion: If the support of $(X,Y)$ is not a rectangle (i.e., $x$ and $y$ ranges depend on each other), then $X$ and $Y$ are not independent. For example, the triangle $\{0 \leq y \leq x \leq 1\}$ is not rectangular, so $X$ and $Y$ from our earlier example are dependent.

Independence vs. uncorrelatedness. If $X \perp Y$, then $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = 0$ (independence implies uncorrelatedness). However, the converse is false in general. The classic counterexample:

Let $X \sim \text{Uniform}(-1, 1)$ and $Y = X^2$. Then:

$$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y] = E[X^3] - 0 \cdot E[X^2] = 0$$

since $E[X^3] = 0$ by symmetry. So $X$ and $Y$ are uncorrelated. Yet $Y$ is completely determined by $X$ (a perfect functional relationship), so they are maximally dependent.

Conditional independence. We write $X \perp Y \mid Z$ to mean that $X$ and $Y$ are independent once we condition on $Z$:

$$f_{X,Y|Z}(x, y \mid z) = f_{X|Z}(x \mid z) \cdot f_{Y|Z}(y \mid z)$$

Conditional independence is the foundation of graphical models (Bayesian networks and Markov random fields), which compactly encode complex joint distributions by specifying conditional independence relationships.

Covariance and Correlation

While independence is a strong property (all aspects of the joint distribution factor), covariance captures a weaker but very useful notion: linear association.

$$\boxed{\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E\bigl[(X - \mu_X)(Y - \mu_Y)\bigr] = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y]}$$

Interpretation: Covariance measures whether $X$ and $Y$ tend to deviate from their means in the same direction (positive covariance) or opposite directions (negative covariance). If $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = 0$, there is no linear tendency (but possibly nonlinear dependence).

Properties of covariance:

  • $\text{Cov}(X, X) = \text{Var}(X)$
  • $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = \text{Cov}(Y, X)$ (symmetric)
  • $\text{Cov}(aX + b, cY + d) = ac \cdot \text{Cov}(X, Y)$ (bilinear)
  • $\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) + 2\text{Cov}(X, Y)$
  • If $X \perp Y$, then $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = 0$

The problem with covariance is that it depends on the scale of $X$ and $Y$. Measuring height in centimeters vs. meters changes the covariance by a factor of 100. The correlation coefficient standardizes:

$$\boxed{\rho(X, Y) = \frac{\text{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y} = \frac{E[XY] - E[X]E[Y]}{\sqrt{\text{Var}(X)\,\text{Var}(Y)}}}$$

Properties of correlation:

  • $-1 \leq \rho(X, Y) \leq 1$ always
  • $\rho = 1$ iff $Y = aX + b$ with $a > 0$ (perfect positive linear relationship)
  • $\rho = -1$ iff $Y = aX + b$ with $a < 0$ (perfect negative linear relationship)
  • $\rho = 0$ means uncorrelated (no linear association)
  • $\rho$ is invariant under positive affine transformations of $X$ or $Y$

Proof that $|\rho| \leq 1$ (via Cauchy–Schwarz).

The Cauchy–Schwarz inequality states that for any random variables $U, V$ with finite second moments:

$$\bigl(E[UV]\bigr)^2 \leq E[U^2] \cdot E[V^2]$$

Let $U = X - \mu_X$ and $V = Y - \mu_Y$. Then:

$$\bigl(\text{Cov}(X,Y)\bigr)^2 = \bigl(E[UV]\bigr)^2 \leq E[U^2] \cdot E[V^2] = \text{Var}(X) \cdot \text{Var}(Y)$$

Dividing both sides by $\text{Var}(X) \cdot \text{Var}(Y)$:

$$\rho^2 \leq 1 \implies |\rho| \leq 1 \quad \square$$

The covariance matrix. For a random vector $\mathbf{X} = (X_1, \ldots, X_n)^\top$, the covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ has entries $\Sigma_{ij} = \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$:

$$\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = E\bigl[(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu})(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^\top\bigr] = \begin{pmatrix} \text{Var}(X_1) & \text{Cov}(X_1, X_2) & \cdots \\ \text{Cov}(X_2, X_1) & \text{Var}(X_2) & \cdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{pmatrix}$$

The covariance matrix is always symmetric and positive semi-definite ($\mathbf{a}^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \mathbf{a} \geq 0$ for all $\mathbf{a}$). In machine learning, the covariance matrix appears in PCA, Gaussian processes, Mahalanobis distance, and the multivariate normal distribution.

Multivariate Transformations

When we define new random variables as functions of existing ones—say $(U, V) = g(X, Y)$—how do we find the joint density of $(U, V)$? The key tool is the Jacobian determinant.

Theorem (Change of Variables). Let $(X, Y)$ have joint density $f_{X,Y}(x, y)$ and let $(U, V) = g(X, Y)$ where $g$ is a one-to-one differentiable transformation with inverse $(X, Y) = g^{-1}(U, V) = (h_1(u,v), h_2(u,v))$. Then:

$$\boxed{f_{U,V}(u, v) = f_{X,Y}\bigl(h_1(u,v), h_2(u,v)\bigr) \cdot |J|}$$

where the Jacobian determinant is:

$$J = \det \begin{pmatrix} \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial v} \\[8pt] \dfrac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \dfrac{\partial y}{\partial v} \end{pmatrix} = \frac{\partial x}{\partial u}\frac{\partial y}{\partial v} - \frac{\partial x}{\partial v}\frac{\partial y}{\partial u}$$

Intuition: The Jacobian $|J|$ is the local "area distortion factor." When transforming from $(x, y)$ to $(u, v)$, a small rectangle $dx \, dy$ gets mapped to a small parallelogram with area $|J| \, du \, dv$. Since probability must be preserved, density must scale inversely with area.

Example 1: Polar coordinates. Let $X, Y$ be independent standard normals. Define $R = \sqrt{X^2 + Y^2}$ (radius) and $\Theta = \arctan(Y/X)$ (angle). The inverse transformation is $X = R\cos\Theta$, $Y = R\sin\Theta$.

$$J = \det \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & r\cos\theta \end{pmatrix} = r\cos^2\theta + r\sin^2\theta = r$$

The joint density of $(X, Y)$ is:

$$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-(x^2+y^2)/2}$$

Substituting and multiplying by $|J| = r$:

$$f_{R,\Theta}(r, \theta) = \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-r^2/2} \cdot r = \underbrace{r \, e^{-r^2/2}}_{\text{Rayleigh}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{1}{2\pi}}_{\text{Uniform}}, \quad r > 0, \; 0 \leq \theta < 2\pi$$

This factors! So $R$ and $\Theta$ are independent, with $R \sim \text{Rayleigh}$ and $\Theta \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 2\pi)$.

Example 2: Sum and difference. Let $U = X + Y$ and $V = X - Y$, so $X = (U+V)/2$ and $Y = (U-V)/2$.

$$J = \det \begin{pmatrix} 1/2 & 1/2 \\ 1/2 & -1/2 \end{pmatrix} = -\frac{1}{2}, \quad |J| = \frac{1}{2}$$
$$f_{U,V}(u,v) = f_{X,Y}\!\left(\frac{u+v}{2}, \frac{u-v}{2}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{2}$$

This is the starting point for deriving the distribution of $X + Y$ (the convolution formula): marginalize out $V$ to get $f_U(u) = \int f_{X,Y}\!\left(\frac{u+v}{2}, \frac{u-v}{2}\right) \frac{1}{2} \, dv$.

The Multinomial Distribution

The multinomial distribution generalizes the binomial to more than two categories. In $n$ independent trials, each trial results in one of $k$ categories with probabilities $p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_k$ ($\sum p_i = 1$). The joint PMF of the counts $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_k$ (where $X_i$ = number of outcomes in category $i$) is:

$$\boxed{P(X_1 = x_1, \ldots, X_k = x_k) = \frac{n!}{x_1! \, x_2! \cdots x_k!} \, p_1^{x_1} p_2^{x_2} \cdots p_k^{x_k}}$$

where $x_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_k = n$ and each $x_i \geq 0$.

Properties:

  • Marginals: Each $X_i \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p_i)$
  • Covariance: $\text{Cov}(X_i, X_j) = -n p_i p_j$ for $i \neq j$ (always negative!)
  • Conditional: $(X_2, \ldots, X_k) \mid X_1 = x_1 \sim \text{Multinomial}\!\left(n - x_1; \frac{p_2}{1-p_1}, \ldots, \frac{p_k}{1-p_1}\right)$
  • Aggregation: If we merge categories $i$ and $j$, the resulting count $X_i + X_j$ has the correct marginal in the reduced multinomial.

Example. Roll a fair die $n = 60$ times. The counts of each face $(X_1, \ldots, X_6) \sim \text{Multinomial}(60; 1/6, \ldots, 1/6)$. Each $X_i \sim \text{Binomial}(60, 1/6)$ with $E[X_i] = 10$, $\text{Var}(X_i) = 50/6 \approx 8.33$. The covariance between any two face counts is $\text{Cov}(X_i, X_j) = -60 \cdot (1/6)^2 = -10/6 \approx -1.67$.

The multinomial distribution is central to natural language processing (word count models, topic models like LDA), genetics (allele frequencies), and categorical data analysis (chi-squared tests).

Order Statistics

Given $n$ i.i.d. random variables $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ with common CDF $F$ and PDF $f$, the order statistics $X_{(1)} \leq X_{(2)} \leq \cdots \leq X_{(n)}$ are the sorted values. The $k$th order statistic $X_{(k)}$ is the $k$th smallest value.

CDF of $X_{(k)}$:

$$F_{X_{(k)}}(x) = P(X_{(k)} \leq x) = \sum_{j=k}^{n} \binom{n}{j} [F(x)]^j [1 - F(x)]^{n-j}$$

This is because $X_{(k)} \leq x$ iff at least $k$ of the $n$ values are $\leq x$.

PDF of $X_{(k)}$:

$$\boxed{f_{X_{(k)}}(x) = \frac{n!}{(k-1)!(n-k)!} [F(x)]^{k-1} [1-F(x)]^{n-k} f(x)}$$

Intuition: For $X_{(k)}$ to be near $x$, exactly $k-1$ of the $n$ values must be below $x$ (probability $[F(x)]^{k-1}$), exactly one must be at $x$ (density $f(x)$), and the remaining $n - k$ must be above $x$ (probability $[1-F(x)]^{n-k}$). The combinatorial factor counts the orderings.

Special cases:

  • Minimum: $f_{X_{(1)}}(x) = n[1 - F(x)]^{n-1} f(x)$
  • Maximum: $f_{X_{(n)}}(x) = n[F(x)]^{n-1} f(x)$
  • Median: For odd $n$, $X_{((n+1)/2)}$

Joint density of all order statistics:

$$f_{X_{(1)}, \ldots, X_{(n)}}(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = n! \prod_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i), \quad x_1 < x_2 < \cdots < x_n$$

Joint density of two order statistics: For $1 \leq j < k \leq n$:

$$f_{X_{(j)}, X_{(k)}}(x, y) = \frac{n!}{(j-1)!(k-j-1)!(n-k)!} [F(x)]^{j-1} [F(y) - F(x)]^{k-j-1} [1-F(y)]^{n-k} f(x)f(y)$$

for $x < y$.

Example. Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ i.i.d., so $F(x) = x$ and $f(x) = 1$ for $x \in [0, 1]$. Then:

$$f_{X_{(k)}}(x) = \frac{n!}{(k-1)!(n-k)!} x^{k-1}(1-x)^{n-k} = \frac{x^{k-1}(1-x)^{n-k}}{B(k, n-k+1)}$$

This is the $\text{Beta}(k, n - k + 1)$ density! The $k$th order statistic of $n$ uniform random variables follows a Beta distribution.

Connections to Machine Learning

Joint distributions are not merely theoretical—they are the language in which modern machine learning models are written. Here we highlight the most important connections.

1. Generative models. A generative model defines a joint distribution $p(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z})$ over observed data $\mathbf{x}$ and latent variables $\mathbf{z}$. The goal is typically to compute the posterior $p(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})$ or the marginal likelihood $p(\mathbf{x}) = \int p(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z}) \, d\mathbf{z}$. Examples include:

  • Variational Autoencoders (VAEs): $p(\mathbf{z})p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z})$
  • Gaussian Mixture Models: $p(z) = \pi_k$, $p(\mathbf{x} \mid z=k) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)$
  • Latent Dirichlet Allocation: Joint over topics and words

2. The multivariate Gaussian. The $d$-dimensional Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})$ with density:

$$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}|^{1/2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})\right)$$

is completely specified by its mean vector $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$. The multivariate Gaussian is central to:

  • PCA: Finding the directions of maximum variance (eigenvectors of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$)
  • Gaussian Processes: Defining distributions over functions via the kernel (covariance function)
  • Kalman Filters: State estimation with Gaussian joint distributions over states and observations
  • Linear Discriminant Analysis: Modeling class-conditional densities as Gaussians

3. Copulas. A copula $C(u_1, \ldots, u_d)$ is a joint CDF on $[0,1]^d$ with uniform marginals. Sklar's theorem states that any joint CDF $F$ can be written as:

$$F(x_1, \ldots, x_d) = C\bigl(F_1(x_1), \ldots, F_d(x_d)\bigr)$$

This separates the marginal distributions from the dependence structure. Copulas are used in finance (modeling correlated asset returns), climate science, and survival analysis.

4. Feature independence and Naive Bayes. The Naive Bayes classifier assumes that features $X_1, \ldots, X_d$ are conditionally independent given the class $Y$:

$$p(x_1, \ldots, x_d \mid y) = \prod_{j=1}^d p(x_j \mid y)$$

This reduces the number of parameters from exponential in $d$ (full joint) to linear in $d$. Despite this strong assumption, Naive Bayes often performs surprisingly well in practice, especially for text classification.

5. The chain rule and autoregressive models. Any joint distribution factors via the chain rule of probability:

$$p(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) = p(x_1) \cdot p(x_2 \mid x_1) \cdot p(x_3 \mid x_1, x_2) \cdots p(x_n \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1})$$

Autoregressive models (GPT, WaveNet, PixelCNN) parameterize each conditional factor with a neural network, building up the full joint distribution one variable at a time. This is how large language models generate text: each token is sampled from the conditional given all previous tokens.

Code: Joint Distributions in Python

Computing joint PMFs, simulating bivariate normals, extracting marginal and conditional distributions from data, testing independence, and implementing the Box–Muller transform via the Jacobian method.

1. Joint PMF Computation and Visualization

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def joint_pmf_dice(n_sides=6):
    """
    Compute the joint PMF of X = min(D1, D2) and Y = max(D1, D2)
    for two fair dice with n_sides faces.

    Returns:
        pmf: (n_sides, n_sides) array where pmf[i, j] = P(X=i+1, Y=j+1)
    """
    pmf = np.zeros((n_sides, n_sides))
    total = n_sides ** 2

    for d1 in range(1, n_sides + 1):
        for d2 in range(1, n_sides + 1):
            x = min(d1, d2)
            y = max(d1, d2)
            pmf[x - 1, y - 1] += 1 / total

    return pmf

# Compute and display joint PMF
pmf = joint_pmf_dice()
print("Joint PMF of (min, max) for two fair dice:")
print("Rows = X (min), Columns = Y (max)\n")

# Pretty-print as fractions
for i in range(6):
    row = []
    for j in range(6):
        val = pmf[i, j]
        if val == 0:

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats

def bivariate_normal_pdf(x, y, mu_x=0, mu_y=0, sigma_x=1, sigma_y=1, rho=0):
    """
    Compute the bivariate normal PDF at point (x, y).

    f(x,y) = (1 / (2*pi*sx*sy*sqrt(1-rho^2))) *
             exp(-Q / (2*(1-rho^2)))

    where Q = (x-mx)^2/sx^2 - 2*rho*(x-mx)*(y-my)/(sx*sy) + (y-my)^2/sy^2
    """
    z_x = (x - mu_x) / sigma_x
    z_y = (y - mu_y) / sigma_y
    Q = z_x**2 - 2*rho*z_x*z_y + z_y**2
    norm = 2 * np.pi * sigma_x * sigma_y * np.sqrt(1 - rho**2)
    return np.exp(-Q / (2 * (1 - rho**2))) / norm

5. Jacobian Transformation: Box–Muller Transform

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats

def box_muller(n):
    """
    Generate n pairs of independent standard normals using the
    Box-Muller transform.

    Theory: If U1, U2 ~ Uniform(0,1) independently, then
        Z1 = sqrt(-2 ln U1) * cos(2*pi*U2)
        Z2 = sqrt(-2 ln U1) * sin(2*pi*U2)
    are independent standard normals.

    This follows from the polar coordinate Jacobian:
        (X, Y) standard normal  <-->  (R, Theta) where
        R = sqrt(-2 ln U1) ~ Rayleigh,  Theta = 2*pi*U2 ~ Uniform(0, 2*pi)

    The Jacobian |d(x,y)/d(r,theta)| = r, which gives:
        f(r, theta) = (1/2*pi) * r * exp(-r^2/2)
    """
    U1 = np.random.uniform(0, 1, n)
    U2 = np.random.uniform(0, 1, n)

    R = np.sqrt(-2 * np.log(U1))
    Theta = 2 * np.pi * U2

    Z1 = R * np.cos(Theta)
    Z2 = R * np.sin(Theta)
    return Z1, Z2

For the joint PDF $f(x, y) = x + y$ on $[0, 1]^2$: (a) compute $E[XY]$, (b) compute $\text{Cov}(X, Y)$, (c) compute the correlation coefficient $\rho(X, Y)$.

on the diamond region described above.

$$Q(y) = \frac{x^2 - 2\rho xy + y^2}{2(1-\rho^2)}$$

Exercise 14: The Law of Total Variance

Prove the law of total variance (Eve's Law): $\text{Var}(Y) = E[\text{Var}(Y \mid X)] + \text{Var}(E[Y \mid X])$. Then apply it: if $N \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$ and $Y \mid N = n \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p)$, find $\text{Var}(Y)$.

Show Solution

Proof. Let $\mu(X) = E[Y \mid X]$ and $v(X) = \text{Var}(Y \mid X) = E[Y^2 \mid X] - (E[Y \mid X])^2$.

By the tower property:

$$E[Y] = E[\mu(X)], \qquad E[Y^2] = E[E[Y^2 \mid X]]$$

Now:

$$\text{Var}(Y) = E[Y^2] - (E[Y])^2 = E[E[Y^2 \mid X]] - (E[\mu(X)])^2$$

From the definition of $v(X)$: $E[Y^2 \mid X] = v(X) + \mu(X)^2$. So:

$$\text{Var}(Y) = E[v(X) + \mu(X)^2] - (E[\mu(X)])^2 = E[v(X)] + E[\mu(X)^2] - (E[\mu(X)])^2$$ $$= E[\text{Var}(Y \mid X)] + \text{Var}(\mu(X)) = E[\text{Var}(Y \mid X)] + \text{Var}(E[Y \mid X]) \quad \square$$

Application. $Y \mid N \sim \text{Binomial}(N, p)$:

  • $E[Y \mid N] = Np$, so $\text{Var}(E[Y \mid N]) = \text{Var}(Np) = p^2\text{Var}(N) = p^2\lambda$
  • $\text{Var}(Y \mid N) = Np(1-p)$, so $E[\text{Var}(Y \mid N)] = E[Np(1-p)] = p(1-p)\lambda$
$$\text{Var}(Y) = p(1-p)\lambda + p^2\lambda = p\lambda(1-p+p) = \boxed{p\lambda}$$

In fact, $Y \sim \text{Poisson}(p\lambda)$ (the thinning property of Poisson processes). $\square$

(b) For a multivariate Gaussian, the joint density is:

$$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}|^{1/2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})\right)$$

If $X_1$ and $X_2$ are uncorrelated, $\Sigma_{12} = 0$, so $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \text{diag}(\sigma_1^2, \sigma_2^2)$ and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} = \text{diag}(1/\sigma_1^2, 1/\sigma_2^2)$.

The quadratic form becomes:

$$(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}) = \frac{(x_1-\mu_1)^2}{\sigma_1^2} + \frac{(x_2-\mu_2)^2}{\sigma_2^2}$$

And $|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}| = \sigma_1^2\sigma_2^2$. Therefore:

$$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_1\sigma_2}\exp\!\left(-\frac{(x_1-\mu_1)^2}{2\sigma_1^2} - \frac{(x_2-\mu_2)^2}{2\sigma_2^2}\right) = f_{X_1}(x_1) \cdot f_{X_2}(x_2)$$

The joint factors, so $X_1 \perp X_2$. $\square$

(c) We need $A\boldsymbol{\Sigma}A^\top$ to be diagonal. Use eigendecomposition: $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = Q\Lambda Q^\top$.

$$\det(\boldsymbol{\Sigma} - \lambda I) = (4-\lambda)(9-\lambda) - 4 = \lambda^2 - 13\lambda + 32 = 0$$ $$\lambda = \frac{13 \pm \sqrt{169 - 128}}{2} = \frac{13 \pm \sqrt{41}}{2} \approx 9.70, \; 3.30$$

Setting $A = Q^\top$ (the matrix of eigenvectors as rows) gives $\mathbf{Y} = Q^\top\mathbf{X}$ with $\text{Cov}(\mathbf{Y}) = Q^\top\boldsymbol{\Sigma}Q = \Lambda$, which is diagonal. So $Y_1 \perp Y_2$.

This is precisely PCA: projecting onto the principal axes decorrelates the variables. For Gaussians, decorrelation equals independence. $\square$

Key Concept

The joint distribution of random variables captures everything about their simultaneous behavior. From the joint, you can derive marginals, conditionals, independence, and any function of the variables.

Prerequisites

  • Random variables (PMF, PDF, CDF)
  • Expectation and variance
  • Multivariate calculus (double integrals)
  • Linear algebra basics (matrices, determinants)

Key Formulas

Joint PMF: $p(x,y) = P(X\!=\!x, Y\!=\!y)$

Joint PDF: $P(A) = \iint_A f(x,y)\,dx\,dy$

Marginal: $f_X(x) = \int f(x,y)\,dy$

Conditional: $f(y|x) = \frac{f(x,y)}{f_X(x)}$

Covariance: $\text{Cov} = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y]$

Correlation: $\rho = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y}$

Jacobian: $f_{U,V} = f_{X,Y} \cdot |J|$
