02. Axioms of Probability

Probability theory, as a mathematical discipline, rests on a remarkably small set of axioms laid down by Andrey Kolmogorov in 1933. From just three simple rules—non-negativity, normalization, and countable additivity—the entire edifice of modern probability, statistics, and probabilistic machine learning follows. This tutorial builds the axiomatic framework from the ground up: sample spaces, events, sigma-algebras, the three axioms, and the powerful theorems they entail.

Why Axiomatize Probability?

For centuries, probability was understood informally—as the ratio of "favorable outcomes" to "total outcomes" (the classical definition due to Laplace), or as the long-run relative frequency of an event (the frequentist interpretation). These intuitions served well for coin flips, dice, and card games, but they crumbled when confronted with deeper questions:

  • What is the probability of a single point on a continuous interval? If we pick a real number uniformly at random from $[0,1]$, each point has probability zero, yet the total probability is one. How do we reconcile this?
  • Can we assign probabilities to every subset of $[0,1]$? Surprisingly, no—doing so leads to contradictions (the Vitali set construction). We need a principled way to say which sets are "measurable."
  • What does "equally likely" even mean? The classical definition is circular: it defines probability in terms of equally likely outcomes, but "equally likely" presupposes a notion of probability.

In 1900, David Hilbert posed his famous list of 23 open problems. The sixth problem called for the axiomatization of physics, and probability—as the mathematical language of uncertainty in physics—was a key part of that program. Paradoxes like the Bertrand paradox (1889), where different "natural" assumptions about randomness yield different answers, made it clear that probability needed rigorous foundations.

In 1933, Andrey Nikolaevich Kolmogorov published Grundbegriffe der Wahrscheinlichkeitsrechnung (Foundations of the Theory of Probability), which provided exactly such foundations. His approach was to build probability theory on top of measure theory, treating probability as a special case of a measure. This turned probability from a collection of clever tricks into a branch of rigorous mathematics, enabling the proofs of limit theorems, the development of stochastic processes, and ultimately the mathematical foundations of machine learning.

Sample Spaces

Every probabilistic model begins with a sample space.

Definition (Sample Space).

The sample space $\Omega$ is the set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment. Each element $\omega \in \Omega$ is called a sample point or outcome.

The sample space must be exhaustive (every conceivable outcome is included) and mutually exclusive (no two outcomes can occur simultaneously). Here are key examples:

Experiment Sample Space $\Omega$ Type
Single coin flip $\{H, T\}$ Finite
Roll a six-sided die $\{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$ Finite
Two coin flips $\{HH, HT, TH, TT\}$ Finite
Flip until first Head $\{H, TH, TTH, TTTH, \ldots\}$ Countably infinite
Pick a random number in $[0,1]$ $[0, 1]$ Uncountable
Lifetime of a lightbulb $[0, \infty)$ Uncountable
Infinite coin flips $\{H, T\}^{\mathbb{N}}$ Uncountable

The distinction between countable and uncountable sample spaces is fundamental. A set is countable if its elements can be put in one-to-one correspondence with (a subset of) the natural numbers $\mathbb{N}$. The set $\mathbb{R}$ (or any interval) is uncountable—as Cantor's diagonal argument famously proves—and this uncountability is precisely what forces us to introduce sigma-algebras.

Events and Sigma-Algebras

An event is a subset of the sample space: $A \subseteq \Omega$. We say event $A$ occurs if the observed outcome $\omega$ belongs to $A$. For instance, if $\Omega = \{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$ for a die roll, then $A = \{2, 4, 6\}$ is the event "the roll is even."

For finite or countable sample spaces, we can safely assign probabilities to every subset. But for uncountable spaces like $[0,1]$, this is impossible without contradictions. The solution is to restrict our attention to a well-behaved collection of subsets called a sigma-algebra (or $\sigma$-algebra).

Definition ($\sigma$-Algebra).

A collection $\mathcal{F}$ of subsets of $\Omega$ is a $\sigma$-algebra (sigma-algebra) if it satisfies:

  1. Contains $\Omega$: $\Omega \in \mathcal{F}$
  2. Closed under complement: If $A \in \mathcal{F}$, then $A^c = \Omega \setminus A \in \mathcal{F}$
  3. Closed under countable unions: If $A_1, A_2, A_3, \ldots \in \mathcal{F}$, then $\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i \in \mathcal{F}$

From these three properties, several consequences follow immediately:

  • $\varnothing \in \mathcal{F}$ (since $\varnothing = \Omega^c$ and $\mathcal{F}$ is closed under complements)
  • $\mathcal{F}$ is closed under countable intersections (by De Morgan's laws: $\bigcap A_i = (\bigcup A_i^c)^c$)
  • $\mathcal{F}$ is closed under set differences: $A \setminus B = A \cap B^c \in \mathcal{F}$

Examples of $\sigma$-algebras:

  1. Trivial $\sigma$-algebra: $\mathcal{F} = \{\varnothing, \Omega\}$. The smallest possible $\sigma$-algebra—we can only distinguish "something happened" from "nothing happened."
  2. Power set: $\mathcal{F} = 2^\Omega = \{A : A \subseteq \Omega\}$. The largest possible $\sigma$-algebra—every subset is an event. This works fine when $\Omega$ is countable.
  3. Borel $\sigma$-algebra: $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ is the $\sigma$-algebra generated by all open intervals $(a,b)$. It contains all open sets, closed sets, countable unions and intersections thereof, but does not contain every subset of $\mathbb{R}$ (e.g., Vitali sets are excluded). This is the standard $\sigma$-algebra for continuous probability.
  4. Generated $\sigma$-algebra: Given any collection $\mathcal{C}$ of subsets of $\Omega$, $\sigma(\mathcal{C})$ denotes the smallest $\sigma$-algebra containing $\mathcal{C}$. For instance, $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}) = \sigma(\{(a,b) : a < b\})$.

Figure 1. The sample space $\Omega$ with events $A$ and $B$. Individual outcomes $\omega_i$ are sample points. The $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F}$ determines which subsets of $\Omega$ we can assign probabilities to.

Why not just use the power set? For finite and countably infinite $\Omega$, we can use $\mathcal{F} = 2^\Omega$. The problem arises for uncountable spaces. In 1905, Giuseppe Vitali showed that if we assign a translation-invariant measure (like Lebesgue measure) to every subset of $[0,1]$, we get a contradiction with the axiom of choice. The Banach-Tarski paradox provides an even more dramatic illustration in $\mathbb{R}^3$. The $\sigma$-algebra is the precise mathematical device that avoids these pathologies by restricting which sets we consider "measurable."

Kolmogorov's Three Axioms

With sample spaces and $\sigma$-algebras in hand, we can now state the axioms. A probability measure is a function $P: \mathcal{F} \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying the following three axioms:

Kolmogorov's Axioms (1933)

Axiom 1 (Non-negativity): For every event $A \in \mathcal{F}$,

$$P(A) \geq 0$$

Axiom 2 (Normalization): The probability of the entire sample space is one:

$$P(\Omega) = 1$$

Axiom 3 (Countable Additivity / $\sigma$-additivity): For any countable sequence of pairwise disjoint events $A_1, A_2, A_3, \ldots \in \mathcal{F}$ (i.e., $A_i \cap A_j = \varnothing$ for $i \neq j$),

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} P(A_i)$$

The triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ is called a probability space. Every rigorous probabilistic statement is a statement about some probability space.

Figure 2. A visual summary of Kolmogorov's three axioms.

Why countable additivity? One might ask whether finite additivity (the rule holding only for finite unions) would suffice. It would not: without countable additivity, we cannot prove the continuity of probability (limits of nested events), the law of large numbers, or the central limit theorem. Countable additivity is what connects the finite world to the infinite, and the infinite is where all the deep theorems live.

Fundamental Properties Derived from the Axioms

A remarkable amount follows from just three axioms. Here we derive the most important properties, showing each proof step explicitly.

Property 1: $P(\varnothing) = 0$

Proof. Write $\Omega = \Omega \cup \varnothing \cup \varnothing \cup \cdots$ (a countable union of disjoint sets). By Axiom 3:

$$P(\Omega) = P(\Omega) + P(\varnothing) + P(\varnothing) + \cdots = P(\Omega) + \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} P(\varnothing)$$

Since $P(\Omega) = 1$ (Axiom 2) is finite, we can subtract it from both sides to get $\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} P(\varnothing) = 0$. Since $P(\varnothing) \geq 0$ (Axiom 1), the only possibility is $P(\varnothing) = 0$. $\square$

Property 2: Complement Rule

For any event $A \in \mathcal{F}$:

$$P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$$

Proof. Since $A$ and $A^c$ are disjoint and $A \cup A^c = \Omega$, Axiom 3 gives $P(A) + P(A^c) = P(\Omega) = 1$. $\square$

Property 3: Monotonicity

If $A \subseteq B$, then $P(A) \leq P(B)$.

Proof. Write $B = A \cup (B \setminus A)$ where $A$ and $B \setminus A$ are disjoint. Then $P(B) = P(A) + P(B \setminus A) \geq P(A)$ since $P(B \setminus A) \geq 0$ by Axiom 1. $\square$

Property 4: Probability is Bounded

For any event $A$: $0 \leq P(A) \leq 1$.

Proof. $P(A) \geq 0$ by Axiom 1. Since $A \subseteq \Omega$, monotonicity gives $P(A) \leq P(\Omega) = 1$. $\square$

Property 5: Inclusion-Exclusion (Two Events)

For any events $A, B \in \mathcal{F}$:

$$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$$

Proof. Decompose $A \cup B$ into three disjoint parts: $A \setminus B$, $A \cap B$, and $B \setminus A$. Then:

$$P(A \cup B) = P(A \setminus B) + P(A \cap B) + P(B \setminus A)$$

Also, $P(A) = P(A \setminus B) + P(A \cap B)$ and $P(B) = P(B \setminus A) + P(A \cap B)$. Therefore:

$$P(A) + P(B) = P(A \setminus B) + 2P(A \cap B) + P(B \setminus A)$$

Subtracting $P(A \cap B)$ from $P(A) + P(B)$ yields $P(A \cup B)$. $\square$

Property 6: Sub-additivity (Union Bound)

For any events $A_1, A_2, \ldots$ (not necessarily disjoint):

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} A_i\right) \leq \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A_i)$$

Proof (by induction). The base case $n=1$ is trivial. For the inductive step, assume the result holds for $n-1$ events. Then:

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} A_i\right) = P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n-1} A_i\right) + P(A_n) - P\!\left(\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n-1} A_i\right) \cap A_n\right) \leq P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n-1} A_i\right) + P(A_n) \leq \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A_i)$$

The union bound is heavily used in machine learning, particularly in bounding the probability of any classifier in a hypothesis class making an error (VC theory and PAC learning).

Probability as a Measure

Kolmogorov's key insight was that probability is a special case of a measure. In measure theory, a measure $\mu$ on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ is a function $\mu: \mathcal{F} \to [0, \infty]$ satisfying:

  1. $\mu(\varnothing) = 0$
  2. Countable additivity: $\mu(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i) = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \mu(A_i)$ for disjoint $A_i$

A probability measure is simply a measure with the additional constraint that $\mu(\Omega) = 1$. This makes $P$ a finite, normalized measure.

The connection to measure theory brings powerful tools:

Measure Theory Probability Theory
Measurable space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ Sample space with events
Measure $\mu$ Probability measure $P$
Measurable function Random variable
Lebesgue integral $\int f \, d\mu$ Expectation $E[X] = \int X \, dP$
$\mu$-almost everywhere Almost surely (a.s.)
Radon-Nikodym derivative $\frac{d\nu}{d\mu}$ Probability density function (pdf)

The Lebesgue measure $\lambda$ on $\mathbb{R}$ assigns to each interval its length: $\lambda([a,b]) = b - a$. It serves as the "reference measure" for continuous distributions. A probability density function $f(x)$ then defines a probability measure via:

$$P(A) = \int_A f(x) \, dx = \int_A f(x) \, d\lambda(x)$$

The density $f$ is precisely the Radon-Nikodym derivative $\frac{dP}{d\lambda}$—the ratio of the probability measure to the Lebesgue measure. This interpretation is essential in variational inference, normalizing flows, and density estimation in ML.

Discrete Probability Spaces

When $\Omega$ is finite or countably infinite, probability theory simplifies considerably. We take $\mathcal{F} = 2^\Omega$ (the power set) and define $P$ through a probability mass function (pmf) $p: \Omega \to [0,1]$ satisfying:

$$\sum_{\omega \in \Omega} p(\omega) = 1$$

The probability of any event $A \subseteq \Omega$ is then:

$$P(A) = \sum_{\omega \in A} p(\omega)$$

Classical probability (Laplace's definition): When all outcomes are equally likely, $p(\omega) = 1/|\Omega|$ for all $\omega$, and:

$$P(A) = \frac{|A|}{|\Omega|} = \frac{\text{number of favorable outcomes}}{\text{total number of outcomes}}$$

Examples:

  • Fair die: $\Omega = \{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$, $p(k) = 1/6$ for each $k$. $P(\text{even}) = P(\{2,4,6\}) = 3/6 = 1/2$.
  • Two fair coins: $\Omega = \{HH, HT, TH, TT\}$, $p(\omega) = 1/4$. $P(\text{at least one head}) = 3/4$.
  • Geometric distribution: Flip a coin with $P(H) = p$ until the first head. $\Omega = \{1, 2, 3, \ldots\}$ (countably infinite), $P(X = k) = (1-p)^{k-1} p$. Verify: $\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} (1-p)^{k-1} p = p \cdot \frac{1}{1-(1-p)} = 1$. The axioms are satisfied.

Continuous Probability

When $\Omega$ is uncountable (e.g., $\mathbb{R}$, $[0,1]$, $\mathbb{R}^n$), we face a fundamental challenge: for any continuous distribution, $P(\{\omega\}) = 0$ for every individual outcome $\omega$. Probabilities are meaningful only for intervals and more general Borel sets.

Why individual points have probability zero: Suppose $P(\{\omega\}) = \varepsilon > 0$ for some $\omega \in [0,1]$ under a "uniform" distribution. Then for any $n$ distinct points, $P(\{\omega_1, \ldots, \omega_n\}) \geq n\varepsilon$. Choosing $n > 1/\varepsilon$ gives $P > 1$, contradicting Axiom 2. So $P(\{\omega\}) = 0$ for all $\omega$.

For continuous distributions, probability is specified through a probability density function (pdf) $f: \mathbb{R} \to [0, \infty)$ satisfying:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \, dx = 1$$

The probability of an interval is:

$$P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f(x) \, dx$$

Note that $f(x)$ itself is not a probability—it can exceed 1. For example, the uniform distribution on $[0, 1/2]$ has $f(x) = 2$ for $x \in [0, 1/2]$. Only when integrated over a set does $f$ yield a probability.

The Vitali set argument (sketch): Assume we can define a translation-invariant probability measure $P$ on all subsets of $[0,1)$. Define an equivalence relation $x \sim y$ iff $x - y \in \mathbb{Q}$. By the axiom of choice, select one representative from each equivalence class to form a set $V$ (the Vitali set). Then the countable translates $V + q$ (mod 1) for $q \in \mathbb{Q} \cap [0,1)$ are disjoint and cover $[0,1)$. By countable additivity, $1 = \sum_{q} P(V + q) = \sum_{q} P(V)$. If $P(V) = 0$, the sum is $0 \neq 1$. If $P(V) > 0$, the sum is $\infty \neq 1$. Contradiction. Hence, $V$ is not measurable, and we must restrict to a $\sigma$-algebra.

Properties of the Cumulative Distribution Function

The cumulative distribution function (CDF) provides a unified description of probability distributions that works for both discrete and continuous cases:

Definition (CDF).

The CDF of a random variable $X$ is the function $F: \mathbb{R} \to [0,1]$ defined by:

$$F(x) = P(X \leq x)$$

Every CDF satisfies these fundamental properties (all derivable from the axioms):

  1. Non-decreasing: If $a \leq b$, then $F(a) \leq F(b)$.
    Proof: $\{X \leq a\} \subseteq \{X \leq b\}$, so monotonicity gives $F(a) = P(X \leq a) \leq P(X \leq b) = F(b)$.
  2. Right-continuous: $\lim_{x \to a^+} F(x) = F(a)$.
    Proof: This follows from continuity of probability applied to the decreasing sequence $\{X \leq a + 1/n\} \downarrow \{X \leq a\}$.
  3. Limits at infinity: $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to -\infty} F(x) = 0$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to +\infty} F(x) = 1$.
    Proof: $\{X \leq -n\} \downarrow \varnothing$ as $n \to \infty$, so $F(-n) \to P(\varnothing) = 0$. Similarly, $\{X \leq n\} \uparrow \Omega$, so $F(n) \to 1$.
  4. Interval probabilities: $$P(a < X \leq b) = F(b) - F(a)$$ This is because $\{X \leq b\} = \{X \leq a\} \cup \{a < X \leq b\}$ (disjoint union).

Important subtlety: For continuous random variables, $P(X = a) = 0$ for any single value $a$, so $P(a \leq X \leq b) = P(a < X < b) = F(b) - F(a)$. For discrete random variables, these can differ: $P(X = a) = F(a) - F(a^-)$ where $F(a^-)$ is the left-hand limit.

When $X$ has a density $f$, the CDF and pdf are related by:

$$F(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(t) \, dt \qquad \text{and} \qquad f(x) = F'(x) \text{ (wherever } F \text{ is differentiable)}$$

Inclusion-Exclusion Principle

We proved the two-event formula $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$ above. The inclusion-exclusion principle generalizes this to any finite number of events.

Theorem (Inclusion-Exclusion).

For events $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$:

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} A_i\right) = \sum_{i} P(A_i) - \sum_{i < j} P(A_i \cap A_j) + \sum_{i < j < k} P(A_i \cap A_j \cap A_k) - \cdots + (-1)^{n+1} P(A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_n)$$

More compactly:

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} A_i\right) = \sum_{\varnothing \neq S \subseteq \{1,\ldots,n\}} (-1)^{|S|+1} P\!\left(\bigcap_{i \in S} A_i\right)$$

Proof by induction. The base case $n=1$ is trivial, and $n=2$ was proved above. For the inductive step, let $B = \bigcup_{i=1}^{n-1} A_i$. Then:

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} A_i\right) = P(B \cup A_n) = P(B) + P(A_n) - P(B \cap A_n)$$

Now $B \cap A_n = \bigcup_{i=1}^{n-1} (A_i \cap A_n)$. Applying the inductive hypothesis to both $P(B)$ and $P(B \cap A_n)$, and collecting terms carefully, yields the formula for $n$ events. $\square$

Figure 3. Inclusion-exclusion for three sets. We add the individual probabilities, subtract the pairwise intersections (which were double-counted), then add back the triple intersection (which was over-subtracted).

Counting interpretation: Inclusion-exclusion is deeply connected to combinatorics. The number of elements in $A_1 \cup \cdots \cup A_n$ follows the same alternating-sum formula with cardinalities replacing probabilities. This connection is used in counting derangements, solving the hat-check problem, and deriving Euler's totient function.

Continuity of Probability

One of the most powerful consequences of countable additivity is that probability behaves continuously with respect to limits of events.

Theorem (Continuity from Below).

If $A_1 \subseteq A_2 \subseteq A_3 \subseteq \cdots$ (an increasing sequence), then:

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i\right) = \lim_{n \to \infty} P(A_n)$$

Proof. Define $B_1 = A_1$ and $B_n = A_n \setminus A_{n-1}$ for $n \geq 2$. The $B_n$ are pairwise disjoint and $\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n = \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n$. By countable additivity:

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n\right) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(B_n) = \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{n=1}^{N} P(B_n) = \lim_{N \to \infty} P(A_N)$$

where the last equality uses the telescoping property $\sum_{n=1}^{N} P(B_n) = P(A_N)$. $\square$

Theorem (Continuity from Above).

If $A_1 \supseteq A_2 \supseteq A_3 \supseteq \cdots$ (a decreasing sequence), then:

$$P\!\left(\bigcap_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i\right) = \lim_{n \to \infty} P(A_n)$$

Proof. Apply continuity from below to the increasing sequence $A_n^c$, then use the complement rule and De Morgan's law: $P(\bigcap A_n) = 1 - P(\bigcup A_n^c) = 1 - \lim P(A_n^c) = \lim P(A_n)$. $\square$

Application: Let $A_n = \{|X| > n\}$ for a random variable $X$. Then $A_1 \supseteq A_2 \supseteq \cdots$ and $\bigcap A_n = \varnothing$ (no real number exceeds all integers). By continuity from above, $P(|X| > n) \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$. This is the foundation of tail bounds, which are central to concentration inequalities in learning theory.

Borel-Cantelli Lemma (a preview): Continuity of probability also leads to the Borel-Cantelli lemma. If $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n) < \infty$, then the probability that infinitely many of the $A_n$ occur is zero:

$$P\!\left(\limsup_{n \to \infty} A_n\right) = P\!\left(\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} \bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_k\right) = 0$$

This is proved by applying continuity from above to $B_n = \bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_k$ and using the union bound: $P(B_n) \leq \sum_{k=n}^{\infty} P(A_k) \to 0$.

Connections to Machine Learning

The axioms of probability are not abstract curiosities—they are the bedrock on which all of probabilistic machine learning rests. Here are the key connections:

1. PAC Learning and the Union Bound

In Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) learning, we want to bound the probability that any hypothesis in a finite class $\mathcal{H}$ has error exceeding $\varepsilon$. The union bound (sub-additivity) gives:

$$P\!\left(\exists h \in \mathcal{H}: \text{err}(h) > \varepsilon\right) \leq \sum_{h \in \mathcal{H}} P(\text{err}(h) > \varepsilon) \leq |\mathcal{H}| \cdot e^{-2m\varepsilon^2}$$

Setting this $\leq \delta$ and solving for $m$ gives the sample complexity $m \geq \frac{1}{2\varepsilon^2} \ln \frac{|\mathcal{H}|}{\delta}$. Without the axioms guaranteeing sub-additivity, this bound would not be valid.

2. Proper Probability Distributions

Every generative model (VAE, GAN, diffusion model, normalizing flow) must produce outputs that satisfy the axioms. A density $p_\theta(x)$ must integrate to 1 (Axiom 2) and be non-negative everywhere (Axiom 1). Normalizing flows achieve this through invertible transformations with tractable Jacobians; diffusion models do it through the score function and Langevin dynamics.

3. Bayesian Inference

Bayes' theorem $P(\theta | D) = \frac{P(D|\theta) P(\theta)}{P(D)}$ requires that prior, likelihood, and posterior are all valid probability measures. The normalization axiom ensures $\int P(\theta | D) \, d\theta = 1$, which is why computing the evidence $P(D) = \int P(D|\theta) P(\theta) \, d\theta$ (often intractable) is so important.

4. Information Theory

Shannon entropy $H(X) = -\sum_x P(x) \log P(x)$ and KL divergence $D_{\text{KL}}(P \| Q) = \sum_x P(x) \log \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$ are defined in terms of probability measures. The non-negativity of KL divergence ($D_{\text{KL}} \geq 0$) relies on the axioms through Jensen's inequality, which itself requires the properties of expectation derived from the probability axioms.

5. Measure-Theoretic Convergence

Modern ML theory uses several modes of convergence (in probability, almost surely, in distribution, in $L^p$), all defined through the probability measure $P$. The relationships between these modes (e.g., a.s. convergence implies convergence in probability) are theorems of measure theory built on the axioms. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for rigorously analyzing the convergence of stochastic gradient descent, MCMC samplers, and reinforcement learning algorithms.

1. Verifying the Axioms with Empirical Frequencies

We simulate random experiments and check that the axioms hold in the limit of many trials. As the number of trials grows, empirical frequencies converge to true probabilities (a consequence of the law of large numbers, which itself depends on the axioms). 

import numpy as np

def verify_axioms(n_trials=100_000, seed=42):
    """Verify Kolmogorov's axioms with empirical frequencies."""
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)

    # Experiment: roll a fair six-sided die
    rolls = rng.integers(1, 7, size=n_trials)

    # Define some events
    A = np.isin(rolls, [2, 4, 6])     # even numbers
    B = np.isin(rolls, [1, 2, 3])     # at most 3
    omega = np.ones(n_trials, dtype=bool)  # entire sample space
    empty = np.zeros(n_trials, dtype=bool)  # empty event

    # Axiom 1: Non-negativity
    p_A = A.mean()
    p_B = B.mean()
    print(f"Axiom 1 (Non-negativity):")
    print(f"  P(A) = {p_A:.4f} >= 0  [True: {p_A >= 0}]")
    print(f"  P(B) = {p_B:.4f} >= 0  [True: {p_B >= 0}]")

    # Axiom 2: Normalization
    p_omega = omega.mean()
    print(f"\nAxiom 2 (Normalization):")
    print(f"  P(Omega) = {p_omega:.4f}  [Expected: 1.0]")

    # Axiom 3: Countable additivity (finite case)
    # A and B are NOT disjoint, so we create disjoint events:
    C = np.isin(rolls, [1, 3, 5])     # odd numbers
    D = np.isin(rolls, [2, 4, 6])     # even numbers (disjoint from C)
    p_C = C.mean()
    p_D = D.mean()
    p_C_union_D = (C | D).mean()
    print(f"\nAxiom 3 (Additivity for disjoint events):")
    print(f"  P(odd) = {p_C:.4f}")
    print(f"  P(even) = {p_D:.4f}")
    print(f"  P(odd) + P(even) = {p_C + p_D:.4f}")
    print(f"  P(odd union even) = {p_C_union_D:.4f}")
    print(f"  Match: {abs(p_C + p_D - p_C_union_D) < 0.01}")

    # Derived properties

2. General Inclusion-Exclusion

An implementation of the inclusion-exclusion formula for computing $P(A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots \cup A_n)$ from intersection probabilities. 

from itertools import combinations

def inclusion_exclusion(events, n_trials=200_000, seed=42):
    """
    Compute P(A1 ∪ A2 ∪ ... ∪ An) via inclusion-exclusion.

    Parameters:
    -----------
    events : list of np.ndarray (boolean)
        Each array is a boolean mask of shape (n_trials,)
        indicating which trials the event occurred in.

    Returns:
    --------
    float : The probability of the union.
    """
    n = len(events)
    total = 0.0

    for k in range(1, n + 1):
        sign = (-1) ** (k + 1)
        for subset in combinations(range(n), k):
            # Intersection of all events in this subset
            intersection = np.ones(len(events[0]), dtype=bool)
            for i in subset:
                intersection &= events[i]
            total += sign * intersection.mean()

    return total

3. A Discrete Probability Space Class

We build a Python class that formally represents a discrete probability space $(\Omega, 2^\Omega, P)$, enforcing the axioms at construction time. 

class DiscreteProbabilitySpace:
    """
    A discrete probability space (Omega, 2^Omega, P).
    Enforces Kolmogorov's axioms at construction.
    """

    def __init__(self, pmf: dict):
        """
        Parameters:
        -----------
        pmf : dict mapping outcome -> probability
              e.g., {'H': 0.5, 'T': 0.5}
        """
        # Axiom 1: Non-negativity
        for outcome, prob in pmf.items():
            if prob < 0:
                raise ValueError(
                    f"Axiom 1 violated: P({outcome}) = {prob} < 0"
                )

        # Axiom 2: Normalization
        total = sum(pmf.values())
        if abs(total - 1.0) > 1e-10:
            raise ValueError(
                f"Axiom 2 violated: sum of probabilities = {total} != 1"
            )

        self.omega = set(pmf.keys())
        self.pmf = dict(pmf)

    def P(self, event):
        """
        Compute P(A) for event A (a set of outcomes).
        Axiom 3 is automatically satisfied for finite spaces:
        P(A) = sum of P({w}) for w in A.
        """
        if not isinstance(event, (set, frozenset)):
            event = {event}
        if not event.issubset(self.omega):
            raise ValueError(f"Event {event} not subset of Omega")
        return sum(self.pmf[w] for w in event)

    def complement(self, event):
        """Return A^c = Omega \ A."""
        return self.omega - event

    def verify_axioms(self):
        """Explicitly verify all three axioms."""
        print("Verifying Kolmogorov's Axioms:")

        # Axiom 1
        all_nonneg = all(p >= 0 for p in self.pmf.values())
        print(f"  Axiom 1 (Non-negativity): {all_nonneg}")

        # Axiom 2
        total = self.P(self.omega)
        print(f"  Axiom 2 (Normalization): P(Omega) = {total}")

        # Axiom 3 (finite additivity check)
        # Check: P(Omega) = sum of P({w}) for all w
        singleton_sum = sum(self.P({w}) for w in self.omega)
        print(f"  Axiom 3 (Additivity): sum P({{w}}) = {singleton_sum}")
        print(f"  All axioms satisfied: {all_nonneg and abs(total-1)<1e-10}")

    def __repr__(self):
        return f"ProbSpace(Omega={self.omega}, pmf={self.pmf})"

4. Empirical CDF Computation

The empirical CDF $\hat{F}_n(x) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbf{1}(X_i \leq x)$ converges uniformly to the true CDF by the Glivenko-Cantelli theorem. Here we compute and compare empirical vs. theoretical CDFs. 

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def empirical_cdf(samples):
    """
    Compute the empirical CDF from samples.

    Returns:
    --------
    x_sorted : sorted sample values
    ecdf     : cumulative probabilities at each value
    """
    x_sorted = np.sort(samples)
    n = len(x_sorted)
    ecdf = np.arange(1, n + 1) / n
    return x_sorted, ecdf

5. Demonstrating Countable Additivity

Countable additivity allows us to work with countably infinite sample spaces. We demonstrate this with the geometric distribution, where the infinite sum of probabilities converges to 1. 

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def geometric_pmf(k, p):
    """P(X = k) = (1-p)^{k-1} * p for k = 1, 2, 3, ..."""
    return (1 - p) ** (k - 1) * p

def verify_countable_additivity(p=0.3, max_k=100):
    """
    Demonstrate that sum_{k=1}^{infinity} P(X=k) = 1
    for the geometric distribution.
    """
    k_values = np.arange(1, max_k + 1)
    probs = geometric_pmf(k_values, p)

    # Partial sums
    partial_sums = np.cumsum(probs)

    print(f"Geometric distribution with p = {p}")
    print(f"{'k':>5} | {'P(X=k)':>12} | {'Sum_{i=1}^{k} P(X=i)':>22}")
    print("-" * 45)
    for k in [1, 2, 3, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100]:
        idx = k - 1
        print(f"{k:>5} | {probs[idx]:>12.8f} | {partial_sums[idx]:>22.15f}")

    print(f"\nTheoretical sum = p / (1-(1-p)) = {p / (1-(1-p)):.15f}")
    print(f"Computed sum (k=1..{max_k}) = {partial_sums[-1]:.15f}")
    print(f"Residual = {1 - partial_sums[-1]:.2e}")

Exercise 15: Bonferroni Inequalities

The Bonferroni inequalities state that truncating the inclusion-exclusion formula yields alternating bounds. Prove the first Bonferroni inequality: for any events $A_1, \ldots, A_n$,

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^n A_i\right) \leq \sum_{i=1}^n P(A_i)$$

Then prove the second: $P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^n A_i\right) \geq \sum_i P(A_i) - \sum_{i < j} P(A_i \cap A_j)$.

Show Solution

First inequality (union bound): Already proved in the theory section by induction. The key insight is that $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) \leq P(A) + P(B)$ since $P(A \cap B) \geq 0$.

Second inequality: We prove by induction on $n$.

Base case ($n=2$): $P(A_1 \cup A_2) = P(A_1) + P(A_2) - P(A_1 \cap A_2)$, which equals the right side. The inequality holds with equality.

Inductive step: Let $B = \bigcup_{i=1}^{n-1} A_i$. Then:

$$P(B \cup A_n) = P(B) + P(A_n) - P(B \cap A_n)$$

Now $P(B \cap A_n) = P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n-1} (A_i \cap A_n)\right) \leq \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} P(A_i \cap A_n)$ by the first Bonferroni inequality.

By the inductive hypothesis, $P(B) \geq \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} P(A_i) - \sum_{1 \leq i < j \leq n-1} P(A_i \cap A_j)$.

Combining:

$$P(B \cup A_n) \geq \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} P(A_i) - \sum_{1 \leq i < j \leq n-1} P(A_i \cap A_j) + P(A_n) - \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} P(A_i \cap A_n)$$ $$= \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A_i) - \sum_{1 \leq i < j \leq n} P(A_i \cap A_j) \qquad \square$$

Exercise 16: Symmetric Difference and Probability

  2. If $P(A) = 0.6$, $P(B) = 0.5$, and $P(A \cap B) = 0.3$, compute $P(A \triangle B)$.
  3. Prove that $P(A \triangle B) = 0$ implies $P(A) = P(B)$.
Show Solution

1. Since $A \setminus B$ and $B \setminus A$ are disjoint:

$$P(A \triangle B) = P(A \setminus B) + P(B \setminus A)$$

Now $P(A \setminus B) = P(A) - P(A \cap B)$ and $P(B \setminus A) = P(B) - P(A \cap B)$, so:

$$P(A \triangle B) = P(A) + P(B) - 2P(A \cap B)$$

2. Substituting:

$$P(A \triangle B) = 0.6 + 0.5 - 2(0.3) = 1.1 - 0.6 = \boxed{0.5}$$

Exercise 20: De Morgan's Laws and Probability

Let $A$, $B$, $C$ be events in a probability space with $P(A) = 0.4$, $P(B) = 0.5$, $P(C) = 0.6$, $P(A \cap B) = 0.2$, $P(A \cap C) = 0.25$, $P(B \cap C) = 0.3$, and $P(A \cap B \cap C) = 0.1$.

Show Solution

1. By De Morgan's law: $(A \cup B \cup C)^c = A^c \cap B^c \cap C^c$. Therefore:

$$P(A^c \cap B^c \cap C^c) = 1 - P(A \cup B \cup C)$$

2. First compute $P(A \cup B \cup C)$ by inclusion-exclusion:

$$P(A^c \cap B^c \cap C^c) = 1 - 0.85 = \boxed{0.15}$$

Exercise 21: Constructing a Probability Measure

Let $\Omega = \mathbb{N} = \{1, 2, 3, \ldots\}$ and $\mathcal{F} = 2^{\mathbb{N}}$. Define $P(\{n\}) = \frac{6}{\pi^2 n^2}$ for $n \in \mathbb{N}$.

  1. Verify that this defines a valid probability measure (i.e., $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(\{n\}) = 1$). You may use the fact that $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6}$ (the Basel problem).
  2. Compute $P(\{n : n \text{ is even}\})$.
  3. Compute $P(\{n : n \text{ is a multiple of 3}\})$.
  4. Verify the inclusion-exclusion formula for $P(\text{even} \cup \text{multiple of 3})$.
Show Solution

1.

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{6}{\pi^2 n^2} = \frac{6}{\pi^2} \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{6}{\pi^2} \cdot \frac{\pi^2}{6} = 1 \qquad \checkmark$$

Non-negativity: $\frac{6}{\pi^2 n^2} > 0$ for all $n$. Countable additivity is automatic since $P(A) = \sum_{n \in A} P(\{n\})$ defines an absolutely convergent series. So $(\mathbb{N}, 2^{\mathbb{N}}, P)$ is a valid probability space.

2. Let $A = \{2, 4, 6, \ldots\}$. Then:

$$P(A) = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{6}{\pi^2 (2k)^2} = \frac{6}{\pi^2} \cdot \frac{1}{4} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2} = \frac{6}{4\pi^2} \cdot \frac{\pi^2}{6} = \frac{1}{4}$$

3. Let $B = \{3, 6, 9, \ldots\}$. Then:

$$P(B) = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{6}{\pi^2 (3k)^2} = \frac{6}{9\pi^2} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2} = \frac{1}{9}$$

4. $A \cap B = \{6, 12, 18, \ldots\}$ (multiples of 6):

$$P(A \cap B) = \frac{6}{36\pi^2} \cdot \frac{\pi^2}{6} = \frac{1}{36}$$

Inclusion-exclusion: $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{9} - \frac{1}{36} = \frac{9 + 4 - 1}{36} = \frac{12}{36} = \frac{1}{3}$.

Direct verification: $A \cup B = \{n : 2|n \text{ or } 3|n\}$. We can verify:

$$P(A \cup B) = \frac{6}{\pi^2} \sum_{\substack{n=1 \\ 2|n \text{ or } 3|n}}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2}$$

This sum equals $\frac{\pi^2}{6} \cdot \frac{1}{3} = \frac{\pi^2}{18}$, giving $P(A \cup B) = \frac{6}{\pi^2} \cdot \frac{\pi^2}{18} = \frac{1}{3}$. $\checkmark$

  2. Show that every closed interval $[a, b]$ is a Borel set.
  3. Show that every countable set (e.g., $\mathbb{Q}$) is a Borel set.
  4. Is $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}) = 2^{\mathbb{R}}$? Explain briefly.
Show Solution

1. For any $x \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$\{x\} = \bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(x - \frac{1}{n}, x + \frac{1}{n}\right)$$

Exercise 23: Continuity of Probability Proof

Prove continuity from above directly from the axioms: If $A_1 \supseteq A_2 \supseteq A_3 \supseteq \cdots$ and $P(A_1) < \infty$, then

$$P\!\left(\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n\right) = \lim_{n \to \infty} P(A_n)$$

Hint: Apply continuity from below to the sequence $B_n = A_1 \setminus A_n$.

Show Solution

Proof. Define $B_n = A_1 \setminus A_n$ for each $n \geq 1$.

Since $A_n \supseteq A_{n+1}$, we have $A_1 \setminus A_n \subseteq A_1 \setminus A_{n+1}$, so $B_1 \subseteq B_2 \subseteq B_3 \subseteq \cdots$ is an increasing sequence.

Moreover:

$$\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n = \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} (A_1 \setminus A_n) = A_1 \setminus \bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n$$

By continuity from below (which we proved from countable additivity):

$$P\!\left(A_1 \setminus \bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n\right) = \lim_{n \to \infty} P(B_n) = \lim_{n \to \infty} P(A_1 \setminus A_n)$$

Since $A_n \subseteq A_1$, we have $P(A_1 \setminus A_n) = P(A_1) - P(A_n)$ (from the additivity of disjoint decomposition $A_1 = A_n \cup (A_1 \setminus A_n)$). Similarly, $P(A_1 \setminus \bigcap A_n) = P(A_1) - P(\bigcap A_n)$. Therefore:

$$P(A_1) - P\!\left(\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n\right) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left[P(A_1) - P(A_n)\right] = P(A_1) - \lim_{n \to \infty} P(A_n)$$

Since $P(A_1) < \infty$, we can cancel it from both sides:

$$P\!\left(\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n\right) = \lim_{n \to \infty} P(A_n) \qquad \square$$

Note: The condition $P(A_1) < \infty$ is automatic for probability measures (since $P(A_1) \leq 1$), but essential for general measures.

Exercise 24: The Borel-Cantelli Lemma

Let $A_1, A_2, A_3, \ldots$ be events with $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n) < \infty$. Define the event "infinitely many $A_n$ occur" as:

$$\{A_n \text{ i.o.}\} = \limsup_{n \to \infty} A_n = \bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} \bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_k$$
  1. Show that $B_n = \bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_k$ is a decreasing sequence.
  2. Use the union bound to show $P(B_n) \leq \sum_{k=n}^{\infty} P(A_k)$.
  3. Use continuity from above to conclude $P(\{A_n \text{ i.o.}\}) = 0$.
Show Solution

1. $B_n = \bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_k$ and $B_{n+1} = \bigcup_{k=n+1}^{\infty} A_k$. Since the union for $B_{n+1}$ is over a subset of the indices used for $B_n$ (removing $A_n$), we have $B_{n+1} \subseteq B_n$. So $B_1 \supseteq B_2 \supseteq B_3 \supseteq \cdots$.

2. By sub-additivity (the union bound):

$$P(B_n) = P\!\left(\bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_k\right) \leq \sum_{k=n}^{\infty} P(A_k)$$

Since $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n) < \infty$, the tail sum $\sum_{k=n}^{\infty} P(A_k) \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$ (the tail of a convergent series goes to zero).

3. By continuity from above:

$$P(\{A_n \text{ i.o.}\}) = P\!\left(\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n\right) = \lim_{n \to \infty} P(B_n) \leq \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{k=n}^{\infty} P(A_k) = 0$$

Since probabilities are non-negative, $P(\{A_n \text{ i.o.}\}) = 0$. $\square$

This says: if the "total probability budget" for the events $A_n$ is finite, then with probability 1, only finitely many of them actually occur.

Now $A$ is cofinite, so $A^c$ is finite, and $B \subseteq A^c$ is finite. $(A \cup B)^c = A^c \cap B^c = A^c \setminus B$, which is a subset of the finite set $A^c$, hence finite. So $A \cup B$ is cofinite. $P(A \cup B) = 1 = 1 + 0 = P(A) + P(B)$. $\checkmark$
  • Both $A$ and $B$ are cofinite: Then $A^c$ and $B^c$ are both finite. Since $A \cap B = \varnothing$, $B \subseteq A^c$, which is finite. But $B$ is cofinite, meaning $B^c$ is finite, so $|B| = \infty$. But $B \subseteq A^c$ and $A^c$ is finite, contradiction. So this case is impossible.

3. Let $A_n = \{n\}$ for $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$. These are pairwise disjoint. Each $A_n$ is finite, so $P(A_n) = 0$. But $\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n = \mathbb{N} = \Omega$, and $P(\Omega) = 1$.

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n\right) = 1 \neq 0 = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n)$$

Countable additivity fails! $\square$

4. Without countable additivity, the following fundamental results would fail:

  • Continuity of probability (limits of nested events)
  • Borel-Cantelli lemma
  • Law of large numbers (both weak and strong)
  • Central limit theorem
  • Any result that involves passing to a limit of a sequence of events

In essence, finite additivity is sufficient for combinatorial probability (finite sample spaces), but the entire theory of convergence and limits—which is what makes probability theory truly powerful—requires countable additivity.

Exercise 26: Measure Extension and Uniqueness

Let $\Omega = (0,1]$ and let $\mathcal{A}$ be the algebra of finite disjoint unions of half-open intervals $(a,b] \subseteq (0,1]$. Define the set function $\mu$ on $\mathcal{A}$ by $\mu((a,b]) = b - a$.

  1. Show that $\mu$ is finitely additive on $\mathcal{A}$.
  2. Show that $\mu$ is countably additive on $\mathcal{A}$ by proving: if $(a,b] = \bigsqcup_{n=1}^{\infty} (a_n, b_n]$ (disjoint), then $b - a = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(b_n - a_n)$.
  3. State the Carathéodory extension theorem and explain what it guarantees about extending $\mu$ to $\sigma(\mathcal{A}) = \mathcal{B}((0,1])$.
Show Solution

1. Let $(a,b] = \bigsqcup_{i=1}^{n} (a_i, b_i]$ be a finite disjoint union with $a = a_1 < b_1 = a_2 < b_2 = \cdots = a_n < b_n = b$ (after reordering). Then:

$$\mu\!\left(\bigsqcup_{i=1}^n (a_i, b_i]\right) = b - a = \sum_{i=1}^n (b_i - a_i) = \sum_{i=1}^n \mu((a_i, b_i])$$

For a general element of $\mathcal{A}$, which is a finite disjoint union of intervals, the same argument applies by treating each component interval separately. $\checkmark$

2. By finite additivity, for each $N$: $\sum_{n=1}^{N} (b_n - a_n) = \mu\!\left(\bigsqcup_{n=1}^{N} (a_n, b_n]\right) \leq \mu((a,b]) = b - a$, since the partial union is contained in $(a,b]$. This gives $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(b_n - a_n) \leq b - a$.

For the reverse inequality, fix $\epsilon > 0$. Cover each $(a_n, b_n]$ by the open interval $(a_n, b_n + \epsilon/2^n)$ and note $[a + \epsilon, b]$ is compact and covered by these open intervals. By Heine-Borel, a finite subcover exists, giving:

$$b - a - \epsilon \leq \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(b_n - a_n + \frac{\epsilon}{2^n}\right) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(b_n - a_n) + \epsilon$$

Since $\epsilon$ is arbitrary, $b - a \leq \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(b_n - a_n)$. Combined: $b - a = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(b_n - a_n)$. $\square$

3. The Carathéodory extension theorem states: if $\mu$ is a countably additive set function on an algebra $\mathcal{A}$ with $\mu(\Omega) < \infty$, then $\mu$ extends uniquely to a measure on $\sigma(\mathcal{A})$. Applied here, it guarantees a unique probability measure (the Lebesgue measure) on the Borel $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{B}((0,1])$ that agrees with interval length on half-open intervals.

Exercise 27: The Cantor Set and Lebesgue Measure

The Cantor set $\mathcal{C}$ is constructed by iteratively removing middle thirds from $[0,1]$: at step 1, remove $(\frac{1}{3}, \frac{2}{3})$; at step 2, remove $(\frac{1}{9}, \frac{2}{9})$ and $(\frac{7}{9}, \frac{8}{9})$; and so on.

  1. Show that the total length removed after $n$ steps is $1 - (2/3)^n$.
  2. Conclude that $\mathcal{C}$ has Lebesgue measure zero.
  3. Show that $\mathcal{C}$ is uncountable (hint: ternary expansions).
  4. Explain why this means there exists an uncountable set with probability zero under the uniform distribution on $[0,1]$.
Show Solution

1. At step $k$, we remove $2^{k-1}$ intervals, each of length $\frac{1}{3^k}$. Total length removed after $n$ steps:

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n} \frac{2^{k-1}}{3^k} = \frac{1}{3} \sum_{k=1}^{n} \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^{k-1} = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1 - (2/3)^n}{1 - 2/3} = 1 - \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n$$

2. The Cantor set is $\mathcal{C} = [0,1] \setminus \bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty} (\text{removed intervals})$. The total length removed is:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \left[1 - \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n\right] = 1$$

Therefore $\lambda(\mathcal{C}) = 1 - 1 = 0$, where $\lambda$ is Lebesgue measure. $\square$

3. Every point in $\mathcal{C}$ has a ternary (base-3) expansion using only digits $0$ and $2$ (no 1s). The map $\phi: \mathcal{C} \to [0,1]$ that replaces every digit $2$ with $1$ and reads the result in binary is a surjection. Since $[0,1]$ is uncountable, so is $\mathcal{C}$. $\square$

4. Under the uniform distribution on $[0,1]$, probability equals Lebesgue measure for Borel sets. Since $\mathcal{C}$ is a closed (hence Borel) set with $\lambda(\mathcal{C}) = 0$, we have $P(X \in \mathcal{C}) = 0$ for $X \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$, even though $\mathcal{C}$ is uncountable. This illustrates that "probability zero" does not mean "impossible" or even "small" in a set-theoretic sense.

Exercise 28: Product Sigma-Algebras and Independence

Let $(\Omega_1, \mathcal{F}_1, P_1)$ and $(\Omega_2, \mathcal{F}_2, P_2)$ be two probability spaces. The product $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F}_1 \otimes \mathcal{F}_2$ on $\Omega_1 \times \Omega_2$ is generated by the measurable rectangles $\{A \times B : A \in \mathcal{F}_1, B \in \mathcal{F}_2\}$.

  1. Show that the collection of measurable rectangles is not itself a $\sigma$-algebra (hint: consider the complement of a rectangle).
  2. Show that for finite spaces $|\Omega_1| = m$, $|\Omega_2| = n$, we have $|\mathcal{F}_1 \otimes \mathcal{F}_2| = 2^{mn}$ when $\mathcal{F}_i = 2^{\Omega_i}$.
  3. Prove that the product measure $P = P_1 \times P_2$ defined by $P(A \times B) = P_1(A) \cdot P_2(B)$ extends to a unique probability measure on $\mathcal{F}_1 \otimes \mathcal{F}_2$.
Show Solution

1. Let $A \subsetneq \Omega_1$ with $A \neq \varnothing$ and $B \subsetneq \Omega_2$ with $B \neq \varnothing$. Consider the complement of the rectangle $A \times B$:

$$(A \times B)^c = (A^c \times B) \cup (A \times B^c) \cup (A^c \times B^c)$$

This is a union of three rectangles, not a single rectangle. In general, $(A \times B)^c$ is not a measurable rectangle, so the collection of rectangles is not closed under complementation and hence not a $\sigma$-algebra. $\square$

2. When $\mathcal{F}_i = 2^{\Omega_i}$, the product space $\Omega_1 \times \Omega_2$ has $mn$ elements. The product $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F}_1 \otimes \mathcal{F}_2$ must contain all singletons $\{(\omega_1, \omega_2)\} = \{\omega_1\} \times \{\omega_2\}$, hence it contains all subsets: $\mathcal{F}_1 \otimes \mathcal{F}_2 = 2^{\Omega_1 \times \Omega_2}$, which has $2^{mn}$ elements. $\square$

3. The set function $P(A \times B) = P_1(A) \cdot P_2(B)$ on measurable rectangles extends to finite disjoint unions by additivity. To apply Carathéodory's extension theorem, we verify countable additivity on the algebra generated by measurable rectangles. This follows from Fubini-Tonelli for indicator functions: for any $E$ in the algebra,

$$P(E) = \int_{\Omega_1} \int_{\Omega_2} \mathbf{1}_E(\omega_1, \omega_2) \, dP_2(\omega_2) \, dP_1(\omega_1)$$

is well-defined and countably additive (by the monotone convergence theorem applied to sums of disjoint sets). By Carathéodory, $P$ extends uniquely to $\mathcal{F}_1 \otimes \mathcal{F}_2$. $\square$

Exercise 29: Dynkin's Pi-Lambda Theorem

A $\pi$-system is a collection of sets closed under finite intersection. A $\lambda$-system (Dynkin system) is a collection $\mathcal{L}$ satisfying: (i) $\Omega \in \mathcal{L}$, (ii) $A \in \mathcal{L} \Rightarrow A^c \in \mathcal{L}$, (iii) if $A_1, A_2, \ldots \in \mathcal{L}$ are pairwise disjoint, then $\bigcup_n A_n \in \mathcal{L}$.

  1. Show that every $\sigma$-algebra is both a $\pi$-system and a $\lambda$-system.
  2. Give an example of a $\lambda$-system that is not a $\sigma$-algebra.
  3. State Dynkin's $\pi$-$\lambda$ theorem and explain why it is useful for proving uniqueness of probability measures.
Show Solution

1. Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a $\sigma$-algebra.

$\pi$-system: $\sigma$-algebras are closed under countable intersection (by De Morgan: $\bigcap_n A_n = (\bigcup_n A_n^c)^c$), hence under finite intersection. $\checkmark$

$\lambda$-system: (i) $\Omega \in \mathcal{F}$ $\checkmark$; (ii) closure under complement $\checkmark$; (iii) for disjoint $A_n$, $\bigcup_n A_n \in \mathcal{F}$ since $\sigma$-algebras are closed under all countable unions (not just disjoint ones). $\checkmark$ $\square$

2. Let $\Omega = \{1,2,3,4\}$. Consider $\mathcal{L} = \{\varnothing, \{1,2\}, \{3,4\}, \{1,3\}, \{2,4\}, \Omega\}$.

This is a $\lambda$-system: $\Omega \in \mathcal{L}$; each set's complement is in $\mathcal{L}$; and the only disjoint unions we can form stay in $\mathcal{L}$.

But it is not a $\sigma$-algebra: $\{1,2\} \cap \{1,3\} = \{1\} \notin \mathcal{L}$, so it fails closure under intersection. $\square$

3. Dynkin's $\pi$-$\lambda$ theorem: If $\mathcal{P}$ is a $\pi$-system and $\mathcal{L}$ is a $\lambda$-system with $\mathcal{P} \subseteq \mathcal{L}$, then $\sigma(\mathcal{P}) \subseteq \mathcal{L}$.

Application to uniqueness: Suppose two probability measures $P$ and $Q$ agree on a $\pi$-system $\mathcal{P}$: $P(A) = Q(A)$ for all $A \in \mathcal{P}$. Define $\mathcal{L} = \{A \in \sigma(\mathcal{P}) : P(A) = Q(A)\}$. One can verify $\mathcal{L}$ is a $\lambda$-system containing $\mathcal{P}$. By Dynkin's theorem, $\sigma(\mathcal{P}) \subseteq \mathcal{L}$, so $P = Q$ on $\sigma(\mathcal{P})$. This is the standard tool for proving that a probability measure is uniquely determined by its values on a generating $\pi$-system (e.g., intervals determine the Lebesgue measure). $\square$

Exercise 30: Non-Measurable Sets and the Axiom of Choice

This exercise outlines the Vitali construction, showing that not every subset of $[0,1]$ can be assigned a probability consistent with the axioms.

  1. Define an equivalence relation on $[0,1]$ by $x \sim y$ iff $x - y \in \mathbb{Q}$. Show this is indeed an equivalence relation.
  2. Using the Axiom of Choice, let $V$ be a set containing exactly one element from each equivalence class (a Vitali set). Let $\{q_n\}_{n=1}^{\infty}$ be an enumeration of $\mathbb{Q} \cap [-1,1]$. Show that $[0,1] \subseteq \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} (V + q_n) \subseteq [-1, 2]$, where $V + q = \{v + q : v \in V\}$ and addition is in $\mathbb{R}$ (not modular).
  3. Show that the sets $V + q_n$ are pairwise disjoint.
  4. Assuming $V$ is Lebesgue measurable with $\lambda(V) = c$, show that countable additivity and translation invariance lead to a contradiction, proving $V$ is not measurable.
Show Solution

1. We verify the three properties of an equivalence relation for $x \sim y \iff x - y \in \mathbb{Q}$:

  • Reflexive: $x - x = 0 \in \mathbb{Q}$, so $x \sim x$. $\checkmark$
  • Symmetric: If $x - y \in \mathbb{Q}$, then $y - x = -(x-y) \in \mathbb{Q}$. $\checkmark$
  • Transitive: If $x - y \in \mathbb{Q}$ and $y - z \in \mathbb{Q}$, then $x - z = (x-y) + (y-z) \in \mathbb{Q}$. $\checkmark$ $\square$

2. For any $x \in [0,1]$, the equivalence class $[x]$ has a representative $v \in V$, so $x - v \in \mathbb{Q}$. Since $x \in [0,1]$ and $v \in [0,1]$, we have $x - v \in [-1,1] \cap \mathbb{Q}$, so $x - v = q_n$ for some $n$. Thus $x = v + q_n \in V + q_n$, proving $[0,1] \subseteq \bigcup_n (V + q_n)$.

For the upper bound: if $v \in V \subseteq [0,1]$ and $q_n \in [-1,1]$, then $v + q_n \in [-1, 2]$, so $\bigcup_n (V + q_n) \subseteq [-1, 2]$. $\square$

3. Suppose $x \in (V + q_m) \cap (V + q_n)$ with $m \neq n$. Then $x = v_1 + q_m = v_2 + q_n$ for some $v_1, v_2 \in V$. So $v_1 - v_2 = q_n - q_m \in \mathbb{Q}$, meaning $v_1 \sim v_2$. Since $V$ contains exactly one element per equivalence class, $v_1 = v_2$, hence $q_m = q_n$, contradicting $m \neq n$. Therefore the sets are pairwise disjoint. $\square$

4. Suppose $V$ is measurable with $\lambda(V) = c$. By translation invariance of Lebesgue measure, $\lambda(V + q_n) = c$ for all $n$. By countable additivity (since the $V + q_n$ are disjoint):

$$\lambda\!\left(\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} (V + q_n)\right) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \lambda(V + q_n) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} c$$

From part 2: $1 \leq \lambda\!\left(\bigcup_n (V + q_n)\right) \leq 3$. But $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} c$ equals $0$ if $c = 0$ and $+\infty$ if $c > 0$. Neither $0$ nor $+\infty$ lies in $[1, 3]$. Contradiction!

Therefore $V$ is not Lebesgue measurable. This is why we need $\sigma$-algebras: we cannot consistently assign probabilities to all subsets of an uncountable space while maintaining countable additivity and translation invariance. $\square$

Key Concept

A probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ consists of a sample space, a $\sigma$-algebra of events, and a probability measure satisfying three axioms: non-negativity, normalization, and countable additivity. All of probability theory is built on these foundations.

Prerequisites

  • Basic set theory (unions, intersections, complements)
  • Algebra and arithmetic
  • Familiarity with series and limits (for continuity results)
  • Optional: measure theory basics

Key Formulas

Axiom 1: $P(A) \geq 0$

Axiom 2: $P(\Omega) = 1$

Axiom 3: $P\!\left(\bigcup A_i\right) = \sum P(A_i)$ (disjoint)

Complement: $P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$

Inclusion-Exclusion:
$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$

Union Bound:
$P(\bigcup A_i) \leq \sum P(A_i)$

CDF: $F(x) = P(X \leq x)$
