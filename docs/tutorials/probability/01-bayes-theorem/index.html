01. Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' theorem is arguably the single most important equation in all of probability and statistics. It tells us how to update beliefs in the light of new evidence. This tutorial derives the theorem from first principles, builds deep intuition through multiple examples, and connects it to machine learning, medical diagnosis, and rational decision-making.

Why Bayes' Theorem?

Consider these everyday questions:

  • A medical test comes back positive. What is the actual probability you have the disease?
  • Your spam filter sees the word "free" in an email. How much more likely is it to be spam?
  • A self-driving car's sensor detects an obstacle. Given sensor noise, what is the probability an obstacle is really there?

All of these ask the same structural question: given observed evidence, what should I believe about a hidden cause? Bayes' theorem provides the unique, mathematically correct answer.

The theorem is named after Reverend Thomas Bayes (1701–1761), whose manuscript An Essay towards solving a Problem in the Doctrine of Chances was published posthumously in 1763. Pierre-Simon Laplace independently discovered and generalized it. Today it is the foundation of Bayesian statistics, machine learning, signal processing, and decision theory.

Conditional Probability

Before we can state Bayes' theorem, we need the concept of conditional probability. The conditional probability of $A$ given $B$ is the probability of $A$ occurring, knowing that $B$ has occurred:

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}, \quad P(B) > 0$$

Intuition: Once we know $B$ has happened, the "universe" of possibilities shrinks from the entire sample space $\Omega$ to just $B$. Within this smaller universe, we ask: what fraction of $B$ also belongs to $A$?

Figure 1. $P(A \mid B)$ is the ratio of the purple intersection to the entire blue circle.

Example: Roll a fair die. Let $A = \{2, 4, 6\}$ (even) and $B = \{1, 2, 3\}$ (at most 3).

  • $P(A \cap B) = P(\{2\}) = 1/6$
  • $P(B) = 3/6 = 1/2$
  • $P(A \mid B) = \frac{1/6}{1/2} = \frac{1}{3}$

Knowing the die shows at most 3, only one of the three equally likely outcomes ($\{1, 2, 3\}$) is even.

The Product Rule

Rearranging the definition of conditional probability gives the product rule (also called the chain rule of probability):

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A \mid B) \, P(B)$$

By symmetry—since $A \cap B = B \cap A$—we also have:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(B \mid A) \, P(A)$$

These two expressions for the same quantity are the key to deriving Bayes' theorem. The product rule also generalizes to chains of events:

$$P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(A) \, P(B \mid A) \, P(C \mid A \cap B)$$

This chain rule is the basis for autoregressive models in machine learning, where the joint probability of a sequence is factored as $P(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{i-1})$.

Deriving Bayes' Theorem

The derivation is beautifully simple. We start with the two expressions for $P(A \cap B)$:

$$P(A \mid B) \, P(B) = P(A \cap B) = P(B \mid A) \, P(A)$$

Dividing both sides by $P(B)$:

$$\boxed{P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A) \, P(A)}{P(B)}}$$

That's it. Three lines of algebra, yet the implications are profound. This equation lets us reverse the direction of conditioning: if we know $P(B \mid A)$ (how likely is the evidence given a cause), we can compute $P(A \mid B)$ (how likely is the cause given the evidence).

Anatomy of the Formula

Each term in Bayes' theorem has a name and a distinct role:

Figure 2. The four components of Bayes' theorem.

Term Symbol Meaning
Prior $P(A)$ Your belief about $A$ before observing any evidence. Encodes background knowledge.
Likelihood $P(B \mid A)$ The probability of observing the evidence $B$ if $A$ is true. Measures how well $A$ explains $B$.
Evidence $P(B)$ The total probability of observing $B$ under all hypotheses. Acts as a normalizing constant.
Posterior $P(A \mid B)$ Your updated belief about $A$ after observing $B$. The output of Bayesian reasoning.

The core insight can be stated in words:

$$\text{Posterior} = \frac{\text{Likelihood} \times \text{Prior}}{\text{Evidence}}$$

Or even more compactly, since the evidence $P(B)$ is just a normalizing constant:

$$\text{Posterior} \propto \text{Likelihood} \times \text{Prior}$$

The Medical Testing Example

This is the canonical example that reveals why Bayes' theorem is so counterintuitive and so important.

Problem Setup

A disease affects 1 in 1000 people. A diagnostic test has:

  • Sensitivity (true positive rate): 99%—if you have the disease, the test is positive 99% of the time
  • Specificity (true negative rate): 95%—if you don't have the disease, the test is negative 95% of the time

Question: You test positive. What is the probability you actually have the disease?

Most people intuitively guess 95% or 99%. The correct answer is shockingly low. Let's compute it.

Let $D$ = "has disease" and $+$ = "tests positive".

  • Prior: $P(D) = 0.001$
  • Likelihood: $P(+ \mid D) = 0.99$
  • False positive rate: $P(+ \mid \neg D) = 1 - 0.95 = 0.05$

First compute the evidence $P(+)$ using the law of total probability:

$$P(+) = P(+ \mid D) P(D) + P(+ \mid \neg D) P(\neg D)$$ $$= (0.99)(0.001) + (0.05)(0.999)$$ $$= 0.00099 + 0.04995 = 0.05094$$

Now apply Bayes' theorem:

$$P(D \mid +) = \frac{P(+ \mid D) \, P(D)}{P(+)} = \frac{0.99 \times 0.001}{0.05094} \approx 0.0194 \approx 1.94\%$$

Despite a 99% sensitive and 95% specific test, a positive result only means a ~2% chance of having the disease! The reason: the disease is so rare (1 in 1000) that the false positives from the 999 healthy people vastly outnumber the true positives from the 1 sick person.

Figure 3. The base rate fallacy visualized. False positives vastly outnumber true positives.

The Law of Total Probability

Computing the evidence $P(B)$ often requires the law of total probability. If $\{A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n\}$ is a partition of the sample space (mutually exclusive and exhaustive), then:

$$P(B) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(B \mid A_i) \, P(A_i)$$

In the common binary case (hypothesis $H$ is true or false):

$$P(B) = P(B \mid H) \, P(H) + P(B \mid \neg H) \, P(\neg H)$$

This gives the full form of Bayes' theorem used in the medical example:

$$P(H \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid H) \, P(H)}{P(B \mid H) \, P(H) + P(B \mid \neg H) \, P(\neg H)}$$

For multiple hypotheses $\{H_1, \ldots, H_n\}$:

$$P(H_k \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid H_k) \, P(H_k)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} P(B \mid H_i) \, P(H_i)}$$

Sequential Updating

One of the most powerful aspects of Bayes' theorem is that updating is iterative. Today's posterior becomes tomorrow's prior.

Suppose we observe evidence $B_1$, then later observe $B_2$. After seeing $B_1$:

$$P(A \mid B_1) = \frac{P(B_1 \mid A) \, P(A)}{P(B_1)}$$

Now use this posterior as the new prior when $B_2$ arrives:

$$P(A \mid B_1, B_2) = \frac{P(B_2 \mid A, B_1) \, P(A \mid B_1)}{P(B_2 \mid B_1)}$$

If $B_1$ and $B_2$ are conditionally independent given $A$ (i.e., $P(B_2 \mid A, B_1) = P(B_2 \mid A)$), this simplifies to:

$$P(A \mid B_1, B_2) \propto P(B_2 \mid A) \, P(B_1 \mid A) \, P(A)$$

Returning to the medical example: If the person takes a second, independent test and it is also positive, the posterior jumps dramatically. Starting from the prior of 1.94% (the posterior from the first test):

$$P(D \mid +, +) = \frac{0.99 \times 0.0194}{0.99 \times 0.0194 + 0.05 \times 0.9806} \approx \frac{0.01921}{0.06824} \approx 28.1\%$$

Two positive tests raise the probability from 0.1% to ~28%. A third positive test would push it to ~88%. Each piece of evidence sharpens our belief.

The Continuous Form

When the hypothesis $\theta$ is a continuous parameter (like the bias of a coin), we replace probabilities with probability densities:

$$p(\theta \mid \text{data}) = \frac{p(\text{data} \mid \theta) \, p(\theta)}{p(\text{data})} = \frac{p(\text{data} \mid \theta) \, p(\theta)}{\int p(\text{data} \mid \theta') \, p(\theta') \, d\theta'}$$

The denominator is now an integral—the marginal likelihood or model evidence. Computing this integral is the central computational challenge of Bayesian inference and motivates techniques like MCMC, variational inference, and conjugate priors.

Example: Beta-Binomial model. Suppose we flip a coin with unknown bias $\theta \in [0,1]$ and observe $k$ heads in $n$ flips.

  • Prior: $p(\theta) = \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta) \propto \theta^{\alpha-1}(1-\theta)^{\beta-1}$
  • Likelihood: $P(\text{data} \mid \theta) = \binom{n}{k} \theta^k (1-\theta)^{n-k}$
  • Posterior: $p(\theta \mid \text{data}) = \text{Beta}(\alpha + k, \, \beta + n - k)$

The Beta distribution is conjugate to the Binomial likelihood: the posterior has the same functional form as the prior. This makes the update rule trivially simple: just add the observed counts to the prior parameters.

The Odds Form of Bayes' Theorem

An elegant alternative formulation uses odds rather than probabilities. The odds of $A$ are:

$$O(A) = \frac{P(A)}{P(\neg A)} = \frac{P(A)}{1 - P(A)}$$

Bayes' theorem in odds form becomes:

$$\underbrace{\frac{P(A \mid B)}{P(\neg A \mid B)}}_{\text{Posterior odds}} = \underbrace{\frac{P(B \mid A)}{P(B \mid \neg A)}}_{\text{Likelihood ratio}} \times \underbrace{\frac{P(A)}{P(\neg A)}}_{\text{Prior odds}}$$

The likelihood ratio $\Lambda = P(B \mid A) / P(B \mid \neg A)$ is called the Bayes factor. It measures the strength of evidence:

  • $\Lambda > 1$: evidence supports $A$
  • $\Lambda < 1$: evidence supports $\neg A$
  • $\Lambda = 1$: evidence is uninformative

Taking logarithms gives an additive form:

$$\log O(A \mid B) = \log O(A) + \log \Lambda$$

Log-odds update additively—each piece of evidence adds (or subtracts) a fixed amount to the log-odds. This is why logistic regression, which models log-odds as a linear function of features, is a natural Bayesian classifier.

Common Pitfalls

1. The Base Rate Fallacy

Ignoring the prior $P(A)$. As the medical example shows, even highly accurate tests produce many false positives when the base rate is low. This is also called the prosecutor's fallacy in legal contexts: confusing $P(\text{evidence} \mid \text{innocent})$ with $P(\text{innocent} \mid \text{evidence})$.

2. Confusing $P(A \mid B)$ with $P(B \mid A)$

These are fundamentally different quantities. $P(\text{rain} \mid \text{clouds})$ is not the same as $P(\text{clouds} \mid \text{rain})$. It always rains when there are clouds—false! It's cloudy when it rains—true. Bayes' theorem is precisely the tool to convert between them.

3. Assuming Uniform Priors

A uniform prior $P(H_i) = 1/n$ seems "non-informative," but it encodes a strong assumption: that all hypotheses are equally likely before seeing data. In many contexts, this is unreasonable (e.g., the prior probability of having a rare disease is not 50%).

4. Ignoring Multiple Testing

If you test many hypotheses, false positives accumulate. Testing 1000 hypotheses at the 5% level yields ~50 false positives on average. Bayesian methods handle this more naturally through the prior, which penalizes unlikely hypotheses.

Connections to Machine Learning

Naive Bayes Classifier

The Naive Bayes classifier applies Bayes' theorem with the "naive" assumption that features are conditionally independent given the class:

$$P(C \mid x_1, \ldots, x_n) \propto P(C) \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i \mid C)$$

Despite its simplicity, it works surprisingly well for text classification and spam filtering.

MAP Estimation

Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation finds the parameter value that maximizes the posterior:

$$\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta \, P(\theta \mid \text{data}) = \arg\max_\theta \, P(\text{data} \mid \theta) \, P(\theta)$$

MAP with a Gaussian prior on $\theta$ is equivalent to L2 regularization (ridge regression). MAP with a Laplace prior gives L1 regularization (LASSO). So regularization is secretly Bayesian!

Bayesian Neural Networks

Instead of learning a single set of weights, BNNs maintain a posterior distribution over weights $P(\mathbf{w} \mid \mathcal{D})$. Predictions integrate over this uncertainty:

$$P(y \mid x, \mathcal{D}) = \int P(y \mid x, \mathbf{w}) \, P(\mathbf{w} \mid \mathcal{D}) \, d\mathbf{w}$$

This gives calibrated uncertainty estimates—critical for safety-sensitive applications like medical diagnosis and autonomous driving.

Basic Bayes' Theorem

def bayes_theorem(prior, likelihood, evidence):
    """
    Compute posterior probability using Bayes' theorem.

    P(A|B) = P(B|A) * P(A) / P(B)

    Parameters:
        prior: P(A) - prior probability of hypothesis
        likelihood: P(B|A) - probability of evidence given hypothesis
        evidence: P(B) - total probability of evidence

    Returns:
        posterior: P(A|B)
    """
    return (likelihood * prior) / evidence


def bayes_binary(prior, sensitivity, specificity):
    """
    Bayes' theorem for a binary hypothesis (e.g., disease/no disease).

    Parameters:
        prior: P(H) - prior probability of hypothesis being true
        sensitivity: P(B|H) - true positive rate
        specificity: P(not B|not H) - true negative rate

    Returns:
        posterior: P(H|B) - probability of hypothesis given positive evidence
    """
    false_positive_rate = 1 - specificity

    # Law of total probability
    evidence = sensitivity * prior + false_positive_rate * (1 - prior)

    # Bayes' theorem
    posterior = (sensitivity * prior) / evidence
    return posterior


# Medical testing example
prior_disease = 0.001      # 1 in 1000
sensitivity = 0.99         # 99% true positive rate
specificity = 0.95         # 95% true negative rate

posterior = bayes_binary(prior_disease, sensitivity, specificity)
print(f"Prior P(Disease) = {prior_disease}")
print(f"Sensitivity = {sensitivity}")
print(f"Specificity = {specificity}")
print(f"Posterior P(Disease | Positive Test) = {posterior:.4f}")
print(f"That's only {posterior*100:.2f}%!")
# Output: Posterior P(Disease | Positive Test) = 0.0194

Multiple Hypotheses

import numpy as np

def bayes_multiple(priors, likelihoods):
    """
    Bayes' theorem for multiple hypotheses.

    P(H_k | B) = P(B | H_k) * P(H_k) / sum_i P(B | H_i) * P(H_i)

    Parameters:
        priors: array of P(H_i) for each hypothesis
        likelihoods: array of P(B | H_i) for each hypothesis

    Returns:
        posteriors: array of P(H_i | B)
    """
    priors = np.array(priors)
    likelihoods = np.array(likelihoods)

    # Numerator: likelihood * prior for each hypothesis
    numerators = likelihoods * priors

    # Evidence: sum of all numerators
    evidence = np.sum(numerators)

    # Posteriors
    posteriors = numerators / evidence
    return posteriors


# Example: Three factories produce widgets
# Factory A (60% of production), Factory B (30%), Factory C (10%)
# Defect rates: A: 2%, B: 3%, C: 5%
# A widget is defective. Which factory most likely made it?

priors = [0.60, 0.30, 0.10]
likelihoods = [0.02, 0.03, 0.05]  # P(defective | factory)

posteriors = bayes_multiple(priors, likelihoods)

for i, (name, pr, lk, po) in enumerate(
    zip(['A', 'B', 'C'], priors, likelihoods, posteriors)):
    print(f"Factory {name}: Prior={pr:.0%}, "
          f"Likelihood={lk:.0%}, Posterior={po:.1%}")

# Factory A: Prior=60%, Likelihood=2%, Posterior=46.2%
# Factory B: Prior=30%, Likelihood=3%, Posterior=34.6%
# Factory C: Prior=10%, Likelihood=5%, Posterior=19.2%

Sequential Bayesian Updating

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.stats import beta

def sequential_coin_update(flips, alpha_prior=1, beta_prior=1):
    """
    Bayesian sequential updating for a coin flip experiment.
    Uses Beta-Binomial conjugacy.

    Prior: Beta(alpha, beta)
    After observing k heads in n flips:
    Posterior: Beta(alpha + k, beta + n - k)
    """
    alpha = alpha_prior
    beta_param = beta_prior

    theta = np.linspace(0, 1, 500)

    fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 3, figsize=(14, 8))
    axes = axes.flatten()

    # Plot prior
    y = beta.pdf(theta, alpha, beta_param)
    axes[0].fill_between(theta, y, alpha=0.3, color='steelblue')
    axes[0].plot(theta, y, color='steelblue', linewidth=2)
    axes[0].set_title(f'Prior: Beta({alpha}, {beta_param})\n0 observations')
    axes[0].set_xlabel('θ (coin bias)')
    axes[0].set_ylabel('Density')

    # Update after each batch of flips
    checkpoints = [1, 3, 10, 30, 100]
    total_heads = 0
    total_flips = 0

    for idx, n_target in enumerate(checkpoints):
        # Flip coins up to this checkpoint
        while total_flips < n_target and total_flips < len(flips):
            if flips[total_flips] == 1:
                total_heads += 1
            total_flips += 1

        a_post = alpha_prior + total_heads
        b_post = beta_prior + total_flips - total_heads

        y = beta.pdf(theta, a_post, b_post)
        ax = axes[idx + 1]
        ax.fill_between(theta, y, alpha=0.3, color='steelblue')
        ax.plot(theta, y, color='steelblue', linewidth=2)
        ax.axvline(x=0.7, color='red', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5, label='True θ=0.7')
        ax.set_title(f'After {total_flips} flips ({total_heads}H)\n'
                     f'Beta({a_post}, {b_post})')
        ax.set_xlabel('θ')
        ax.legend(fontsize=8)

    plt.suptitle('Sequential Bayesian Updating of Coin Bias', fontsize=14, fontweight='bold')
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.savefig('sequential_update.png', dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight')
    plt.show()

np.random.seed(42)
flips = np.random.binomial(1, 0.7, size=100)
sequential_coin_update(flips, alpha_prior=1, beta_prior=1)

Naive Bayes Classifier from Scratch

import numpy as np
from collections import defaultdict

class NaiveBayesClassifier:
    """
    Naive Bayes classifier for text (bag-of-words).

    P(class | document) ∝ P(class) * ∏ P(word_i | class)

    Uses Laplace smoothing to handle unseen words.
    """

    def __init__(self, alpha=1.0):
        self.alpha = alpha  # Laplace smoothing parameter
        self.class_priors = {}
        self.word_probs = {}
        self.vocab = set()

    def fit(self, documents, labels):
        """Train on a list of (tokenized) documents and labels."""
        # Count classes
        class_counts = defaultdict(int)
        word_counts = defaultdict(lambda: defaultdict(int))

        for doc, label in zip(documents, labels):
            class_counts[label] += 1
            for word in doc:
                word_counts[label][word] += 1
                self.vocab.add(word)

        total_docs = len(labels)
        V = len(self.vocab)

        # Compute priors P(class)
        for cls, count in class_counts.items():
            self.class_priors[cls] = count / total_docs

        # Compute likelihoods P(word | class) with Laplace smoothing
        for cls in class_counts:
            total_words = sum(word_counts[cls].values())
            self.word_probs[cls] = {}
            for word in self.vocab:
                count = word_counts[cls].get(word, 0)
                self.word_probs[cls][word] = (count + self.alpha) / (total_words + self.alpha * V)

    def predict_log_proba(self, document):
        """Compute log posterior for each class."""
        scores = {}
        for cls in self.class_priors:
            # Log prior
            log_prob = np.log(self.class_priors[cls])
            # Sum of log likelihoods
            for word in document:
                if word in self.vocab:
                    log_prob += np.log(self.word_probs[cls][word])
            scores[cls] = log_prob
        return scores

    def predict(self, document):
        """Predict the most likely class."""
        scores = self.predict_log_proba(document)
        return max(scores, key=scores.get)


# Example: Spam classification
train_docs = [
    ["free", "money", "win", "prize", "click"],
    ["free", "offer", "limited", "buy", "now"],
    ["win", "lottery", "million", "free", "cash"],
    ["meeting", "agenda", "project", "deadline"],
    ["report", "quarterly", "revenue", "growth"],
    ["lunch", "team", "meeting", "tomorrow", "project"],
]
train_labels = ["spam", "spam", "spam", "ham", "ham", "ham"]

clf = NaiveBayesClassifier(alpha=1.0)
clf.fit(train_docs, train_labels)

# Test
test_doc = ["free", "meeting", "project"]
prediction = clf.predict(test_doc)
log_probs = clf.predict_log_proba(test_doc)
print(f"Test document: {test_doc}")
print(f"Prediction: {prediction}")
print(f"Log probabilities: { {k: f'{v:.3f}' for k, v in log_probs.items()} }")

Visualizing the Base Rate Fallacy

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def plot_base_rate_effect(sensitivity=0.99, specificity=0.95):
    """
    Show how the posterior P(Disease|+) varies with the base rate P(Disease).
    """
    base_rates = np.logspace(-5, -0.3, 500)  # from 0.001% to ~50%

    posteriors = []
    for p in base_rates:
        fpr = 1 - specificity
        evidence = sensitivity * p + fpr * (1 - p)
        posterior = (sensitivity * p) / evidence
        posteriors.append(posterior)

    fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))
    ax.plot(base_rates * 100, np.array(posteriors) * 100,
            linewidth=2.5, color='#6366f1')

    # Mark the medical example point
    p_med = 0.001
    fpr = 1 - specificity
    ev = sensitivity * p_med + fpr * (1 - p_med)
    post_med = (sensitivity * p_med) / ev
    ax.plot(p_med * 100, post_med * 100, 'ro', markersize=10, zorder=5)
    ax.annotate(f'  Base rate = 0.1%\n  Posterior = {post_med*100:.1f}%',
                xy=(p_med * 100, post_med * 100),
                fontsize=11, color='red')

    ax.set_xscale('log')
    ax.set_xlabel('Base Rate P(Disease) [%]', fontsize=13)
    ax.set_ylabel('Posterior P(Disease | Positive Test) [%]', fontsize=13)

Exercise 1: Basic Application

A jar contains 3 red balls and 7 blue balls. A ball is drawn at random. If the ball is red, a fair coin is flipped; if blue, a biased coin (P(Heads) = 0.8) is flipped. The coin shows Heads. What is the probability the ball was red?

Show Solution

Let $R$ = red ball, $H$ = heads.

  • $P(R) = 3/10$, $P(B) = 7/10$
  • $P(H \mid R) = 0.5$ (fair coin)
  • $P(H \mid B) = 0.8$ (biased coin)

By Bayes' theorem:

$$P(R \mid H) = \frac{P(H \mid R) \, P(R)}{P(H \mid R) \, P(R) + P(H \mid B) \, P(B)}$$ $$= \frac{0.5 \times 0.3}{0.5 \times 0.3 + 0.8 \times 0.7} = \frac{0.15}{0.15 + 0.56} = \frac{0.15}{0.71} \approx 0.2113$$

Answer: $P(R \mid H) \approx 21.1\%$

Exercise 2: Weather Forecast

The weather forecast says there is a 40% chance of rain tomorrow. Historically, when it actually rains, the forecast predicts rain 80% of the time. When it doesn't rain, the forecast incorrectly predicts rain 10% of the time. Given the forecast predicts rain, what is the actual probability of rain?

Show Solution

Let $R$ = rain, $F$ = forecast says rain.

  • $P(R) = 0.4$, $P(\neg R) = 0.6$
  • $P(F \mid R) = 0.8$
  • $P(F \mid \neg R) = 0.1$
$$P(R \mid F) = \frac{P(F \mid R) \, P(R)}{P(F \mid R) \, P(R) + P(F \mid \neg R) \, P(\neg R)}$$ $$= \frac{0.8 \times 0.4}{0.8 \times 0.4 + 0.1 \times 0.6} = \frac{0.32}{0.32 + 0.06} = \frac{0.32}{0.38} \approx 0.842$$

Exercise 21: Derive the Posterior for Gaussian-Gaussian

Given a Gaussian prior $\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)$ and a single observation $x \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, \sigma^2)$, derive the posterior distribution $p(\theta \mid x)$. Show it is Gaussian and find its mean and variance.

Show Solution

Posterior $\propto$ Likelihood $\times$ Prior:

$$p(\theta \mid x) \propto p(x \mid \theta) \, p(\theta) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x - \theta)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma_0^2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(\theta - \mu_0)^2}{2\sigma_0^2}\right)$$

Combining the exponents (completing the square in $\theta$):

$$-\frac{(x - \theta)^2}{2\sigma^2} - \frac{(\theta - \mu_0)^2}{2\sigma_0^2} = -\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{\sigma_0^2}\right)\theta^2 + \left(\frac{x}{\sigma^2} + \frac{\mu_0}{\sigma_0^2}\right)\theta + \text{const}$$

Define precision $\tau = 1/\sigma^2$, $\tau_0 = 1/\sigma_0^2$. The coefficient of $-\theta^2/2$ is $(\tau + \tau_0)$, so the posterior precision is:

$$\tau_{\text{post}} = \tau_0 + \tau \quad \Rightarrow \quad \sigma_{\text{post}}^2 = \frac{1}{\tau_0 + \tau} = \frac{\sigma_0^2 \sigma^2}{\sigma_0^2 + \sigma^2}$$

The posterior mean is:

$$\mu_{\text{post}} = \frac{\tau_0 \mu_0 + \tau x}{\tau_0 + \tau} = \frac{\mu_0 / \sigma_0^2 + x / \sigma^2}{1/\sigma_0^2 + 1/\sigma^2}$$

This is a precision-weighted average of the prior mean and the observation.

Answer: $\theta \mid x \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\frac{\sigma^2 \mu_0 + \sigma_0^2 x}{\sigma^2 + \sigma_0^2}, \; \frac{\sigma^2 \sigma_0^2}{\sigma^2 + \sigma_0^2}\right)$

Exercise 22: MAP = L2 Regularization

Prove that MAP estimation with a Gaussian prior $\theta_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\theta^2)$ on each parameter of a linear regression model with Gaussian noise is equivalent to ridge regression (L2 regularization). Find the regularization parameter $\lambda$ in terms of $\sigma^2$ and $\sigma_\theta^2$.

Show Solution

Log-posterior:

$$\log p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{y}) = \log p(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \boldsymbol{\theta}) + \log p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) + \text{const}$$

Gaussian likelihood: $p(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \prod_i \mathcal{N}(y_i \mid \mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\theta}, \sigma^2)$

$$\log p(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \boldsymbol{\theta}) = -\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\theta})^2$$ $$= -\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2$$

Gaussian prior: $p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \prod_j \mathcal{N}(\theta_j \mid 0, \sigma_\theta^2)$

$$\log p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = -\frac{d}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma_\theta^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma_\theta^2} \|\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2$$

MAP objective (maximizing log-posterior = minimizing negative):

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \left\{\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2 + \frac{1}{2\sigma_\theta^2}\|\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2\right\}$$

Multiplying through by $2\sigma^2$:

$$= \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \left\{\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2 + \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma_\theta^2}\|\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2\right\}$$

This is exactly ridge regression with:

$$\boxed{\lambda = \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma_\theta^2}}$$

Answer: A small prior variance $\sigma_\theta^2$ (strong prior toward zero) gives large $\lambda$ (heavy regularization). A large prior variance (weak prior) gives small $\lambda$ (light regularization).

Exercise 24: Posterior Predictive Distribution

You have observed $k$ heads in $n$ coin flips with a $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ prior on the bias $\theta$. Derive the posterior predictive probability that the next flip is heads: $P(x_{n+1} = H \mid \text{data})$. Show it equals the posterior mean of $\theta$.

Show Solution

The posterior predictive marginalizes over the posterior:

$$P(x_{n+1} = H \mid \text{data}) = \int_0^1 P(H \mid \theta) \, p(\theta \mid \text{data}) \, d\theta = \int_0^1 \theta \, p(\theta \mid \text{data}) \, d\theta$$

This is exactly $E[\theta \mid \text{data}]$, the posterior mean!

With posterior $\text{Beta}(\alpha + k, \beta + n - k)$:

$$P(x_{n+1} = H \mid \text{data}) = E[\theta \mid \text{data}] = \frac{\alpha + k}{\alpha + \beta + n}$$

This is Laplace's rule of succession (with $\alpha = \beta = 1$):

$$P(x_{n+1} = H \mid k \text{ heads in } n \text{ flips}) = \frac{k + 1}{n + 2}$$

Answer: $P(x_{n+1} = H \mid \text{data}) = (\alpha + k) / (\alpha + \beta + n)$. The posterior predictive always equals the posterior mean because the likelihood is linear in $\theta$.

Exercise 25: Information Gain

Define the information gain from observing $B$ as the KL divergence from prior to posterior: $\text{IG} = D_{KL}(P(A \mid B) \| P(A))$. For the medical testing example (prevalence 0.1%, sensitivity 99%, specificity 95%), compute the expected information gain $E_B[\text{IG}]$ over both possible test outcomes.

Show Solution

We have a binary hypothesis $D \in \{0, 1\}$ and binary test result $T \in \{+, -\}$.

Prior: $P(D=1) = 0.001$, $P(D=0) = 0.999$

Test positive: $P(+) = 0.05094$

  • $P(D=1 \mid +) = 0.01943$
  • $P(D=0 \mid +) = 0.98057$

Test negative: $P(-) = 0.94906$

  • $P(D=1 \mid -) = \frac{0.01 \times 0.001}{0.94906} \approx 0.00001054$
  • $P(D=0 \mid -) \approx 0.99998946$

KL divergence for positive result:

$$D_{KL}^{+} = 0.01943 \ln\frac{0.01943}{0.001} + 0.98057 \ln\frac{0.98057}{0.999} \approx 0.01943 \times 2.966 + 0.98057 \times (-0.01879)$$ $$\approx 0.05763 - 0.01842 = 0.03921 \text{ nats}$$

KL divergence for negative result:

$$D_{KL}^{-} = 0.00001054 \ln\frac{0.00001054}{0.001} + 0.99998946 \ln\frac{0.99998946}{0.999}$$ $$\approx 0.00001054 \times (-4.554) + 0.99998946 \times 0.000990 \approx -0.0000480 + 0.000990 = 0.000942 \text{ nats}$$

Expected information gain:

$$E[\text{IG}] = P(+) \cdot D_{KL}^{+} + P(-) \cdot D_{KL}^{-}$$ $$= 0.05094 \times 0.03921 + 0.94906 \times 0.000942 \approx 0.001997 + 0.000894 = 0.00289 \text{ nats}$$

Answer: Expected IG $\approx 0.00289$ nats $\approx 0.00417$ bits. The expected information gain is small because the test rarely changes our belief much—positive results are common but mostly false positives, and negative results are common but barely change the already-low prior.

Exercise 26: Bayesian Model Comparison

You are comparing two models: $M_1$ says a coin is fair ($\theta = 0.5$) and $M_2$ says the coin has an unknown bias with $\theta \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$. You observe 9 heads in 10 flips. Compute the Bayes factor $\text{BF}_{21} = P(\text{data} \mid M_2) / P(\text{data} \mid M_1)$. Which model does the evidence favor?

Show Solution

Model 1 (fair coin, point hypothesis):

$$P(\text{data} \mid M_1) = \binom{10}{9} (0.5)^{10} = 10 \times \frac{1}{1024} = \frac{10}{1024} \approx 0.00977$$

Model 2 (unknown bias, marginal likelihood):

$$P(\text{data} \mid M_2) = \int_0^1 P(\text{data} \mid \theta) \, p(\theta \mid M_2) \, d\theta = \int_0^1 \binom{10}{9} \theta^9 (1-\theta)^1 \cdot 1 \, d\theta$$ $$= 10 \int_0^1 \theta^9 (1-\theta) \, d\theta = 10 \cdot B(10, 2) = 10 \cdot \frac{\Gamma(10)\Gamma(2)}{\Gamma(12)} = 10 \cdot \frac{9! \cdot 1!}{11!} = 10 \cdot \frac{1}{110} = \frac{1}{11} \approx 0.09091$$

Bayes factor:

$$\text{BF}_{21} = \frac{P(\text{data} \mid M_2)}{P(\text{data} \mid M_1)} = \frac{1/11}{10/1024} = \frac{1024}{110} \approx 9.31$$

Answer: $\text{BF}_{21} \approx 9.3$, which is "strong evidence" in favor of $M_2$ (biased coin) over $M_1$ (fair coin) on the Jeffreys scale. The data (9/10 heads) is about 9 times more likely under the biased-coin model.

Exercise 27: Conjugate Prior for the Poisson

Show that the Gamma distribution is the conjugate prior for the Poisson likelihood. Given a $\text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$ prior on the rate parameter $\lambda$ and observations $x_1, \ldots, x_n \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, derive the posterior distribution.

Show Solution

Prior: $p(\lambda) = \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta) = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} \lambda^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta\lambda}$

Likelihood:

$$P(\mathbf{x} \mid \lambda) = \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{\lambda^{x_i} e^{-\lambda}}{x_i!} = \frac{\lambda^{\sum x_i} e^{-n\lambda}}{\prod x_i!}$$

Posterior $\propto$ Likelihood $\times$ Prior:

$$p(\lambda \mid \mathbf{x}) \propto \lambda^{\sum x_i} e^{-n\lambda} \cdot \lambda^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta\lambda} = \lambda^{(\alpha + \sum x_i) - 1} e^{-(\beta + n)\lambda}$$

This is the kernel of a Gamma distribution:

$$\boxed{p(\lambda \mid \mathbf{x}) = \text{Gamma}\!\left(\alpha + \sum_{i=1}^n x_i, \; \beta + n\right)}$$

Interpretation:

  • Shape: $\alpha_{\text{post}} = \alpha + \sum x_i$ (prior "pseudo-count" plus observed total count)
  • Rate: $\beta_{\text{post}} = \beta + n$ (prior "pseudo-observations" plus number of observations)
  • Posterior mean: $E[\lambda \mid \mathbf{x}] = \frac{\alpha + \sum x_i}{\beta + n}$

Since the posterior is the same family (Gamma) as the prior, the Gamma is indeed conjugate to the Poisson. $\square$

Exercise 28: Bayesian Linear Regression

In Bayesian linear regression with prior $\mathbf{w} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma_w^2 \mathbf{I})$ and likelihood $\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{w} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}, \sigma^2\mathbf{I})$, derive the posterior mean and covariance of $\mathbf{w}$.

Show Solution

Define prior precision $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}_0 = \frac{1}{\sigma_w^2}\mathbf{I}$ and likelihood precision per sample $\frac{1}{\sigma^2}$.

Log-posterior:

$$\log p(\mathbf{w} \mid \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{X}) = -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}\|^2 - \frac{1}{2\sigma_w^2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 + \text{const}$$ $$= -\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{w}^\top\!\left(\frac{1}{\sigma^2}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X} + \frac{1}{\sigma_w^2}\mathbf{I}\right)\mathbf{w} + \mathbf{w}^\top\!\left(\frac{1}{\sigma^2}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y}\right) + \text{const}$$

This is a quadratic in $\mathbf{w}$, so the posterior is Gaussian.

Posterior covariance:

$$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\text{post}} = \left(\frac{1}{\sigma^2}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X} + \frac{1}{\sigma_w^2}\mathbf{I}\right)^{-1}$$

Posterior mean:

$$\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{post}} = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\text{post}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y} = \left(\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X} + \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma_w^2}\mathbf{I}\right)^{-1}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y}$$

Answer: $\mathbf{w} \mid \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{X} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{post}}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\text{post}})$ where:

$$\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{post}} = \left(\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X} + \lambda\mathbf{I}\right)^{-1}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y}, \quad \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\text{post}} = \sigma^2\left(\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X} + \lambda\mathbf{I}\right)^{-1}$$

with $\lambda = \sigma^2/\sigma_w^2$. The posterior mean is the ridge regression solution, confirming the MAP connection from Exercise 22.

Exercise 29: Bayesian Hypothesis Testing with Continuous Evidence

You receive a signal $x$ that is either noise $x \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ (hypothesis $H_0$) or signal-plus-noise $x \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, 1)$ with $\mu > 0$ (hypothesis $H_1$). With equal priors, derive the posterior probability of $H_1$ as a function of $x$ and $\mu$. For $\mu = 2$, at what threshold $x^*$ does the posterior probability of $H_1$ exceed 95%?

Show Solution

With equal priors $P(H_0) = P(H_1) = 1/2$:

$$P(H_1 \mid x) = \frac{p(x \mid H_1)}{p(x \mid H_0) + p(x \mid H_1)}$$

Likelihood ratio:

$$\Lambda(x) = \frac{p(x \mid H_1)}{p(x \mid H_0)} = \frac{\exp(-(x-\mu)^2/2)}{\exp(-x^2/2)} = \exp\!\left(\mu x - \frac{\mu^2}{2}\right)$$

Posterior:

$$P(H_1 \mid x) = \frac{\Lambda(x)}{1 + \Lambda(x)} = \frac{1}{1 + \exp(-\mu x + \mu^2/2)} = \sigma\!\left(\mu x - \frac{\mu^2}{2}\right)$$

where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function! This is a logistic function of the observation $x$.

Finding threshold for 95% ($\mu = 2$):

$$P(H_1 \mid x) > 0.95 \iff \Lambda(x) > 19 \iff \exp(2x - 2) > 19 \iff 2x - 2 > \ln 19$$ $$x > \frac{\ln 19 + 2}{2} = \frac{2.944 + 2}{2} = 2.472$$

Answer: $P(H_1 \mid x) = \sigma(\mu x - \mu^2/2)$. For $\mu = 2$, the threshold is $x^* \approx 2.47$.

Exercise 30: Cromwell's Rule and Improper Priors

(a) Cromwell's Rule states that one should not assign probability 0 or 1 to any hypothesis that is not logically impossible. Using the odds form of Bayes' theorem, prove that if $P(H) = 0$, no amount of evidence can ever make $P(H \mid B) > 0$. (b) Show that the improper prior $p(\theta) = 1$ for $\theta \in (-\infty, \infty)$ on a Gaussian mean leads to a proper posterior when combined with at least one observation. (c) Give an example where an improper prior leads to an improper posterior.

Show Solution

(a) In odds form: $O(H \mid B) = \Lambda \cdot O(H)$.

If $P(H) = 0$, then $O(H) = P(H)/P(\neg H) = 0/1 = 0$.

For any finite Bayes factor $\Lambda$:

$$O(H \mid B) = \Lambda \times 0 = 0 \implies P(H \mid B) = 0$$

No finite amount of evidence can change a prior of exactly 0. The hypothesis is permanently "dead." This is why Cromwell advised: "I beseech you, in the bowels of Christ, think it possible that you may be mistaken." In Bayesian terms: never assign $P(H) = 0$ to a conceivable hypothesis. $\square$

(b) Improper prior: $p(\theta) \propto 1$ for $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$. This doesn't integrate to 1.

With one observation $x \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, \sigma^2)$:

$$p(\theta \mid x) \propto p(x \mid \theta) \cdot p(\theta) \propto \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x - \theta)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) \cdot 1 = \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x - \theta)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

This is the kernel of $\mathcal{N}(x, \sigma^2)$, which integrates to $\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2} < \infty$. So the posterior is proper: $\theta \mid x \sim \mathcal{N}(x, \sigma^2)$. $\square$

(c) Consider the model $x \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with both $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ unknown and the (improper) prior $p(\mu, \sigma^2) \propto 1/\sigma^2$ (Jeffreys' prior). With zero observations ($n = 0$):

$$p(\mu, \sigma^2 \mid \text{no data}) \propto p(\text{no data} \mid \mu, \sigma^2) \cdot p(\mu, \sigma^2) = 1 \cdot \frac{1}{\sigma^2}$$

This does not integrate: $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\int_0^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \, d\sigma^2 \, d\mu = \infty$. The posterior is improper.

More practically: take $x_1, \ldots, x_n \sim \text{Uniform}(0, \theta)$ with improper prior $p(\theta) \propto 1/\theta$ and observe no data. Then $p(\theta \mid \text{no data}) \propto 1/\theta$, which diverges at both 0 and $\infty$. With data, $p(\theta \mid x_{(n)}) \propto \theta^{-n} \cdot \theta^{-1}$ for $\theta \geq x_{(n)}$, which is proper for $n \geq 1$. $\square$
