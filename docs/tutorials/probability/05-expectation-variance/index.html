05. Expectation & Variance

Expectation and variance are the two most fundamental numerical summaries of any probability distribution. Expectation captures the center—the long-run average—while variance captures the spread—how far values typically fall from that center. This tutorial develops both concepts from first principles, proves their key properties, and connects them directly to machine learning through the bias-variance tradeoff.

Why Expectation?

A probability distribution can be complicated—it assigns a probability to every possible outcome. But in practice, we often need a single number that summarizes "what to expect." This is exactly what the expected value (or expectation, or mean) provides.

Consider a gambler evaluating a bet. The game costs $10 to play. With probability $1/6$ you win $50, and with probability $5/6$ you win nothing. Should you play? The expected value of your net gain is:

$$E[\text{gain}] = \frac{1}{6}(50 - 10) + \frac{5}{6}(0 - 10) = \frac{40}{6} - \frac{50}{6} = -\frac{10}{6} \approx -\$1.67$$

On average, you lose $1.67 per play. The expected value reveals this game is unfavorable even though the prize looks attractive.

The long-run average interpretation. If you repeat a random experiment independently many times, the average of the observed values converges to the expected value. This is the Law of Large Numbers, which we will encounter formally in a later tutorial. For now, the intuition is:

$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i \xrightarrow{n \to \infty} E[X]$$

Fair games. A game is called fair if the expected net gain is zero: $E[\text{gain}] = 0$. In the gambling example above, the game would be fair if the entry fee were $50/6 \approx \$8.33$. Casinos ensure that every game has a negative expected value for the player—this is the house edge.

Beyond gambling, expected value appears everywhere: insurance companies use it to set premiums, decision theorists use it to rank alternatives, and machine learning models minimize expected loss over the data distribution.

Definition of Expected Value

The expected value of a random variable $X$ is a weighted average of all possible values, where the weights are the probabilities.

Discrete case. If $X$ takes values $x_1, x_2, \ldots$ with probability mass function $p(x_i) = P(X = x_i)$:

$$\boxed{E[X] = \sum_{i} x_i \, p(x_i)}$$

Continuous case. If $X$ has probability density function $f(x)$:

$$\boxed{E[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x \, f(x) \, dx}$$

Conditions for existence. The expected value exists (is well-defined and finite) if and only if $\sum_i |x_i| \, p(x_i) < \infty$ (discrete) or $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |x| \, f(x) \, dx < \infty$ (continuous). The absolute convergence requirement prevents situations where rearranging terms changes the sum. The Cauchy distribution is the classic example where $E[X]$ does not exist: $f(x) = \frac{1}{\pi(1 + x^2)}$ has tails so heavy that $\int |x| f(x) \, dx = \infty$.

Examples:

  • Fair die: $E[X] = \sum_{k=1}^{6} k \cdot \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1+2+3+4+5+6}{6} = \frac{21}{6} = 3.5$
  • Bernoulli($p$): $E[X] = 0 \cdot (1-p) + 1 \cdot p = p$
  • Exponential($\lambda$): $E[X] = \int_0^{\infty} x \lambda e^{-\lambda x} dx = \frac{1}{\lambda}$ (by integration by parts)
  • Uniform$(a, b)$: $E[X] = \int_a^b x \cdot \frac{1}{b-a} \, dx = \frac{a + b}{2}$

Figure 1. The expected value $E[X]$ is the balance point (center of mass) of the probability distribution. If you cut out the histogram from cardboard, it would balance on a needle placed at $E[X]$.

The "balance point" analogy is more than a metaphor. In physics, the center of mass of a system of point masses $m_i$ at positions $x_i$ is $\bar{x} = \frac{\sum m_i x_i}{\sum m_i}$. Since probabilities sum to 1, the expected value has the identical form.

Law of the Unconscious Statistician (LOTUS)

Suppose you know the distribution of $X$ and want to find $E[g(X)]$ for some function $g$. One approach: determine the distribution of $Y = g(X)$ first, then compute $E[Y]$. But this is often tedious. LOTUS provides a shortcut:

$$\boxed{E[g(X)] = \sum_{x} g(x) \, p(x) \quad \text{(discrete)} \qquad E[g(X)] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x) \, f(x) \, dx \quad \text{(continuous)}}$$

The name "unconscious statistician" is somewhat tongue-in-cheek: the formula is so natural that many students use it without realizing it requires proof. The key insight is that you can compute the expectation of any function of $X$ using the original distribution of $X$, without needing the distribution of $g(X)$.

Why is LOTUS useful? Consider finding $E[X^2]$. By LOTUS:

$$E[X^2] = \sum_x x^2 \, p(x) \quad \text{or} \quad E[X^2] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x^2 \, f(x) \, dx$$

This is far easier than first finding the distribution of $Y = X^2$ and then computing $E[Y]$. We will see that $E[X^2]$ plays a central role in computing variance.

Example: Let $X \sim \text{Uniform}\{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$ (fair die). Find $E[X^2]$.

$$E[X^2] = \sum_{k=1}^{6} k^2 \cdot \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1 + 4 + 9 + 16 + 25 + 36}{6} = \frac{91}{6} \approx 15.17$$

Example: Let $X \sim \text{Exponential}(\lambda)$. Find $E[e^{tX}]$ for $t < \lambda$.

$$E[e^{tX}] = \int_0^{\infty} e^{tx} \cdot \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \, dx = \lambda \int_0^{\infty} e^{-({\lambda - t})x} \, dx = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}$$

This is the moment generating function of the exponential distribution, which we will study in detail in Tutorial 09.

Linearity of Expectation

The single most powerful property of expectation is its linearity:

$$\boxed{E[aX + b] = aE[X] + b}$$ $$\boxed{E[X + Y] = E[X] + E[Y]}$$

The second equation is remarkable: it holds always, even when $X$ and $Y$ are dependent! This makes linearity of expectation an extraordinarily versatile tool.

Proof of $E[X + Y] = E[X] + E[Y]$ (discrete case):

$$E[X + Y] = \sum_x \sum_y (x + y) \, p(x, y) = \sum_x \sum_y x \, p(x, y) + \sum_x \sum_y y \, p(x, y)$$
$$= \sum_x x \underbrace{\sum_y p(x, y)}_{p_X(x)} + \sum_y y \underbrace{\sum_x p(x, y)}_{p_Y(y)} = E[X] + E[Y]$$

The proof uses only the definition of marginal distributions—no independence assumption is needed!

By induction, linearity extends to any finite sum: $E\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i X_i + b\right] = \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i E[X_i] + b$.

Application: Expected number of fixed points. Suppose $n$ people each put a hat in a pile, and each randomly picks one. How many people get their own hat back?

Let $X_i = 1$ if person $i$ gets their own hat (a fixed point), and $X_i = 0$ otherwise. The total number of matches is $X = \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i$. By linearity:

$$E[X] = \sum_{i=1}^{n} E[X_i] = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(X_i = 1) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{n} = 1$$

On average, exactly one person gets their own hat, regardless of $n$. This elegant result would be very difficult to derive by computing $P(X = k)$ directly. The $X_i$'s are dependent (if person 1 gets their hat, it affects others), but linearity of expectation does not care.

Variance

Expectation tells us the center of a distribution, but two distributions can share the same mean yet look completely different. We need a measure of spread.

Motivation. A natural first attempt might be $E[X - \mu]$ where $\mu = E[X]$, but this is always zero (positive and negative deviations cancel). Using the absolute deviation $E[|X - \mu|]$ works but is analytically inconvenient (not differentiable). Instead, we square the deviations:

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(X) = E[(X - \mu)^2]}$$

The computational formula is often more convenient:

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2}$$

Proof:

$$\text{Var}(X) = E[(X - \mu)^2] = E[X^2 - 2\mu X + \mu^2]$$
$$= E[X^2] - 2\mu E[X] + \mu^2 = E[X^2] - 2\mu^2 + \mu^2 = E[X^2] - \mu^2$$

A useful mnemonic: "the mean of the square minus the square of the mean."

Standard deviation is simply $\sigma_X = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}$. It has the same units as $X$ (variance has squared units), making it more interpretable.

Examples:

  • Bernoulli($p$): $E[X] = p$, $E[X^2] = p$ (since $X^2 = X$ for $X \in \{0,1\}$), so $\text{Var}(X) = p - p^2 = p(1-p)$. Maximum at $p = 1/2$.
  • Fair die: $E[X] = 3.5$, $E[X^2] = 91/6$, so $\text{Var}(X) = 91/6 - (3.5)^2 = 91/6 - 49/4 = 35/12 \approx 2.92$.
  • Exponential($\lambda$): $E[X] = 1/\lambda$, $E[X^2] = 2/\lambda^2$, so $\text{Var}(X) = 2/\lambda^2 - 1/\lambda^2 = 1/\lambda^2$.

Figure 2. Two distributions sharing the same mean $\mu$ but with very different variances. The blue (solid) curve has low variance—values cluster near the mean. The red (dashed) curve has high variance—values are more spread out.

Properties of Variance

Variance behaves differently from expectation under linear transformations:

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(aX + b) = a^2 \, \text{Var}(X)}$$

Proof:

$$\text{Var}(aX + b) = E[(aX + b - E[aX + b])^2] = E[(aX + b - aE[X] - b)^2] = E[a^2(X - E[X])^2] = a^2 \text{Var}(X)$$

Key observations: adding a constant $b$ shifts the distribution but does not change its spread, so variance is unaffected. Scaling by $a$ scales the spread, but variance involves squared deviations, so the factor is $a^2$.

Variance of a sum: For two random variables $X$ and $Y$:

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) + 2\text{Cov}(X, Y)}$$

Proof:

$$\text{Var}(X + Y) = E[(X + Y - E[X+Y])^2]$$
$$= E[((X - \mu_X) + (Y - \mu_Y))^2]$$
$$= E[(X - \mu_X)^2] + 2E[(X - \mu_X)(Y - \mu_Y)] + E[(Y - \mu_Y)^2]$$
$$= \text{Var}(X) + 2\text{Cov}(X, Y) + \text{Var}(Y)$$

If $X$ and $Y$ are independent (or merely uncorrelated), then $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = 0$ and we get the simpler result:

$$\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) \quad \text{(when } X \perp Y \text{)}$$

For $n$ independent random variables: $\text{Var}\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i)$. This is crucial: while the mean of a sum equals the sum of means always, the variance of a sum equals the sum of variances only for independent (or uncorrelated) variables.

Important consequence for the sample mean: If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are i.i.d. with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$:

$$\text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \text{Var}\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i\right) = \frac{1}{n^2} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sigma^2 = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$$

The variance of the sample mean shrinks as $1/n$—this is why averaging reduces noise.

Covariance and Correlation

Covariance measures the degree to which two random variables move together:

$$\boxed{\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[(X - \mu_X)(Y - \mu_Y)] = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y]}$$

If $\text{Cov}(X, Y) > 0$: when $X$ is above its mean, $Y$ tends to be above its mean too (positive association). If $\text{Cov}(X, Y) < 0$: they tend to move in opposite directions. If $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = 0$: the variables are uncorrelated.

Properties of covariance:

  • $\text{Cov}(X, X) = \text{Var}(X)$
  • $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = \text{Cov}(Y, X)$ (symmetric)
  • $\text{Cov}(aX + b, Y) = a\,\text{Cov}(X, Y)$ (linear in each argument)
  • $\text{Cov}(X + Y, Z) = \text{Cov}(X, Z) + \text{Cov}(Y, Z)$ (additive)
  • If $X \perp Y$, then $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = 0$ (but the converse is false)

Correlation is the standardized version of covariance:

$$\boxed{\text{Cor}(X, Y) = \rho_{XY} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \, \sigma_Y}}$$

Correlation is dimensionless and satisfies $-1 \leq \rho_{XY} \leq 1$ (by the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality). The extreme values $\rho = \pm 1$ occur if and only if $Y$ is a linear function of $X$: $Y = aX + b$.

Uncorrelated $\neq$ independent. This is a critical distinction. Let $X \sim \text{Uniform}\{-1, 0, 1\}$ and $Y = X^2$. Then:

$$E[XY] = E[X \cdot X^2] = E[X^3] = \frac{(-1)^3 + 0^3 + 1^3}{3} = 0$$
$$E[X] = 0, \quad \text{so } \text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y] = 0 - 0 = 0$$

So $X$ and $Y$ are uncorrelated. But $Y$ is completely determined by $X$—they are maximally dependent! Uncorrelatedness only rules out linear dependence.

Higher Moments

The $k$-th raw moment (or moment about the origin) is $\mu_k' = E[X^k]$. The $k$-th central moment (moment about the mean) is $\mu_k = E[(X - \mu)^k]$.

  • $k = 1$: The first moment is the mean. $\mu_1' = E[X] = \mu$.
  • $k = 2$: The second central moment is the variance. $\mu_2 = \text{Var}(X)$.
  • $k = 3$: The third central moment relates to skewness.
  • $k = 4$: The fourth central moment relates to kurtosis.

Skewness measures asymmetry of the distribution:

$$\text{Skew}(X) = E\!\left[\left(\frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\right)^3\right] = \frac{\mu_3}{\sigma^3}$$
  • $\text{Skew} > 0$: right-tailed (tail extends to the right)
  • $\text{Skew} < 0$: left-tailed (tail extends to the left)
  • $\text{Skew} = 0$: symmetric (e.g., Normal, Uniform)

Kurtosis measures the "tailedness" of the distribution:

$$\text{Kurt}(X) = E\!\left[\left(\frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\right)^4\right] = \frac{\mu_4}{\sigma^4}$$

The Normal distribution has kurtosis 3. The excess kurtosis is $\text{Kurt}(X) - 3$, which is zero for the Normal. Distributions with excess kurtosis $> 0$ (leptokurtic) have heavier tails than the Normal; those with excess kurtosis $< 0$ (platykurtic) have lighter tails.

Why moments matter: Under certain regularity conditions, the sequence of all moments $\{E[X^k]\}_{k=1}^{\infty}$ uniquely determines the distribution. This is the basis of the method of moments for parameter estimation.

Conditional Expectation

Just as conditional probability refines our beliefs given partial information, conditional expectation refines our expected value given partial information.

Definition. The conditional expectation of $X$ given $Y = y$ is:

$$E[X \mid Y = y] = \sum_x x \, p_{X|Y}(x \mid y) \quad \text{(discrete)}$$
$$E[X \mid Y = y] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x \, f_{X|Y}(x \mid y) \, dx \quad \text{(continuous)}$$

Note that $E[X \mid Y = y]$ is a number (it depends on the specific value $y$). When we write $E[X \mid Y]$ (without specifying the value), we treat it as a random variable—a function of $Y$.

Law of Iterated Expectation (Tower Property): This is one of the most powerful results in all of probability:

$$\boxed{E[X] = E\!\big[E[X \mid Y]\big]}$$

In words: the overall expected value of $X$ equals the expected value of the conditional expected values. To find $E[X]$, you can "condition on" any convenient random variable $Y$, compute $E[X \mid Y]$ for each possible value of $Y$, and then average over $Y$.

Proof (discrete case):

$$E\!\big[E[X \mid Y]\big] = \sum_y E[X \mid Y = y] \cdot P(Y = y)$$
$$= \sum_y \left(\sum_x x \, P(X = x \mid Y = y)\right) P(Y = y) = \sum_y \sum_x x \, P(X = x, Y = y)$$
$$= \sum_x x \sum_y P(X = x, Y = y) = \sum_x x \, P(X = x) = E[X] \qquad \square$$

Example: Random number of coin flips. You roll a fair die to get $N$, then flip a fair coin $N$ times. Let $X$ = number of heads. What is $E[X]$?

$$E[X \mid N = n] = n \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{n}{2}$$
$$E[X] = E\!\left[E[X \mid N]\right] = E\!\left[\frac{N}{2}\right] = \frac{E[N]}{2} = \frac{3.5}{2} = 1.75$$

Example: Random walk stopping time. You flip a fair coin until you get heads. Let $T$ be the number of flips. Find $E[T]$ using conditioning.

Condition on the first flip. With probability $1/2$ we get heads immediately ($T = 1$). With probability $1/2$ we get tails, and then we're back where we started, needing $1 + E[T]$ more flips. So:

$$E[T] = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 1 + \frac{1}{2}(1 + E[T]) = 1 + \frac{1}{2}E[T]$$
$$\frac{1}{2}E[T] = 1 \implies E[T] = 2$$

Conditional Variance

The conditional variance $\text{Var}(X \mid Y = y) = E[(X - E[X \mid Y = y])^2 \mid Y = y]$ measures the remaining uncertainty in $X$ after observing $Y = y$.

The Law of Total Variance (also called the variance decomposition formula or Eve's law) provides a beautiful decomposition:

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(X) = E\!\big[\text{Var}(X \mid Y)\big] + \text{Var}\!\big(E[X \mid Y]\big)}$$

This says: total variance = within-group variance + between-group variance.

  • $E[\text{Var}(X \mid Y)]$: the average variance within each group defined by $Y$ (unexplained variance)
  • $\text{Var}(E[X \mid Y])$: the variance of the group means (explained variance)

Proof: We use the identity $\text{Var}(Z) = E[Z^2] - (E[Z])^2$ applied to $Z = X \mid Y$:

$$\text{Var}(X \mid Y) = E[X^2 \mid Y] - (E[X \mid Y])^2$$

Taking expectations of both sides:

$$E[\text{Var}(X \mid Y)] = E[E[X^2 \mid Y]] - E[(E[X \mid Y])^2] = E[X^2] - E[(E[X \mid Y])^2]$$

For the second term, note that:

$$\text{Var}(E[X \mid Y]) = E[(E[X \mid Y])^2] - (E[E[X \mid Y]])^2 = E[(E[X \mid Y])^2] - (E[X])^2$$

Adding:

$$E[\text{Var}(X \mid Y)] + \text{Var}(E[X \mid Y]) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = \text{Var}(X) \qquad \square$$

Example: Random coin flips (continued). Roll a die $N$, flip $N$ coins, $X$ = number of heads.

$$\text{Var}(X \mid N = n) = n \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{n}{4}$$
$$E[\text{Var}(X \mid N)] = E\!\left[\frac{N}{4}\right] = \frac{E[N]}{4} = \frac{3.5}{4} = 0.875$$
$$\text{Var}(E[X \mid N]) = \text{Var}\!\left(\frac{N}{2}\right) = \frac{1}{4}\text{Var}(N) = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{35}{12} = \frac{35}{48} \approx 0.729$$
$$\text{Var}(X) = 0.875 + 0.729 = \frac{77}{48} \approx 1.604$$

Connections to Machine Learning

Expectation and variance are not just abstract concepts—they are the language in which machine learning is formulated.

Loss functions as expectations. When we train a model $\hat{f}$ to predict $Y$ from $X$, we minimize the expected loss (or risk):

$$R(\hat{f}) = E[L(Y, \hat{f}(X))]$$

For squared loss $L(y, \hat{y}) = (y - \hat{y})^2$, the optimal predictor is the conditional expectation $\hat{f}^*(x) = E[Y \mid X = x]$. This shows that conditional expectation is not just a theoretical construct but the optimal prediction under squared loss.

The Bias-Variance Tradeoff. Consider the mean squared error of an estimator $\hat{\theta}$ for a parameter $\theta$:

$$\boxed{\text{MSE}(\hat{\theta}) = \text{Bias}(\hat{\theta})^2 + \text{Var}(\hat{\theta})}$$

where $\text{Bias}(\hat{\theta}) = E[\hat{\theta}] - \theta$.

Proof:

$$\text{MSE}(\hat{\theta}) = E[(\hat{\theta} - \theta)^2] = E[(\hat{\theta} - E[\hat{\theta}] + E[\hat{\theta}] - \theta)^2]$$
$$= E[(\hat{\theta} - E[\hat{\theta}])^2] + 2(E[\hat{\theta}] - \theta)\underbrace{E[\hat{\theta} - E[\hat{\theta}]]}_{=0} + (E[\hat{\theta}] - \theta)^2$$
$$= \text{Var}(\hat{\theta}) + \text{Bias}(\hat{\theta})^2$$

Figure 3. The bias-variance tradeoff. As model complexity increases, bias decreases (the model can fit the data better) but variance increases (the model becomes more sensitive to the specific training set). The total MSE = Bias$^2$ + Variance is minimized at an intermediate complexity level.

Gradient descent minimizes expected loss. In stochastic gradient descent (SGD), we approximate the gradient of the expected loss using mini-batches:

$$\nabla_\theta R(\theta) = E\!\left[\nabla_\theta L(Y, f_\theta(X))\right] \approx \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^{m} \nabla_\theta L(y_i, f_\theta(x_i))$$

The mini-batch gradient is an unbiased estimator of the true gradient: its expectation equals the true gradient. But it has variance, which causes the noisy trajectory of SGD. Reducing batch size increases variance; increasing it reduces variance but is computationally more expensive. This is yet another instance of the bias-variance tradeoff.

Regularization as variance reduction. Techniques like L2 regularization (ridge regression) intentionally introduce a small amount of bias to achieve a larger reduction in variance, lowering the overall MSE. This is a direct application of the MSE decomposition.

Ensemble methods. Bagging (bootstrap aggregating) reduces prediction variance by averaging predictions from multiple models trained on bootstrap samples. If individual models have variance $\sigma^2$ and pairwise correlation $\rho$, the ensemble variance is:

$$\text{Var}(\bar{f}) = \rho \sigma^2 + \frac{1 - \rho}{n}\sigma^2$$

As $n \to \infty$, this approaches $\rho \sigma^2$—the irreducible variance due to correlation between models. Random forests further reduce $\rho$ by randomizing feature selection.

Computing Expectation and Variance

import numpy as np

def expected_value_discrete(values, probabilities):
    """
    Compute E[X] for a discrete random variable.

    Parameters:
        values: array of possible values x_i
        probabilities: array of P(X = x_i)

    Returns:
        E[X] = sum(x_i * p_i)
    """
    values = np.array(values)
    probabilities = np.array(probabilities)
    assert np.isclose(probabilities.sum(), 1.0), "Probabilities must sum to 1"
    return np.sum(values * probabilities)


def variance_discrete(values, probabilities):
    """
    Compute Var(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 for a discrete random variable.
    """
    values = np.array(values)
    probabilities = np.array(probabilities)
    mean = expected_value_discrete(values, probabilities)
    mean_sq = expected_value_discrete(values**2, probabilities)
    return mean_sq - mean**2


# --- Fair die ---
vals = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]
probs = [1/6] * 6

print("=== Fair Die ===")
print(f"E[X]   = {expected_value_discrete(vals, probs):.4f}")       # 3.5
print(f"Var(X) = {variance_discrete(vals, probs):.4f}")             # 2.9167
print(f"SD(X)  = {np.sqrt(variance_discrete(vals, probs)):.4f}")    # 1.7078

# --- Simulation verification ---
np.random.seed(42)
n_samples = 1_000_000
samples = np.random.choice(vals, size=n_samples, p=probs)

print(f"\nSimulated E[X]   = {samples.mean():.4f}")
print(f"Simulated Var(X) = {samples.var():.4f}")

# --- Bernoulli(p) ---
p = 0.3
print(f"\n=== Bernoulli(p={p}) ===")
print(f"E[X]   = {expected_value_discrete([0, 1], [1-p, p]):.4f}")  # 0.3
print(f"Var(X) = {variance_discrete([0, 1], [1-p, p]):.4f}")        # 0.21

# --- Continuous: Exponential(lambda) via simulation ---
lam = 2.0
exp_samples = np.random.exponential(scale=1/lam, size=n_samples)
print(f"\n=== Exponential(lambda={lam}) ===")
print(f"Theoretical E[X]   = {1/lam:.4f}")
print(f"Simulated   E[X]   = {exp_samples.mean():.4f}")
print(f"Theoretical Var(X) = {1/lam**2:.4f}")
print(f"Simulated   Var(X) = {exp_samples.var():.4f}")

Linearity of Expectation: The Hat Problem

import numpy as np

def hat_experiment(n_people, n_trials=100_000):
    """
    Simulate the hat problem: n people randomly pick from n hats.
    Count how many people get their own hat (fixed points of a permutation).

    By linearity of expectation, E[fixed points] = 1, regardless of n.
    """
    fixed_points = np.zeros(n_trials)

    for trial in range(n_trials):
        # Random permutation: person i gets hat perm[i]
        perm = np.random.permutation(n_people)
        # Count fixed points: positions where perm[i] == i
        fixed_points[trial] = np.sum(perm == np.arange(n_people))

    return fixed_points

import numpy as np

def covariance(X, Y):
    """Compute Cov(X, Y) = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y]"""
    return np.mean(X * Y) - np.mean(X) * np.mean(Y)


def correlation(X, Y):
    """Compute Cor(X, Y) = Cov(X, Y) / (SD(X) * SD(Y))"""
    cov = covariance(X, Y)
    return cov / (np.std(X) * np.std(Y))

Bias-Variance Tradeoff Visualization

import numpy as np

def true_function(x):
    """The true underlying function we're trying to learn."""
    return np.sin(2 * x) + 0.5 * np.cos(4 * x)


def generate_data(n_points, noise_std=0.3):
    """Generate noisy training data."""
    X = np.sort(np.random.uniform(0, 2 * np.pi, n_points))
    y = true_function(X) + np.random.normal(0, noise_std, n_points)
    return X, y


def polynomial_fit(X_train, y_train, X_test, degree):
    """Fit a polynomial of given degree and return predictions."""
    coeffs = np.polyfit(X_train, y_train, degree)
    return np.polyval(coeffs, X_test)


def bias_variance_experiment(degrees, n_train=20, n_datasets=200,
                              noise_std=0.3):
    """
    Estimate bias^2, variance, and MSE for polynomial models
    of different degrees.

    For each degree:
      - Generate many training datasets
      - Fit a polynomial on each
      - Compute predictions at test points
      - Estimate Bias^2 = (E[f_hat] - f_true)^2
      - Estimate Variance = Var(f_hat)
      - MSE = Bias^2 + Variance + noise^2
    """
    X_test = np.linspace(0.1, 2 * np.pi - 0.1, 50)
    y_true = true_function(X_test)

    results = {'degree': [], 'bias_sq': [], 'variance': [], 'mse': []}

    for deg in degrees:
        predictions = np.zeros((n_datasets, len(X_test)))

        for i in range(n_datasets):
            X_train, y_train = generate_data(n_train, noise_std)
            predictions[i] = polynomial_fit(X_train, y_train, X_test, deg)

        # Average prediction across datasets
        mean_pred = predictions.mean(axis=0)

        # Bias^2: (E[f_hat(x)] - f(x))^2, averaged over test points
        bias_sq = np.mean((mean_pred - y_true)**2)

        # Variance: E[(f_hat(x) - E[f_hat(x)])^2], averaged over test points
        variance = np.mean(predictions.var(axis=0))

        # MSE: E[(f_hat(x) - y)^2] including noise
        mse = bias_sq + variance + noise_std**2

        results['degree'].append(deg)
        results['bias_sq'].append(bias_sq)
        results['variance'].append(variance)
        results['mse'].append(mse)

        print(f"Degree {deg:>2d}: Bias^2 = {bias_sq:.4f}, "
              f"Var = {variance:.4f}, MSE = {mse:.4f}")

    return results


np.random.seed(42)
degrees = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, 10, 15, 18]

print("=== Bias-Variance Tradeoff ===")
print(f"True function: sin(2x) + 0.5*cos(4x)")
print(f"Training size: 20 points, Noise std: 0.3\n")

results = bias_variance_experiment(degrees)

best_idx = np.argmin(results['mse'])
print(f"\nBest degree: {results['degree'][best_idx]} "
      f"(MSE = {results['mse'][best_idx]:.4f})")
print(f"  Bias^2   = {results['bias_sq'][best_idx]:.4f}")
print(f"  Variance = {results['variance'][best_idx]:.4f}")
print(f"  Noise^2  = {0.3**2:.4f}")

Exercise 1: Expected Value of Two Dice

You roll two fair six-sided dice. Let $S$ be the sum of the two dice. Compute $E[S]$ and $\text{Var}(S)$.

Exercise 2: Coin Flip Expectation

You flip a fair coin 100 times. Let $X$ be the number of heads. Find $E[X]$ and $\text{Var}(X)$.

Show Solution

$$\text{Var}(X) = \sum_{i=1}^{100} \text{Var}(X_i) = 100 \cdot \frac{1}{4} = 25$$

Answer: $E[X] = 50$, $\text{Var}(X) = 25$, $\text{SD}(X) = 5$.

$$\text{Var}(Y) = \text{Var}(3X - 7) = 3^2 \text{Var}(X) = 9 \cdot 4 = 36$$

Answer: $E[Y] = 8$, $\text{Var}(Y) = 36$, $\text{SD}(Y) = 6$.

$$E[e^X] = \int_0^1 e^x \cdot 1 \, dx = \left[e^x\right]_0^1 = e - 1 \approx 1.7183$$

Exercise 16: Covariance from Joint PMF

Two sensors $X$ and $Y$ each read 0 or 1 with joint PMF: $P(0,0) = 0.3$, $P(0,1) = 0.1$, $P(1,0) = 0.2$, $P(1,1) = 0.4$. Compute $\text{Cov}(X,Y)$ and determine whether $X$ and $Y$ are independent.

Marginals: $P(X=0) = 0.4$, $P(X=1) = 0.6$, $P(Y=0) = 0.5$, $P(Y=1) = 0.5$.

Exercise 18: Conditional Variance Formula

Prove that $\text{Var}(X \mid Y = y) = E[X^2 \mid Y = y] - (E[X \mid Y = y])^2$. Then apply it: let $(X, Y)$ have joint density $f(x,y) = 2$ on the triangle $0 < x < y < 1$. Find $\text{Var}(X \mid Y = y)$.

Show Solution

Proof: By definition, $\text{Var}(X \mid Y = y) = E[(X - E[X \mid Y=y])^2 \mid Y = y]$. Let $\mu = E[X \mid Y=y]$. Then:

$$E[(X - \mu)^2 \mid Y=y] = E[X^2 - 2\mu X + \mu^2 \mid Y=y] = E[X^2 \mid Y=y] - 2\mu^2 + \mu^2 = E[X^2 \mid Y=y] - \mu^2 \quad \square$$

Application: First, the marginal of $Y$: $f_Y(y) = \int_0^y 2 \, dx = 2y$ for $0 < y < 1$.

Conditional density: $f_{X|Y}(x \mid y) = \frac{f(x,y)}{f_Y(y)} = \frac{2}{2y} = \frac{1}{y}$ for $0 < x < y$.

This is $\text{Uniform}(0, y)$, so:

$$E[X \mid Y = y] = \frac{y}{2}, \quad \text{Var}(X \mid Y = y) = \frac{y^2}{12}$$

Answer: $\text{Var}(X \mid Y = y) = y^2/12$.

  • $\text{Cov}(A,C) = 0.4 \times 10 \times 10 = 40$

Exercise 20: Computing Higher Moments

Let $X \sim \text{Exponential}(\lambda)$ with PDF $f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}$ for $x \geq 0$. (a) Compute $E[X^n]$ for general $n$ using integration by parts or the MGF. (b) Find the skewness $\gamma_1 = E\left[\left(\frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\right)^3\right]$.

Show Solution

(a) The MGF of $X$ is $M(t) = E[e^{tX}] = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}$ for $t < \lambda$.

The $n$-th moment is $E[X^n] = M^{(n)}(0)$. We have $M(t) = \lambda(\lambda - t)^{-1}$, so:

$$M^{(n)}(t) = \lambda \cdot n! \cdot (\lambda - t)^{-(n+1)}$$ $$E[X^n] = M^{(n)}(0) = \frac{n!}{\lambda^n}$$

In particular: $E[X] = 1/\lambda$, $E[X^2] = 2/\lambda^2$, $E[X^3] = 6/\lambda^3$.

(b) $\mu = 1/\lambda$, $\sigma^2 = E[X^2] - \mu^2 = 2/\lambda^2 - 1/\lambda^2 = 1/\lambda^2$, so $\sigma = 1/\lambda$.

The third central moment:

$$E[(X-\mu)^3] = E[X^3] - 3\mu E[X^2] + 3\mu^2 E[X] - \mu^3$$ $$= \frac{6}{\lambda^3} - \frac{3}{\lambda}\cdot\frac{2}{\lambda^2} + \frac{3}{\lambda^2}\cdot\frac{1}{\lambda} - \frac{1}{\lambda^3} = \frac{6 - 6 + 3 - 1}{\lambda^3} = \frac{2}{\lambda^3}$$ $$\gamma_1 = \frac{E[(X-\mu)^3]}{\sigma^3} = \frac{2/\lambda^3}{1/\lambda^3} = 2$$

Answer: $E[X^n] = n!/\lambda^n$. The skewness is $\gamma_1 = 2$ (positive, meaning right-skewed), which is the same for all exponential distributions regardless of $\lambda$.

Exercise 21: Proving Variance Properties

(a) Prove that $\text{Var}(X) \geq 0$ for any random variable $X$, with equality if and only if $X$ is a constant a.s.

(b) Prove that for any constants $a, b$ and random variables $X, Y$: $\text{Cov}(aX + b, Y) = a\,\text{Cov}(X, Y)$.

(c) Prove the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality for random variables: $|\text{Cov}(X, Y)| \leq \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)\,\text{Var}(Y)}$, i.e., $|\rho| \leq 1$.

Show Solution

(a) $\text{Var}(X) = E[(X - \mu)^2]$. Since $(X - \mu)^2 \geq 0$ always, its expectation is non-negative. Equality holds iff $(X - \mu)^2 = 0$ a.s., i.e., $X = \mu$ with probability 1. $\square$

(b)

$$\text{Cov}(aX + b, Y) = E[(aX + b)(Y)] - E[aX + b]E[Y]$$ $$= E[aXY + bY] - (aE[X] + b)E[Y]$$ $$= aE[XY] + bE[Y] - aE[X]E[Y] - bE[Y]$$ $$= a(E[XY] - E[X]E[Y]) = a\,\text{Cov}(X, Y) \quad \square$$

(c) For any $t \in \mathbb{R}$, consider $\text{Var}(X + tY) \geq 0$:

$$\text{Var}(X + tY) = \text{Var}(X) + 2t\,\text{Cov}(X,Y) + t^2\text{Var}(Y) \geq 0$$

This is a quadratic in $t$ that is always non-negative, so its discriminant must be non-positive:

$$\Delta = 4\,\text{Cov}(X,Y)^2 - 4\,\text{Var}(X)\,\text{Var}(Y) \leq 0$$ $$\implies \text{Cov}(X,Y)^2 \leq \text{Var}(X)\,\text{Var}(Y)$$ $$\implies |\text{Cov}(X,Y)| \leq \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)\,\text{Var}(Y)} \quad \square$$

Exercise 22: Law of Total Variance Application

A factory has two machines. Machine A (used 60% of the time) produces items with mean weight 10g and standard deviation 1g. Machine B (used 40% of the time) produces items with mean weight 12g and standard deviation 2g. Find the overall mean and variance of item weights.

Show Solution

Let $W$ = weight, $M$ = machine used. $P(M=A) = 0.6$, $P(M=B) = 0.4$.

Overall mean (law of iterated expectation):

$$E[W] = E[E[W \mid M]] = P(A) \cdot E[W \mid A] + P(B) \cdot E[W \mid B]$$ $$= 0.6 \cdot 10 + 0.4 \cdot 12 = 6 + 4.8 = 10.8 \text{g}$$

Law of total variance:

$$\text{Var}(W) = E[\text{Var}(W \mid M)] + \text{Var}(E[W \mid M])$$

Within-group variance:

$$E[\text{Var}(W \mid M)] = 0.6 \cdot 1^2 + 0.4 \cdot 2^2 = 0.6 + 1.6 = 2.2$$

Between-group variance: $E[W \mid M]$ takes value 10 with prob 0.6, and 12 with prob 0.4.

$$E[E[W \mid M]] = 10.8 \text{ (computed above)}$$ $$\text{Var}(E[W \mid M]) = 0.6(10 - 10.8)^2 + 0.4(12 - 10.8)^2$$ $$= 0.6(0.64) + 0.4(1.44) = 0.384 + 0.576 = 0.96$$

Total variance:

$$\text{Var}(W) = 2.2 + 0.96 = 3.16$$

Answer: $E[W] = 10.8$g, $\text{Var}(W) = 3.16$g$^2$, $\text{SD}(W) \approx 1.78$g.

Note that the total SD (1.78g) is larger than either machine's individual SD. The between-group variance (machines have different means) adds to the overall spread.

Exercise 23: Bias-Variance Decomposition

Consider estimating $\theta$ from i.i.d. samples $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ from $\text{Normal}(\theta, \sigma^2)$.

(a) The MLE is $\hat{\theta}_1 = \bar{X}$. Show it is unbiased and compute its MSE.

(b) Consider the "shrinkage" estimator $\hat{\theta}_2 = c\bar{X}$ for some constant $0 < c < 1$. Compute its bias, variance, and MSE. Show that for certain values of $\theta$ and $c$, $\hat{\theta}_2$ has lower MSE than $\hat{\theta}_1$.

Show Solution

(a) $\bar{X} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$.

Bias: $E[\bar{X}] = \theta$, so $\text{Bias} = 0$ (unbiased).

Variance: $\text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \sigma^2/n$.

MSE: $\text{MSE}(\hat{\theta}_1) = 0 + \sigma^2/n = \sigma^2/n$.

(b) $\hat{\theta}_2 = c\bar{X}$.

Bias: $E[\hat{\theta}_2] = c\theta$, so $\text{Bias} = c\theta - \theta = (c-1)\theta$.

Variance: $\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_2) = c^2 \cdot \sigma^2/n$.

MSE:

$$\text{MSE}(\hat{\theta}_2) = (c-1)^2\theta^2 + \frac{c^2\sigma^2}{n}$$

We want $\text{MSE}(\hat{\theta}_2) < \text{MSE}(\hat{\theta}_1)$:

$$(c-1)^2\theta^2 + \frac{c^2\sigma^2}{n} < \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$$ $$(c-1)^2\theta^2 < \frac{(1-c^2)\sigma^2}{n} = \frac{(1-c)(1+c)\sigma^2}{n}$$

Dividing by $(1-c) > 0$ (since $c < 1$):

$$(1-c)\theta^2 < \frac{(1+c)\sigma^2}{n}$$

For example, with $c = 0.9$, $\sigma^2 = 1$, $n = 10$:

$$0.1\theta^2 < \frac{1.9}{10} = 0.19 \implies \theta^2 < 1.9 \implies |\theta| < 1.38$$

Answer: When $|\theta|$ is small relative to $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$, the biased shrinkage estimator achieves lower MSE. The variance reduction (from $\sigma^2/n$ to $c^2\sigma^2/n$) outweighs the introduced bias. This is the essence of regularization in ML.

Exercise 24: Conditional Expectation as Projection

Let $(X, Y)$ be jointly distributed with $E[X] = E[Y] = 0$, $\text{Var}(X) = \sigma_X^2$, $\text{Var}(Y) = \sigma_Y^2$, $\text{Cor}(X, Y) = \rho$.

(a) Find the constant $c$ that minimizes $E[(Y - cX)^2]$ (the best linear predictor of $Y$ from $X$).

(b) Show that the prediction error $Y - cX$ is uncorrelated with $X$.

(c) Compute the mean squared prediction error $E[(Y - cX)^2]$ and interpret it.

Show Solution

(a) Expand and minimize:

$$E[(Y - cX)^2] = E[Y^2] - 2cE[XY] + c^2E[X^2] = \sigma_Y^2 - 2c\rho\sigma_X\sigma_Y + c^2\sigma_X^2$$

Taking the derivative with respect to $c$ and setting to zero:

$$-2\rho\sigma_X\sigma_Y + 2c\sigma_X^2 = 0 \implies c^* = \frac{\rho\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\text{Var}(X)}$$

This is the regression coefficient.

(b)

$$\text{Cov}(Y - c^*X, X) = \text{Cov}(Y, X) - c^*\text{Var}(X)$$ $$= \rho\sigma_X\sigma_Y - \frac{\rho\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X}\sigma_X^2 = \rho\sigma_X\sigma_Y - \rho\sigma_X\sigma_Y = 0 \quad \square$$

The residual is uncorrelated with the predictor. This is the orthogonality condition, analogous to a geometric projection.

(c)

$$E[(Y - c^*X)^2] = \sigma_Y^2 - 2c^*\rho\sigma_X\sigma_Y + (c^*)^2\sigma_X^2$$ $$= \sigma_Y^2 - 2\frac{\rho\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X}\rho\sigma_X\sigma_Y + \frac{\rho^2\sigma_Y^2}{\sigma_X^2}\sigma_X^2$$ $$= \sigma_Y^2 - 2\rho^2\sigma_Y^2 + \rho^2\sigma_Y^2 = \sigma_Y^2(1 - \rho^2)$$

Answer: The MSE is $\sigma_Y^2(1 - \rho^2)$.

Interpretation: $\rho^2$ is the fraction of variance in $Y$ "explained" by $X$ ($R^2$ in regression). When $\rho = \pm 1$, the prediction is perfect. When $\rho = 0$, the best linear predictor is just 0 (the unconditional mean), and the error equals $\text{Var}(Y)$.

Exercise 25: The Rao-Blackwell Theorem

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. $\text{Bernoulli}(p)$ and let $T = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ be the sufficient statistic.

(a) Show that $\hat{p}_1 = X_1$ is an unbiased estimator of $p$ with $\text{Var}(\hat{p}_1) = p(1-p)$.

(b) Compute $\hat{p}_2 = E[X_1 \mid T]$ and show it equals $T/n = \bar{X}$.

(c) Show that $\text{Var}(\hat{p}_2) \leq \text{Var}(\hat{p}_1)$ and compute the variance reduction. Why is this guaranteed by the Rao-Blackwell theorem?

Show Solution

(a) $E[\hat{p}_1] = E[X_1] = p$ (unbiased). $\text{Var}(\hat{p}_1) = \text{Var}(X_1) = p(1-p)$.

(b) By symmetry, given $T = t$, each $X_i$ is equally likely to contribute to the sum. So:

$$E[X_1 \mid T = t] = \frac{t}{n}$$

Formally: given $T = t$, the conditional distribution of $(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ is uniform over all binary vectors summing to $t$. In any such vector, $X_1 = 1$ for exactly $\binom{n-1}{t-1}$ of the $\binom{n}{t}$ vectors. So $P(X_1 = 1 \mid T = t) = \frac{\binom{n-1}{t-1}}{\binom{n}{t}} = \frac{t}{n}$.

Therefore $\hat{p}_2 = E[X_1 \mid T] = T/n = \bar{X}$.

(c) $\text{Var}(\hat{p}_2) = \text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \frac{p(1-p)}{n}$.

The variance reduction is:

$$\text{Var}(\hat{p}_1) - \text{Var}(\hat{p}_2) = p(1-p) - \frac{p(1-p)}{n} = p(1-p)\left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right) = p(1-p) \cdot \frac{n-1}{n}$$

By the law of total variance:

$$\text{Var}(X_1) = E[\text{Var}(X_1 \mid T)] + \text{Var}(E[X_1 \mid T])$$ $$p(1-p) = E[\text{Var}(X_1 \mid T)] + \text{Var}(\hat{p}_2)$$

Since $E[\text{Var}(X_1 \mid T)] \geq 0$, we get $\text{Var}(\hat{p}_2) \leq p(1-p) = \text{Var}(\hat{p}_1)$.

The Rao-Blackwell theorem guarantees: if $\hat{p}_1$ is any unbiased estimator and $T$ is a sufficient statistic, then $\hat{p}_2 = E[\hat{p}_1 \mid T]$ is also unbiased and has $\text{Var}(\hat{p}_2) \leq \text{Var}(\hat{p}_1)$. The proof is exactly the law of total variance argument above. Conditioning on a sufficient statistic can never increase variance. $\square$

Exercise 26: Jensen’s Inequality Applications

(a) State Jensen’s inequality. (b) Use it to prove that $E[X^2] \geq (E[X])^2$ (i.e., variance is non-negative). (c) Show that for a positive random variable $X$, $E[1/X] \geq 1/E[X]$. (d) Prove that $E[\log X] \leq \log E[X]$ for $X > 0$ and discuss why this matters for the EM algorithm.

Show Solution

(a) If $g$ is a convex function and $E[|X|] < \infty$, then $g(E[X]) \leq E[g(X)]$. If $g$ is concave, the inequality reverses: $g(E[X]) \geq E[g(X)]$.

(b) $g(x) = x^2$ is convex (since $g''(x) = 2 > 0$). By Jensen’s inequality:

$$(E[X])^2 = g(E[X]) \leq E[g(X)] = E[X^2]$$

Therefore $\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 \geq 0$. $\square$

(c) $g(x) = 1/x$ is convex for $x > 0$ (since $g''(x) = 2/x^3 > 0$). By Jensen:

$$E[1/X] = E[g(X)] \geq g(E[X]) = \frac{1}{E[X]} \quad \square$$

(d) $g(x) = \log x$ is concave (since $g''(x) = -1/x^2 < 0$). Jensen for concave functions gives:

$$E[\log X] \leq \log E[X]$$

In the EM algorithm, this inequality is used to construct a lower bound (ELBO) on the log-likelihood: $\log p(x) = \log E_{Z|x}[p(x,Z)/p(Z|x)] \geq E_{Z|x}[\log(p(x,Z)/q(Z))]$ for any distribution $q$. Maximizing the ELBO is tractable even when direct maximization of the log-likelihood is not. $\square$

Exercise 27: Optimal Prediction — Conditional Expectation Minimizes MSE

Prove that among all functions $g(X)$, the conditional expectation $E[Y \mid X]$ minimizes the mean squared error $E[(Y - g(X))^2]$. That is, show $E[(Y - g(X))^2] \geq E[(Y - E[Y \mid X])^2]$ for any measurable $g$.

Show Solution

Let $m(X) = E[Y \mid X]$. Write the error as:

$$Y - g(X) = (Y - m(X)) + (m(X) - g(X))$$

Squaring and taking expectation:

$$E[(Y - g(X))^2] = E[(Y - m(X))^2] + 2E[(Y - m(X))(m(X) - g(X))] + E[(m(X) - g(X))^2]$$

The cross term vanishes. Using the tower property with conditioning on $X$:

$$E[(Y - m(X))(m(X) - g(X))] = E\big[E[(Y - m(X))(m(X) - g(X)) \mid X]\big]$$ $$= E\big[(m(X) - g(X)) \cdot E[Y - m(X) \mid X]\big]$$

Since $E[Y - m(X) \mid X] = E[Y \mid X] - m(X) = 0$, the cross term is zero.

Therefore:

$$E[(Y - g(X))^2] = E[(Y - m(X))^2] + E[(m(X) - g(X))^2] \geq E[(Y - m(X))^2]$$

with equality iff $g(X) = m(X) = E[Y \mid X]$ a.s. $\square$

Interpretation: The conditional expectation is the best predictor of $Y$ given $X$ under squared loss. This is the theoretical foundation for regression in machine learning.

Exercise 28: Rao-Blackwell Theorem — Poisson Example

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. $\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$. We want to estimate $\theta = e^{-\lambda} = P(X_1 = 0)$. (b) The sufficient statistic is $T = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$. Compute $\hat{\theta}_2 = E[\hat{\theta}_1 \mid T]$. (c) Show that $\hat{\theta}_2$ has strictly smaller variance.

Show Solution

(a) $E[\hat{\theta}_1] = P(X_1 = 0) = e^{-\lambda} = \theta$. So it is unbiased. Since $\hat{\theta}_1 \sim \text{Bernoulli}(e^{-\lambda})$:

Exercise 29: Variance Reduction via Control Variates

(a) Show $E[Z] = \mu$ for any $c$. (b) Find the optimal $c^*$ that minimizes $\text{Var}(Z)$. (c) Show that the variance reduction factor is $1 - \rho^2_{XY}$. (d) If $\rho_{XY} = 0.9$, by what factor is the variance reduced?

Exercise 30: Entropy and Variance — Connections

(a) For a continuous random variable $X$ with differential entropy $h(X) = -\int f(x)\log f(x)\,dx$, show that among all distributions on $\mathbb{R}$ with variance $\sigma^2$, the Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ maximizes $h(X)$, with $h(X) = \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e \sigma^2)$. (b) Use this to show that $h(X) \leq \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e\,\text{Var}(X))$ for any distribution. (c) Compare the entropy of $\text{Uniform}(-a, a)$ (which has variance $a^2/3$) with the Gaussian of the same variance.

Show Solution

(a) We maximize $h(X) = -\int f(x) \log f(x)\,dx$ subject to $\int f(x)\,dx = 1$, $\int x f(x)\,dx = \mu$, and $\int (x-\mu)^2 f(x)\,dx = \sigma^2$.

Using Lagrange multipliers with the calculus of variations, we set:

$$\frac{\delta}{\delta f}\left[-\int f \log f \, dx - \lambda_0 \int f\,dx - \lambda_1 \int xf\,dx - \lambda_2 \int (x-\mu)^2 f\,dx\right] = 0$$ $$-\log f(x) - 1 - \lambda_0 - \lambda_1 x - \lambda_2(x-\mu)^2 = 0$$ $$f(x) = \exp\!\left(-1 - \lambda_0 - \lambda_1 x - \lambda_2(x-\mu)^2\right)$$

This is a Gaussian. Matching constraints gives $f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$.

Its entropy:

$$h(\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)) = -E[\log f(X)] = E\left[\frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) + \frac{(X-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right] = \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) + \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e\sigma^2)$$

(b) Since Gaussian maximizes entropy at fixed variance, for any $X$ with $\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2$:

$$h(X) \leq h(\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)) = \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e\sigma^2) = \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e\,\text{Var}(X)) \quad \square$$

(c) $\text{Uniform}(-a, a)$: $\sigma^2 = a^2/3$, $h(U) = \log(2a)$.

Gaussian with same variance: $h(G) = \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e \cdot a^2/3)$.

The entropy gap:

$$h(G) - h(U) = \frac{1}{2}\log\!\left(\frac{2\pi e a^2/3}{4a^2}\right) = \frac{1}{2}\log\!\left(\frac{\pi e}{6}\right) \approx \frac{1}{2}\log(1.4261) \approx 0.176 \text{ nats}$$

The Gaussian has higher entropy, confirming the maximum entropy property. The difference $D_{\text{KL}}(f \| g) = h(G) - h(U) \geq 0$ quantifies how "non-Gaussian" the uniform distribution is. $\square$

Key Concept

Expectation summarizes a distribution's location (center), variance summarizes its spread—together they characterize a distribution's shape and are the foundation of statistical inference and machine learning.
