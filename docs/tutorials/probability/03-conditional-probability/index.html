03. Conditional Probability & Independence

Conditional probability is the cornerstone of probabilistic reasoning: it tells us how to update our beliefs when we receive new information. Independence captures the idea that sometimes information about one event tells us nothing about another. Together, these concepts form the foundation for Bayes' theorem, graphical models, and nearly every probabilistic method in machine learning.

What is Conditional Probability?

In everyday reasoning, we constantly adjust our beliefs in light of new information:

  • You see dark clouds forming. Your estimate of rain today goes up.
  • A patient tests positive for a disease. The doctor's estimate of infection goes up—but by how much?
  • A spam filter sees the word "lottery" in an email. The probability that the email is spam increases.
  • A self-driving car's lidar detects a shape ahead. The probability of an obstacle changes depending on the sensor reading.

All of these situations share a common structure: we have some prior belief about an event, and then we observe evidence that causes us to revise that belief. The mathematical framework that makes this precise is conditional probability.

The key insight is that when we learn that some event $B$ has occurred, the effective sample space shrinks. We are no longer considering all possible outcomes—only those outcomes that are compatible with $B$. Conditional probability quantifies what happens to the probability of another event $A$ within this restricted sample space.

Historically, the formal definition emerged in the 18th century from the work of Thomas Bayes and Pierre-Simon Laplace. But the intuitive idea—that new information changes what we should believe—is as old as rational thought itself.

Definition of Conditional Probability

Let $A$ and $B$ be events in a probability space with $P(B) > 0$. The conditional probability of $A$ given $B$ is defined as:

$$\boxed{P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}}$$

Reading this formula: $P(A \mid B)$ is read "the probability of $A$ given $B$." The vertical bar "$\mid$" means "given that" or "conditional on." Everything to the right of the bar is what we assume has happened; everything to the left is what we want the probability of.

Geometric intuition: Think of the sample space $\Omega$ as a large rectangle. Events $A$ and $B$ are regions within it. When we condition on $B$, we "zoom in" to the region $B$—it becomes our new universe. The conditional probability $P(A \mid B)$ is the fraction of this new universe that also belongs to $A$.

Figure 1. Conditioning on $B$ restricts the sample space to the blue circle. $P(A \mid B)$ is the ratio of the purple intersection to the entire blue region.

Example (dice): Roll a fair die. Let $A = \{2, 4, 6\}$ (even) and $B = \{4, 5, 6\}$ (at least 4).

  • $P(A \cap B) = P(\{4, 6\}) = 2/6 = 1/3$
  • $P(B) = P(\{4, 5, 6\}) = 3/6 = 1/2$
  • $P(A \mid B) = \frac{1/3}{1/2} = \frac{2}{3}$

Knowing the die shows at least 4, two of the three equally likely outcomes ($\{4, 5, 6\}$) are even. The conditional probability ($2/3$) differs from the unconditional probability $P(A) = 1/2$.

Example (cards): Draw a card from a standard 52-card deck. Let $A$ = "the card is an ace" and $B$ = "the card is a spade."

  • $P(A \cap B) = P(\text{Ace of Spades}) = 1/52$
  • $P(B) = 13/52 = 1/4$
  • $P(A \mid B) = \frac{1/52}{1/4} = \frac{1}{13}$

Among the 13 spades, exactly one is an ace. Notice that $P(A \mid B) = 1/13 = P(A)$—knowing the suit tells us nothing about the rank. This is an example of independence, which we will formalize shortly.

Important properties of conditional probability:

  • $P(B \mid B) = 1$ (if $B$ occurred, then $B$ occurred)
  • $P(A \mid B) \geq 0$ for all events $A$
  • If $A_1, A_2, \ldots$ are mutually exclusive events, then $P\!\left(\bigcup_i A_i \mid B\right) = \sum_i P(A_i \mid B)$

In other words, $P(\cdot \mid B)$ is itself a valid probability measure on $\Omega$. Every theorem that holds for ordinary probabilities also holds for conditional probabilities. This is a powerful observation: it means we can "condition and then reason" as if the conditional distribution were the whole story.

The Multiplication Rule

Rearranging the definition of conditional probability gives the multiplication rule (also called the product rule):

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A \mid B) \, P(B)$$

By symmetry—since $A \cap B = B \cap A$—we also have:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(B \mid A) \, P(A)$$

For three events, we can extend this step by step:

$$\boxed{P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(A) \, P(B \mid A) \, P(C \mid A \cap B)}$$

Derivation:

  • Start: $P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(C \mid A \cap B) \cdot P(A \cap B)$   (by the definition applied to $C$ and $A \cap B$)
  • Expand $P(A \cap B) = P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A)$
  • Combine: $P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(A) \cdot P(B \mid A) \cdot P(C \mid A \cap B)$

Example: An urn contains 5 red and 3 blue balls. You draw three balls without replacement. What is the probability of drawing red, then blue, then red?

  • $P(R_1) = 5/8$
  • $P(B_2 \mid R_1) = 3/7$   (after removing a red ball, 3 blue out of 7 remain)
  • $P(R_3 \mid R_1 \cap B_2) = 4/6 = 2/3$   (4 red out of 6 remain)
  • $P(R_1 \cap B_2 \cap R_3) = \frac{5}{8} \cdot \frac{3}{7} \cdot \frac{2}{3} = \frac{30}{168} = \frac{5}{28}$

Independence

Two events $A$ and $B$ are independent if knowing that one occurred gives no information about whether the other occurred. Formally:

$$\boxed{A \perp B \iff P(A \cap B) = P(A) \, P(B)}$$

Equivalent formulations (when $P(B) > 0$ and $P(A) > 0$):

  • $P(A \mid B) = P(A)$   (learning $B$ doesn't change the probability of $A$)
  • $P(B \mid A) = P(B)$   (learning $A$ doesn't change the probability of $B$)
  • $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \, P(B)$   (the joint factors into the product of marginals)

All three are logically equivalent. The third form is taken as the definition because it does not require $P(A) > 0$ or $P(B) > 0$.

Examples of independence:

  • Coin flips: On two fair coin flips, let $A$ = "first flip is heads" and $B$ = "second flip is heads." Then $P(A) = 1/2$, $P(B) = 1/2$, and $P(A \cap B) = 1/4 = P(A) \cdot P(B)$. Independent.
  • Card rank and suit: In a standard deck, the event "card is an ace" and "card is a spade" are independent: $P(\text{Ace} \cap \text{Spade}) = 1/52 = (4/52)(13/52) = P(\text{Ace}) \cdot P(\text{Spade})$.

Non-examples (dependence):

  • Drawing without replacement: Draw two cards from a deck. Let $A$ = "first card is an ace" and $B$ = "second card is an ace." Then $P(A) = 4/52$ but $P(A \cap B) = (4/52)(3/51) \neq (4/52)(4/52) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$. Not independent.
  • Dice sum: Roll two dice. Let $A$ = "first die is 6" and $B$ = "sum is 10." Then $P(A) = 1/6$ and $P(B) = 3/36 = 1/12$, but $P(A \cap B) = P(\{(6,4)\}) = 1/36 \neq 1/72 = P(A) \cdot P(B)$. Not independent.

Warning: Independence is not the same as "mutually exclusive." In fact, if $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive (disjoint) with $P(A) > 0$ and $P(B) > 0$, they are never independent: $P(A \cap B) = 0 \neq P(A) \, P(B) > 0$. Disjoint events carry maximal information about each other—if one happens, the other cannot.

Conditional Independence

Events $A$ and $B$ are conditionally independent given $C$ if:

$$\boxed{P(A \cap B \mid C) = P(A \mid C) \, P(B \mid C)}$$

We write this as $A \perp B \mid C$. Equivalently: $P(A \mid B \cap C) = P(A \mid C)$, meaning once we know $C$, learning $B$ gives no additional information about $A$.

Critical point: Conditional independence and marginal (unconditional) independence are different properties. Neither implies the other!

Example 1: Conditionally independent but marginally dependent.

Consider a coin that is either fair ($C_F$, probability 0.5) or double-headed ($C_D$, probability 0.5). Let $A$ = "first flip is heads" and $B$ = "second flip is heads."

  • Given the coin type, the flips are independent: $P(A \cap B \mid C_F) = (1/2)(1/2) = P(A \mid C_F) \cdot P(B \mid C_F)$ and $P(A \cap B \mid C_D) = 1 \cdot 1 = P(A \mid C_D) \cdot P(B \mid C_D)$.
  • Marginally, $P(A) = P(A \mid C_F) \cdot 1/2 + P(A \mid C_D) \cdot 1/2 = 1/4 + 1/2 = 3/4$. Similarly $P(B) = 3/4$. And $P(A \cap B) = P(A \cap B \mid C_F) \cdot 1/2 + P(A \cap B \mid C_D) \cdot 1/2 = 1/8 + 1/2 = 5/8$. But $P(A) \cdot P(B) = 9/16 \neq 5/8$. So $A$ and $B$ are not marginally independent.

Intuitively: seeing heads on the first flip makes it more likely the coin is double-headed, which makes heads on the second flip more likely. The hidden variable (coin type) introduces dependence.

Example 2: Marginally independent but conditionally dependent.

Suppose two independent fair coins are flipped. Let $A$ = "first coin is heads," $B$ = "second coin is heads," and $C$ = "the two coins show the same face." Then $A \perp B$ (marginal independence), but $P(A \mid B \cap C) = 1 \neq 1/2 = P(A \mid C)$. Conditioning on $C$ creates dependence between $A$ and $B$. This phenomenon is called explaining away (or Berkson's paradox) and is fundamental in Bayesian networks.

The Law of Total Probability

Suppose $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$ form a partition of the sample space: they are mutually exclusive, and $A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots \cup A_n = \Omega$, with $P(A_i) > 0$ for all $i$. Then for any event $B$:

$$\boxed{P(B) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(B \mid A_i) \, P(A_i)}$$

Intuition: To compute the total probability of $B$, we "decompose" the sample space into scenarios $A_1, \ldots, A_n$. In each scenario, we know the conditional probability of $B$. We then take a weighted average, where the weights are the probabilities of the scenarios.

Derivation: Since $\{A_i\}$ partitions $\Omega$, we have $B = B \cap \Omega = B \cap (A_1 \cup \cdots \cup A_n) = (B \cap A_1) \cup \cdots \cup (B \cap A_n)$. Since the $A_i$ are mutually exclusive, so are the $B \cap A_i$, and by the addition axiom:

$$P(B) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(B \cap A_i) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(B \mid A_i) \, P(A_i)$$

Figure 2. Tree diagram for the law of total probability with a 3-way partition $\{A_1, A_2, A_3\}$. The green leaves are the paths that lead to $B$.

Example: A factory has three machines. Machine 1 produces 50% of items with 2% defect rate, Machine 2 produces 30% with 3% defect rate, Machine 3 produces 20% with 5% defect rate. What is the overall defect rate?

$$P(D) = P(D \mid M_1) P(M_1) + P(D \mid M_2) P(M_2) + P(D \mid M_3) P(M_3)$$ $$= (0.02)(0.50) + (0.03)(0.30) + (0.05)(0.20) = 0.01 + 0.009 + 0.01 = 0.029$$

The overall defect rate is 2.9%.

Bayes' Theorem Revisited

Now we can derive Bayes' theorem as a direct consequence of the definition of conditional probability. From our two expressions for $P(A \cap B)$:

$$P(A \mid B) \, P(B) = P(A \cap B) = P(B \mid A) \, P(A)$$

Dividing both sides by $P(B)$:

$$\boxed{P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A) \, P(A)}{P(B)}}$$

Combined with the law of total probability for the denominator:

$$P(A_j \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A_j) \, P(A_j)}{\sum_{i=1}^n P(B \mid A_i) \, P(A_i)}$$

This is the full form of Bayes' theorem for a partition. It shows how conditional probability, the multiplication rule, and the law of total probability all work together. For a deep dive into Bayes' theorem with many more examples, see Tutorial 01: Bayes' Theorem.

Continuing the factory example: If a randomly selected item is defective, what is the probability it came from Machine 3?

$$P(M_3 \mid D) = \frac{P(D \mid M_3) \, P(M_3)}{P(D)} = \frac{(0.05)(0.20)}{0.029} = \frac{0.01}{0.029} \approx 0.345$$

Machine 3 produces only 20% of items, but because its defect rate is highest, it is responsible for about 34.5% of all defective items.

Simpson's Paradox

Simpson's Paradox is one of the most surprising phenomena in statistics. A trend that appears in several groups of data reverses when the groups are combined. It shows that conditioning can completely change the direction of an association.

The Berkeley Admissions case (1973): UC Berkeley was sued for gender bias because overall admission rates showed men were admitted at a significantly higher rate than women. However, when the data was broken down by department, women had equal or higher admission rates in most departments! The resolution: women tended to apply to more competitive departments with lower admission rates for everyone.

Department Men Applied Men Admitted Women Applied Women Admitted
A (Easy) 800 62% 100 82%
B (Hard) 200 30% 900 33%
Overall 1000 56% 1000 38%

Let us verify with simplified numbers. For men: $(0.62)(800) + (0.30)(200) = 496 + 60 = 556$ admitted out of 1000, so 55.6%. For women: $(0.82)(100) + (0.33)(900) = 82 + 297 = 379$ admitted out of 1000, so 37.9%. Men have a higher overall rate, yet women have a higher rate in both departments!

Figure 3. Simpson's Paradox: women outperform men in each department, but men have a higher overall admission rate due to differing application patterns.

In probability notation: Let $A$ = admitted, $W$ = woman, $D_j$ = department $j$. Simpson's paradox says it is possible that:

$$P(A \mid W, D_j) > P(A \mid W^c, D_j) \quad \text{for all } j$$

yet

$$P(A \mid W) < P(A \mid W^c)$$

The paradox is resolved by recognizing that the confounding variable (department choice) is unequally distributed between men and women. This is why conditioning on the right variables is essential in causal reasoning. Simpson's paradox is one of the strongest arguments for always examining conditional distributions, not just aggregates.

The Chain Rule of Probability

The multiplication rule generalizes to any number of events. For events $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$:

$$\boxed{P(A_1 \cap A_2 \cap \cdots \cap A_n) = P(A_1) \prod_{k=2}^{n} P(A_k \mid A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_{k-1})}$$

Written out:

$$P(A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_n) = P(A_1) \cdot P(A_2 \mid A_1) \cdot P(A_3 \mid A_1 \cap A_2) \cdots P(A_n \mid A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_{n-1})$$

Proof by induction: The base case $n = 2$ is the multiplication rule. Assuming it holds for $n - 1$ events, apply the multiplication rule to the pair $\{A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_{n-1}\}$ and $\{A_n\}$:

$$P(A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_n) = P(A_n \mid A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_{n-1}) \cdot P(A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_{n-1})$$

Then expand $P(A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_{n-1})$ using the induction hypothesis.

Connection to machine learning: In autoregressive language models (GPT, etc.), the joint probability of a sequence of tokens $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_T$ is factored using exactly this chain rule:

$$P(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_T) = P(x_1) \prod_{t=2}^{T} P(x_t \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{t-1})$$

Each factor $P(x_t \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{t-1})$ is modeled by a neural network (the transformer). The chain rule is not an approximation here—it is an exact identity. The approximation enters in how we model each conditional distribution.

Connection to graphical models: In a Bayesian network, the chain rule combined with conditional independence assumptions simplifies the joint distribution. If $X_i$ is conditionally independent of its non-descendants given its parents $\text{Pa}(X_i)$, then:

$$P(X_1, \ldots, X_n) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(X_i \mid \text{Pa}(X_i))$$

This is a dramatic simplification: instead of needing $P(X_i \mid X_1, \ldots, X_{i-1})$ (exponentially many parameters), we only need $P(X_i \mid \text{Pa}(X_i))$ (a few parameters per node).

Independence of Multiple Events

For two events, independence means $P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B)$. For three or more events, we need to be more careful.

Pairwise independence: Events $A_1, \ldots, A_n$ are pairwise independent if for every pair $i \neq j$:

$$P(A_i \cap A_j) = P(A_i) \, P(A_j)$$

Mutual independence: Events $A_1, \ldots, A_n$ are mutually independent if for every subset $S \subseteq \{1, \ldots, n\}$:

$$\boxed{P\!\left(\bigcap_{i \in S} A_i\right) = \prod_{i \in S} P(A_i)}$$

For $n = 3$ events $A, B, C$, mutual independence requires all four conditions:

  1. $P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B)$
  2. $P(A \cap C) = P(A) P(C)$
  3. $P(B \cap C) = P(B) P(C)$
  4. $P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(A) P(B) P(C)$

Conditions 1–3 are pairwise independence; condition 4 is the extra "triple" condition. Pairwise independence does not imply mutual independence.

Classic counterexample (Bernstein, 1946): Toss two fair coins. Define:

  • $A$ = "first coin is heads"
  • $B$ = "second coin is heads"
  • $C$ = "the two coins show the same face" (both heads or both tails)

Then:

  • $P(A) = P(B) = P(C) = 1/2$
  • $P(A \cap B) = 1/4 = P(A)P(B)$   (pairwise independent)
  • $P(A \cap C) = P(\text{HH}) = 1/4 = P(A)P(C)$   (pairwise independent)
  • $P(B \cap C) = P(\text{HH}) = 1/4 = P(B)P(C)$   (pairwise independent)
  • $P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(\text{HH}) = 1/4 \neq 1/8 = P(A)P(B)P(C)$   (not mutually independent!)

Pairwise independence holds, but mutual independence fails. The triple intersection has probability $1/4$ instead of the required $1/8$. Knowing any two of these events determines the third.

For $n$ events, mutual independence requires checking $2^n - n - 1$ conditions (all subsets of size 2 or more). For $n = 10$, that is $2^{10} - 11 = 1013$ conditions!

Connections to Machine Learning

Conditional probability and independence are not just abstract mathematics—they are the language in which machine learning is written.

1. Naive Bayes Classifier.

The Naive Bayes classifier assumes that features $X_1, \ldots, X_d$ are conditionally independent given the class label $Y$:

$$P(X_1, \ldots, X_d \mid Y = c) = \prod_{j=1}^{d} P(X_j \mid Y = c)$$

This "naive" conditional independence assumption reduces the number of parameters from exponential ($O(k^d)$ for $k$ categories per feature) to linear ($O(kd)$). Despite being unrealistic, it works surprisingly well in practice for text classification, spam filtering, and medical diagnosis.

2. The Markov Property.

A stochastic process $X_0, X_1, X_2, \ldots$ satisfies the Markov property if:

$$P(X_{t+1} \mid X_0, X_1, \ldots, X_t) = P(X_{t+1} \mid X_t)$$

The future is conditionally independent of the past given the present. This is a conditional independence assumption that makes Markov chains, hidden Markov models (HMMs), and reinforcement learning (MDPs) tractable. See Tutorial 11: Markov Chains.

3. Bayesian Networks (Graphical Models).

A Bayesian network encodes conditional independence relationships as a directed acyclic graph (DAG). Each variable is conditionally independent of its non-descendants given its parents. This structure enables efficient inference and learning. The d-separation criterion provides a graphical test for conditional independence, which directly follows from the chain rule and the factorization property.

4. Conditional Random Fields (CRFs).

CRFs model the conditional distribution $P(Y \mid X)$ directly, using an undirected graphical model. The conditional independence structure encoded in the graph determines which features interact. CRFs are widely used for sequence labeling (named entity recognition, part-of-speech tagging).

5. Independence Testing.

Testing whether variables are independent from data is a fundamental statistical task. The chi-squared test, mutual information, and kernel-based methods (HSIC) are all tools for this. Feature selection methods often test for conditional independence to identify which features are relevant given others.

1. Computing Conditional Probabilities from Data

import numpy as np

def conditional_probability(data_A, data_B):
    """
    Compute P(A|B) from boolean arrays.

    P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B) = count(A and B) / count(B)

    Parameters:
        data_A: boolean array, True where event A occurs
        data_B: boolean array, True where event B occurs

    Returns:
        P(A|B): conditional probability
    """
    n = len(data_A)
    count_B = np.sum(data_B)
    count_A_and_B = np.sum(data_A & data_B)

    if count_B == 0:
        raise ValueError("P(B) = 0: cannot condition on event with zero probability")

    return count_A_and_B / count_B


# Example: Simulating dice rolls
np.random.seed(42)
n_rolls = 100_000
die = np.random.randint(1, 7, size=n_rolls)

# Events
A = (die % 2 == 0)       # Even: {2, 4, 6}
B = (die >= 4)            # At least 4: {4, 5, 6}

# Compute
p_A = np.mean(A)
p_B = np.mean(B)
p_A_given_B = conditional_probability(A, B)
p_A_and_B = np.mean(A & B)

print("Dice Roll Simulation (n = {:,})".format(n_rolls))
print(f"  P(Even)               = {p_A:.4f}  (exact: 0.5000)")
print(f"  P(≥ 4)                = {p_B:.4f}  (exact: 0.5000)")
print(f"  P(Even ∩ ≥ 4)         = {p_A_and_B:.4f}  (exact: 0.3333)")
print(f"  P(Even | ≥ 4)         = {p_A_given_B:.4f}  (exact: 0.6667)")
print(f"  P(Even) × P(≥ 4)      = {p_A * p_B:.4f}")
print(f"  Independent?           {abs(p_A_and_B - p_A * p_B) < 0.01}")

# Output:
# P(Even | ≥ 4)         ≈ 0.6667  (exact: 2/3)
# Not independent since P(A∩B) ≈ 1/3 ≠ 1/4 = P(A)P(B)


# Example with cards: rank and suit ARE independent
deck = [(rank, suit) for rank in range(13) for suit in range(4)]
n_draws = 100_000
draws = [deck[i] for i in np.random.randint(0, 52, size=n_draws)]
is_ace = np.array([d[0] == 0 for d in draws])
is_spade = np.array([d[1] == 0 for d in draws])

p_ace = np.mean(is_ace)
p_spade = np.mean(is_spade)
p_ace_given_spade = conditional_probability(is_ace, is_spade)
p_joint = np.mean(is_ace & is_spade)

print("\nCard Draw Simulation (n = {:,})".format(n_draws))
print(f"  P(Ace)                = {p_ace:.4f}  (exact: {1/13:.4f})")
print(f"  P(Spade)              = {p_spade:.4f}  (exact: 0.2500)")
print(f"  P(Ace | Spade)        = {p_ace_given_spade:.4f}  (exact: {1/13:.4f})")
print(f"  P(Ace) × P(Spade)     = {p_ace * p_spade:.4f}")
print(f"  P(Ace ∩ Spade)        = {p_joint:.4f}  (exact: {1/52:.4f})")
print(f"  Independent?           {abs(p_joint - p_ace * p_spade) < 0.005}")

2. Testing Independence with the Chi-Squared Test

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

def test_independence(data_A, data_B, alpha=0.05):
    """
    Test whether two binary events are independent using
    Pearson's chi-squared test.

    Creates a 2x2 contingency table and tests if the observed
    frequencies differ significantly from expected (under independence).

    H0: A and B are independent
    H1: A and B are dependent
    """
    # Build 2x2 contingency table
    #              B=True    B=False
    # A=True      [n11]     [n10]
    # A=False     [n01]     [n00]
    n11 = np.sum(data_A & data_B)
    n10 = np.sum(data_A & ~data_B)
    n01 = np.sum(~data_A & data_B)
    n00 = np.sum(~data_A & ~data_B)

    contingency = np.array([[n11, n10],
                            [n01, n00]])

    # Chi-squared test
    chi2, p_value, dof, expected = stats.chi2_contingency(contingency)

    print("Contingency Table:")
    print(f"                B=True   B=False")
    print(f"  A=True        {n11:>6}   {n10:>6}")
    print(f"  A=False       {n01:>6}   {n00:>6}")
    print(f"\nChi-squared statistic: {chi2:.4f}")
    print(f"p-value:               {p_value:.6f}")
    print(f"Degrees of freedom:    {dof}")
    print(f"Conclusion at α={alpha}:  ", end="")
    if p_value < alpha:

    else:

    return chi2, p_value

import numpy as np
from itertools import product

def chain_rule_joint(conditionals):
    """
    Compute joint probability P(A1 ∩ A2 ∩ ... ∩ An) using the chain rule.

    P(A1,...,An) = P(A1) * P(A2|A1) * P(A3|A1,A2) * ... * P(An|A1,...,An-1)

    Parameters:
        conditionals: list of floats
            [P(A1), P(A2|A1), P(A3|A1,A2), ..., P(An|A1,...,An-1)]

    Returns:
        joint: P(A1 ∩ A2 ∩ ... ∩ An)
    """
    joint = 1.0
    for i, p in enumerate(conditionals):
        joint *= p
    return joint

5. Naive Bayes: Conditional Independence in Action

import numpy as np
from collections import defaultdict

class NaiveBayesClassifier:
    """
    Bernoulli Naive Bayes from scratch.

    Core assumption (conditional independence):
        P(x1, x2, ..., xd | y) = ∏ P(xj | y)

    This means features are independent given the class label.
    While rarely true, this simplification works remarkably well.
    """

    def __init__(self, alpha=1.0):
        """alpha: Laplace smoothing parameter."""
        self.alpha = alpha
        self.class_priors = {}
        self.feature_probs = {}

    def fit(self, X, y):
        """
        Learn P(y) and P(xj | y) from training data.

        Parameters:
            X: (n_samples, n_features) binary array
            y: (n_samples,) class labels
        """
        classes = np.unique(y)
        n_samples, n_features = X.shape

        for c in classes:
            X_c = X[y == c]
            # P(y = c)
            self.class_priors[c] = len(X_c) / n_samples
            # P(xj = 1 | y = c) with Laplace smoothing
            self.feature_probs[c] = (X_c.sum(axis=0) + self.alpha) / \
                                     (len(X_c) + 2 * self.alpha)

    def predict_log_proba(self, X):
        """
        Compute log P(y | x) ∝ log P(y) + Σ log P(xj | y) for each class.
        Uses the chain rule + conditional independence assumption.
        """
        scores = {}
        for c in self.class_priors:
            log_prior = np.log(self.class_priors[c])
            # Conditional independence: sum of log probabilities
            log_likelihood = np.sum(
                X * np.log(self.feature_probs[c]) +
                (1 - X) * np.log(1 - self.feature_probs[c]),
                axis=1
            )
            scores[c] = log_prior + log_likelihood
        return scores

    def predict(self, X):
        scores = self.predict_log_proba(X)
        classes = list(scores.keys())
        score_matrix = np.column_stack([scores[c] for c in classes])
        return np.array([classes[i] for i in np.argmax(score_matrix, axis=1)])

Exercise 1: Basic Conditional Probability (Cards)

A card is drawn from a standard 52-card deck. Given that the card is a face card (Jack, Queen, or King), what is the probability it is a heart?

Show Solution

Let $A$ = "heart" and $B$ = "face card."

  • There are 12 face cards total (3 per suit $\times$ 4 suits), so $P(B) = 12/52$.
  • There are 3 face cards that are hearts (J, Q, K of hearts), so $P(A \cap B) = 3/52$.
$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} = \frac{3/52}{12/52} = \frac{3}{12} = \frac{1}{4}$$

Answer: $P(\text{heart} \mid \text{face card}) = 1/4 = 0.25$

This equals $P(\text{heart}) = 13/52 = 1/4$, so "heart" and "face card" are independent—knowing the card is a face card doesn't change the probability of it being a heart.

Exercise 2: Dice Conditional Probability

Two fair dice are rolled. Given that the sum is 8, what is the probability that at least one die shows a 6?

Show Solution

Let $A$ = "at least one die shows 6" and $B$ = "sum is 8."

The outcomes where the sum is 8 are: $(2,6), (3,5), (4,4), (5,3), (6,2)$. So $|B| = 5$.

Among these, the outcomes where at least one die is 6: $(2,6), (6,2)$. So $|A \cap B| = 2$.

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{|A \cap B|}{|B|} = \frac{2}{5}$$

Answer: $P(\text{at least one 6} \mid \text{sum is 8}) = 2/5 = 0.4$

Exercise 3: Testing Independence

A fair die is rolled. Let $A = \{1, 2, 3\}$ (at most 3) and $B = \{1, 3, 5\}$ (odd). Are $A$ and $B$ independent?

Show Solution

We check whether $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$.

  • $P(A) = 3/6 = 1/2$
  • $P(B) = 3/6 = 1/2$
  • $A \cap B = \{1, 3\}$, so $P(A \cap B) = 2/6 = 1/3$
  • $P(A) \cdot P(B) = 1/2 \cdot 1/2 = 1/4$

Since $1/3 \neq 1/4$, we conclude that $A$ and $B$ are not independent.

Equivalently: $P(A \mid B) = P(A \cap B) / P(B) = (1/3)/(1/2) = 2/3 \neq 1/2 = P(A)$. Learning $B$ changes the probability of $A$.

Answer: $A$ and $B$ are not independent.

$$P(S) = P(W_1) \cdot P(W_2 \mid W_1) \cdot P(W_3 \mid W_1 \cap W_2)$$ $$= 0.95 \times 0.90 \times 0.98 = 0.8379$$

So $P(S^c) = 1 - 0.8379 = 0.1621$.

For the second part, we need $P(W_3^c \mid S^c)$. Note that $S^c$ occurs if any component fails. The event "Component 3 failed and the system failed" is $W_1 \cap W_2 \cap W_3^c$ (Components 1 and 2 worked but 3 failed, since if 1 or 2 failed, 3's status might not matter—but the system already fails).

Actually, $W_3^c \cap S^c$ includes all scenarios where Component 3 fails (regardless of others). But the question asks specifically about Component 3 being the cause. Let's compute $P(W_1 \cap W_2 \cap W_3^c)$:

$$P(W_1 \cap W_2 \cap W_3^c) = P(W_1) \cdot P(W_2 \mid W_1) \cdot P(W_3^c \mid W_1 \cap W_2)$$ $$= 0.95 \times 0.90 \times 0.02 = 0.0171$$

This is the probability that only Component 3 caused the failure. Then:

$$P(W_1 \cap W_2 \cap W_3^c \mid S^c) = \frac{P(W_1 \cap W_2 \cap W_3^c)}{P(S^c)} = \frac{0.0171}{0.1621} \approx 0.1055$$

Answer: $P(\text{system works}) \approx 0.838$. $P(\text{only Component 3 failed} \mid \text{system failed}) \approx 10.6\%$.

Exercise 22: Pairwise vs. Mutual Independence

Let $\Omega$ be the set of outcomes from rolling two fair dice. Define events: $A$ = "first die is odd," $B$ = "second die is odd," $C$ = "sum is odd." (a) Prove that $A$, $B$, $C$ are pairwise independent. (b) Prove that $A$, $B$, $C$ are not mutually independent. (c) More generally, prove that for any three events that are pairwise independent with $P(A) = P(B) = P(C) = 1/2$, mutual independence fails if and only if $P(A \cap B \cap C) \neq 1/8$.

Show Solution

(a) Pairwise independence:

$\Omega = \{(i,j) : 1 \leq i,j \leq 6\}$ with $|\Omega| = 36$.

  • $P(A) = P(\text{1st odd}) = 1/2$, $P(B) = P(\text{2nd odd}) = 1/2$, $P(C) = P(\text{sum odd}) = 1/2$

Check $A \cap B$: first odd AND second odd. This is 9 outcomes: $\{(1,1),(1,3),\ldots,(5,5)\}$. So $P(A \cap B) = 9/36 = 1/4 = P(A)P(B)$. ✓

Check $A \cap C$: first odd AND sum odd. Sum is odd iff exactly one die is odd. If first is odd and sum is odd, then second must be even. So $A \cap C = \{(i,j) : i \text{ odd}, j \text{ even}\}$, which has $3 \times 3 = 9$ outcomes. $P(A \cap C) = 9/36 = 1/4 = P(A)P(C)$. ✓

Check $B \cap C$: by symmetry, same as $A \cap C$. $P(B \cap C) = 1/4$. ✓

(b) Not mutually independent:

$A \cap B \cap C$ = "first odd, second odd, and sum odd." But if both dice are odd, the sum is even. So $A \cap B \cap C = \emptyset$!

$$P(A \cap B \cap C) = 0 \neq \frac{1}{8} = P(A)P(B)P(C)$$

Mutual independence fails. Knowing any two of $A$, $B$, $C$ determines the third. $\square$

Exercise 23: Conditional Independence Does Not Imply Marginal Independence

Prove the following: If $A \perp B \mid C$ (conditional independence), it does not necessarily follow that $A \perp B$ (marginal independence). Provide a rigorous proof by constructing a counterexample and formally verifying all conditions.

Show Solution

Counterexample: Consider a bag with two coins: one fair (F) and one with two heads (D). A coin is chosen uniformly at random. Let $C$ = "fair coin chosen." Let $A$ = "first flip is heads" and $B$ = "second flip is heads."

Step 1: Verify $A \perp B \mid C$.

Given $C$ (fair coin): $P(A \mid C) = 1/2$, $P(B \mid C) = 1/2$, and $P(A \cap B \mid C) = 1/4 = P(A \mid C) \cdot P(B \mid C)$. ✓

Given $C^c$ (double-headed coin): $P(A \mid C^c) = 1$, $P(B \mid C^c) = 1$, and $P(A \cap B \mid C^c) = 1 = 1 \cdot 1 = P(A \mid C^c) \cdot P(B \mid C^c)$. ✓

So $A \perp B \mid C$ holds.

Step 2: Show $A \not\perp B$.

By the law of total probability:

$$P(A) = P(A \mid C)P(C) + P(A \mid C^c)P(C^c) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{3}{4}$$ $$P(B) = \frac{3}{4} \quad \text{(by symmetry)}$$ $$P(A \cap B) = P(A \cap B \mid C)P(C) + P(A \cap B \mid C^c)P(C^c) = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{2} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{5}{8}$$ $$P(A) \cdot P(B) = \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{3}{4} = \frac{9}{16}$$

Since $5/8 = 10/16 \neq 9/16 = P(A) \cdot P(B)$, the events $A$ and $B$ are not marginally independent.

Conclusion: We have $A \perp B \mid C$ but $A \not\perp B$. Conditional independence does not imply marginal independence. $\square$

Intuition: Seeing heads on the first flip provides evidence about the hidden variable $C$ (it makes the double-headed coin more likely), which in turn affects the probability of the second flip. The latent variable creates marginal dependence even though the flips are conditionally independent given the coin type.

Exercise 24: Chain Rule and Bayesian Network Factorization

Consider four random variables $A$, $B$, $C$, $D$ with the following conditional independence structure (a Bayesian network): $A \to B \to C \to D$ (a chain). This means $B$ depends only on $A$, $C$ depends only on $B$, and $D$ depends only on $C$. (a) Write the joint distribution $P(A, B, C, D)$ using the chain rule and then simplify using the conditional independence assumptions. (b) Prove that $A \perp D \mid B$ (i.e., $A$ and $D$ are conditionally independent given $B$). (c) Prove that $A \perp D \mid C$ as well.

Show Solution

(a) The chain rule gives:

$$P(A, B, C, D) = P(A) \cdot P(B \mid A) \cdot P(C \mid A, B) \cdot P(D \mid A, B, C)$$

Now apply the Markov structure of the chain $A \to B \to C \to D$:

  • $P(C \mid A, B) = P(C \mid B)$  (since $C$ depends only on its parent $B$)
  • $P(D \mid A, B, C) = P(D \mid C)$  (since $D$ depends only on its parent $C$)
$$\boxed{P(A, B, C, D) = P(A) \cdot P(B \mid A) \cdot P(C \mid B) \cdot P(D \mid C)}$$

(b) Prove $A \perp D \mid B$:

We need to show $P(A, D \mid B) = P(A \mid B) \cdot P(D \mid B)$. Marginalize $C$:

$$P(A, D \mid B) = \sum_C P(A, C, D \mid B) = \sum_C \frac{P(A, B, C, D)}{P(B)}$$ $$= \sum_C \frac{P(A) P(B \mid A) P(C \mid B) P(D \mid C)}{P(B)}$$ $$= \frac{P(A) P(B \mid A)}{P(B)} \sum_C P(C \mid B) P(D \mid C)$$ $$= P(A \mid B) \cdot P(D \mid B)$$

where we used $P(A)P(B \mid A)/P(B) = P(A \mid B)$ (Bayes' theorem) and $\sum_C P(C \mid B) P(D \mid C) = P(D \mid B)$ (law of total probability). $\square$

(c) Prove $A \perp D \mid C$:

Similarly:

$$P(A, D \mid C) = \sum_B \frac{P(A, B, C, D)}{P(C)} = \sum_B \frac{P(A) P(B \mid A) P(C \mid B) P(D \mid C)}{P(C)}$$ $$= P(D \mid C) \sum_B \frac{P(A) P(B \mid A) P(C \mid B)}{P(C)}$$ $$= P(D \mid C) \cdot \frac{\sum_B P(A, B, C)}{P(C)} = P(D \mid C) \cdot P(A \mid C)$$

Therefore $P(A, D \mid C) = P(A \mid C) \cdot P(D \mid C)$, proving $A \perp D \mid C$. $\square$

Key insight: In a chain $A \to B \to C \to D$, conditioning on any intermediate node "blocks" the flow of information. This is a special case of d-separation in Bayesian networks.

Exercise 25: The Monty Hall Problem via Conditional Probability

In the Monty Hall problem, there are 3 doors. Behind one is a car (prize), and behind the other two are goats. You pick door 1. The host, who knows where the car is, opens one of the remaining doors to reveal a goat (say door 3). Should you switch to door 2? (a) Using conditional probability, compute $P(\text{car behind door 2} \mid \text{host opens door 3})$. (b) Generalize: with $n$ doors, you pick one, and the host opens $n - 2$ goat doors. What is the probability of winning if you switch? (c) Prove that switching is always at least as good as staying for $n \geq 3$.

Show Solution

(a) Let $C_i$ = "car is behind door $i$" and $H_3$ = "host opens door 3."

Priors: $P(C_1) = P(C_2) = P(C_3) = 1/3$.

Host's behavior:

  • $P(H_3 \mid C_1) = 1/2$  (car behind door 1: host chooses randomly between doors 2 and 3)
  • $P(H_3 \mid C_2) = 1$  (car behind door 2: host must open door 3)
  • $P(H_3 \mid C_3) = 0$  (car behind door 3: host cannot open it)

By the law of total probability:

$$P(H_3) = P(H_3 \mid C_1)P(C_1) + P(H_3 \mid C_2)P(C_2) + P(H_3 \mid C_3)P(C_3)$$ $$= \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{3} + 0 \cdot \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{2}$$

By Bayes' theorem:

$$P(C_2 \mid H_3) = \frac{P(H_3 \mid C_2) P(C_2)}{P(H_3)} = \frac{1 \times 1/3}{1/2} = \frac{2}{3}$$

Also: $P(C_1 \mid H_3) = \frac{(1/2)(1/3)}{1/2} = 1/3$. Switching to door 2 doubles your chances!

(b) Generalization to $n$ doors:

You pick door 1. The host opens $n - 2$ doors with goats, leaving your door and one other (say door $k$). If you stay, you win iff the car was behind door 1: $P(\text{win by staying}) = 1/n$.

If you switch, you win iff the car was behind any of the other $n - 1$ doors. Since the host eliminated $n - 2$ goat doors, all the "probability mass" of those $n - 1$ doors concentrates on the single remaining door:

$$P(\text{win by switching}) = \frac{n - 1}{n}$$

For $n = 3$: $P(\text{switch wins}) = 2/3$. For $n = 100$: $P(\text{switch wins}) = 99/100$!

(c) Proof that switching is at least as good:

For $n \geq 3$: $P(\text{switch wins}) = (n-1)/n$ and $P(\text{stay wins}) = 1/n$.

$$(n-1)/n \geq 1/n \iff n - 1 \geq 1 \iff n \geq 2$$

For $n \geq 3$, we have strict inequality: $(n-1)/n > 1/n$. Switching is strictly better than staying for all $n \geq 3$. For $n = 2$, switching and staying are equivalent (both $1/2$). $\square$

Exercise 26: Coupling Argument for Total Variation

Let $P$ and $Q$ be two probability distributions on a finite set $\Omega$. The total variation distance is $d_{TV}(P, Q) = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{\omega} |P(\omega) - Q(\omega)|$. Show Solution

For any event $A$: $P(A) - Q(A) = P(X \in A) - P(Y \in A) \leq P(X \in A, Y \notin A) \leq P(X \neq Y)$.

Taking the supremum over $A$: $\sup_A |P(A) - Q(A)| \leq P(X \neq Y)$. Since $d_{TV}(P,Q) = \sup_A |P(A) - Q(A)|$, the bound holds.

Step 2: Construct an optimal coupling.

Let $S = \{\omega : P(\omega) \geq Q(\omega)\}$. (a) For $n=2$, exchangeability means $P(X_1 = a, X_2 = b) = P(X_1 = b, X_2 = a)$ for all $a,b$. So $P(1,0) = P(0,1) = q$ for some $q$. Let $P(1,1) = r$ and $P(0,0) = s$ with $r + 2q + s = 1$.

We need a distribution $\mu$ on $[0,1]$ such that for Bernoulli($p$): $P(X_1 = a, X_2 = b) = \int_0^1 p^{a+b}(1-p)^{2-a-b} \, d\mu(p)$.

This gives: $r = \int p^2 \, d\mu(p) = E[p^2]$, $q = \int p(1-p) \, d\mu(p) = E[p] - E[p^2]$, $s = \int (1-p)^2 \, d\mu(p) = 1 - 2E[p] + E[p^2]$.

(b) Let $m_1 = E[p]$ and $m_2 = E[p^2]$. Then $m_1 = r + q$ (the marginal $P(X_1 = 1)$) and $m_2 = r$. Any distribution $\mu$ with these moments works. The simplest: a two-point distribution placing mass at $p_1, p_2$ chosen to match $m_1$ and $m_2$. For example, if $r = 0.3$ and $q = 0.2$, then $m_1 = 0.5$, $m_2 = 0.3$. The mixing variance is $m_2 - m_1^2 = 0.05$. $\square$

Exercise 29: Conditional Expectation as Projection

Let $X$ and $Y$ be square-integrable random variables. (a) Prove that $E[X \mid Y]$ minimizes $E[(X - g(Y))^2]$ over all measurable functions $g$. (b) Show that the "residual" $X - E[X \mid Y]$ is uncorrelated with any function of $Y$: $\text{Cov}(X - E[X \mid Y], h(Y)) = 0$ for all square-integrable $h$. (c) Interpret this geometrically as a projection in $L^2$ space.

Show Solution

(a) Let $g^*(Y) = E[X \mid Y]$. For any $g$:

$$E[(X - g(Y))^2] = E[(X - g^*(Y) + g^*(Y) - g(Y))^2]$$ $$= E[(X - g^*)^2] + 2E[(X - g^*)(g^* - g)] + E[(g^* - g)^2]$$

The cross-term: $E[(X - g^*)(g^* - g)] = E[E[(X - g^*)(g^* - g) \mid Y]]$. Since $g^* - g$ is a function of $Y$:

$$= E[(g^* - g) \cdot E[X - g^* \mid Y]] = E[(g^* - g) \cdot 0] = 0$$

because $E[X - E[X \mid Y] \mid Y] = 0$. Therefore $E[(X - g)^2] = E[(X - g^*)^2] + E[(g^* - g)^2] \geq E[(X - g^*)^2]$. $\square$

(b) $\text{Cov}(X - g^*, h(Y)) = E[(X - g^*)h(Y)] = E[E[(X - g^*)h(Y) \mid Y]] = E[h(Y) \cdot E[X - g^* \mid Y]] = 0$. $\square$

(c) In $L^2(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$, the set of functions of $Y$ forms a closed subspace. $E[X \mid Y]$ is the orthogonal projection of $X$ onto this subspace. The "residual" $X - E[X \mid Y]$ is perpendicular to the subspace, exactly as in finite-dimensional least squares.

Exercise 30: Borel-Cantelli and Independence

Let $\{A_n\}$ be a sequence of events. (a) Prove the first Borel-Cantelli lemma: if $\sum_{n=1}^\infty P(A_n) < \infty$, then $P(\limsup A_n) = 0$ (i.e., only finitely many $A_n$ occur a.s.). (b) Prove the second Borel-Cantelli lemma: if the $A_n$ are independent and $\sum P(A_n) = \infty$, then $P(\limsup A_n) = 1$. (c) Give an example showing independence is necessary in (b).

Show Solution

(a) $\limsup A_n = \bigcap_{n=1}^\infty \bigcup_{k=n}^\infty A_k$. For any $n$: $P(\bigcup_{k=n}^\infty A_k) \leq \sum_{k=n}^\infty P(A_k)$. Since $\sum P(A_k) < \infty$, the tail $\sum_{k=n}^\infty P(A_k) \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$. By continuity of probability (nested decreasing events): $P(\limsup A_n) = \lim_{n \to \infty} P(\bigcup_{k=n}^\infty A_k) = 0$. $\square$

(b) It suffices to show $P(\bigcup_{k=n}^\infty A_k) = 1$ for all $n$, equivalently $P(\bigcap_{k=n}^m A_k^c) \to 0$ as $m \to \infty$.

By independence: $P(\bigcap_{k=n}^m A_k^c) = \prod_{k=n}^m (1 - P(A_k))$.

Using $1 - x \leq e^{-x}$: $\prod_{k=n}^m (1 - P(A_k)) \leq \exp(-\sum_{k=n}^m P(A_k)) \to 0$ since $\sum P(A_k) = \infty$. $\square$

(c) Let $A_n = A$ for all $n$, where $P(A) = 1/2$. Then $\sum P(A_n) = \infty$, but $P(\limsup A_n) = P(A) = 1/2 \neq 1$. The events are not independent (they are identical!).

Key Concept

Conditional probability $P(A \mid B)$ measures the probability of $A$ when the sample space is restricted to $B$. Independence means this restriction has no effect: $P(A \mid B) = P(A)$.

Prerequisites

  • Basic probability (sample spaces, events)
  • Set operations ($\cap$, $\cup$, complement)
  • Axioms of probability (Tutorial 02)

Key Formulas

$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$

$P(A \cap B) = P(A \mid B) \, P(B)$

$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A) P(A)}{P(B)}$

$P(B) = \sum_i P(B \mid A_i) P(A_i)$

$A \perp B \iff P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)$
