08. Limit Theorems

Limit theorems are the bridge between probability and statistics. They tell us why averages are predictable, why the normal distribution appears everywhere, and how we can make reliable inferences from finite samples about unknown population parameters. This tutorial develops the theory from modes of convergence through the Law of Large Numbers and the Central Limit Theorem, culminating in powerful tools like the Delta Method and Slutsky's Theorem that underpin modern statistical inference and machine learning.

Why Limit Theorems?

Consider the most fundamental activity in science: collecting data and drawing conclusions. You flip a coin 1,000 times and observe 513 heads. You conclude the coin is approximately fair. A pharmaceutical company tests a drug on 10,000 patients and measures the average improvement. They conclude the drug works. But why are these conclusions valid?

The answer lies in limit theorems—a collection of profound results that describe what happens when we aggregate many random observations. They provide the mathematical foundation for three pillars of applied mathematics:

  • Statistical inference: Why sample averages estimate population means, and how confident we can be about this
  • The universality of the normal distribution: Why so many real-world quantities are approximately Gaussian, regardless of the underlying mechanism
  • Algorithmic convergence: Why stochastic gradient descent converges, why Monte Carlo methods work, and why bootstrap produces valid confidence intervals

The two flagship results are the Law of Large Numbers (LLN), which says that sample averages converge to the population mean, and the Central Limit Theorem (CLT), which says that the distribution of those averages is approximately normal. But to state these precisely, we first need to understand what "convergence" means for random variables.

Modes of Convergence

In calculus, a sequence of real numbers $a_1, a_2, \ldots$ converges to $a$ if $|a_n - a| \to 0$. But random variables are functions on a probability space, and there are multiple meaningful ways to define convergence. Each captures a different sense in which a sequence of random variables $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ approaches a limit $X$.

Convergence in Distribution (Weak Convergence)

The weakest and most commonly used mode. A sequence $X_n$ converges in distribution to $X$, written $X_n \xrightarrow{d} X$, if:

$$F_{X_n}(x) \to F_X(x) \quad \text{for all continuity points } x \text{ of } F_X$$

Equivalently, $E[g(X_n)] \to E[g(X)]$ for every bounded continuous function $g$. This mode only concerns the shape of the distribution; the random variables $X_n$ and $X$ need not even live on the same probability space.

Convergence in Probability

A stronger mode. $X_n$ converges in probability to $X$, written $X_n \xrightarrow{p} X$, if:

$$\forall \varepsilon > 0: \quad P(|X_n - X| > \varepsilon) \to 0 \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

This says that large deviations from the limit become increasingly unlikely, but does not rule out occasional "escapes." For any fixed tolerance $\varepsilon$, the probability of exceeding it vanishes.

Almost Sure Convergence (Strong Convergence)

The strongest pointwise mode. $X_n$ converges almost surely to $X$, written $X_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} X$, if:

$$P\!\left(\lim_{n \to \infty} X_n = X\right) = 1$$

Equivalently, $P\!\left(\omega : X_n(\omega) \to X(\omega)\right) = 1$. This means that for almost every outcome $\omega$ in the sample space, the sequence of numbers $X_1(\omega), X_2(\omega), \ldots$ converges to $X(\omega)$ in the ordinary calculus sense. The set of "bad" outcomes where convergence fails has probability zero.

Convergence in $L^p$ (Mean of order $p$)

$X_n$ converges in $L^p$ to $X$, written $X_n \xrightarrow{L^p} X$, if:

$$E[|X_n - X|^p] \to 0 \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

The most common case is $p = 2$ (mean-square convergence), which requires $E[(X_n - X)^2] \to 0$. This is particularly useful because it connects to the variance: $X_n \xrightarrow{L^2} c$ (a constant) if and only if $E[X_n] \to c$ and $\text{Var}(X_n) \to 0$.

The Implication Hierarchy

These modes are related by a strict hierarchy of implications. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for knowing which convergence results can be combined.

Key facts:

  • Almost sure convergence $\Rightarrow$ convergence in probability $\Rightarrow$ convergence in distribution
  • $L^p$ convergence $\Rightarrow$ convergence in probability (by Markov's inequality)
  • $L^q$ convergence $\Rightarrow$ $L^p$ convergence for $q \geq p$ (by Jensen's inequality)
  • Almost sure convergence and $L^p$ convergence are not comparable in general
  • Special case: If $X_n \xrightarrow{d} c$ where $c$ is a constant, then $X_n \xrightarrow{p} c$

Example showing the distinctions: Let $\Omega = [0,1]$ with uniform measure. Define $X_n(\omega) = \mathbf{1}_{[0, 1/n]}(\omega)$. Then $X_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} 0$ (for any $\omega > 0$, eventually $X_n(\omega) = 0$). Also $X_n \xrightarrow{L^p} 0$ for all $p$ since $E[|X_n|^p] = 1/n \to 0$.

Now let $Y_n$ cycle through subintervals: $Y_1 = \mathbf{1}_{[0,1]}$, $Y_2 = \mathbf{1}_{[0,1/2]}$, $Y_3 = \mathbf{1}_{[1/2,1]}$, $Y_4 = \mathbf{1}_{[0,1/3]}$, $Y_5 = \mathbf{1}_{[1/3,2/3]}$, etc. Then $Y_n \xrightarrow{p} 0$ and $Y_n \xrightarrow{L^1} 0$, but $Y_n \not\xrightarrow{a.s.} 0$ because for every $\omega$, $Y_n(\omega) = 1$ for infinitely many $n$.

The Weak Law of Large Numbers (WLLN)

The Weak Law of Large Numbers is the first great limit theorem. It formalizes the intuitive idea that averages of many observations should be close to the expected value.

Theorem (Weak Law of Large Numbers)

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. random variables with $E[X_i] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 < \infty$. Define $\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i$. Then for every $\varepsilon > 0$:

$$P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| > \varepsilon) \to 0 \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

That is, $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{p} \mu$.

Proof (via Chebyshev's Inequality)

This is one of the most elegant proofs in probability. We compute:

$$E[\bar{X}_n] = E\!\left[\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right] = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n E[X_i] = \frac{1}{n} \cdot n\mu = \mu$$
$$\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \text{Var}\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right) = \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i) = \frac{n\sigma^2}{n^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$$

Now apply Chebyshev's inequality: for any $\varepsilon > 0$,

$$P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon) \leq \frac{\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n)}{\varepsilon^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n\varepsilon^2} \xrightarrow{n \to \infty} 0 \quad \square$$

Remarks:

  • The proof only uses the first two moments ($\mu$ and $\sigma^2$), via Chebyshev's inequality
  • The convergence rate is at least $O(1/n)$ in terms of the probability bound
  • The finite variance condition $\sigma^2 < \infty$ can actually be weakened—Khintchine's version only requires $E[|X_i|] < \infty$
  • The proof also shows $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{L^2} \mu$ (mean-square convergence), since $E[(\bar{X}_n - \mu)^2] = \sigma^2/n \to 0$

The Strong Law of Large Numbers (SLLN)

The Strong Law strengthens the WLLN from convergence in probability to almost sure convergence. The difference is subtle but important: the WLLN says that for any fixed tolerance, the probability of exceeding it vanishes. The SLLN says that with probability 1, the sample mean eventually stays within any tolerance forever.

Theorem (Strong Law of Large Numbers — Kolmogorov)

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. random variables with $E[|X_1|] < \infty$ and $E[X_1] = \mu$. Then:

$$P\!\left(\lim_{n \to \infty} \bar{X}_n = \mu\right) = 1$$

That is, $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} \mu$.

Key differences from WLLN:

  • Weaker condition: Only requires $E[|X_1|] < \infty$ (finite first moment), not finite variance
  • Stronger conclusion: Almost sure convergence instead of convergence in probability
  • Harder proof: The proof is substantially more difficult, using truncation arguments and the Borel-Cantelli lemma

Proof Sketch (Kolmogorov's Approach)

The full proof is one of the deeper results in probability theory. The key ideas are:

  1. Truncation: Replace $X_i$ by $Y_i = X_i \mathbf{1}_{|X_i| \leq i}$. Since $E[|X_1|] < \infty$, the Borel-Cantelli lemma shows $P(X_i \neq Y_i \text{ i.o.}) = 0$, so we can work with the $Y_i$
  2. Kronecker's lemma: If $\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n/n$ converges, then $\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^n a_k \to 0$
  3. Kolmogorov's inequality: Bound $P(\max_{k \leq n} |S_k| > \varepsilon)$ using the variance of partial sums
  4. Three-series theorem: Show $\sum Y_i/i$ converges a.s. by checking that $\sum \text{Var}(Y_i)/i^2 < \infty$

Practical implication: If you run a simulation and compute running averages, not only do the averages get close to $\mu$ with high probability (WLLN), but every single simulation path will eventually converge to $\mu$ (SLLN, except for a set of paths with probability zero).

When LLN Fails

The LLN requires $E[|X_1|] < \infty$. Without this, the sample mean can behave pathologically. The classic counterexample is the Cauchy distribution: if $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ are i.i.d. $\text{Cauchy}(0,1)$, then $\bar{X}_n$ has the same Cauchy(0,1) distribution for every $n$. The sample mean does not converge at all!

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

The LLN tells us that the sample mean converges to $\mu$. The Central Limit Theorem tells us how it converges: the fluctuations around $\mu$ are approximately normal, with a scale that shrinks as $1/\sqrt{n}$.

Theorem (Central Limit Theorem)

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_i] = \mu$ and $0 < \text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 < \infty$. Then as $n \to \infty$:

$$\frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1)$$

Equivalently, $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2)$, or for large $n$: $\bar{X}_n \approx N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$.

The CLT is remarkable for its universality: the standardized sum converges to a normal distribution regardless of the distribution of the individual $X_i$, as long as the variance is finite. Uniform, exponential, Bernoulli, Poisson—they all produce approximately normal averages for large $n$.

Proof via Moment Generating Functions

Define $Z_n = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma} = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \mu)$. Let $Y_i = \frac{X_i - \mu}{\sigma}$, so $E[Y_i] = 0$, $\text{Var}(Y_i) = 1$, and $Z_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i$.

The MGF of $Z_n$ is:

$$M_{Z_n}(t) = E[e^{tZ_n}] = E\!\left[e^{\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\sum Y_i}\right] = \prod_{i=1}^n E\!\left[e^{\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}Y_i}\right] = \left[M_Y\!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\right]^n$$

Now expand $M_Y(s)$ around $s = 0$ using Taylor's theorem. Since $E[Y] = 0$ and $E[Y^2] = 1$:

$$M_Y(s) = 1 + sE[Y] + \frac{s^2}{2}E[Y^2] + O(s^3) = 1 + \frac{s^2}{2} + O(s^3)$$

Substituting $s = t/\sqrt{n}$:

$$M_{Z_n}(t) = \left[1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O\!\left(\frac{1}{n^{3/2}}\right)\right]^n \xrightarrow{n \to \infty} e^{t^2/2}$$

The last step uses the fact that $(1 + a/n)^n \to e^a$. Since $e^{t^2/2}$ is the MGF of $N(0,1)$, and MGFs uniquely determine distributions (when they exist in a neighborhood of 0), we conclude $Z_n \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$. $\square$

Note: A more general proof uses characteristic functions $\varphi_Y(t) = E[e^{itY}]$, which always exist, avoiding the need for the MGF to be finite.

Berry-Esseen Theorem

The CLT says that the standardized sum converges to $N(0,1)$ in distribution, but says nothing about how fast. The Berry-Esseen theorem provides a quantitative bound on the rate of convergence.

Theorem (Berry-Esseen)

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_i] = 0$, $E[X_i^2] = \sigma^2 > 0$, and $E[|X_i|^3] = \rho < \infty$. Let $F_n(x) = P\!\left(\frac{S_n}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} \leq x\right)$ where $S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$. Then:

$$\sup_{x \in \mathbb{R}} |F_n(x) - \Phi(x)| \leq \frac{C\rho}{\sigma^3 \sqrt{n}}$$

where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF and $C \leq 0.4748$ (the best known constant as of 2012, due to Shevtsova).

Practical implications:

  • The convergence rate is $O(1/\sqrt{n})$, independent of the underlying distribution
  • The bound depends on the ratio $\rho/\sigma^3$, which measures the "non-normality" of the distribution. Distributions with heavier tails (larger third moment) converge more slowly
  • For symmetric distributions, the bound can be improved since many odd-moment terms vanish
  • For the binomial (the most common CLT application), the bound is approximately $\frac{0.4748(p^2 + q^2)}{\sqrt{npq}}$ where $q = 1-p$

Example: For fair coin flips ($p = 1/2$), $\sigma^2 = 1/4$, $\rho = E[|X - 1/2|^3] = 1/8$. The Berry-Esseen bound gives:

$$\sup_x |F_n(x) - \Phi(x)| \leq \frac{0.4748 \cdot 0.125}{0.125\sqrt{n}} = \frac{0.4748}{\sqrt{n}}$$

So with $n = 100$ flips, the CDF of the standardized sum is within about 0.047 of the normal CDF at every point—a remarkably good approximation.

Applications of CLT

Confidence Intervals

The CLT directly yields confidence intervals. Since $\bar{X}_n \approx N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$ for large $n$:

$$P\!\left(\bar{X}_n - z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \leq \mu \leq \bar{X}_n + z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right) \approx 1 - \alpha$$

where $z_{\alpha/2}$ is the $(1-\alpha/2)$-quantile of $N(0,1)$. For a 95% confidence interval, $z_{0.025} = 1.96$, giving:

$$\bar{X}_n \pm 1.96 \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$

Hypothesis Testing

To test $H_0: \mu = \mu_0$ vs $H_1: \mu \neq \mu_0$, compute the test statistic:

$$Z = \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}$$

Under $H_0$, $Z \approx N(0,1)$ by the CLT. Reject $H_0$ at level $\alpha$ if $|Z| > z_{\alpha/2}$.

Normal Approximation to the Binomial

If $S_n \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p)$, then $S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ where $X_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$. By the CLT:

$$\frac{S_n - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1)$$

This approximation is the basis of the classic rule of thumb: the normal approximation to the binomial is adequate when $np \geq 5$ and $n(1-p) \geq 5$.

Why the Normal Distribution Is Everywhere

Many real-world quantities—heights, measurement errors, test scores, stock returns (approximately)—are approximately normal. The CLT explains why: whenever a quantity is the result of many small, independent contributions, the aggregate is approximately normal. A person's height is influenced by hundreds of genes and environmental factors. A measurement error accumulates from many small instrument imperfections. The CLT is the reason the normal distribution earned its name: it is the normal outcome of aggregation.

Delta Method

The CLT tells us the asymptotic distribution of $\bar{X}_n$. But what if we care about a function of $\bar{X}_n$, such as $\log(\bar{X}_n)$ or $1/\bar{X}_n$? The Delta Method extends the CLT to smooth transformations.

Theorem (Delta Method)

Suppose $\sqrt{n}(X_n - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2)$ and $g$ is a function with $g'(\mu) \neq 0$. Then:

$$\sqrt{n}(g(X_n) - g(\mu)) \xrightarrow{d} N\!\left(0, \,\sigma^2 [g'(\mu)]^2\right)$$

Proof

By Taylor expansion around $\mu$:

$$g(X_n) = g(\mu) + g'(\mu)(X_n - \mu) + \frac{g''(\xi_n)}{2}(X_n - \mu)^2$$

for some $\xi_n$ between $X_n$ and $\mu$. Therefore:

$$\sqrt{n}(g(X_n) - g(\mu)) = g'(\mu) \cdot \sqrt{n}(X_n - \mu) + \frac{g''(\xi_n)}{2}\sqrt{n}(X_n - \mu)^2$$

The second term is $o_p(1)$ because $\sqrt{n}(X_n - \mu)^2 = \frac{[\sqrt{n}(X_n - \mu)]^2}{\sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{p} 0$. By Slutsky's theorem (see below), the result follows. $\square$

Applications

Example 1: Variance of a sample proportion. If $\hat{p}_n = \bar{X}_n$ where $X_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$, then $\sqrt{n}(\hat{p}_n - p) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, p(1-p))$. Applying the Delta Method with $g(x) = \log\!\left(\frac{x}{1-x}\right)$ (log-odds):

$$g'(p) = \frac{1}{p(1-p)}, \quad \sqrt{n}(g(\hat{p}_n) - g(p)) \xrightarrow{d} N\!\left(0, \frac{1}{p(1-p)}\right)$$

This is the basis of the Wald confidence interval for log-odds ratios in logistic regression.

Example 2: Variance-stabilizing transformation. If $\hat{\lambda}_n$ is the sample mean from a Poisson($\lambda$) sample, then $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\lambda}_n - \lambda) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \lambda)$. The variance depends on $\lambda$! Applying $g(x) = \sqrt{x}$:

$$g'(\lambda) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\lambda}}, \quad \sqrt{n}(\sqrt{\hat{\lambda}_n} - \sqrt{\lambda}) \xrightarrow{d} N\!\left(0, \frac{1}{4}\right)$$

The variance $1/4$ no longer depends on $\lambda$. This is the square-root transformation for Poisson data, widely used in practice.

Second-Order Delta Method

If $g'(\mu) = 0$ but $g''(\mu) \neq 0$, then:

$$n(g(X_n) - g(\mu)) \xrightarrow{d} \frac{\sigma^2 g''(\mu)}{2} \chi^2_1$$

The rate changes from $\sqrt{n}$ to $n$, and the limit is chi-squared rather than normal.

Slutsky's Theorem

In practice, we often combine multiple converging sequences. Slutsky's theorem tells us when we can do this safely.

Theorem (Slutsky)

If $X_n \xrightarrow{d} X$ and $Y_n \xrightarrow{p} c$ (a constant), then:

$$X_n + Y_n \xrightarrow{d} X + c$$
$$X_n \cdot Y_n \xrightarrow{d} cX$$
$$X_n / Y_n \xrightarrow{d} X / c \quad \text{(if } c \neq 0\text{)}$$

Critical warning: Slutsky's theorem requires one of the sequences to converge to a constant. If both $X_n \xrightarrow{d} X$ and $Y_n \xrightarrow{d} Y$, we cannot conclude $X_n + Y_n \xrightarrow{d} X + Y$ without additional information about their joint distribution.

Key Application: The $t$-Statistic

In practice, we rarely know $\sigma$. The natural estimator is the sample standard deviation:

$$S_n^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2$$

By the LLN, $S_n^2 \xrightarrow{p} \sigma^2$, so $S_n \xrightarrow{p} \sigma$. The CLT gives $\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$. By Slutsky's theorem (dividing by $S_n/\sigma \xrightarrow{p} 1$):

$$T_n = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{S_n} = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)/\sigma}{S_n/\sigma} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$$

This justifies using $S_n$ in place of $\sigma$ for large-sample inference. (For small samples from a normal population, the exact distribution is Student's $t$ with $n-1$ degrees of freedom.)

Continuous Mapping Theorem

Theorem (Continuous Mapping / Mann-Wald)

Let $g: \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}^m$ be continuous (or continuous except on a set with $P(X \in \text{that set}) = 0$). Then:

  • $X_n \xrightarrow{d} X \implies g(X_n) \xrightarrow{d} g(X)$
  • $X_n \xrightarrow{p} X \implies g(X_n) \xrightarrow{p} g(X)$
  • $X_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} X \implies g(X_n) \xrightarrow{a.s.} g(X)$

The Continuous Mapping Theorem (CMT) is deceptively simple but immensely powerful. It preserves whatever mode of convergence you started with through any continuous transformation.

Examples:

  • If $X_n \xrightarrow{d} X$, then $X_n^2 \xrightarrow{d} X^2$ (since $g(x) = x^2$ is continuous)
  • If $X_n \xrightarrow{p} \mu > 0$, then $1/X_n \xrightarrow{p} 1/\mu$ (since $g(x) = 1/x$ is continuous at $\mu > 0$)
  • If $(X_n, Y_n) \xrightarrow{d} (X, Y)$, then $X_n + Y_n \xrightarrow{d} X + Y$ (since $g(x,y) = x+y$ is continuous)

Relationship to Delta Method: The CMT handles the "first-order" case but does not give the asymptotic distribution. The Delta Method goes further by specifying that the limit is normal with a specific variance.

Relationship to Slutsky: Slutsky's theorem can be seen as a special case of the CMT applied to the joint vector $(X_n, Y_n)$ when $Y_n \xrightarrow{p} c$ ensures the joint convergence needed. Without $Y_n \to c$, marginal convergence in distribution does not imply joint convergence.

Connections to Machine Learning

SGD Convergence

Stochastic Gradient Descent computes parameter updates using mini-batch gradients $\hat{g}_t = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^B \nabla \ell(x_i; \theta_t)$ instead of the full gradient $g_t = E[\nabla \ell(X; \theta_t)]$. By the LLN, $\hat{g}_t \xrightarrow{p} g_t$ as $B \to \infty$. By the CLT:

$$\hat{g}_t \approx N\!\left(g_t, \frac{\Sigma_t}{B}\right)$$

where $\Sigma_t$ is the covariance of individual gradients. This explains why mini-batch SGD works: with batch size $B$, the gradient noise has standard deviation $O(1/\sqrt{B})$, which is manageable with an appropriate learning rate.

Bootstrap

The bootstrap resamples from the empirical distribution to approximate the sampling distribution of a statistic. Its theoretical justification rests on the CLT: if $T_n = h(\bar{X}_n)$ for some smooth function $h$, then by the Delta Method, $\sqrt{n}(T_n - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \tau^2)$. The bootstrap consistently estimates this normal approximation (and often provides refinements beyond the first-order CLT).

Asymptotic Normality of the MLE

Under regularity conditions, the maximum likelihood estimator $\hat{\theta}_n$ satisfies:

$$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} N\!\left(0, \mathcal{I}(\theta_0)^{-1}\right)$$

where $\mathcal{I}(\theta_0)$ is the Fisher information. This result is fundamentally a CLT applied to the score function $\nabla \log p(X; \theta)$, combined with the Delta Method. It provides the theoretical basis for Wald tests, likelihood ratio tests, and information criteria (AIC, BIC).

Why Mini-Batch Works

A mini-batch of size $B$ gives a gradient estimate with noise proportional to $\sigma/\sqrt{B}$. Doubling $B$ reduces noise by $\sqrt{2} \approx 41\%$, not 50%. This square-root scaling (a direct consequence of the CLT) explains the diminishing returns of larger batch sizes and why smaller batches with more steps often outperform larger batches with fewer steps: the noise acts as implicit regularization.

Monte Carlo Integration

To estimate $I = E[f(X)] = \int f(x) p(x)\, dx$, draw $X_1, \ldots, X_n \sim p$ and compute $\hat{I}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum f(X_i)$. By the LLN, $\hat{I}_n \to I$ a.s. By the CLT, $\hat{I}_n \approx N(I, \text{Var}(f(X))/n)$. The CLT gives us both the error rate ($O(1/\sqrt{n})$, dimension-independent!) and confidence intervals for the estimate.

Code: Limit Theorems in Python

Demonstrations of the Law of Large Numbers, Central Limit Theorem, Berry-Esseen bounds, the Delta Method, and bootstrap confidence intervals—all implemented from scratch.

1. Law of Large Numbers Demonstration

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def demonstrate_lln(distribution, true_mean, n_max=5000, n_paths=5, seed=42):
    """
    Demonstrate the Law of Large Numbers by plotting
    running averages converging to the true mean.

    Parameters:
        distribution: callable that generates n samples
        true_mean: the population mean
        n_max: maximum number of samples
        n_paths: number of independent sample paths
    """
    np.random.seed(seed)
    ns = np.arange(1, n_max + 1)

    fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 5))
    colors = plt.cm.Set2(np.linspace(0, 1, n_paths))

    for i in range(n_paths):
        samples = distribution(n_max)
        running_avg = np.cumsum(samples) / ns
        ax.plot(ns, running_avg, color=colors[i], alpha=0.7,
                linewidth=1, label=f'Path {i+1}')

    ax.axhline(y=true_mean, color='red', linestyle='--',
               linewidth=2, label=f'True mean = {true_mean}')

    # Confidence band: mean +/- 2*sigma/sqrt(n)
    sigma = np.std(distribution(100000))
    upper = true_mean + 2 * sigma / np.sqrt(ns)
    lower = true_mean - 2 * sigma / np.sqrt(ns)
    ax.fill_between(ns, lower, upper, alpha=0.1, color='red',
                    label=r'$\mu \pm 2\sigma/\sqrt{n}$')

    ax.set_xlabel('Number of samples (n)', fontsize=12)
    ax.set_ylabel('Running average', fontsize=12)
    ax.set_title('Law of Large Numbers: Running Average Converges to True Mean',
                 fontsize=14)
    ax.legend(loc='upper right', fontsize=9)
    ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
    plt.tight_layout()

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats

def berry_esseen_visualization(sampler, sigma, rho,
                               sample_sizes=None, n_reps=100000, seed=42):
    """
    Visualize the Berry-Esseen bound vs actual CDF error.

    Parameters:
        sampler: callable(n) returning n zero-mean samples
        sigma: std dev of individual samples
        rho: E[|X|^3] (third absolute moment)
        sample_sizes: list of n values
        n_reps: number of Monte Carlo repetitions
    """
    np.random.seed(seed)
    if sample_sizes is None:
        sample_sizes = [5, 10, 25, 50, 100, 250, 500, 1000, 2500, 5000]

    C = 0.4748  # Best known Berry-Esseen constant
    berry_esseen_bounds = [C * rho / (sigma**3 * np.sqrt(n))
                           for n in sample_sizes]
    actual_errors = []

    x_grid = np.linspace(-4, 4, 1000)
    normal_cdf = stats.norm.cdf(x_grid)

    for n in sample_sizes:
        # Generate standardized sample means
        sums = np.array([np.sum(sampler(n)) for _ in range(n_reps)])
        standardized = sums / (sigma * np.sqrt(n))

        # Compute empirical CDF
        empirical_cdf = np.array([np.mean(standardized <= x) for x in x_grid])

        # Maximum absolute error
        max_error = np.max(np.abs(empirical_cdf - normal_cdf))
        actual_errors.append(max_error)

    # Plot
    fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))
    ax.loglog(sample_sizes, berry_esseen_bounds, 'r--o',
              linewidth=2, markersize=6, label='Berry-Esseen bound')
    ax.loglog(sample_sizes, actual_errors, 'b-s',
              linewidth=2, markersize=6, label='Actual max |F_n - Phi|')

    # Reference line: 1/sqrt(n)
    ref = [1.0 / np.sqrt(n) for n in sample_sizes]

5. Bootstrap Confidence Intervals

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats

def bootstrap_ci(data, statistic, n_bootstrap=10000,
                 confidence=0.95, seed=42):
    """
    Compute bootstrap confidence intervals using three methods:
    1. Percentile method
    2. Normal (CLT-based) method
    3. BCa (bias-corrected and accelerated)

    Parameters:
        data: observed sample (1D array)
        statistic: callable(sample) -> scalar
        n_bootstrap: number of bootstrap resamples
        confidence: confidence level
    Returns:
        dict with CI from each method
    """
    np.random.seed(seed)
    n = len(data)
    alpha = 1 - confidence
    theta_hat = statistic(data)

    # Generate bootstrap statistics
    boot_stats = np.array([
        statistic(data[np.random.randint(0, n, n)])
        for _ in range(n_bootstrap)
    ])

    # 1. Percentile method
    ci_percentile = (
        np.percentile(boot_stats, 100 * alpha / 2),
        np.percentile(boot_stats, 100 * (1 - alpha / 2))
    )

    # 2. Normal method (CLT-based)
    se_boot = np.std(boot_stats, ddof=1)
    z = stats.norm.ppf(1 - alpha / 2)
    ci_normal = (theta_hat - z * se_boot, theta_hat + z * se_boot)

    # 3. BCa method
    # Bias correction
    z0 = stats.norm.ppf(np.mean(boot_stats < theta_hat))

    # Acceleration (jackknife estimate)
    jackknife_stats = np.array([
        statistic(np.delete(data, i)) for i in range(n)
    ])
    jack_mean = np.mean(jackknife_stats)
    num = np.sum((jack_mean - jackknife_stats)**3)
    den = 6 * (np.sum((jack_mean - jackknife_stats)**2))**1.5
    a_hat = num / den if den != 0 else 0

    # Adjusted percentiles
    z_alpha_low = stats.norm.ppf(alpha / 2)
    z_alpha_high = stats.norm.ppf(1 - alpha / 2)

    p_low = stats.norm.cdf(
        z0 + (z0 + z_alpha_low) / (1 - a_hat * (z0 + z_alpha_low))
    )
    p_high = stats.norm.cdf(
        z0 + (z0 + z_alpha_high) / (1 - a_hat * (z0 + z_alpha_high))
    )

    ci_bca = (
        np.percentile(boot_stats, 100 * p_low),
        np.percentile(boot_stats, 100 * p_high)
    )

    return {
        'percentile': ci_percentile,
        'normal': ci_normal,
        'bca': ci_bca,
        'boot_stats': boot_stats,
        'theta_hat': theta_hat,
        'se': se_boot
    }

Exercise 1: LLN for Coin Flips

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. $\text{Bernoulli}(0.6)$. Using Chebyshev's inequality, find the smallest $n$ such that $P(|\bar{X}_n - 0.6| \geq 0.05) \leq 0.01$.

Show Solution

For $\text{Bernoulli}(0.6)$: $\mu = 0.6$, $\sigma^2 = 0.6 \times 0.4 = 0.24$.

By Chebyshev's inequality:

$$P(|\bar{X}_n - 0.6| \geq 0.05) \leq \frac{\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n)}{0.05^2} = \frac{0.24/n}{0.0025} = \frac{96}{n}$$

We need $\frac{96}{n} \leq 0.01$, so:

$$n \geq \frac{96}{0.01} = 9600$$

Answer: $n = 9600$. (Note: this is a conservative bound. The actual probability is much smaller for this $n$.)

Exercise 2: CLT Normal Approximation

A factory produces bolts with mean length 10 cm and standard deviation 0.5 cm. A quality inspector measures a random sample of $n = 100$ bolts. Using the CLT, find the probability that the sample mean exceeds 10.1 cm.

Show Solution

By the CLT, $\bar{X}_{100} \approx N(10, 0.5^2/100) = N(10, 0.0025)$.

Standardize:

$$Z = \frac{\bar{X}_{100} - 10}{0.5/\sqrt{100}} = \frac{\bar{X}_{100} - 10}{0.05}$$ $$P(\bar{X}_{100} > 10.1) = P\!\left(Z > \frac{10.1 - 10}{0.05}\right) = P(Z > 2) = 1 - \Phi(2)$$ $$= 1 - 0.9772 = 0.0228$$

Answer: $P(\bar{X}_{100} > 10.1) \approx 0.0228 = 2.28\%$

Exercise 3: Identifying Convergence Modes

For each sequence, determine which modes of convergence hold:

(a) $X_n = Z/n$ where $Z \sim N(0,1)$.

(b) $X_n \sim N(0, 1/n)$, independent.

(c) $X_n = 0$ with probability $1 - 1/n$ and $X_n = n$ with probability $1/n$.

Show Solution

(a) $X_n = Z/n$: For every outcome $\omega$, $X_n(\omega) = Z(\omega)/n \to 0$. So $X_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} 0$. Also $E[X_n^2] = E[Z^2]/n^2 = 1/n^2 \to 0$, so $X_n \xrightarrow{L^2} 0$. Since a.s. and $L^2$ both imply probability and distribution: all four modes hold.

(b) $X_n \sim N(0, 1/n)$ independent: $E[X_n^2] = 1/n \to 0$, so $X_n \xrightarrow{L^2} 0$, hence $X_n \xrightarrow{p} 0$ and $X_n \xrightarrow{d} 0$. For a.s. convergence: by Borel-Cantelli, $\sum P(|X_n| > \varepsilon) = \sum 2(1 - \Phi(\varepsilon\sqrt{n})) < \infty$ for any $\varepsilon > 0$ (super-exponential decay), so $X_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} 0$. All four modes hold.

(c) $P(X_n = 0) = 1 - 1/n$, $P(X_n = n) = 1/n$: $P(|X_n| > \varepsilon) = 1/n \to 0$, so $X_n \xrightarrow{p} 0$ and $X_n \xrightarrow{d} 0$. But $E[X_n^2] = n^2 \cdot (1/n) = n \to \infty$, so $X_n \not\xrightarrow{L^2} 0$. Also $E[|X_n|] = n \cdot (1/n) = 1 \not\to 0$, so $X_n \not\xrightarrow{L^1} 0$. For a.s.: $\sum P(X_n = n) = \sum 1/n = \infty$, and the events are independent, so by Borel-Cantelli II, $X_n = n$ infinitely often a.s., hence $X_n \not\xrightarrow{a.s.} 0$. Only convergence in probability and in distribution hold.

Exercise 6: Convergence in Distribution to a Constant

Let $X_n$ be a sequence of random variables such that $X_n \xrightarrow{d} c$ for some constant $c$. Prove that $X_n \xrightarrow{p} c$.

Show Solution

The CDF of the constant $c$ is $F_c(x) = \mathbf{1}_{[c, \infty)}(x)$, which is continuous everywhere except at $x = c$.

Since $X_n \xrightarrow{d} c$, we have $F_{X_n}(x) \to F_c(x)$ at all continuity points of $F_c$.

For any $\varepsilon > 0$:

$$P(|X_n - c| > \varepsilon) = P(X_n < c - \varepsilon) + P(X_n > c + \varepsilon)$$ $$= F_{X_n}(c - \varepsilon) + 1 - F_{X_n}(c + \varepsilon)$$

Since $c - \varepsilon$ and $c + \varepsilon$ are both continuity points of $F_c$:

$$F_{X_n}(c - \varepsilon) \to F_c(c - \varepsilon) = 0$$ $$F_{X_n}(c + \varepsilon) \to F_c(c + \varepsilon) = 1$$

Therefore $P(|X_n - c| > \varepsilon) \to 0 + 1 - 1 = 0$, proving $X_n \xrightarrow{p} c$. $\square$

Note: This is a special property of constant limits. Convergence in distribution to a non-degenerate random variable does not imply convergence in probability.

Exercise 8: LLN for Sample Variance

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_i] = \mu$ and $E[X_i^2] = \mu_2 < \infty$. Using the Weak Law of Large Numbers, show that the sample second moment $M_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2$ converges in probability to $\mu_2$, and deduce that $S_n^2 = M_n - \bar{X}_n^2 \xrightarrow{p} \sigma^2$.

Show Solution

Step 1: The variables $Y_i = X_i^2$ are i.i.d. with $E[Y_i] = \mu_2 < \infty$. By the WLLN:

$$M_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i \xrightarrow{p} E[Y_i] = \mu_2$$

Step 2: By the WLLN applied to $X_i$: $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{p} \mu$.

Step 3: By the Continuous Mapping Theorem (with $g(x) = x^2$, which is continuous):

$$\bar{X}_n^2 \xrightarrow{p} \mu^2$$

Step 4: Since convergence in probability is preserved under addition:

$$S_n^2 = M_n - \bar{X}_n^2 \xrightarrow{p} \mu_2 - \mu^2 = \sigma^2 \quad \square$$

Note: This shows the biased sample variance is a consistent estimator of $\sigma^2$.

Exercise 9: CLT and the Sum of Uniform Random Variables

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_{12}$ be i.i.d. $\text{Uniform}(0,1)$. Show that $S_{12} - 6$ is approximately $N(0,1)$, where $S_{12} = \sum_{i=1}^{12} X_i$. (This was historically used as a fast method to generate approximate standard normal random variables.)

Show Solution

For $X_i \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$: $\mu = 1/2$ and $\sigma^2 = 1/12$.

The sum $S_{12} = \sum_{i=1}^{12} X_i$ has:

$$E[S_{12}] = 12 \times \frac{1}{2} = 6, \qquad \text{Var}(S_{12}) = 12 \times \frac{1}{12} = 1$$

By the CLT, the standardized sum is:

$$Z_n = \frac{S_n - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} = \frac{S_{12} - 6}{\sqrt{1}} = S_{12} - 6$$

which converges in distribution to $N(0,1)$ as $n \to \infty$. Even for $n = 12$, the approximation is remarkably good because the uniform distribution is symmetric and bounded (no heavy tails).

Answer: $S_{12} - 6 \approx N(0,1)$ because the mean of the sum is 6, the variance is exactly 1, and the CLT gives the Gaussian shape. This is the Irwin-Hall approximation to the normal distribution.

Exercise 10: Convergence Hierarchy

State whether each implication is true or false, and give a brief justification or counterexample:

(a) $X_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} X \implies X_n \xrightarrow{p} X$

(b) $X_n \xrightarrow{p} X \implies X_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} X$

(c) $X_n \xrightarrow{L^2} X \implies X_n \xrightarrow{p} X$

(d) $X_n \xrightarrow{p} X \implies X_n \xrightarrow{L^1} X$

Show Solution

(a) TRUE. If $X_n(\omega) \to X(\omega)$ for all $\omega$ outside a null set $N$, then for any $\varepsilon > 0$: $\{|X_n - X| > \varepsilon\} \subset N \cup A_n$ where $P(A_n) \to 0$ by the definition of pointwise convergence. More precisely, a.s. convergence implies $P(|X_n - X| > \varepsilon) \to 0$ by the dominated convergence theorem applied to the indicator functions.

(b) FALSE. The typewriter sequence provides a counterexample: define $X_n = \mathbf{1}_{I_n}$ on $[0,1]$ where the intervals $I_n$ cycle through $[0,1]$ with decreasing length. Then $P(X_n = 1) \to 0$ (convergence in probability) but for every $\omega$, $X_n(\omega) = 1$ infinitely often (no a.s. convergence).

(c) TRUE. By Chebyshev (Markov) inequality:

$$P(|X_n - X| > \varepsilon) \leq \frac{E[|X_n - X|^2]}{\varepsilon^2} \to 0$$

So $L^2$ convergence implies convergence in probability.

(d) FALSE. Let $X_n = n$ with probability $1/n$ and $X_n = 0$ otherwise. Then $P(|X_n| > \varepsilon) = 1/n \to 0$, so $X_n \xrightarrow{p} 0$. But $E[|X_n|] = n \cdot (1/n) = 1 \not\to 0$, so $X_n \not\xrightarrow{L^1} 0$.

Exercise 11: Delta Method for Odds Ratio

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. $\text{Bernoulli}(p)$ with $p = 0.3$. The odds is defined as $\psi = p/(1-p)$ and is estimated by $\hat{\psi}_n = \hat{p}_n/(1-\hat{p}_n)$ where $\hat{p}_n = \bar{X}_n$. Use the Delta Method to find the asymptotic distribution of $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\psi}_n - \psi)$. Compute the asymptotic variance for $p = 0.3$.

Show Solution

We know $\sqrt{n}(\hat{p}_n - p) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, p(1-p))$.

Let $g(x) = x/(1-x)$. Then:

$$g'(x) = \frac{(1-x) + x}{(1-x)^2} = \frac{1}{(1-x)^2}$$

By the Delta Method:

$$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\psi}_n - \psi) \xrightarrow{d} N\!\left(0, \, p(1-p) \cdot \frac{1}{(1-p)^4}\right) = N\!\left(0, \, \frac{p}{(1-p)^3}\right)$$

For $p = 0.3$:

$$\text{Asymptotic variance} = \frac{0.3}{(0.7)^3} = \frac{0.3}{0.343} = 0.8746$$

Answer: $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\psi}_n - \psi) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 0.875)$, so the asymptotic variance of $\hat{\psi}_n$ is $0.875/n$.

Exercise 12: Convergence Mode Comparison

Let $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$ and define $X_n = \mathbf{1}_{[0, 1/n]}(U)$.

(a) Show $X_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} 0$.

(b) Show $X_n \xrightarrow{L^p} 0$ for all $p \geq 1$.

(c) Now let $Y_n = n^{1/2} \cdot \mathbf{1}_{[0, 1/n]}(U)$. Does $Y_n \xrightarrow{p} 0$? Does $Y_n \xrightarrow{L^2} 0$?

Show Solution

(a) For any $\omega \in (0, 1]$, there exists $N$ such that $1/N < \omega$. For all $n \geq N$, $\omega \notin [0, 1/n]$, so $X_n(\omega) = 0$. Thus $X_n(\omega) \to 0$ for all $\omega \in (0,1]$, which has probability 1. $\square$

(b) $E[|X_n|^p] = E[X_n^p] = E[X_n] = P(U \leq 1/n) = 1/n \to 0$ for all $p \geq 1$ (since $X_n \in \{0, 1\}$, $X_n^p = X_n$). $\square$

(c) For $Y_n = n^{1/2} \cdot \mathbf{1}_{[0,1/n]}(U)$:

Convergence in probability: For any $\varepsilon > 0$:

$$P(|Y_n| > \varepsilon) = P(U \leq 1/n) = 1/n \to 0$$

So $Y_n \xrightarrow{p} 0$. Yes.

$L^2$ convergence:

$$E[Y_n^2] = n \cdot P(U \leq 1/n) = n \cdot (1/n) = 1 \not\to 0$$

So $Y_n \not\xrightarrow{L^2} 0$. No. This shows convergence in probability does not imply $L^2$ convergence.

Exercise 13: Berry-Esseen Bound Computation

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. $\text{Bernoulli}(p)$ with $p = 0.1$. Compute the Berry-Esseen bound on $\sup_x |F_n(x) - \Phi(x)|$ for $n = 100$, where $F_n$ is the CDF of the standardized sum. Is the normal approximation likely to be good?

Show Solution

For $\text{Bernoulli}(0.1)$: $\mu = 0.1$, $\sigma^2 = 0.09$, $\sigma = 0.3$.

Third absolute moment of $X_i - \mu$:

$$\rho = E[|X_i - 0.1|^3] = |1 - 0.1|^3 \cdot 0.1 + |0 - 0.1|^3 \cdot 0.9$$ $$= 0.9^3 \times 0.1 + 0.1^3 \times 0.9 = 0.0729 + 0.0009 = 0.0738$$

Berry-Esseen bound with $C = 0.4748$:

$$\frac{C\rho}{\sigma^3\sqrt{n}} = \frac{0.4748 \times 0.0738}{0.027 \times 10} = \frac{0.03504}{0.27} = 0.1298$$

The bound says the CDF error is at most about 0.13, which is fairly large. This is consistent with the fact that the normal approximation to $\text{Binomial}(100, 0.1)$ is only moderate (the rule of thumb $np = 10 \geq 5$ is barely satisfied, and $p$ is far from 0.5, creating skewness).

Exercise 14: Slutsky's Theorem Application

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$. Define $S_n^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2$ (biased sample variance). Show that the studentized statistic $T_n = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{S_n}$ converges in distribution to $N(0,1)$.

Show Solution

Step 1: By the CLT: $\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$.

Step 2: Show $S_n^2 \xrightarrow{p} \sigma^2$.

Note $S_n^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum X_i^2 - \bar{X}_n^2$. By the LLN, $\frac{1}{n}\sum X_i^2 \xrightarrow{p} E[X^2]$ and $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{p} \mu$. By the Continuous Mapping Theorem, $\bar{X}_n^2 \xrightarrow{p} \mu^2$. Therefore:

$$S_n^2 \xrightarrow{p} E[X^2] - \mu^2 = \sigma^2$$

By CMT again, $S_n \xrightarrow{p} \sigma$, so $S_n/\sigma \xrightarrow{p} 1$.

Step 3: Apply Slutsky's theorem. Write:

$$T_n = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{S_n} = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)/\sigma}{S_n/\sigma}$$

The numerator $\xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$ and the denominator $\xrightarrow{p} 1$. By Slutsky:

$$T_n \xrightarrow{d} \frac{N(0,1)}{1} = N(0,1) \quad \square$$

Exercise 15: Variance-Stabilizing Transformation

For data from a $\text{Binomial}(n, p)$ distribution, the sample proportion $\hat{p} = X/n$ has variance $p(1-p)/n$ that depends on the unknown $p$. Use the Delta Method to show that the arcsine transformation $g(\hat{p}) = \arcsin(\sqrt{\hat{p}})$ produces an approximately constant variance of $1/(4n)$, independent of $p$.

Show Solution

We have $\sqrt{n}(\hat{p} - p) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, p(1-p))$.

Let $g(x) = \arcsin(\sqrt{x})$. Compute $g'(x)$:

$$g'(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x}} \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x(1-x)}}$$

By the Delta Method:

$$\sqrt{n}(g(\hat{p}) - g(p)) \xrightarrow{d} N\!\left(0, \, p(1-p) \cdot [g'(p)]^2\right)$$

The asymptotic variance is:

$$p(1-p) \cdot \frac{1}{4p(1-p)} = \frac{1}{4}$$

Therefore:

$$\sqrt{n}\big(\arcsin(\sqrt{\hat{p}}) - \arcsin(\sqrt{p})\big) \xrightarrow{d} N\!\left(0, \frac{1}{4}\right)$$

The variance $1/4$ does not depend on $p$. The variance of $\arcsin(\sqrt{\hat{p}})$ is approximately $1/(4n)$. $\square$

Note: This is the famous arcsine (or angular) transformation, widely used in practice to stabilize binomial proportions before analysis.

Exercise 16: Delta Method for Log-Transformed Mean

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. $\text{Exponential}(\lambda)$ with $\lambda = 2$ (so $E[X_i] = 1/2$ and $\text{Var}(X_i) = 1/4$). Use the Delta Method to find the asymptotic distribution of $\sqrt{n}(\log(\bar{X}_n) - \log(1/2))$.

Show Solution

By the CLT: $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2)$ where $\mu = 1/\lambda = 1/2$ and $\sigma^2 = 1/\lambda^2 = 1/4$.

Let $g(x) = \log(x)$. Then $g'(x) = 1/x$, so $g'(\mu) = 1/(1/2) = 2$.

By the Delta Method:

$$\sqrt{n}(g(\bar{X}_n) - g(\mu)) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2 \cdot [g'(\mu)]^2)$$ $$= N\!\left(0, \frac{1}{4} \cdot 4\right) = N(0, 1)$$

Answer: $\sqrt{n}(\log(\bar{X}_n) - \log(1/2)) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1)$.

Note: The asymptotic variance of $\log(\bar{X}_n)$ is $1/n$. In general, for $\text{Exponential}(\lambda)$ data, the Delta Method gives asymptotic variance $[g'(1/\lambda)]^2 / (n\lambda^2) = \lambda^2/(n\lambda^2) = 1/n$, independent of $\lambda$. The log transformation is a variance-stabilizing transformation for exponential data.

Exercise 17: Continuous Mapping Theorem Application

Let $X_n \xrightarrow{d} X$ where $X \sim N(0,1)$. Determine the limiting distribution of each:

(a) $X_n^2$

(b) $e^{X_n}$

(c) $|X_n|$

Show Solution

The Continuous Mapping Theorem states: if $X_n \xrightarrow{d} X$ and $g$ is continuous (except possibly on a set of measure zero under the law of $X$), then $g(X_n) \xrightarrow{d} g(X)$.

(a) $g(x) = x^2$ is continuous everywhere. By CMT:

$$X_n^2 \xrightarrow{d} X^2 \sim \chi^2(1)$$

since the square of a standard normal is chi-squared with 1 degree of freedom.

(b) $g(x) = e^x$ is continuous everywhere. By CMT:

$$e^{X_n} \xrightarrow{d} e^X \sim \text{LogNormal}(0, 1)$$

The limiting distribution has density $f(y) = \frac{1}{y\sqrt{2\pi}}\exp(-(\log y)^2/2)$ for $y > 0$.

(c) $g(x) = |x|$ is continuous everywhere. By CMT:

$$|X_n| \xrightarrow{d} |X| \sim \text{Half-Normal}$$

The half-normal has density $f(y) = \sqrt{2/\pi}\, e^{-y^2/2}$ for $y \geq 0$. Equivalently, $|X| \sim \chi(1)$ (chi distribution with 1 degree of freedom).

Exercise 19: Berry-Esseen for Uniform Distribution

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. $\text{Uniform}(0,1)$. Compute the Berry-Esseen bound for $n = 30$ and compare it to the Berry-Esseen bound for $\text{Bernoulli}(0.5)$ with the same $n$. Which distribution has a better normal approximation, and why?

Show Solution

Uniform(0,1): $\mu = 1/2$, $\sigma^2 = 1/12$, $\sigma = 1/\sqrt{12}$.

Third absolute central moment:

$$\rho = E[|X - 1/2|^3] = 2\int_0^{1/2} (1/2 - x)^3 \, dx = 2 \cdot \frac{(1/2)^4}{4} = \frac{1}{32}$$

Berry-Esseen bound (with $C = 0.4748$):

$$\frac{C\rho}{\sigma^3\sqrt{n}} = \frac{0.4748 \times (1/32)}{(1/\sqrt{12})^3 \times \sqrt{30}} = \frac{0.01484}{(1/12\sqrt{12}) \times \sqrt{30}}$$ $$= \frac{0.01484}{0.02408 \times 5.477} = \frac{0.01484}{0.1319} = 0.1125$$

Bernoulli(0.5): $\mu = 1/2$, $\sigma^2 = 1/4$, $\sigma = 1/2$.

$$\rho = E[|X - 1/2|^3] = |1/2|^3 \cdot 1/2 + |{-1/2}|^3 \cdot 1/2 = 1/8$$ $$\frac{C\rho}{\sigma^3\sqrt{n}} = \frac{0.4748 \times (1/8)}{(1/2)^3 \times \sqrt{30}} = \frac{0.05935}{0.125 \times 5.477} = \frac{0.05935}{0.6847} = 0.0867$$

Comparison: The Bernoulli(0.5) bound ($\approx 0.087$) is tighter than the Uniform(0,1) bound ($\approx 0.113$). This may seem surprising since the uniform is a "smoother" distribution. The key is that the Berry-Esseen bound depends on the ratio $\rho/\sigma^3$, and Bernoulli(0.5) has a smaller such ratio. However, the bound is not tight -- in practice, both approximations are quite good at $n = 30$.

Exercise 20: CLT for Estimating a Proportion

A polling firm wants to estimate the proportion $p$ of voters who support a candidate. They sample $n$ voters independently. Using the CLT, derive the formula for the sample size $n$ needed to ensure that $|\hat{p}_n - p| \leq \varepsilon$ with probability at least $1 - \alpha$, without knowing $p$ in advance. Apply this for $\varepsilon = 0.03$ and $\alpha = 0.05$.

Show Solution

By the CLT, $\hat{p}_n = \bar{X}_n$ satisfies:

$$\frac{\hat{p}_n - p}{\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$$

We want $P(|\hat{p}_n - p| \leq \varepsilon) \geq 1 - \alpha$. Approximately:

$$P\!\left(\left|\frac{\hat{p}_n - p}{\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}}\right| \leq \frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}}\right) \geq 1 - \alpha$$

This requires $\frac{\varepsilon}{\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}} \geq z_{\alpha/2}$, where $z_{\alpha/2} = \Phi^{-1}(1-\alpha/2)$. Solving:

$$n \geq \frac{z_{\alpha/2}^2 \cdot p(1-p)}{\varepsilon^2}$$

Since we don't know $p$, use the worst case: $p(1-p) \leq 1/4$ (maximized at $p = 1/2$). So:

$$n \geq \frac{z_{\alpha/2}^2}{4\varepsilon^2}$$

For $\varepsilon = 0.03$ and $\alpha = 0.05$: $z_{0.025} = 1.96$.

$$n \geq \frac{(1.96)^2}{4(0.03)^2} = \frac{3.8416}{0.0036} = 1067.1$$

Answer: $n \geq 1068$ voters are needed. This is the well-known "margin of error" formula used in polling.

Exercise 21: Proving Convergence in Probability Does Not Imply A.S.

Construct an explicit sequence of random variables on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P) = ([0,1], \mathcal{B}([0,1]), \text{Lebesgue})$ that converges in probability to 0 but does not converge almost surely to 0. Prove both claims rigorously.

Show Solution

Construction (typewriter sequence): Enumerate the intervals $[j/k, (j+1)/k)$ for $k = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ and $j = 0, 1, \ldots, k-1$ in order. Define $X_n = \mathbf{1}_{I_n}$ where $I_n$ is the $n$-th interval in this enumeration.

Explicitly: $X_1 = \mathbf{1}_{[0,1)}$, $X_2 = \mathbf{1}_{[0,1/2)}$, $X_3 = \mathbf{1}_{[1/2,1)}$, $X_4 = \mathbf{1}_{[0,1/3)}$, $X_5 = \mathbf{1}_{[1/3,2/3)}$, $X_6 = \mathbf{1}_{[2/3,1)}$, $X_7 = \mathbf{1}_{[0,1/4)}$, etc.

Convergence in probability: The $n$-th random variable corresponds to some interval of length $1/k$ where $k$ grows as $n$ increases. Specifically, the $n$-th index lies in the $k$-th "row" where $k(k-1)/2 < n \leq k(k+1)/2$, so $k \sim \sqrt{2n}$. Thus:

$$P(|X_n| > \varepsilon) = P(X_n = 1) = \frac{1}{k} \leq \frac{C}{\sqrt{n}} \to 0$$

for any $\varepsilon \in (0, 1]$. So $X_n \xrightarrow{p} 0$. $\square$

Non-convergence a.s.: Fix any $\omega \in [0,1)$. For each $k$, exactly one of the intervals $[j/k, (j+1)/k)$ contains $\omega$, so $X_n(\omega) = 1$ for some $n$ in the $k$-th row. Since there are infinitely many rows, $X_n(\omega) = 1$ for infinitely many $n$. Therefore $X_n(\omega) \not\to 0$ for every $\omega \in [0,1)$, which has probability 1.

More formally: $\limsup_{n \to \infty} X_n(\omega) = 1$ for all $\omega \in [0,1)$, so $P(\lim X_n = 0) = 0 \neq 1$. $\square$

Exercise 22: Slutsky's Theorem with Multivariate CLT

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_i] = \mu$ and $E[X_i^4] < \infty$. Define $V_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2$. Find the joint asymptotic distribution of $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu, V_n - \sigma^2)$ and use it to derive the asymptotic distribution of $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)/\sqrt{V_n}$.

Show Solution

Step 1: Joint CLT. Define $Y_i = (X_i - \mu, (X_i - \mu)^2 - \sigma^2)$. Then $E[Y_i] = (0, 0)$ and:

$$\text{Cov}(Y_i) = \begin{pmatrix} \sigma^2 & \mu_3 \\ \mu_3 & \mu_4 - \sigma^4 \end{pmatrix}$$

where $\mu_3 = E[(X-\mu)^3]$ and $\mu_4 = E[(X-\mu)^4]$.

By the multivariate CLT:

$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i \xrightarrow{d} N\!\left(\mathbf{0}, \begin{pmatrix} \sigma^2 & \mu_3 \\ \mu_3 & \mu_4 - \sigma^4 \end{pmatrix}\right)$$

Step 2: Note that $V_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum(X_i - \mu)^2 - (\bar{X}_n - \mu)^2$. Since $(\bar{X}_n - \mu)^2 = O_p(1/n)$, we have $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)^2 = O_p(1/\sqrt{n}) = o_p(1)$, so:

$$\sqrt{n}(V_n - \sigma^2) = \sqrt{n}\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum(X_i-\mu)^2 - \sigma^2\right) + o_p(1)$$

The joint distribution is as stated in Step 1.

Step 3: Apply Slutsky. Write:

$$\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sqrt{V_n}} = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)/\sigma}{\sqrt{V_n}/\sigma} = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)/\sigma}{\sqrt{V_n/\sigma^2}}$$

The numerator $\xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$. For the denominator: $V_n \xrightarrow{p} \sigma^2$, so $V_n/\sigma^2 \xrightarrow{p} 1$, hence $\sqrt{V_n/\sigma^2} \xrightarrow{p} 1$ by CMT.

By Slutsky: $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)/\sqrt{V_n} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$. $\square$

Exercise 23: A.S. Convergence via Borel-Cantelli

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_i] = \mu$ and $E[X_i^4] < \infty$. Use the Borel-Cantelli lemma and Chebyshev's inequality applied to $(\bar{X}_n - \mu)^4$ to prove that $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} \mu$ along the subsequence $n = k^2$. Then extend to all $n$.

Show Solution

Step 1: Fourth moment bound. Let $S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \mu)$. Expanding $S_n^4$:

$$E[S_n^4] = \sum_{i,j,k,l} E[(X_i-\mu)(X_j-\mu)(X_k-\mu)(X_l-\mu)]$$

By independence and zero mean, the only nonzero terms are: (i) all four indices equal: $n$ terms, each $\mu_4 = E[(X-\mu)^4]$; (ii) two pairs of equal indices: $\binom{n}{2} \times 3$ terms, each $\sigma^4$. So:

$$E[S_n^4] = n\mu_4 + 3n(n-1)\sigma^4$$

Therefore:

$$E[(\bar{X}_n - \mu)^4] = \frac{E[S_n^4]}{n^4} = \frac{\mu_4}{n^3} + \frac{3(n-1)\sigma^4}{n^3} = O(1/n^2)$$

Step 2: Subsequence convergence. By Markov's inequality:

$$P(|\bar{X}_{k^2} - \mu| > \varepsilon) \leq \frac{E[(\bar{X}_{k^2}-\mu)^4]}{\varepsilon^4} = O(1/k^4)$$

Since $\sum_{k=1}^\infty 1/k^4 < \infty$, by the first Borel-Cantelli lemma:

$$P(|\bar{X}_{k^2} - \mu| > \varepsilon \text{ i.o.}) = 0$$

for every $\varepsilon > 0$. Taking $\varepsilon = 1/m$ and a countable intersection: $\bar{X}_{k^2} \xrightarrow{a.s.} \mu$.

Step 3: Extension to all $n$. For $k^2 \leq n < (k+1)^2$:

$$\bar{X}_n - \bar{X}_{k^2} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i - \frac{1}{k^2}\sum_{i=1}^{k^2} X_i$$

Writing $\bar{X}_n = \frac{k^2}{n}\bar{X}_{k^2} + \frac{n-k^2}{n}\bar{Y}$ where $\bar{Y}$ is the mean of $X_{k^2+1}, \ldots, X_n$:

$$|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \leq \frac{k^2}{n}|\bar{X}_{k^2} - \mu| + \frac{n-k^2}{n}|\bar{Y} - \mu|$$

Since $n - k^2 \leq 2k + 1$ and $n \geq k^2$, the ratio $(n-k^2)/n \leq (2k+1)/k^2 \to 0$. Combined with the fact that $\bar{Y}$ is bounded in probability by the WLLN, the interpolation error vanishes a.s. $\square$

Exercise 24: Multivariate Delta Method

Let $(X_1, Y_1), \ldots, (X_n, Y_n)$ be i.i.d. pairs with $E[X_i] = \mu_X$, $E[Y_i] = \mu_Y$, and covariance matrix $\Sigma$. Define the ratio estimator $R_n = \bar{X}_n / \bar{Y}_n$ for $\theta = \mu_X / \mu_Y$ (assuming $\mu_Y \neq 0$). Use the multivariate Delta Method to find the asymptotic distribution of $\sqrt{n}(R_n - \theta)$.

Show Solution

By the multivariate CLT:

$$\sqrt{n}\begin{pmatrix}\bar{X}_n - \mu_X \\ \bar{Y}_n - \mu_Y\end{pmatrix} \xrightarrow{d} N\!\left(\mathbf{0}, \Sigma\right), \quad \Sigma = \begin{pmatrix}\sigma_X^2 & \sigma_{XY} \\ \sigma_{XY} & \sigma_Y^2\end{pmatrix}$$

Let $g(x, y) = x/y$. The gradient is:

$$\nabla g(x, y) = \begin{pmatrix}1/y \\ -x/y^2\end{pmatrix}$$

At $(\mu_X, \mu_Y)$:

$$\nabla g(\mu_X, \mu_Y) = \begin{pmatrix}1/\mu_Y \\ -\mu_X/\mu_Y^2\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{\mu_Y}\begin{pmatrix}1 \\ -\theta\end{pmatrix}$$

By the multivariate Delta Method:

$$\sqrt{n}(R_n - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \nabla g^\top \Sigma \nabla g)$$

Computing the asymptotic variance:

$$\tau^2 = \nabla g^\top \Sigma \nabla g = \frac{1}{\mu_Y^2}\begin{pmatrix}1 & -\theta\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}\sigma_X^2 & \sigma_{XY} \\ \sigma_{XY} & \sigma_Y^2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1 \\ -\theta\end{pmatrix}$$ $$= \frac{1}{\mu_Y^2}\left(\sigma_X^2 - 2\theta\sigma_{XY} + \theta^2\sigma_Y^2\right)$$

Answer: $\sqrt{n}(R_n - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} N\!\left(0, \frac{\sigma_X^2 - 2\theta\sigma_{XY} + \theta^2\sigma_Y^2}{\mu_Y^2}\right)$

This is the standard result for ratio estimators, used extensively in survey sampling and econometrics.

Exercise 25: CLT for Dependent Random Variables

Consider the AR(1) process $X_t = \phi X_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$ where $|\phi| < 1$ and $\varepsilon_t \sim N(0, \sigma_\varepsilon^2)$ are i.i.d. The stationary distribution has $E[X_t] = 0$ and $\text{Var}(X_t) = \sigma_\varepsilon^2/(1-\phi^2)$.

(a) Show that $\text{Cov}(X_t, X_{t+h}) = \phi^h \sigma_\varepsilon^2/(1-\phi^2)$.

(b) Prove that $\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^n X_t \xrightarrow{p} 0$.

(c) Find the asymptotic distribution of $\sqrt{n}\bar{X}_n$ and show that the asymptotic variance differs from $\sigma_X^2$ by the factor $(1+\phi)/(1-\phi)$.

Show Solution

(a) From the AR(1) recursion: $X_{t+h} = \phi^h X_t + \sum_{j=0}^{h-1}\phi^j \varepsilon_{t+h-j}$.

$$\text{Cov}(X_t, X_{t+h}) = E[X_t X_{t+h}] = E\!\left[X_t\!\left(\phi^h X_t + \sum_{j=0}^{h-1}\phi^j \varepsilon_{t+h-j}\right)\right]$$

Since $X_t$ is independent of $\varepsilon_{t+1}, \ldots, \varepsilon_{t+h}$:

$$= \phi^h E[X_t^2] = \phi^h \cdot \frac{\sigma_\varepsilon^2}{1-\phi^2} \quad \square$$

(b) Compute $\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n)$:

$$\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{s=1}^n\sum_{t=1}^n \text{Cov}(X_s, X_t) = \frac{\sigma_X^2}{n^2}\sum_{s=1}^n\sum_{t=1}^n \phi^{|s-t|}$$

The double sum equals $\sum_{h=-(n-1)}^{n-1}(n-|h|)\phi^{|h|}$. Since $|\phi| < 1$:

$$\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \frac{\sigma_X^2}{n}\left(\sum_{h=-\infty}^{\infty}\phi^{|h|} + O(1/n)\right) \to 0$$

Since $E[\bar{X}_n] = 0$ and $\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) \to 0$, by Chebyshev: $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{p} 0$. $\square$

(c) The key quantity is the long-run variance:

$$\sigma_{\text{LR}}^2 = \sum_{h=-\infty}^{\infty}\text{Cov}(X_0, X_h) = \sigma_X^2 \sum_{h=-\infty}^{\infty}\phi^{|h|} = \sigma_X^2 \cdot \frac{1+\phi}{1-\phi}$$

where we used $\sum_{h=-\infty}^{\infty}\phi^{|h|} = \frac{1+\phi}{1-\phi}$ for $|\phi| < 1$.

By the CLT for stationary mixing sequences:

$$\sqrt{n}\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma_{\text{LR}}^2) = N\!\left(0, \frac{\sigma_\varepsilon^2}{(1-\phi)^2}\right)$$

Compared to the i.i.d. case (which would give variance $\sigma_X^2 = \sigma_\varepsilon^2/(1-\phi^2)$), the asymptotic variance is multiplied by:

$$\frac{\sigma_{\text{LR}}^2}{\sigma_X^2} = \frac{1+\phi}{1-\phi}$$

For positive autocorrelation ($\phi > 0$), this factor exceeds 1, meaning $\bar{X}_n$ is less precise than in the i.i.d. case. For $\phi = 0.9$, the factor is $19$—you need 19 times as many dependent observations to achieve the same precision as independent ones! $\square$

Exercise 26: Second-Order Delta Method

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$. Suppose $g$ is twice differentiable with $g'(\mu) = 0$ and $g''(\mu) \neq 0$. Show that the usual (first-order) Delta Method fails and prove instead that:

$$n(g(\bar{X}_n) - g(\mu)) \xrightarrow{d} \frac{g''(\mu)\sigma^2}{2} \chi^2(1)$$
Show Solution

Why the first-order method fails: The first-order Delta Method gives $\sqrt{n}(g(\bar{X}_n) - g(\mu)) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2 [g'(\mu)]^2)$. When $g'(\mu) = 0$, this yields $\sqrt{n}(g(\bar{X}_n) - g(\mu)) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 0) = 0$, a degenerate limit that provides no distributional information.

Second-order expansion: By Taylor's theorem:

$$g(\bar{X}_n) = g(\mu) + g'(\mu)(\bar{X}_n - \mu) + \frac{g''(\mu)}{2}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)^2 + R_n$$

Since $g'(\mu) = 0$:

$$g(\bar{X}_n) - g(\mu) = \frac{g''(\mu)}{2}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)^2 + R_n$$

Scaling: Multiply both sides by $n$:

$$n(g(\bar{X}_n) - g(\mu)) = \frac{g''(\mu)}{2} \cdot n(\bar{X}_n - \mu)^2 + nR_n$$

Main term: By the CLT, $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)/\sigma \xrightarrow{d} Z \sim N(0,1)$, so:

$$n(\bar{X}_n - \mu)^2 = \sigma^2 \left(\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma}\right)^2 \xrightarrow{d} \sigma^2 Z^2 = \sigma^2 \chi^2(1)$$

Remainder: $R_n = O(|\bar{X}_n - \mu|^3)$, so $nR_n = O(n \cdot n^{-3/2}) = O(n^{-1/2}) = o_p(1)$.

Combining: By Slutsky's theorem:

$$n(g(\bar{X}_n) - g(\mu)) \xrightarrow{d} \frac{g''(\mu)\sigma^2}{2}\chi^2(1) \quad \square$$

Example: If $X_i \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ and $g(x) = (x - \mu)^2$, then $g'(\mu) = 0$ and $g''(\mu) = 2$. So $n \cdot (\bar{X}_n - \mu)^2 \xrightarrow{d} \sigma^2 \chi^2(1)$.

Exercise 27: Proving the WLLN via Characteristic Functions

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with mean $\mu$ and finite variance $\sigma^2$. Using the fact that the characteristic function of $X_i$ satisfies $\varphi_X(t) = 1 + i\mu t - \frac{\sigma^2 + \mu^2}{2}t^2 + o(t^2)$ as $t \to 0$, prove the Weak Law of Large Numbers by showing $\varphi_{\bar{X}_n}(t) \to e^{i\mu t}$.

Show Solution

Step 1: CF of the sample mean. Since $\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ and the $X_i$ are i.i.d.:

$$\varphi_{\bar{X}_n}(t) = E[e^{it\bar{X}_n}] = E\!\left[\prod_{i=1}^n e^{itX_i/n}\right] = \left[\varphi_X(t/n)\right]^n$$

Step 2: Taylor expansion. For $t/n$ near 0:

$$\varphi_X(t/n) = 1 + i\mu \frac{t}{n} - \frac{\sigma^2 + \mu^2}{2}\frac{t^2}{n^2} + o(t^2/n^2)$$ $$= 1 + \frac{i\mu t}{n} + O(1/n^2)$$

Step 3: Take the $n$-th power. Using the fact that $(1 + a_n/n)^n \to e^{a}$ when $a_n \to a$:

$$\left[\varphi_X(t/n)\right]^n = \left[1 + \frac{i\mu t}{n} + O(1/n^2)\right]^n$$

More precisely, write $\varphi_X(t/n) = 1 + \frac{i\mu t}{n} + \frac{r_n}{n}$ where $r_n = O(1/n)$. Then:

$$\log\left[\varphi_X(t/n)\right] = \frac{i\mu t}{n} + \frac{r_n}{n} + O(1/n^2)$$ $$n \log\left[\varphi_X(t/n)\right] = i\mu t + r_n + O(1/n) \to i\mu t$$

Therefore:

$$\varphi_{\bar{X}_n}(t) = \left[\varphi_X(t/n)\right]^n \to e^{i\mu t}$$

Step 4: Conclude. The function $e^{i\mu t}$ is the characteristic function of the constant $\mu$ (i.e., $P(X = \mu) = 1$). By Lévy's continuity theorem, convergence of characteristic functions to a CF implies convergence in distribution:

$$\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{d} \mu$$

Since the limit is a constant, convergence in distribution implies convergence in probability (Exercise 6):

$$\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{p} \mu \quad \square$$

Exercise 28: CLT for the Sample Variance

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_i] = \mu$, $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2$, and $E[(X_i - \mu)^4] = \mu_4 < \infty$. Define the sample variance $S_n^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2$. Find the asymptotic distribution of $\sqrt{n}(S_n^2 - \sigma^2)$.

Show Solution

Step 1: Write $S_n^2 = \frac{n}{n-1}\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum X_i^2 - \bar{X}_n^2\right)$. Since $n/(n-1) \to 1$, by Slutsky it suffices to find the asymptotic distribution of $\sqrt{n}\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum(X_i - \mu)^2 - \sigma^2\right)$.

Step 2: Let $Y_i = (X_i - \mu)^2$. Then $E[Y_i] = \sigma^2$ and:

$$\text{Var}(Y_i) = E[Y_i^2] - (E[Y_i])^2 = E[(X_i-\mu)^4] - \sigma^4 = \mu_4 - \sigma^4$$

Step 3: By the CLT applied to $Y_1, Y_2, \ldots$:

$$\sqrt{n}\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i-\mu)^2 - \sigma^2\right) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \mu_4 - \sigma^4)$$

Step 4: Show the correction from $\mu$ to $\bar{X}_n$ is negligible. Write:

$$\frac{1}{n}\sum(X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum(X_i-\mu)^2 - (\bar{X}_n - \mu)^2$$

We have $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)^2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\left[\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)\right]^2 = O_p(1/\sqrt{n}) = o_p(1)$.

Step 5: Also $\sqrt{n}(S_n^2 - \frac{n}{n-1}\hat{\sigma}^2) = \sqrt{n} \cdot \frac{1}{n-1}\hat{\sigma}^2 = O_p(1/\sqrt{n}) = o_p(1)$ where $\hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum(X_i-\bar{X}_n)^2$.

Result:

$$\sqrt{n}(S_n^2 - \sigma^2) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \mu_4 - \sigma^4)$$

Special case: If $X_i \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, then $\mu_4 = 3\sigma^4$, so the asymptotic variance is $3\sigma^4 - \sigma^4 = 2\sigma^4$, giving $\sqrt{n}(S_n^2 - \sigma^2) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 2\sigma^4)$.

Exercise 29: Convergence Rate of the Maximum

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. $\text{Uniform}(0, \theta)$ and let $M_n = \max(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$.

(a) Show that $M_n \xrightarrow{p} \theta$.

(b) Find the exact distribution of $n(\theta - M_n)$ and show it converges in distribution to $\text{Exponential}(1/\theta)$.

(c) Explain why the CLT does not apply to $M_n$ and identify the relevant convergence rate.

Show Solution

(a) The CDF of $M_n$ is:

$$P(M_n \leq x) = P(\text{all } X_i \leq x) = (x/\theta)^n \quad \text{for } 0 \leq x \leq \theta$$

For any $\varepsilon > 0$:

$$P(|M_n - \theta| > \varepsilon) = P(M_n < \theta - \varepsilon) = \left(\frac{\theta - \varepsilon}{\theta}\right)^n = \left(1 - \frac{\varepsilon}{\theta}\right)^n \to 0$$

So $M_n \xrightarrow{p} \theta$. $\square$

(b) Let $W_n = n(\theta - M_n)$. For $w > 0$:

$$P(W_n > w) = P(M_n < \theta - w/n) = \left(\frac{\theta - w/n}{\theta}\right)^n = \left(1 - \frac{w}{n\theta}\right)^n$$

As $n \to \infty$:

$$\left(1 - \frac{w}{n\theta}\right)^n \to e^{-w/\theta}$$

Therefore $P(W_n \leq w) \to 1 - e^{-w/\theta}$, which is the CDF of $\text{Exponential}(1/\theta)$.

$$n(\theta - M_n) \xrightarrow{d} \text{Exponential}(1/\theta) \quad \square$$

(c) The CLT applies to sums (or averages) of i.i.d. random variables and gives a $\sqrt{n}$ convergence rate. The maximum $M_n$ is not a sum; it is an extreme value statistic. The convergence rate is $n$ (not $\sqrt{n}$), which is much faster. This is because the maximum of uniform random variables concentrates at rate $1/n$ around $\theta$, not at rate $1/\sqrt{n}$. The limiting distribution is exponential (a type of extreme value distribution for bounded distributions), not Gaussian. The appropriate framework is extreme value theory (Fisher-Tippett-Gnedenko theorem), not the CLT.

Exercise 30: CLT Proof via Moment Generating Functions

Prove the Central Limit Theorem for i.i.d. random variables with finite MGF. That is, let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_i] = 0$, $\text{Var}(X_i) = 1$, and $M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}] < \infty$ for $|t| < t_0$. Prove that $Z_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$ by showing $M_{Z_n}(t) \to e^{t^2/2}$.

Show Solution

Step 1: MGF of $Z_n$.

$$M_{Z_n}(t) = E\!\left[\exp\!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right)\right] = \prod_{i=1}^n E\!\left[e^{tX_i/\sqrt{n}}\right] = \left[M_X(t/\sqrt{n})\right]^n$$

Step 2: Taylor expansion of $M_X$. Since $E[X] = 0$ and $E[X^2] = 1$:

$$M_X(s) = 1 + sE[X] + \frac{s^2}{2}E[X^2] + O(s^3) = 1 + \frac{s^2}{2} + O(s^3)$$

Substituting $s = t/\sqrt{n}$:

$$M_X(t/\sqrt{n}) = 1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2})$$

Step 3: Take the $n$-th power via logarithm.

$$\log M_{Z_n}(t) = n \log M_X(t/\sqrt{n}) = n \log\!\left(1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2})\right)$$

Using $\log(1 + u) = u - u^2/2 + O(u^3)$ for small $u$:

$$= n\!\left(\frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2}) - \frac{1}{2}\!\left(\frac{t^2}{2n}\right)^{\!2} + \cdots\right)$$ $$= \frac{t^2}{2} + O(n^{-1/2}) - \frac{t^4}{8n} + \cdots$$ $$\to \frac{t^2}{2} \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

Step 4: Conclude.

$$M_{Z_n}(t) \to e^{t^2/2}$$

The function $e^{t^2/2}$ is the MGF of $N(0,1)$, and it is finite in a neighborhood of 0. By the MGF convergence theorem (if $M_{X_n}(t) \to M_X(t)$ for all $t$ in a neighborhood of 0 and $M_X$ is the MGF of some distribution, then $X_n \xrightarrow{d} X$):

$$Z_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1) \quad \square$$

Remark: This proof requires the MGF to exist, which is stronger than the minimal assumption ($\sigma^2 < \infty$). The general proof uses characteristic functions (which always exist) and the same Taylor expansion strategy with $\varphi_X(t) = 1 - t^2/2 + o(t^2)$.

Key Concept

Limit theorems explain why averages are well-behaved and why the normal distribution appears everywhere. The LLN says averages converge; the CLT says the fluctuations are Gaussian.

Prerequisites

  • Expectation and variance
  • Probability inequalities (Markov, Chebyshev)
  • Moment generating functions
  • Basic real analysis (limits, series)
