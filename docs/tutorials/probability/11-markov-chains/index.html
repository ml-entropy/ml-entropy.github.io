11. Markov Chains

A Markov chain is a stochastic process whose future depends only on the present, never on the past. This deceptively simple idea—the Markov property—gives rise to an extraordinarily rich theory with deep connections to linear algebra, graph theory, and modern machine learning. From Google's PageRank algorithm to MCMC sampling in Bayesian inference, Markov chains are everywhere.

What is a Markov Chain?

A discrete-time Markov chain (DTMC) is a sequence of random variables $X_0, X_1, X_2, \ldots$ taking values in a countable state space $S$ such that the Markov property holds:

$$\boxed{P(X_{n+1} = j \mid X_n = i, X_{n-1} = i_{n-1}, \ldots, X_0 = i_0) = P(X_{n+1} = j \mid X_n = i)}$$

In words: given the present state, the future is independent of the past. The entire history of the process is summarized in the current state. This is sometimes called the memoryless property or the property of having no memory.

We write $p_{ij} = P(X_{n+1} = j \mid X_n = i)$ for the transition probability from state $i$ to state $j$. When these probabilities do not depend on the time step $n$, we say the chain is time-homogeneous—the case we focus on throughout this tutorial.

Why is the Markov property so powerful? Because it means the entire stochastic dynamics are captured by a single matrix of transition probabilities. Instead of tracking an exponentially growing tree of histories, we only need to know where we are right now.

Examples of Markov Chains

  • Random walk on the integers: At each step, move right with probability $p$ or left with probability $1 - p$. The current position is the state; where you go next depends only on where you are.
  • Weather model: If today is sunny, tomorrow is sunny with probability 0.8 and rainy with probability 0.2. If today is rainy, tomorrow is sunny with probability 0.4 and rainy with probability 0.6. The weather tomorrow depends only on today.
  • PageRank: A random surfer clicks links on the web. At each page, the surfer randomly follows one of the outgoing links. The next page depends only on the current page.
  • Board games: In Snakes and Ladders or Monopoly, your next position depends only on your current position and the dice roll—not how you got there.
  • Nucleotide sequences: In a simple DNA model, the next base (A, C, G, T) depends probabilistically on the current base.

What is NOT a Markov Chain?

A process where the future depends on more than just the present state is not Markov. For example, if tomorrow's weather depends on both today's and yesterday's weather, the raw process $(X_n)$ is not Markov. However, we can often enlarge the state space: define $Y_n = (X_{n-1}, X_n)$ as pairs of consecutive days; then $(Y_n)$ is Markov. This trick of state augmentation is widely used in practice.

Transition Matrix

The transition probabilities of a Markov chain with finite state space $S = \{1, 2, \ldots, N\}$ are organized into a transition matrix (or stochastic matrix):

$$P = \begin{pmatrix} p_{11} & p_{12} & \cdots & p_{1N} \\ p_{21} & p_{22} & \cdots & p_{2N} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ p_{N1} & p_{N2} & \cdots & p_{NN} \end{pmatrix}$$

where $p_{ij} = P(X_{n+1} = j \mid X_n = i) \geq 0$ and each row sums to 1:

$$\sum_{j=1}^{N} p_{ij} = 1 \quad \text{for all } i$$

A matrix with non-negative entries and rows summing to 1 is called a row-stochastic matrix. (Some references use column-stochastic matrices instead; be careful about conventions.)

Example (Weather): With states $S = \{\text{Sunny}, \text{Rainy}\}$:

$$P = \begin{pmatrix} 0.8 & 0.2 \\ 0.4 & 0.6 \end{pmatrix}$$

Row 1 says: if today is sunny, there is 0.8 probability of sunny tomorrow and 0.2 probability of rainy tomorrow.

$n$-Step Transition Probabilities

What is the probability of going from state $i$ to state $j$ in exactly $n$ steps? We write $p_{ij}^{(n)} = P(X_{m+n} = j \mid X_m = i)$. The key result is:

$$\boxed{p_{ij}^{(n)} = (P^n)_{ij}}$$

The $n$-step transition probabilities are given by the $(i,j)$ entry of the matrix $P$ raised to the $n$-th power. This is a direct consequence of the Markov property and the law of total probability.

Chapman–Kolmogorov Equations

The Chapman–Kolmogorov equations express this matrix multiplication in summation form. For any $0 \leq m \leq n$:

$$p_{ij}^{(n)} = \sum_{k \in S} p_{ik}^{(m)} \, p_{kj}^{(n-m)}$$

In matrix notation: $P^{(n)} = P^{(m)} \cdot P^{(n-m)}$, i.e., $P^n = P^m \cdot P^{n-m}$. This is simply the associativity of matrix multiplication, but it has a beautiful probabilistic interpretation: to go from $i$ to $j$ in $n$ steps, we sum over all possible intermediate states $k$ at time $m$, multiplying the probability of reaching $k$ in $m$ steps by the probability of going from $k$ to $j$ in $n - m$ steps.

Distribution at time $n$: If $\boldsymbol{\mu}_0 = (\mu_0(1), \ldots, \mu_0(N))$ is the initial distribution (a row vector), then the distribution at time $n$ is:

$$\boldsymbol{\mu}_n = \boldsymbol{\mu}_0 \, P^n$$

State Diagrams

A Markov chain can be visualized as a directed weighted graph (a state transition diagram). Each state is a node, and each transition probability $p_{ij} > 0$ is a directed edge from $i$ to $j$ labeled with the probability.

Reading a state diagram: (1) each node is a state; (2) the arrows leaving a node represent possible transitions with their probabilities; (3) all arrow weights leaving a node must sum to 1; (4) a self-loop means the chain can stay in the same state.

Classification of States

Understanding the long-run behavior of a Markov chain requires classifying its states. Two fundamental concepts are communication and recurrence.

Accessibility and Communication

State $j$ is accessible from state $i$ (written $i \to j$) if there exists $n \geq 0$ such that $p_{ij}^{(n)} > 0$—that is, there is a positive probability of reaching $j$ from $i$ in some number of steps.

Two states $i$ and $j$ communicate (written $i \leftrightarrow j$) if $i \to j$ and $j \to i$. Communication is an equivalence relation: it is reflexive ($i \leftrightarrow i$), symmetric (by definition), and transitive (if $i \leftrightarrow j$ and $j \leftrightarrow k$, then $i \leftrightarrow k$).

The equivalence classes under communication are called communicating classes.

Irreducibility

A Markov chain is irreducible if there is only one communicating class—that is, every state communicates with every other state. In graph terms, the transition diagram is strongly connected: you can get from any state to any other state.

Irreducibility is a crucial property: it means the chain can explore the entire state space, no matter where it starts. Most of the elegant theory (stationary distributions, ergodic theorems) requires irreducibility.

Recurrence and Transience

Let $f_i = P(\text{chain ever returns to } i \mid X_0 = i)$ be the probability of returning to state $i$ starting from $i$.

  • Recurrent: $f_i = 1$. Starting from $i$, the chain is guaranteed to return to $i$ (and hence returns infinitely often).
  • Transient: $f_i < 1$. There is a positive probability of never returning to $i$. Starting from $i$, the expected number of returns is finite: $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} p_{ii}^{(n)} < \infty$.

Criterion: State $i$ is recurrent if and only if:

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} p_{ii}^{(n)} = \infty$$

and transient if this sum is finite. Intuitively, if the chain visits $i$ infinitely often in expectation, it must be recurrent.

Key fact: Recurrence (and transience) is a class property. If one state in a communicating class is recurrent, then all states in that class are recurrent. Similarly for transience.

Positive Recurrence vs. Null Recurrence

For a recurrent state $i$, let $m_i = E[\text{return time to } i \mid X_0 = i]$ be the mean return time. A recurrent state is:

  • Positive recurrent: $m_i < \infty$. The chain returns to $i$ in finite expected time.
  • Null recurrent: $m_i = \infty$. The chain is certain to return, but the expected time is infinite.

For finite state spaces, all recurrent states are positive recurrent (null recurrence only occurs in infinite state spaces). An irreducible chain on a finite state space is always positive recurrent.

Periodicity

The period of a state $i$ is:

$$d(i) = \gcd\{n \geq 1 : p_{ii}^{(n)} > 0\}$$

That is, $d(i)$ is the greatest common divisor of all return times that have positive probability. If $d(i) = 1$, the state is aperiodic; if $d(i) > 1$, the state is periodic with period $d(i)$.

Examples:

  • Aperiodic: The weather chain above is aperiodic because each state has a self-loop ($p_{ii} > 0$), so $p_{ii}^{(1)} > 0$ and therefore $\gcd$ includes 1.
  • Period 2: A simple random walk on $\{0, 1, 2\}$ where you always move to an adjacent state has period 2—you can only return to your starting state in an even number of steps.

Key facts about periodicity:

  • Periodicity is a class property: all states in a communicating class share the same period.
  • If any state has a self-loop ($p_{ii} > 0$), the chain is aperiodic.
  • An irreducible chain is called aperiodic if all its states are aperiodic (equivalently, if any one state is aperiodic).

Why does periodicity matter? A periodic chain cannot converge to a stationary distribution in the usual sense: the distribution oscillates with period $d$. The fundamental convergence theorem requires aperiodicity.

Stationary Distributions

A probability distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi} = (\pi_1, \pi_2, \ldots, \pi_N)$ is a stationary distribution (or steady-state distribution, or invariant distribution) of the Markov chain if:

$$\boxed{\boldsymbol{\pi} P = \boldsymbol{\pi} \quad \text{with} \quad \sum_{i} \pi_i = 1, \;\; \pi_i \geq 0}$$

In other words, $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ is a left eigenvector of $P$ with eigenvalue 1. If the chain starts in the stationary distribution ($\boldsymbol{\mu}_0 = \boldsymbol{\pi}$), then $\boldsymbol{\mu}_n = \boldsymbol{\pi} P^n = \boldsymbol{\pi}$ for all $n$—the distribution never changes. This is why it is called "stationary."

In component form, the stationarity equation reads:

$$\pi_j = \sum_{i \in S} \pi_i \, p_{ij} \quad \text{for all } j \in S$$

This says: the probability of being in state $j$ at equilibrium equals the total probability flowing into $j$ from all states (including $j$ itself).

Existence and Uniqueness

The following results guide when a stationary distribution exists and is unique:

Condition Existence Uniqueness
Finite state space Always exists (at least one) Not necessarily unique
Irreducible, positive recurrent Exists Unique
Irreducible, finite Exists Unique
Irreducible, null recurrent Does not exist N/A

When $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ is unique, it is related to mean return times: $\pi_i = 1 / m_i$, where $m_i$ is the expected return time to state $i$. States visited more frequently have larger stationary probabilities.

Example: For the 2-state weather chain $P = \begin{pmatrix} 0.8 & 0.2 \\ 0.4 & 0.6 \end{pmatrix}$, we solve $\boldsymbol{\pi} P = \boldsymbol{\pi}$:

$$\pi_1 = 0.8\pi_1 + 0.4\pi_2, \quad \pi_2 = 0.2\pi_1 + 0.6\pi_2$$

From the first equation: $0.2\pi_1 = 0.4\pi_2$, so $\pi_1 = 2\pi_2$. With the constraint $\pi_1 + \pi_2 = 1$:

$$\boldsymbol{\pi} = \left(\frac{2}{3}, \frac{1}{3}\right)$$

In the long run, the weather is sunny about 2/3 of the time and rainy about 1/3 of the time.

Detailed Balance

A distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ satisfies the detailed balance equations (also called reversibility) with respect to $P$ if:

$$\boxed{\pi_i \, p_{ij} = \pi_j \, p_{ji} \quad \text{for all } i, j}$$

This says: the probability flow from $i$ to $j$ equals the flow from $j$ to $i$ at equilibrium. It is like saying every microscopic process is in balance with its reverse.

Key result: If $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ satisfies detailed balance with $P$, then $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ is a stationary distribution of $P$.

Proof: Summing both sides of $\pi_i p_{ij} = \pi_j p_{ji}$ over $i$:

$$\sum_i \pi_i \, p_{ij} = \sum_i \pi_j \, p_{ji} = \pi_j \sum_i p_{ji} = \pi_j \cdot 1 = \pi_j$$

which is exactly $\boldsymbol{\pi} P = \boldsymbol{\pi}$. $\square$

The converse is not true: a stationary distribution may exist without satisfying detailed balance. A chain satisfying detailed balance is called reversible because the chain run backwards in time has the same transition probabilities.

Why is detailed balance useful?

  • It is often easier to verify than the full stationarity equation, because we only need to check pairwise conditions instead of solving a system.
  • It is the foundation of MCMC algorithms like Metropolis-Hastings: we design a chain that satisfies detailed balance with respect to a target distribution, thereby guaranteeing it converges to that distribution.

Example: For the 2-state weather chain, check detailed balance with $\boldsymbol{\pi} = (2/3, 1/3)$:

  • $\pi_1 p_{12} = (2/3)(0.2) = 2/15$
  • $\pi_2 p_{21} = (1/3)(0.4) = 2/15$ ✓

The 2-state weather chain is reversible.

Ergodic Theorem

The ergodic theorem is the crown jewel of Markov chain theory. It tells us that for "well-behaved" chains, time averages converge to ensemble averages.

A Markov chain is called ergodic if it is irreducible, aperiodic, and positive recurrent. (For finite state spaces, irreducible + aperiodic is sufficient, since positive recurrence is automatic.)

Ergodic Theorem (Convergence Theorem)

$$\text{If the chain is ergodic with stationary distribution } \boldsymbol{\pi}, \text{ then:}$$ $$\boxed{\lim_{n \to \infty} p_{ij}^{(n)} = \pi_j \quad \text{for all } i, j}$$ $$\text{and for any function } f: S \to \mathbb{R},$$ $$\boxed{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f(X_k) \xrightarrow{a.s.} \sum_{j \in S} f(j) \, \pi_j = E_\pi[f]}$$

This theorem has two profound parts:

  1. Convergence to stationarity: No matter where the chain starts, the distribution at time $n$ converges to $\boldsymbol{\pi}$. The chain "forgets" its initial condition.
  2. Time averages = space averages: The long-run fraction of time spent in state $j$ equals $\pi_j$. More generally, the time average of any function along the chain converges to its expectation under $\boldsymbol{\pi}$.

Figure 3. Starting from Sunny (probability 1), the distribution converges to the stationary distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ (dashed lines) within about 6–8 steps.

Rate of convergence: The speed at which $P^n$ converges to $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ is governed by the spectral gap $\gamma = 1 - |\lambda_2|$, where $\lambda_2$ is the second largest eigenvalue of $P$ in absolute value. The total variation distance decreases as:

$$\|P^n(i, \cdot) - \boldsymbol{\pi}\|_{TV} \leq C \, |\lambda_2|^n$$

A larger spectral gap means faster convergence (faster "mixing").

Absorption Probabilities

An absorbing state is a state $a$ with $p_{aa} = 1$: once the chain enters $a$, it stays there forever. A chain with at least one absorbing state and where every state can reach some absorbing state is called an absorbing chain.

For an absorbing chain, we often want to compute:

  1. The absorption probabilities: starting from transient state $i$, what is the probability of being absorbed into absorbing state $a$?
  2. The expected time to absorption: how many steps on average before the chain reaches an absorbing state?

The Fundamental Matrix

Reorder the states so that transient states come first, then absorbing states. The transition matrix takes the canonical form:

$$P = \begin{pmatrix} Q & R \\ \mathbf{0} & I \end{pmatrix}$$

where $Q$ is the $t \times t$ matrix of transitions among transient states, $R$ gives transitions from transient to absorbing states, $I$ is the identity (absorbing states stay), and $\mathbf{0}$ shows absorbing states cannot reach transient states.

The fundamental matrix is:

$$N = (I - Q)^{-1} = I + Q + Q^2 + Q^3 + \cdots$$

The entry $N_{ij}$ gives the expected number of times the chain visits transient state $j$ starting from transient state $i$ (before absorption).

  • Expected absorption time from state $i$: $t_i = \sum_j N_{ij}$ (sum of row $i$ of $N$), or in vector form: $\mathbf{t} = N \mathbf{1}$.
  • Absorption probabilities: $B = N R$, where $B_{ia}$ is the probability of being absorbed into state $a$ starting from transient state $i$.

The Gambler's Ruin Problem

A classic application: a gambler starts with $\$i$ and plays a fair game, winning or losing $\$1$ each round. The game ends when the gambler reaches $\$0$ (ruin) or $\$N$ (target). States 0 and $N$ are absorbing.

Let $r_i$ = probability of ruin starting from $\$i$. By conditioning on the first step:

$$r_i = p \, r_{i+1} + (1-p) \, r_{i-1}, \quad r_0 = 1, \quad r_N = 0$$

For a fair game ($p = 1/2$): $r_i = 1 - i/N$. The gambler with $\$1$ facing a casino with $\$999{,}999$ has a 99.9999% chance of ruin.

For an unfair game ($p \neq 1/2$): $r_i = \frac{(q/p)^i - (q/p)^N}{1 - (q/p)^N}$ where $q = 1-p$.

Markov Chains and Linear Algebra

The connection between Markov chains and linear algebra is deep and beautiful. Every transition matrix is a linear operator, and eigenvalue analysis reveals the chain's long-term behavior.

Eigenvalue Structure of Stochastic Matrices

Every (row-)stochastic matrix $P$ has the following eigenvalue properties:

  1. $\lambda_1 = 1$ is always an eigenvalue. The all-ones vector $\mathbf{1}$ is a right eigenvector: $P\mathbf{1} = \mathbf{1}$ (because rows sum to 1). The stationary distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ is a left eigenvector: $\boldsymbol{\pi}P = \boldsymbol{\pi}$.
  2. All eigenvalues satisfy $|\lambda| \leq 1$. This follows from the Gershgorin circle theorem or from the fact that $P$ maps probability distributions to probability distributions.
  3. If the chain is irreducible and aperiodic, then $\lambda_1 = 1$ is the unique eigenvalue of magnitude 1.

Perron–Frobenius Theorem

The Perron–Frobenius theorem (for non-negative matrices) guarantees that an irreducible stochastic matrix has:

  • A unique eigenvalue of maximum modulus, equal to 1 (the Perron root).
  • A corresponding positive left eigenvector (the stationary distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi} > 0$).
  • If the matrix is also aperiodic (i.e., primitive), then all other eigenvalues satisfy $|\lambda| < 1$, and $P^n \to \mathbf{1}^\top \boldsymbol{\pi}$ geometrically.

Spectral Gap and Mixing Time

The spectral gap is $\gamma = 1 - |\lambda_2|$ where $\lambda_2$ is the second-largest eigenvalue in absolute value. The mixing time $t_{\text{mix}}(\varepsilon)$ is the time needed for the chain to be within total variation distance $\varepsilon$ of stationarity:

$$t_{\text{mix}}(\varepsilon) \approx \frac{\log(1/\varepsilon)}{1 - |\lambda_2|} = \frac{\log(1/\varepsilon)}{\gamma}$$

Chains with a large spectral gap mix quickly (converge fast); chains with $|\lambda_2| \approx 1$ mix slowly. This is directly relevant to MCMC: a slowly mixing chain requires very long runs to produce reliable samples.

Spectral Decomposition

If $P$ is diagonalizable with eigenvalues $1 = \lambda_1 > |\lambda_2| \geq \ldots \geq |\lambda_N|$, left eigenvectors $\boldsymbol{v}_k$, and right eigenvectors $\boldsymbol{u}_k$, then:

$$P^n = \sum_{k=1}^{N} \lambda_k^n \, \boldsymbol{u}_k \boldsymbol{v}_k^\top$$

As $n \to \infty$, only the $k=1$ term survives (since $|\lambda_k| < 1$ for $k \geq 2$): $P^n \to \boldsymbol{u}_1 \boldsymbol{v}_1^\top = \mathbf{1} \boldsymbol{\pi}^\top$. The convergence rate is $O(|\lambda_2|^n)$.

Connections to ML

Markov chains are not just a theoretical curiosity—they are at the heart of several of the most important algorithms in machine learning and artificial intelligence.

Hidden Markov Models (HMMs)

An HMM augments a Markov chain with observations: the underlying state follows a Markov chain (hidden), and at each time step, we observe a noisy emission that depends on the current hidden state. HMMs are used in:

  • Speech recognition (phonemes as hidden states, acoustic features as observations)
  • Part-of-speech tagging in NLP (tags are hidden, words are observed)
  • Gene finding in computational biology (exon/intron states, nucleotide observations)

The three classic HMM algorithms—Forward (likelihood), Viterbi (decoding), and Baum-Welch (parameter estimation)—all exploit the Markov structure for efficient dynamic programming.

Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)

MCMC is the primary computational tool for Bayesian inference when closed-form solutions are intractable. The idea is simple and powerful:

  1. Design a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the target posterior $\pi(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \text{data})$.
  2. Run the chain long enough for it to converge (the "burn-in" period).
  3. Collect samples from the chain; by the ergodic theorem, time averages approximate posterior expectations.

The Metropolis-Hastings algorithm constructs such a chain by proposing moves and accepting or rejecting them to ensure detailed balance. Gibbs sampling is a special case where each variable is updated conditionally on all others.

PageRank Algorithm

Google's original PageRank algorithm models a "random surfer" on the web as a Markov chain. The surfer follows a random outgoing link with probability $\alpha$ and jumps to a random page with probability $1 - \alpha$ (the "teleportation" or "damping" factor, typically $\alpha = 0.85$). The stationary distribution of this chain gives the PageRank scores:

$$P = \alpha \, M + (1 - \alpha) \, \frac{1}{N} \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top$$

where $M$ is the normalized adjacency matrix of the web graph. The teleportation ensures irreducibility and aperiodicity, guaranteeing convergence to a unique stationary distribution.

Reinforcement Learning (MDPs)

A Markov Decision Process (MDP) extends a Markov chain by adding actions and rewards. At each state, the agent chooses an action that determines the transition probabilities to the next state, and receives a reward. The goal is to find a policy (mapping from states to actions) that maximizes expected cumulative reward.

Under a fixed policy, the MDP reduces to a Markov chain. Algorithms like value iteration, policy iteration, and Q-learning all rely on the Markov property for their correctness and efficiency.

Natural Language Processing

$n$-gram language models are Markov chains where the state is the last $(n-1)$ words. A bigram model is a first-order Markov chain: $P(\text{word}_{t+1} \mid \text{word}_t, \text{word}_{t-1}, \ldots) \approx P(\text{word}_{t+1} \mid \text{word}_t)$. While modern transformer-based models go far beyond this, the Markov assumption remains a fundamental baseline and conceptual building block.

1. Simulating a Markov Chain

import numpy as np

def simulate_markov_chain(P, initial_state, n_steps):
    """
    Simulate a discrete-time Markov chain.

    Parameters:
        P: Transition matrix (N x N numpy array, rows sum to 1)
        initial_state: Starting state index (0-based)
        n_steps: Number of steps to simulate

    Returns:
        states: List of visited states
    """
    n_states = P.shape[0]
    states = [initial_state]
    current = initial_state

    for _ in range(n_steps):
        # Sample next state from the transition probabilities
        current = np.random.choice(n_states, p=P[current])
        states.append(current)

    return states


def estimate_transition_matrix(states, n_states):
    """
    Estimate the transition matrix from an observed sequence of states.

    Parameters:
        states: List of observed state indices
        n_states: Number of states in the chain

    Returns:
        P_hat: Estimated transition matrix
    """
    counts = np.zeros((n_states, n_states))
    for i in range(len(states) - 1):
        counts[states[i], states[i + 1]] += 1

    # Normalize each row
    row_sums = counts.sum(axis=1, keepdims=True)
    row_sums[row_sums == 0] = 1  # avoid division by zero
    P_hat = counts / row_sums
    return P_hat

2. Stationary Distribution via Eigenvalue Decomposition

import numpy as np
from scipy import linalg

def stationary_distribution_eigen(P):
    """
    Compute the stationary distribution using eigenvalue decomposition.

    The stationary distribution pi satisfies pi @ P = pi,
    which means pi is a left eigenvector of P with eigenvalue 1.

    Equivalently, pi^T is a right eigenvector of P^T with eigenvalue 1.
    """
    # Find eigenvalues and right eigenvectors of P^T
    eigenvalues, eigenvectors = linalg.eig(P.T)

    # Find the eigenvector corresponding to eigenvalue 1
    # (eigenvalue closest to 1, due to floating-point)
    idx = np.argmin(np.abs(eigenvalues - 1.0))

    # Extract the eigenvector and normalize to a probability distribution
    pi = eigenvectors[:, idx].real
    pi = pi / pi.sum()
    return pi


def stationary_distribution_solve(P):
    """
    Compute stationary distribution by solving the linear system directly.

    We solve: pi @ P = pi, sum(pi) = 1
    Rewritten: pi @ (P - I) = 0, sum(pi) = 1
    """
    n = P.shape[0]
    # (P^T - I) @ pi^T = 0, replace one equation with sum = 1
    A = (P.T - np.eye(n))
    A[-1, :] = 1  # replace last equation with normalization
    b = np.zeros(n)
    b[-1] = 1
    pi = linalg.solve(A, b)
    return pi


def stationary_distribution_power(P, tol=1e-12, max_iter=1000):
    """
    Compute stationary distribution by repeated matrix multiplication (power method).
    """
    n = P.shape[0]
    pi = np.ones(n) / n  # uniform initial distribution
    for i in range(max_iter):
        pi_new = pi @ P
        if np.max(np.abs(pi_new - pi)) < tol:
            print(f"  Converged in {i + 1} iterations")
            return pi_new
        pi = pi_new
    print(f"  Warning: did not converge in {max_iter} iterations")
    return pi

3. PageRank Algorithm

import numpy as np

def pagerank(adjacency, alpha=0.85, tol=1e-10, max_iter=100):
    """
    Compute PageRank scores for a directed graph.

    Parameters:
        adjacency: Adjacency matrix (A[i,j] = 1 if page i links to page j)
        alpha: Damping factor (probability of following a link vs teleporting)
        tol: Convergence tolerance
        max_iter: Maximum number of iterations

    Returns:
        ranks: PageRank scores (stationary distribution of the random surfer)
    """
    n = adjacency.shape[0]

    # Build the column-normalized link matrix M
    # M[j,i] = 1/L(i) if page i links to page j, else 0
    # where L(i) = number of outgoing links from page i
    out_degree = adjacency.sum(axis=1)

    # Handle dangling nodes (pages with no outgoing links)
    dangling = (out_degree == 0)
    out_degree[dangling] = 1  # prevent division by zero

    # Row-normalized transition matrix
    M = adjacency / out_degree[:, np.newaxis]

    # For dangling nodes, distribute equally to all pages
    M[dangling] = 1.0 / n

    # Google matrix: G = alpha * M + (1 - alpha) * (1/n) * ones
    # Power iteration: pi = pi @ G
    pi = np.ones(n) / n

    for iteration in range(max_iter):
        pi_new = alpha * (pi @ M) + (1 - alpha) / n
        # Add contribution from dangling nodes
        # (already handled above in M)

        if np.max(np.abs(pi_new - pi)) < tol:
            print(f"Converged in {iteration + 1} iterations")
            return pi_new
        pi = pi_new

    print(f"Warning: did not converge in {max_iter} iterations")
    return pi

4. Gambler's Ruin Simulation

import numpy as np

def gamblers_ruin_simulation(initial_wealth, target, p=0.5, n_simulations=10000):
    """
    Simulate the gambler's ruin problem.

    Parameters:
        initial_wealth: Starting amount ($i)
        target: Target amount ($N) - game ends at 0 (ruin) or N (win)
        p: Probability of winning each round
        n_simulations: Number of independent games to simulate

    Returns:
        ruin_prob: Estimated probability of ruin
        avg_duration: Average number of rounds until game ends
    """
    ruin_count = 0
    total_duration = 0

    for _ in range(n_simulations):
        wealth = initial_wealth
        steps = 0
        while 0 < wealth < target:
            if np.random.random() < p:
                wealth += 1
            else:
                wealth -= 1
            steps += 1
        if wealth == 0:
            ruin_count += 1
        total_duration += steps

    ruin_prob = ruin_count / n_simulations
    avg_duration = total_duration / n_simulations
    return ruin_prob, avg_duration


def gamblers_ruin_exact(initial_wealth, target, p=0.5):
    """
    Compute exact ruin probability and expected duration analytically.
    """
    i, N = initial_wealth, target

    if p == 0.5:
        ruin_prob = 1 - i / N
        expected_duration = i * (N - i)
    else:
        q = 1 - p
        r = q / p
        ruin_prob = (r**i - r**N) / (1 - r**N)
        expected_duration = (i / (q - p)) - (N / (q - p)) * (1 - r**i) / (1 - r**N)

    return ruin_prob, expected_duration

5. Mixing Time Estimation and Convergence

import numpy as np
from scipy import linalg

def analyze_mixing(P, state_names=None):
    """
    Analyze mixing properties of a Markov chain:
    - Eigenvalues and spectral gap
    - Estimated mixing time
    - Convergence from different starting states
    """
    n = P.shape[0]
    if state_names is None:
        state_names = [f"State {i}" for i in range(n)]

    # Compute eigenvalues
    eigenvalues = linalg.eigvals(P)
    eigenvalues = np.sort(np.abs(eigenvalues))[::-1]  # sort by magnitude

    print("Eigenvalues (by magnitude):")
    for i, ev in enumerate(eigenvalues):
        print(f"  |lambda_{i+1}| = {ev:.6f}")

    # Spectral gap
    lambda_2 = eigenvalues[1]
    spectral_gap = 1 - lambda_2
    print(f"\nSpectral gap: gamma = 1 - |lambda_2| = {spectral_gap:.6f}")

    # Mixing time estimate
    for eps in [0.25, 0.1, 0.01]:
        t_mix = np.log(1 / eps) / spectral_gap if spectral_gap > 0 else float('inf')
        print(f"  Mixing time (eps={eps}): ~{t_mix:.1f} steps")

    # Compute stationary distribution
    evals, evecs = linalg.eig(P.T)
    idx = np.argmin(np.abs(evals - 1.0))
    pi = evecs[:, idx].real
    pi = pi / pi.sum()

    print(f"\nStationary distribution:")
    for name, prob in zip(state_names, pi):
        print(f"  pi({name}) = {prob:.6f}")

    # Track convergence from each starting state
    print("\nTotal variation distance from stationarity:")
    print(f"{'Step':>6}", end="")
    for name in state_names:
        print(f"  {name:>10}", end="")
    print()

    for step in [0, 1, 2, 3, 5, 10, 20, 50]:
        Pn = np.linalg.matrix_power(P, step)
        print(f"{step:>6}", end="")
        for i in range(n):
            # Total variation distance
            tv = 0.5 * np.sum(np.abs(Pn[i] - pi))
            print(f"  {tv:>10.6f}", end="")
        print()

    return pi, eigenvalues, spectral_gap

Exercise 7: Proving Recurrence of the Simple Random Walk

Consider a simple symmetric random walk on $\mathbb{Z}$: at each step, move right with probability $1/2$ or left with probability $1/2$. Show that state 0 is recurrent by computing $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} p_{00}^{(n)}$.

Show Solution

The walk can return to 0 only at even times. At time $2n$, the walk is at 0 if and only if it took exactly $n$ right steps and $n$ left steps out of $2n$ steps.

$$p_{00}^{(2n)} = \binom{2n}{n} \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{2n}$$

Using Stirling's approximation $n! \approx \sqrt{2\pi n}(n/e)^n$:

$$\binom{2n}{n} = \frac{(2n)!}{(n!)^2} \approx \frac{\sqrt{4\pi n}(2n/e)^{2n}}{2\pi n \cdot (n/e)^{2n}} = \frac{4^n}{\sqrt{\pi n}}$$

Therefore:

$$p_{00}^{(2n)} \approx \frac{4^n}{\sqrt{\pi n}} \cdot \frac{1}{4^n} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi n}}$$

Now summing:

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} p_{00}^{(n)} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} p_{00}^{(2n)} \approx \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi n}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}} \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} = \infty$$

since $\sum 1/\sqrt{n}$ diverges (it is a $p$-series with $p = 1/2 < 1$).

Answer: Since $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} p_{00}^{(n)} = \infty$, state 0 is recurrent. (In fact, it is null recurrent since $E[\text{return time}] = \infty$.) $\square$

Exercise 8: Detailed Balance Verification

Consider the "Ehrenfest model": $N$ molecules are divided between two containers. At each step, a random molecule is chosen and moved to the other container. The state is the number of molecules in container 1 ($k = 0, 1, \ldots, N$). The transition probabilities are $p_{k,k+1} = (N-k)/N$ and $p_{k,k-1} = k/N$. Show that the binomial distribution $\pi_k = \binom{N}{k}(1/2)^N$ satisfies detailed balance.

Show Solution

We need to check $\pi_k \, p_{k,k+1} = \pi_{k+1} \, p_{k+1,k}$ for all $k$.

Left side:

$$\pi_k \, p_{k,k+1} = \binom{N}{k}\frac{1}{2^N} \cdot \frac{N-k}{N}$$

Right side:

$$\pi_{k+1} \, p_{k+1,k} = \binom{N}{k+1}\frac{1}{2^N} \cdot \frac{k+1}{N}$$

We need to show these are equal. Using $\binom{N}{k+1} = \binom{N}{k} \cdot \frac{N-k}{k+1}$:

$$\pi_{k+1} \, p_{k+1,k} = \binom{N}{k} \cdot \frac{N-k}{k+1} \cdot \frac{1}{2^N} \cdot \frac{k+1}{N} = \binom{N}{k} \cdot \frac{1}{2^N} \cdot \frac{N-k}{N}$$

This equals $\pi_k \, p_{k,k+1}$. $\square$

Answer: Detailed balance holds, confirming that $\pi_k = \binom{N}{k}(1/2)^N$ is the stationary distribution. This makes physical sense: at equilibrium, each molecule is equally likely to be in either container, so the number in container 1 follows $\text{Binomial}(N, 1/2)$.

$$E_i[T] = i(N - i)$$

With $i = 5$, $N = 10$:

$$E_5[T] = 5 \times 5 = 25 \text{ rounds}$$

Derivation of expected duration: Let $d_i = E_i[T]$. Conditioning on the first step:

$$d_i = 1 + \frac{1}{2}d_{i-1} + \frac{1}{2}d_{i+1}, \quad d_0 = d_N = 0$$

Rearranging: $d_{i+1} - 2d_i + d_{i-1} = -2$. This is a second-order recurrence with solution $d_i = i(N - i)$.

Answer: Ruin probability = 50%, expected duration = 25 rounds.

Exercise 10: Stationary Distribution of a 3-State Chain

Find the stationary distribution for the transition matrix: $$P = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1/2 & 1/2 \\ 1/3 & 1/3 & 1/3 \\ 1/2 & 1/4 & 1/4 \end{pmatrix}$$ Is this chain irreducible? Is it aperiodic?

Show Solution

Irreducibility: From any state, we can reach any other state: $1 \to 2$, $2 \to 1$, $1 \to 3$, $3 \to 1$, $2 \to 3$, $3 \to 2$. Yes, the chain is irreducible.

Aperiodicity: $p_{22} = 1/3 > 0$ (state 2 has a self-loop), so the chain is aperiodic.

Stationary distribution: Solve $\boldsymbol{\pi} P = \boldsymbol{\pi}$ with $\pi_1 + \pi_2 + \pi_3 = 1$:

The equations $\pi_j = \sum_i \pi_i p_{ij}$:

$$\pi_1 = \frac{1}{3}\pi_2 + \frac{1}{2}\pi_3$$ $$\pi_2 = \frac{1}{2}\pi_1 + \frac{1}{3}\pi_2 + \frac{1}{4}\pi_3$$ $$\pi_3 = \frac{1}{2}\pi_1 + \frac{1}{3}\pi_2 + \frac{1}{4}\pi_3$$

From equations 2 and 3: $\pi_2 = \pi_3$ (they have identical right-hand sides). Call $\pi_2 = \pi_3 = x$.

From equation 1: $\pi_1 = \frac{1}{3}x + \frac{1}{2}x = \frac{5}{6}x$.

Normalization: $\frac{5}{6}x + x + x = 1 \implies \frac{17}{6}x = 1 \implies x = \frac{6}{17}$.

$$\boldsymbol{\pi} = \left(\frac{5}{17}, \frac{6}{17}, \frac{6}{17}\right) \approx (0.294, 0.353, 0.353)$$

Answer: The chain is irreducible and aperiodic with stationary distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi} = (5/17, 6/17, 6/17)$.

Exercise 11: Spectral Gap Analysis

For the "lazy" random walk on the complete graph $K_N$, at each step the walker stays with probability $1/2$ and moves to a uniformly random other vertex with probability $1/2$. (a) Write the transition matrix. (b) Find all eigenvalues. (c) Compute the spectral gap and the mixing time (to within total variation distance $\varepsilon$).

Show Solution

(a) The transition matrix is:

$$P = \frac{1}{2}I + \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{N-1}(J - I) = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 - \frac{1}{N-1}\right)I + \frac{1}{2(N-1)}J$$ $$= \frac{N-2}{2(N-1)}I + \frac{1}{2(N-1)}J$$

where $J$ is the all-ones matrix. In component form: $p_{ii} = 1/2$ and $p_{ij} = 1/(2(N-1))$ for $i \neq j$.

(b) Since $P$ is of the form $\alpha I + \beta J$ (with $\alpha = \frac{N-2}{2(N-1)}$, $\beta = \frac{1}{2(N-1)}$), the eigenvalues are:

  • $J$ has eigenvalue $N$ (with eigenvector $\mathbf{1}$) and eigenvalue 0 (with multiplicity $N-1$).
  • So $P$ has eigenvalue $\alpha + N\beta = \frac{N-2}{2(N-1)} + \frac{N}{2(N-1)} = \frac{2N-2}{2(N-1)} = 1$ (once), and eigenvalue $\alpha + 0 = \frac{N-2}{2(N-1)}$ (with multiplicity $N-1$).

(c) The second eigenvalue is $\lambda_2 = \frac{N-2}{2(N-1)}$.

Spectral gap:

$$\gamma = 1 - |\lambda_2| = 1 - \frac{N-2}{2(N-1)} = \frac{2(N-1) - (N-2)}{2(N-1)} = \frac{N}{2(N-1)}$$

For large $N$: $\gamma \approx 1/2$.

Mixing time:

$$t_{\text{mix}}(\varepsilon) \approx \frac{\log(1/\varepsilon)}{\gamma} = \frac{2(N-1)}{N} \log(1/\varepsilon) \approx 2\log(1/\varepsilon)$$

Answer: The lazy random walk on $K_N$ mixes in $O(\log(1/\varepsilon))$ time, independent of $N$! This is because the complete graph is maximally connected, so the spectral gap $\gamma \approx 1/2$ does not shrink with graph size.

Exercise 12: Gambler's Ruin with Unfair Odds

Derive the ruin probability for the gambler's ruin problem with $p \neq 1/2$. Starting with $\$i$, target $\$N$, winning probability $p$ per round, find $r_i = P(\text{ruin} \mid X_0 = i)$ by solving the recurrence relation.

Show Solution

Let $q = 1 - p$ and $r_i = P(\text{ruin} \mid X_0 = i)$. Conditioning on the first step:

$$r_i = p \, r_{i+1} + q \, r_{i-1}, \quad r_0 = 1, \quad r_N = 0$$

Rearranging: $p \, r_{i+1} - r_i + q \, r_{i-1} = 0$, or $p(r_{i+1} - r_i) = q(r_i - r_{i-1})$.

Let $\Delta_i = r_i - r_{i-1}$. Then $\Delta_{i+1} = (q/p)\Delta_i$, so $\Delta_i = (q/p)^{i-1}\Delta_1$.

Summing the telescoping series:

$$r_i - r_0 = \sum_{k=1}^{i} \Delta_k = \Delta_1 \sum_{k=0}^{i-1}\left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^k$$

Let $\rho = q/p$. If $\rho \neq 1$ (i.e., $p \neq 1/2$):

$$r_i = 1 + \Delta_1 \cdot \frac{\rho^i - 1}{\rho - 1}$$

Using boundary condition $r_N = 0$:

$$0 = 1 + \Delta_1 \cdot \frac{\rho^N - 1}{\rho - 1} \implies \Delta_1 = -\frac{\rho - 1}{\rho^N - 1}$$

Substituting back:

$$r_i = 1 - \frac{\rho^i - 1}{\rho^N - 1} = \frac{\rho^N - \rho^i}{\rho^N - 1} = \frac{(q/p)^i - (q/p)^N}{1 - (q/p)^N}$$

(Noting that $(q/p)^N - 1 = -(1 - (q/p)^N)$ and simplifying signs.)

Answer:

$$\boxed{r_i = \frac{(q/p)^i - (q/p)^N}{1 - (q/p)^N}, \quad p \neq \frac{1}{2}}$$

Special cases:

  • If $p < 1/2$: $(q/p) > 1$, so $r_i \to 1$ as $N \to \infty$. Against a house with infinite capital, ruin is certain.
  • If $p > 1/2$: $(q/p) < 1$, so $r_i \to (q/p)^i$ as $N \to \infty$. With a favorable game and infinite horizon, there is still a positive chance of ruin.

Exercise 13: Ergodic Theorem Application

A machine can be in one of three states: Working (W), Degraded (D), or Failed (F). The transition matrix is: $$P = \begin{pmatrix} 0.8 & 0.15 & 0.05 \\ 0.4 & 0.4 & 0.2 \\ 0.1 & 0.3 & 0.6 \end{pmatrix}$$ The machine produces revenue $\$100$/hour when Working, $\$40$/hour when Degraded, and $\$0$ when Failed. Each transition takes 1 hour. (a) Find the stationary distribution. (b) Using the ergodic theorem, find the long-run average revenue per hour. (c) A maintenance policy repairs the machine when it fails, making it Working in the next step (changing row 3 to $(1, 0, 0)$). How does this change the long-run revenue?

Show Solution

(a) Solve $\boldsymbol{\pi} P = \boldsymbol{\pi}$:

$$\pi_W = 0.8\pi_W + 0.4\pi_D + 0.1\pi_F$$ $$\pi_D = 0.15\pi_W + 0.4\pi_D + 0.3\pi_F$$ $$\pi_F = 0.05\pi_W + 0.2\pi_D + 0.6\pi_F$$

From equation 1: $0.2\pi_W = 0.4\pi_D + 0.1\pi_F$, so $2\pi_W = 4\pi_D + \pi_F$.

From equation 3: $0.4\pi_F = 0.05\pi_W + 0.2\pi_D$, so $\pi_F = 0.125\pi_W + 0.5\pi_D$.

Substituting into the first: $2\pi_W = 4\pi_D + 0.125\pi_W + 0.5\pi_D = 4.5\pi_D + 0.125\pi_W$

$1.875\pi_W = 4.5\pi_D$, so $\pi_D = \frac{1.875}{4.5}\pi_W = \frac{5}{12}\pi_W$.

Then $\pi_F = 0.125\pi_W + 0.5 \cdot \frac{5}{12}\pi_W = 0.125\pi_W + \frac{5}{24}\pi_W = \frac{3+5}{24}\pi_W = \frac{1}{3}\pi_W$.

Normalization: $\pi_W(1 + 5/12 + 1/3) = \pi_W \cdot \frac{12+5+4}{12} = \pi_W \cdot \frac{21}{12} = 1$.

$$\pi_W = \frac{12}{21} = \frac{4}{7}, \quad \pi_D = \frac{5}{21}, \quad \pi_F = \frac{4}{21}$$

(b) By the ergodic theorem, the long-run average revenue is:

$$E_\pi[\text{revenue}] = 100\pi_W + 40\pi_D + 0\pi_F = 100 \cdot \frac{4}{7} + 40 \cdot \frac{5}{21} = \frac{400}{7} + \frac{200}{21} = \frac{1200 + 200}{21} = \frac{1400}{21} \approx \$66.67/\text{hr}$$

(c) With the maintenance policy, row 3 becomes $(1, 0, 0)$. The new system:

$$\pi_W' = 0.8\pi_W' + 0.4\pi_D' + 1.0\pi_F'$$

Solving the new system: $0.2\pi_W' = 0.4\pi_D' + \pi_F'$. From eq 3: $\pi_F' = 0.05\pi_W' + 0.2\pi_D'$. Substituting: $0.2\pi_W' = 0.4\pi_D' + 0.05\pi_W' + 0.2\pi_D' = 0.6\pi_D' + 0.05\pi_W'$, so $0.15\pi_W' = 0.6\pi_D'$, giving $\pi_D' = 0.25\pi_W'$.

Then $\pi_F' = 0.05\pi_W' + 0.05\pi_W' = 0.1\pi_W'$.

Normalization: $\pi_W'(1 + 0.25 + 0.1) = 1$, so $\pi_W' = 1/1.35 \approx 0.741$, $\pi_D' \approx 0.185$, $\pi_F' \approx 0.074$.

New revenue: $100(0.741) + 40(0.185) + 0(0.074) \approx \$81.48/\text{hr}$.

Answer: The maintenance policy increases long-run revenue from $\approx\$66.67$/hr to $\approx\$81.48$/hr, a 22% improvement.

Exercise 14: Designing a Metropolis-Hastings Chain

You want to sample from the discrete distribution $\pi = (0.1, 0.3, 0.4, 0.2)$ on states $\{1, 2, 3, 4\}$ using the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm with a uniform proposal (propose any state with equal probability $1/4$). (a) Write out the acceptance probability formula. (b) Compute the full transition matrix of the MH chain. (c) Verify that detailed balance holds with respect to $\pi$.

Show Solution

(a) The Metropolis-Hastings acceptance probability for proposing move $i \to j$ is:

$$\alpha(i, j) = \min\left(1, \frac{\pi_j \, q(i \mid j)}{\pi_i \, q(j \mid i)}\right)$$

Since the proposal is symmetric ($q(j \mid i) = q(i \mid j) = 1/4$), this simplifies to the Metropolis acceptance:

$$\alpha(i, j) = \min\left(1, \frac{\pi_j}{\pi_i}\right)$$

(b) The transition probabilities are $p_{ij} = q(j|i) \cdot \alpha(i,j) = \frac{1}{4}\min\left(1, \frac{\pi_j}{\pi_i}\right)$ for $i \neq j$, and $p_{ii} = 1 - \sum_{j \neq i} p_{ij}$.

Computing off-diagonal entries (for $i \neq j$, multiply $1/4$ by $\min(1, \pi_j/\pi_i)$):

  • $p_{12} = \frac{1}{4}\min(1, 0.3/0.1) = \frac{1}{4} \cdot 1 = 0.25$
  • $p_{13} = \frac{1}{4}\min(1, 0.4/0.1) = 0.25$
  • $p_{14} = \frac{1}{4}\min(1, 0.2/0.1) = 0.25$
  • $p_{21} = \frac{1}{4}\min(1, 0.1/0.3) = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{3} = 0.0833$
  • $p_{23} = \frac{1}{4}\min(1, 0.4/0.3) = 0.25$
  • $p_{24} = \frac{1}{4}\min(1, 0.2/0.3) = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{2}{3} = 0.1667$
  • $p_{31} = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{4} = 0.0625$
  • $p_{32} = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{3}{4} = 0.1875$
  • $p_{34} = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 0.125$
  • $p_{41} = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 0.125$
  • $p_{42} = \frac{1}{4} \cdot 1 = 0.25$
  • $p_{43} = \frac{1}{4} \cdot 1 = 0.25$

Self-loop probabilities (rejection probability):

  • $p_{11} = 1 - (0.25 + 0.25 + 0.25) = 0.25$
  • $p_{22} = 1 - (0.0833 + 0.25 + 0.1667) = 0.5$
  • $p_{33} = 1 - (0.0625 + 0.1875 + 0.125) = 0.625$
  • $p_{44} = 1 - (0.125 + 0.25 + 0.25) = 0.375$

(c) Check detailed balance $\pi_i p_{ij} = \pi_j p_{ji}$ for a few pairs:

  • $(1,2)$: $0.1 \times 0.25 = 0.025$ vs $0.3 \times 0.0833 = 0.025$ ✓
  • $(2,3)$: $0.3 \times 0.25 = 0.075$ vs $0.4 \times 0.1875 = 0.075$ ✓
  • $(1,4)$: $0.1 \times 0.25 = 0.025$ vs $0.2 \times 0.125 = 0.025$ ✓

Answer: Detailed balance holds by construction—this is exactly how Metropolis-Hastings is designed! The acceptance ratio ensures $\pi_i q_{ij} \alpha_{ij} = \pi_j q_{ji} \alpha_{ji}$, which gives $\pi_i p_{ij} = \pi_j p_{ji}$.

Key Concept

The Markov property says the future depends only on the present, never on the past. This memoryless structure makes the entire dynamics computable from a single transition matrix.

Prerequisites

  • Conditional probability
  • Matrix multiplication

Key Formulas

Markov property:

$P(X_{n+1}=j \mid X_n=i, \ldots) = P(X_{n+1}=j \mid X_n=i)$

Chapman–Kolmogorov:

$p_{ij}^{(n)} = \sum_k p_{ik}^{(m)} p_{kj}^{(n-m)}$

Stationary distribution:

$\boldsymbol{\pi} P = \boldsymbol{\pi}, \quad \sum_i \pi_i = 1$

Detailed balance:

$\pi_i p_{ij} = \pi_j p_{ji}$

Ergodic theorem:

$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f(X_k) \to E_\pi[f]$
