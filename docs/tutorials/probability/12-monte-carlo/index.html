12. Monte Carlo Methods

Monte Carlo methods use random sampling to estimate quantities that are analytically intractable. From estimating integrals and computing expectations to sampling from complex posterior distributions, these methods are the computational backbone of modern Bayesian statistics, machine learning, and scientific simulation. This tutorial builds from basic Monte Carlo integration through rejection and importance sampling to the powerful family of Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms.

Why Monte Carlo?

In probability and statistics, we constantly need to compute expectations:

$$E_p[g(X)] = \int g(x) \, p(x) \, dx$$

When $X$ is low-dimensional and $p$ is a nice distribution, we can compute this analytically or with numerical quadrature. But in practice, this integral is often intractable:

  • High dimensions. Numerical quadrature on a grid in $d$ dimensions requires $O(n^d)$ points—the curse of dimensionality makes this impossible for $d \geq 10$.
  • Complex distributions. In Bayesian inference, the posterior $p(\theta \mid \text{data}) \propto p(\text{data} \mid \theta) \, p(\theta)$ often has no closed form, and the normalizing constant $p(\text{data}) = \int p(\text{data} \mid \theta) \, p(\theta) \, d\theta$ is itself an intractable integral.
  • Non-standard geometry. The region of integration may be complicated (e.g., the volume of a high-dimensional polytope).

Monte Carlo methods solve these problems by replacing deterministic computation with random sampling. The fundamental insight: if we can draw samples $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_N \sim p(x)$, then we can approximate any expectation as a sample average:

$$\hat{\mu}_N = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} g(x_i) \xrightarrow{a.s.} E_p[g(X)] \quad \text{as } N \to \infty$$

Historical origin. The name "Monte Carlo" was coined by Stanislaw Ulam and John von Neumann during the Manhattan Project in the late 1940s. Ulam, recovering from an illness, was playing solitaire and wondered about the probability of winning. Rather than computing it analytically, he realized it would be easier to play many games and count the wins. He and von Neumann applied this idea to neutron diffusion problems in nuclear physics—calculations far too complex for analytical methods. The name "Monte Carlo" was a reference to the famous casino, chosen by their colleague Nicholas Metropolis.

The beauty of Monte Carlo is that its convergence rate is $O(1/\sqrt{N})$ regardless of dimension. Whether you are integrating over 2 dimensions or 2,000, you need roughly the same number of samples for a given accuracy. This is what makes Monte Carlo the only viable approach for many problems in modern statistics and machine learning.

Basic Monte Carlo Integration

The simplest Monte Carlo method estimates an expectation by averaging function values at random sample points. Suppose we want to compute:

$$I = E_p[g(X)] = \int g(x) \, p(x) \, dx$$

Draw $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_N$ independently from $p(x)$. The Monte Carlo estimator is:

$$\hat{I}_N = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} g(x_i)$$

Properties of the estimator:

  • Unbiased: $E[\hat{I}_N] = I$ (immediately from linearity of expectation).
  • Consistent: $\hat{I}_N \xrightarrow{a.s.} I$ as $N \to \infty$ by the Strong Law of Large Numbers.
  • Variance: $\text{Var}(\hat{I}_N) = \frac{\sigma^2}{N}$ where $\sigma^2 = \text{Var}_p[g(X)]$.
  • CLT: $\sqrt{N}(\hat{I}_N - I) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$, giving us approximate confidence intervals $\hat{I}_N \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \hat{\sigma}/\sqrt{N}$.

The standard error is $O(1/\sqrt{N})$, so to halve the error you need four times as many samples. This is worse than deterministic methods in low dimensions (e.g., Simpson's rule achieves $O(1/N^4)$ in 1D), but Monte Carlo wins decisively in high dimensions because its rate does not depend on $d$.

Example: Estimating $\pi$. Let $g(x, y) = \mathbf{1}[x^2 + y^2 \leq 1]$ and $(x, y) \sim \text{Uniform}([-1,1]^2)$. Then $E[g] = \pi/4$ (the area of the unit circle divided by the area of the square). So $\hat{\pi} = 4 \cdot \hat{I}_N$.

Generating Random Variables

Monte Carlo methods require samples from specific distributions. Given a source of uniform random numbers $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ (provided by any pseudorandom number generator), how do we produce samples from an arbitrary distribution?

Inverse Transform Method

If $F$ is the CDF of the target distribution and $F^{-1}$ is its quantile function (generalized inverse), then:

$$X = F^{-1}(U) \sim F \quad \text{where } U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$$

Proof: $P(X \leq x) = P(F^{-1}(U) \leq x) = P(U \leq F(x)) = F(x)$. $\square$

Example: For the exponential distribution $F(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}$, we have $F^{-1}(u) = -\frac{1}{\lambda} \ln(1 - u)$. So $X = -\frac{1}{\lambda}\ln(1 - U) \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$. Since $1 - U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$, we can simplify to $X = -\frac{1}{\lambda}\ln U$.

Box-Muller Transform

The inverse CDF of the Gaussian has no closed form, so we use a clever trick. Given two independent uniforms $U_1, U_2 \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$:

$$Z_1 = \sqrt{-2 \ln U_1} \cos(2\pi U_2), \quad Z_2 = \sqrt{-2 \ln U_1} \sin(2\pi U_2)$$

Then $Z_1, Z_2$ are independent $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ random variables. This works because the transformation maps the uniform square to the 2D Gaussian via polar coordinates: $R = \sqrt{-2\ln U_1}$ has the correct Rayleigh distribution for the radius of a 2D Gaussian, and $\Theta = 2\pi U_2$ is uniform on $[0, 2\pi)$.

When neither the inverse CDF method nor specialized transforms are available, we turn to the more general methods described next: rejection sampling, importance sampling, and MCMC.

Rejection Sampling

Rejection sampling (also called accept-reject sampling) is a general technique for sampling from a distribution $p(x)$ that we can evaluate (up to a normalizing constant) but cannot sample from directly. The idea: enclose $p(x)$ under an easy-to-sample "envelope" and filter out samples that fall outside $p$.

The Algorithm

Suppose we want to sample from a target $p(x)$ and we have a proposal distribution $q(x)$ that we can easily sample from, with the property that there exists a constant $M \geq 1$ such that $p(x) \leq M \cdot q(x)$ for all $x$.

  1. Draw $x \sim q(x)$ (a proposal sample).
  2. Draw $u \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$.
  3. If $u \leq \frac{p(x)}{M \cdot q(x)}$, accept $x$; otherwise reject and go to step 1.

The accepted samples are exact draws from $p(x)$.

Why it works: The joint density of the accepted $(x, u)$ pairs, where $u$ is uniform under $p(x) / (M q(x))$, marginalizes over $u$ to give exactly $p(x)$. More formally, $P(\text{accept} \mid X = x) = p(x)/(Mq(x))$, so the density of accepted $x$ is $q(x) \cdot p(x)/(Mq(x)) / P(\text{accept}) = p(x)/M \cdot (1/(1/M)) = p(x)$.

Acceptance probability: $P(\text{accept}) = 1/M$. So the expected number of proposals per accepted sample is $M$. This means we want $M$ to be as small as possible—i.e., the proposal $q$ should closely match $p$.

Figure 1. Rejection sampling: proposals drawn uniformly under the envelope $Mq(x)$. Points falling below $p(x)$ (green) are accepted; those between $p(x)$ and $Mq(x)$ (red) are rejected.

Limitations. Rejection sampling becomes extremely inefficient in high dimensions. The acceptance rate $1/M$ typically decreases exponentially with dimension, because the volume ratio between the target and envelope distributions shrinks rapidly. For a $d$-dimensional Gaussian target with a uniform envelope on a hypercube, the acceptance rate scales as $(2\pi e)^{-d/2}$—essentially zero for $d > 20$. This motivates the more sophisticated methods that follow.

Importance Sampling

Importance sampling is a technique for estimating expectations under one distribution using samples from another. Unlike rejection sampling, it uses all proposed samples (none are discarded), weighting each by how much the target and proposal distributions differ.

We want to compute $I = E_p[g(X)] = \int g(x) p(x) \, dx$ but cannot sample from $p$. Instead, we sample from a proposal $q(x)$ with $q(x) > 0$ whenever $p(x) g(x) \neq 0$:

$$I = \int g(x) p(x) \, dx = \int g(x) \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} q(x) \, dx = E_q\!\left[g(X) \cdot w(X)\right]$$

where the importance weights are:

$$w(x) = \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}$$

The importance sampling estimator is:

$$\hat{I}_N^{\text{IS}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} g(x_i) \, w(x_i), \quad x_i \sim q$$

This estimator is unbiased with variance:

$$\text{Var}_q[\hat{I}_N^{\text{IS}}] = \frac{1}{N} \text{Var}_q\!\left[g(X) \cdot w(X)\right] = \frac{1}{N}\left(E_q\!\left[g(X)^2 w(X)^2\right] - I^2\right)$$

Optimal Proposal

The variance-minimizing proposal is $q^*(x) \propto |g(x)| \, p(x)$. This gives zero variance when $g(x) \geq 0$ everywhere, but it requires knowing the very integral we are trying to compute. In practice, we choose $q$ to approximate $q^*$: heavier tails than $p$ (to avoid infinite variance from large weights) and concentrated where $|g(x)| p(x)$ is large.

Self-Normalized Importance Sampling

When we only know $p$ up to a normalizing constant—i.e., $p(x) = \tilde{p}(x)/Z$ where $\tilde{p}$ is computable but $Z$ is not—we use the self-normalized estimator:

$$\hat{I}_N^{\text{SNIS}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} g(x_i) \, \tilde{w}(x_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \tilde{w}(x_i)}, \quad \tilde{w}(x_i) = \frac{\tilde{p}(x_i)}{q(x_i)}$$

This is biased but consistent (the bias vanishes as $N \to \infty$). It is also more robust than the standard estimator because the normalization stabilizes extreme weights.

Danger: weight degeneracy. If $q$ is a poor match for $p$, a few samples will have enormous weights while the rest have near-zero weights. The effective sample size $N_{\text{eff}} = \frac{(\sum w_i)^2}{\sum w_i^2}$ can be far smaller than $N$, making the estimate unreliable. This problem worsens exponentially with dimension, which again motivates MCMC methods.

Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)

The central challenge: we want to sample from a target distribution $\pi(x)$ (often a Bayesian posterior) that is known only up to a normalizing constant. Rejection and importance sampling fail in high dimensions. The breakthrough idea of MCMC:

Key Idea: Construct a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the target $\pi(x)$. Run the chain long enough, and the samples it produces will be (approximately) draws from $\pi$.

Recall from Tutorial 11 that a Markov chain with transition kernel $T(x' \mid x)$ has a stationary distribution $\pi$ if:

$$\pi(x') = \int T(x' \mid x) \, \pi(x) \, dx$$

A sufficient condition is detailed balance (reversibility):

$$\pi(x) \, T(x' \mid x) = \pi(x') \, T(x \mid x')$$

If the chain is also irreducible (can reach any state from any other) and aperiodic (does not cycle with a fixed period), then the ergodic theorem guarantees:

$$\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} g(x_i) \xrightarrow{a.s.} E_\pi[g(X)] \quad \text{as } N \to \infty$$

even though the samples are not independent (they are correlated along the chain). This is the MCMC analog of the law of large numbers. The art of MCMC design lies in constructing chains that mix rapidly—i.e., that explore the target distribution efficiently with low autocorrelation.

Metropolis-Hastings Algorithm

The Metropolis-Hastings (MH) algorithm is the most general and widely used MCMC method. It was introduced by Metropolis et al. (1953) for symmetric proposals and generalized by Hastings (1970) to asymmetric proposals.

The Algorithm

Given current state $x_t$:

  1. Propose: Draw $x' \sim q(x' \mid x_t)$ from a proposal distribution.
  2. Compute acceptance ratio:
    $$\alpha(x', x_t) = \min\!\left(1, \; \frac{\pi(x') \, q(x_t \mid x')}{\pi(x_t) \, q(x' \mid x_t)}\right)$$
  3. Accept or reject: Draw $u \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$.
    • If $u \leq \alpha$: set $x_{t+1} = x'$ (accept the proposal).
    • If $u > \alpha$: set $x_{t+1} = x_t$ (stay at current state).

Crucial point: Only the ratio $\pi(x')/\pi(x_t)$ appears, so any normalizing constant in $\pi$ cancels. This is why MH works for unnormalized posteriors.

Proof of Detailed Balance

The MH transition kernel is $T(x' \mid x) = q(x' \mid x) \alpha(x', x) + r(x)\delta(x' - x)$ where $r(x) = 1 - \int q(x' \mid x)\alpha(x',x)\,dx'$ is the probability of rejection. For the acceptance part:

$$\pi(x) \, q(x' \mid x) \, \alpha(x', x) = \pi(x) \, q(x' \mid x) \, \min\!\left(1, \frac{\pi(x')q(x \mid x')}{\pi(x)q(x' \mid x)}\right) = \min\!\left(\pi(x)q(x' \mid x), \; \pi(x')q(x \mid x')\right)$$

This expression is symmetric in $(x, x')$, so $\pi(x)q(x'|x)\alpha(x',x) = \pi(x')q(x|x')\alpha(x,x')$. Detailed balance holds. $\square$

Special Case: Random Walk Metropolis

When the proposal is symmetric—$q(x' \mid x) = q(x \mid x')$, e.g., $x' = x + \epsilon$ with $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)$—the proposal ratio cancels and the acceptance simplifies to:

$$\alpha = \min\!\left(1, \frac{\pi(x')}{\pi(x)}\right)$$

This is the original Metropolis algorithm. The chain always moves to higher-density regions and sometimes moves to lower-density regions (with probability $\pi(x')/\pi(x)$), allowing it to explore the full distribution.

Figure 2. Metropolis-Hastings random walk on a bimodal target. The chain traverses from mode 1 to mode 2, spending more time in higher-density regions. Rejected proposals (red) cause the chain to stay put.

Choosing the Proposal Scale

The proposal variance $\sigma^2$ involves a trade-off:

  • $\sigma$ too small: Almost all proposals are accepted (high acceptance rate), but the chain moves in tiny steps and explores slowly (high autocorrelation).
  • $\sigma$ too large: Proposals often land in low-density regions and are rejected (low acceptance rate), so the chain gets stuck.

The theoretical optimal acceptance rate for a $d$-dimensional Gaussian target with a Gaussian random walk proposal is approximately 23.4% (Roberts, Gelman, and Gilks, 1997). This rule of thumb is widely used in practice for tuning MH algorithms.

Gibbs Sampling

Gibbs sampling, introduced by Geman and Geman (1984) in the context of image processing, is a special case of Metropolis-Hastings where every proposal is accepted. The idea: instead of proposing moves in all dimensions simultaneously, update one coordinate (or block of coordinates) at a time, sampling from its full conditional distribution.

The Algorithm

Suppose $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_d)$ and we want to sample from $\pi(x_1, \ldots, x_d)$. Given current state $(x_1^{(t)}, \ldots, x_d^{(t)})$, one sweep of systematic scan Gibbs sampling produces:

  1. Draw $x_1^{(t+1)} \sim \pi(x_1 \mid x_2^{(t)}, x_3^{(t)}, \ldots, x_d^{(t)})$
  2. Draw $x_2^{(t+1)} \sim \pi(x_2 \mid x_1^{(t+1)}, x_3^{(t)}, \ldots, x_d^{(t)})$
  3. $\vdots$
  4. Draw $x_d^{(t+1)} \sim \pi(x_d \mid x_1^{(t+1)}, x_2^{(t+1)}, \ldots, x_{d-1}^{(t+1)})$

Each step uses the most recently updated values of all other coordinates.

Why Acceptance is Always 1

Gibbs sampling can be viewed as a special case of MH where the proposal for coordinate $j$ is $q(x_j' \mid \mathbf{x}_{-j}) = \pi(x_j' \mid \mathbf{x}_{-j})$. The MH acceptance ratio becomes:

$$\alpha = \min\!\left(1, \frac{\pi(x_j') \pi(x_j^{(t)} \mid \mathbf{x}_{-j})}{\pi(x_j^{(t)}) \pi(x_j' \mid \mathbf{x}_{-j})}\right)$$

Using $\pi(x_j, \mathbf{x}_{-j}) = \pi(x_j \mid \mathbf{x}_{-j})\pi(\mathbf{x}_{-j})$, the ratio simplifies to $\frac{\pi(\mathbf{x}_{-j})\pi(\mathbf{x}_{-j})}{\pi(\mathbf{x}_{-j})\pi(\mathbf{x}_{-j})} = 1$. So every proposal is accepted.

When to Use Gibbs Sampling

Gibbs sampling is ideal when:

  • The full conditionals $\pi(x_j \mid \mathbf{x}_{-j})$ have known forms (e.g., conjugate models in Bayesian statistics).
  • The target is a joint distribution of many variables with local dependencies (e.g., graphical models, topic models like LDA).

It can be slow when variables are strongly correlated, since one-at-a-time updates cannot make large joint moves. Block Gibbs sampling mitigates this by updating groups of correlated variables together. Random scan Gibbs picks a coordinate uniformly at random to update rather than cycling deterministically.

Classic example: For the bivariate normal $\begin{pmatrix}X_1 \\ X_2\end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix}1 & \rho \\ \rho & 1\end{pmatrix}\right)$, the full conditionals are:

$$X_1 \mid X_2 = x_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(\rho x_2, \; 1 - \rho^2), \quad X_2 \mid X_1 = x_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(\rho x_1, \; 1 - \rho^2)$$

When $|\rho|$ is close to 1, Gibbs sampling mixes slowly because the chain must follow the narrow correlation "ridge" one coordinate at a time.

Diagnostics and Convergence

MCMC produces a correlated stream of samples that eventually represents the target distribution, but "eventually" is the critical word. How do we know when the chain has converged? This is one of the hardest problems in applied MCMC—there is no guaranteed finite-time convergence test—but several diagnostic tools help.

Burn-in

The initial portion of the chain is typically discarded as burn-in. During burn-in, the chain is migrating from its (possibly poor) starting point toward the high-probability region of $\pi$. A common rule of thumb is to discard the first 50% of samples, though this is conservative.

Thinning

To reduce storage and autocorrelation, one can keep every $k$-th sample. While thinning does not improve the Monte Carlo estimate per unit of computation (Link and Eaton, 2012), it is useful for memory management and downstream analysis.

Trace Plots

A trace plot shows the sampled value of a parameter vs. iteration number. A well-mixing chain shows a "hairy caterpillar" pattern with no trends. A poorly-mixing chain shows long plateaus, slow drifts, or jumps between modes.

Figure 3. Trace plot of an MCMC chain. The burn-in period (red) shows the chain migrating from its starting value to the stationary distribution. After convergence (green), the chain fluctuates around the true mean.

Autocorrelation

The autocorrelation function (ACF) at lag $k$ measures how correlated sample $t$ is with sample $t + k$. For a well-mixing chain, the ACF drops quickly to zero. High autocorrelation means the chain is taking many steps to produce effectively independent samples.

Gelman-Rubin $\hat{R}$ Statistic

Run $m$ independent chains from dispersed starting points. The Gelman-Rubin diagnostic compares the between-chain variance $B$ to the within-chain variance $W$:

$$\hat{R} = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{V}}{W}}, \quad \hat{V} = \frac{N-1}{N}W + \frac{1}{N}B$$

If chains have converged to the same distribution, $\hat{R} \approx 1$. The standard threshold is $\hat{R} < 1.01$ (or the older, more lenient $\hat{R} < 1.1$). Values significantly above 1 indicate that chains are exploring different regions and need to run longer.

Effective Sample Size (ESS)

Due to autocorrelation, $N$ MCMC samples carry less information than $N$ independent samples. The effective sample size estimates how many independent samples the chain is equivalent to:

$$N_{\text{eff}} = \frac{N}{1 + 2\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \rho_k}$$

where $\rho_k$ is the autocorrelation at lag $k$. A well-mixing chain has $N_{\text{eff}}$ close to $N$; a poorly-mixing chain can have $N_{\text{eff}} \ll N$.

Hamiltonian Monte Carlo

Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC), also called Hybrid Monte Carlo, uses gradient information to make large, informed moves through the parameter space, dramatically reducing the random walk behavior of standard MH. It was introduced by Duane et al. (1987) for lattice field theory simulations and popularized in statistics by Neal (2011).

The Physics Analogy

Think of the parameter $\theta$ as the position of a ball on a surface whose height is $-\log \pi(\theta)$ (lower where $\pi$ is higher). HMC introduces an auxiliary momentum variable $p$ and defines the Hamiltonian:

$$H(\theta, p) = U(\theta) + K(p) = -\log \pi(\theta) + \frac{1}{2}p^T M^{-1} p$$

where $U(\theta) = -\log \pi(\theta)$ is the potential energy and $K(p) = \frac{1}{2}p^T M^{-1}p$ is the kinetic energy with mass matrix $M$. The joint distribution $\pi(\theta, p) \propto e^{-H(\theta,p)}$ marginalizes to $\pi(\theta)$.

The Algorithm

  1. Resample momentum: Draw $p \sim \mathcal{N}(0, M)$.
  2. Simulate Hamiltonian dynamics: Starting from $(\theta, p)$, run $L$ steps of the leapfrog integrator:
    $$p_{t+\epsilon/2} = p_t - \frac{\epsilon}{2}\nabla_\theta U(\theta_t)$$ $$\theta_{t+\epsilon} = \theta_t + \epsilon M^{-1} p_{t+\epsilon/2}$$ $$p_{t+\epsilon} = p_{t+\epsilon/2} - \frac{\epsilon}{2}\nabla_\theta U(\theta_{t+\epsilon})$$
  3. Metropolis correction: Accept $(\theta^*, p^*)$ with probability $\min(1, e^{-H(\theta^*,p^*) + H(\theta, p)})$.

The leapfrog integrator is symplectic (volume-preserving and time-reversible), which ensures detailed balance. In exact Hamiltonian dynamics, $H$ is conserved so acceptance would be 100%; the leapfrog introduces small errors that the Metropolis step corrects.

Why HMC is Better in High Dimensions

Random walk MH explores a $d$-dimensional space by making random steps, requiring $O(d)$ steps to traverse the distribution (and $O(d^2)$ for the mixing time). HMC uses the gradient $\nabla \log \pi(\theta)$ to move along "level curves" of the distribution, requiring only $O(d^{1/4})$ leapfrog steps per effective sample. This makes HMC dramatically more efficient for models with hundreds or thousands of parameters.

NUTS: The No-U-Turn Sampler

HMC has two tuning parameters: step size $\epsilon$ and number of leapfrog steps $L$. The No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS), proposed by Hoffman and Gelman (2014), eliminates the need to choose $L$ by automatically determining when the simulated trajectory starts to turn back on itself (a "U-turn"). NUTS is the default algorithm in modern probabilistic programming frameworks like Stan, PyMC, and NumPyro.

Connections to Machine Learning

Monte Carlo methods are deeply embedded in modern machine learning:

Bayesian Neural Networks

Instead of finding a single point estimate $\hat{\mathbf{w}}$ of neural network weights, Bayesian inference seeks the full posterior $p(\mathbf{w} \mid \mathcal{D})$. MCMC (especially HMC/NUTS) can sample from this posterior, providing uncertainty estimates for predictions. However, the high dimensionality of neural networks (millions of parameters) makes this extremely challenging, and approximations like variational inference or stochastic gradient MCMC are typically used instead.

Variational Inference as an Alternative

Where MCMC produces exact samples (asymptotically), variational inference (VI) approximates the posterior $p(\theta \mid \mathcal{D})$ with a simpler distribution $q_\phi(\theta)$ by minimizing $\text{KL}(q_\phi \| p(\cdot \mid \mathcal{D}))$. VI is faster but biased; MCMC is unbiased but slower. The choice depends on the problem: MCMC for accuracy-critical applications, VI for scalability.

Diffusion Models

Modern generative models like DDPM (Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models) are closely related to MCMC. The reverse diffusion process can be viewed as a Markov chain that transforms noise into data, with each denoising step analogous to an MCMC transition. Score-based models use $\nabla_x \log p(x)$—the same gradient information that powers HMC—to guide the generative process. The connection to Langevin dynamics (a continuous-time MCMC algorithm) is particularly direct.

Particle Filtering

Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC), also called particle filtering, extends importance sampling to sequential settings. It maintains a weighted set of "particles" that approximate a filtering distribution $p(x_t \mid y_{1:t})$ as new observations arrive. Applications include robot localization, tracking, and online Bayesian inference. When particle weights become degenerate, resampling (a form of selection) redistributes the particles—an idea also used in evolutionary algorithms.

Reinforcement Learning

Monte Carlo methods are foundational in RL: Monte Carlo policy evaluation estimates the value function $V^\pi(s) = E_\pi[\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t R_t \mid S_0 = s]$ by averaging returns from sampled episodes. Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), used by AlphaGo and its successors, combines random rollouts with tree search to evaluate game positions.

1. Estimating Pi with Monte Carlo

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def estimate_pi(N):
    """
    Estimate pi by Monte Carlo: sample uniformly in [-1,1]^2,
    count fraction inside the unit circle, multiply by 4.
    """
    x = np.random.uniform(-1, 1, N)
    y = np.random.uniform(-1, 1, N)
    inside = x**2 + y**2 <= 1.0
    pi_hat = 4.0 * np.mean(inside)
    return pi_hat, x, y, inside

# Run with different sample sizes
np.random.seed(42)
sample_sizes = [100, 1_000, 10_000, 100_000, 1_000_000]
for N in sample_sizes:
    pi_hat = estimate_pi(N)[0]
    error = abs(pi_hat - np.pi)
    print(f"N = {N:>10,}:  pi_hat = {pi_hat:.6f},  error = {error:.6f}")

# Visualize convergence
N_max = 50_000
pi_hat, x, y, inside = estimate_pi(N_max)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(12, 5))

# Left: scatter plot
ax = axes[0]
ax.scatter(x[inside][:2000], y[inside][:2000], s=1, c='#10b981', alpha=0.5)
ax.scatter(x[~inside][:2000], y[~inside][:2000], s=1, c='#ef4444', alpha=0.5)
theta = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 100)
ax.plot(np.cos(theta), np.sin(theta), 'k-', linewidth=1.5)
ax.set_aspect('equal')
ax.set_title(f'Monte Carlo Estimation of pi (N={N_max:,})')
ax.legend(['Unit circle', 'Inside', 'Outside'], markerscale=5)

# Right: convergence plot
ax = axes[1]
cumulative = 4.0 * np.cumsum(inside) / np.arange(1, N_max + 1)

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats

def target_pdf(x):
    """
    Non-standard target: mixture of two Gaussians.
    p(x) = 0.3 * N(-2, 0.5^2) + 0.7 * N(3, 1^2)
    """
    return 0.3 * stats.norm.pdf(x, -2, 0.5) + 0.7 * stats.norm.pdf(x, 3, 1)

def rejection_sampling(target, N_desired, proposal_dist, M):
    """
    Rejection sampling from target using a proposal distribution.

    Parameters:
        target: function, target PDF (unnormalized is ok if M adjusted)
        N_desired: number of accepted samples desired
        proposal_dist: scipy.stats distribution for proposals
        M: constant such that target(x) <= M * proposal_dist.pdf(x)

    Returns:
        samples: accepted samples from target
        n_total: total proposals generated
    """
    samples = []
    n_total = 0

    while len(samples) < N_desired:
        # Propose from q(x)
        x = proposal_dist.rvs()
        # Uniform for acceptance
        u = np.random.uniform()
        # Accept with probability target(x) / (M * q(x))
        acceptance_prob = target(x) / (M * proposal_dist.pdf(x))
        if u <= acceptance_prob:
            samples.append(x)
        n_total += 1

    return np.array(samples), n_total

3. Importance Sampling with Variance Comparison

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

def importance_sampling_demo():
    """
    Estimate P(X > 4) where X ~ N(0,1) using:
    1. Naive Monte Carlo (direct sampling from N(0,1))
    2. Importance sampling with shifted proposal N(4,1)

    The true value is about 3.17e-5 -- very rare event.
    """
    np.random.seed(42)
    true_value = 1 - stats.norm.cdf(4)  # P(X > 4) ~ 3.167e-5
    print(f"True P(X > 4) = {true_value:.6e}")
    print("=" * 60)

    N = 100_000

    # --- Method 1: Naive Monte Carlo ---
    samples_naive = np.random.randn(N)
    estimates_naive = (samples_naive > 4).astype(float)
    mc_estimate = np.mean(estimates_naive)
    mc_stderr = np.std(estimates_naive) / np.sqrt(N)

    print(f"\nNaive MC (N={N:,}):")
    print(f"  Estimate: {mc_estimate:.6e}")
    print(f"  Std Error: {mc_stderr:.6e}")
    print(f"  Relative Error: {mc_stderr / max(mc_estimate, 1e-10):.2%}")

    # --- Method 2: Importance Sampling with N(4, 1) proposal ---
    # Target: p(x) = N(0,1),  Proposal: q(x) = N(4,1)
    # g(x) = 1[x > 4]
    # Weight: w(x) = p(x) / q(x) = N(x;0,1) / N(x;4,1)
    proposal_mean = 4.0
    samples_is = np.random.randn(N) + proposal_mean  # samples from N(4,1)

    # Importance weights
    log_weights = stats.norm.logpdf(samples_is, 0, 1) - stats.norm.logpdf(samples_is, proposal_mean, 1)
    weights = np.exp(log_weights)

    g_values = (samples_is > 4).astype(float)
    weighted_values = g_values * weights

    is_estimate = np.mean(weighted_values)
    is_stderr = np.std(weighted_values) / np.sqrt(N)

    print(f"\nImportance Sampling (N={N:,}, proposal=N(4,1)):")
    print(f"  Estimate: {is_estimate:.6e}")
    print(f"  Std Error: {is_stderr:.6e}")
    print(f"  Relative Error: {is_stderr / is_estimate:.2%}")

    # --- Variance comparison ---
    variance_ratio = mc_stderr**2 / is_stderr**2 if is_stderr > 0 else float('inf')
    print(f"\nVariance reduction factor: {variance_ratio:.1f}x")
    print(f"IS is equivalent to {variance_ratio:.0f}x more naive MC samples")

    # --- Effective Sample Size ---
    normalized_weights = weights / np.sum(weights)
    ess = 1.0 / np.sum(normalized_weights**2)
    print(f"\nEffective Sample Size: {ess:.0f} out of {N:,} ({ess/N:.1%})")

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def metropolis_hastings(log_target, x0, proposal_std, n_samples, burn_in=0):
    """
    Random Walk Metropolis-Hastings sampler.

    Parameters:
        log_target: function, log of (unnormalized) target density
        x0: initial state (scalar or array)
        proposal_std: standard deviation of Gaussian proposal
        n_samples: total number of samples (including burn-in)
        burn_in: number of initial samples to discard

    Returns:
        samples: array of shape (n_samples - burn_in,)
        acceptance_rate: fraction of proposals accepted
    """
    dim = np.size(x0)
    x = np.array(x0, dtype=float)
    samples = np.zeros((n_samples, dim)) if dim > 1 else np.zeros(n_samples)
    n_accepted = 0

    for i in range(n_samples):
        # Propose
        x_proposal = x + np.random.randn(*np.shape(x)) * proposal_std

        # Log acceptance ratio (proposal is symmetric, so q cancels)
        log_alpha = log_target(x_proposal) - log_target(x)

        # Accept or reject
        if np.log(np.random.uniform()) < log_alpha:
            x = x_proposal
            n_accepted += 1

        samples[i] = x

    acceptance_rate = n_accepted / n_samples
    return samples[burn_in:], acceptance_rate

5. Gibbs Sampler for Bivariate Normal

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def gibbs_bivariate_normal(mu1, mu2, sigma1, sigma2, rho, n_samples, burn_in=0):
    """
    Gibbs sampler for the bivariate normal distribution.

    Target: (X1, X2) ~ N(mu, Sigma) where
        Sigma = [[sigma1^2, rho*sigma1*sigma2],
                 [rho*sigma1*sigma2, sigma2^2]]

    Full conditionals:
        X1 | X2=x2 ~ N(mu1 + rho*(sigma1/sigma2)*(x2 - mu2), sigma1^2*(1-rho^2))
        X2 | X1=x1 ~ N(mu2 + rho*(sigma2/sigma1)*(x1 - mu1), sigma2^2*(1-rho^2))
    """
    samples = np.zeros((n_samples, 2))
    x1, x2 = 0.0, 0.0  # Initialize

    cond_var1 = sigma1**2 * (1 - rho**2)
    cond_var2 = sigma2**2 * (1 - rho**2)
    cond_std1 = np.sqrt(cond_var1)
    cond_std2 = np.sqrt(cond_var2)

    for i in range(n_samples):
        # Sample X1 | X2 = x2
        cond_mean1 = mu1 + rho * (sigma1 / sigma2) * (x2 - mu2)
        x1 = np.random.normal(cond_mean1, cond_std1)

        # Sample X2 | X1 = x1
        cond_mean2 = mu2 + rho * (sigma2 / sigma1) * (x1 - mu1)
        x2 = np.random.normal(cond_mean2, cond_std2)

        samples[i] = [x1, x2]

    return samples[burn_in:]

Exercise 1: Estimating an Integral

Use Monte Carlo sampling to estimate $I = \int_0^1 e^{-x^2} \, dx$. (a) Write the estimator explicitly. (b) Using $N = 10{,}000$ samples, give an approximate 95% confidence interval for your estimate. (c) What is the true value (to 4 decimal places)?

Show Solution

$$\hat{I}_N = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N e^{-U_i^2}, \quad U_i \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$$

(b) We need $\hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{i=1}^N (e^{-U_i^2} - \hat{I}_N)^2$. The 95% CI is $\hat{I}_N \pm 1.96 \cdot \hat{\sigma}/\sqrt{N}$.

Exercise 2: Inverse Transform Sampling

The Pareto distribution has CDF $F(x) = 1 - (x_m/x)^\alpha$ for $x \geq x_m > 0$. (a) Derive the inverse CDF $F^{-1}(u)$. (b) Give a procedure to sample from $\text{Pareto}(\alpha, x_m)$ using a uniform random variable. (c) Verify by computing $E[X]$ from your formula and comparing to the known mean $\alpha x_m / (\alpha - 1)$ (for $\alpha > 1$).

Show Solution

(a) Set $u = F(x) = 1 - (x_m/x)^\alpha$. Solving for $x$:

$$(x_m/x)^\alpha = 1 - u \implies x = x_m (1-u)^{-1/\alpha}$$

So $F^{-1}(u) = x_m (1 - u)^{-1/\alpha}$.

(b) Draw $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$, then $X = x_m (1 - U)^{-1/\alpha} = x_m U^{-1/\alpha}$ (since $1-U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$).

(c) $E[X] = E[x_m U^{-1/\alpha}] = x_m \int_0^1 u^{-1/\alpha} du = x_m \left[\frac{u^{1-1/\alpha}}{1-1/\alpha}\right]_0^1 = \frac{x_m}{1 - 1/\alpha} = \frac{\alpha x_m}{\alpha - 1}$.

This matches the known Pareto mean. $\square$

Exercise 3: Simple Rejection Sampling

You want to sample from $f(x) = 2x$ on $[0, 1]$. (a) Using $\text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ as the proposal, what is the smallest valid $M$? (b) What is the acceptance probability? (c) Write the accept-reject criterion explicitly.

Show Solution

(c) Generate $X \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$ and $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$. Accept $X$ if:

$$U \leq \frac{f(X)}{M \cdot q(X)} = \frac{2X}{2 \cdot 1} = X$$

So: accept $X$ if $U \leq X$. Intuitively, points near $x = 1$ are accepted more often, matching the increasing density $f(x) = 2x$. $\square$

Exercise 4: Monte Carlo Error

You estimate $E[X] = \mu$ using $N$ i.i.d. samples with sample mean $\bar{X}_N$ and sample variance $s^2 = 10$. (a) How many samples are needed for the standard error to be below 0.01? Exercise 6: Importance Sampling Design

You want to estimate $P(X > 5)$ where $X \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. (a) Show that naive Monte Carlo would need approximately $N = 3.5 \times 10^8$ samples for a relative error below 10%. (b) Design an importance sampling estimator using $q = \mathcal{N}(5, 1)$ as the proposal. Write the weight function. (c) Argue qualitatively why this proposal is much better than the naive approach.

Show Solution

(a) Let $p = P(X > 5) \approx 2.87 \times 10^{-7}$. With naive MC, $\hat{p} = \frac{1}{N}\sum \mathbf{1}[X_i > 5]$ has variance $p(1-p)/N \approx p/N$.

Relative error $= \text{SE}/p = \sqrt{p/N}/p = 1/\sqrt{Np}$. For RE $< 0.1$:

$$N > \frac{1}{0.01 \times p} = \frac{1}{0.01 \times 2.87 \times 10^{-7}} \approx 3.49 \times 10^8$$

(b) With $q = \mathcal{N}(5, 1)$, draw $X_i \sim q$ and compute:

$$\hat{p} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \mathbf{1}[X_i > 5] \cdot w(X_i), \quad w(x) = \frac{\phi(x)}{\phi(x-5)} = \exp\!\left(-5x + \frac{25}{2}\right)$$

where $\phi$ is the standard normal PDF.

(c) The proposal $\mathcal{N}(5,1)$ places about 50% of its mass above 5, so roughly half of all samples contribute to the estimate. In contrast, the standard normal puts only $2.87 \times 10^{-7}$ of its mass there. The importance weights correct for this shift, giving a vastly lower-variance estimate. The effective sample size per sample is increased by a factor of roughly $p_q(X > 5)/p(X > 5) \approx 10^6$.

Exercise 7: Metropolis-Hastings Acceptance Rate Tuning

Consider a Metropolis algorithm targeting $\pi(x) = \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ with proposal $x' = x + \epsilon$ where $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$. (a) Derive the acceptance probability $\alpha(x', x)$ as a function of $x, x', \sigma$. (b) Show that for $\sigma \to 0$, the acceptance rate approaches 1 but the chain mixes slowly. (c) Show that for $\sigma \to \infty$, the acceptance rate approaches 0. (d) For a 1D Gaussian target, the optimal $\sigma$ gives acceptance rate $\approx 44\%$. What is this optimal $\sigma$?

Show Solution

(a) Since the proposal is symmetric ($q(x'|x) = q(x|x')$):

$$\alpha(x', x) = \min\!\left(1, \frac{\pi(x')}{\pi(x)}\right) = \min\!\left(1, \exp\!\left(-\frac{x'^2 - x^2}{2}\right)\right)$$

(b) As $\sigma \to 0$, proposals $x' = x + \epsilon$ are very close to $x$, so $\pi(x')/\pi(x) \approx 1$ and almost every proposal is accepted. But each step moves a distance $\sim \sigma \to 0$, so $N$ steps traverse only distance $\sim \sigma\sqrt{N}$. To cross the distribution (scale $\sim 1$), we need $N \sim 1/\sigma^2 \to \infty$ steps. The chain mixes very slowly.

(c) As $\sigma \to \infty$, proposals land far from $x$ where $\pi(x')$ is exponentially small, so $\pi(x')/\pi(x) \approx 0$ and the acceptance rate $\to 0$. The chain gets stuck.

(d) For a 1D target, the optimal acceptance rate is about 44% (Roberts and Rosenthal, 2001). For $\pi = \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, this corresponds to $\sigma \approx 2.38 / \sqrt{1} = 2.38$. (The general rule is $\sigma_{\text{opt}} \approx 2.38 \cdot \sigma_{\text{target}} / \sqrt{d}$ for a $d$-dimensional target, giving 23.4% acceptance in high dimensions.)

Exercise 8: Gibbs Sampler Derivation

Consider the joint distribution $p(x, y) \propto e^{-x} \cdot x^{y} / y!$ for $x > 0$ and $y \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$. (a) Identify the full conditional $p(y \mid x)$. (b) Identify the full conditional $p(x \mid y)$. (c) Describe the Gibbs sampling algorithm for this distribution.

Show Solution

(a) $p(y \mid x) \propto p(x, y) \propto \frac{x^y}{y!}$ (treating $x$ as fixed). This is the PMF of $\text{Poisson}(x)$. So $Y \mid X = x \sim \text{Poisson}(x)$.

(b) $p(x \mid y) \propto p(x, y) \propto e^{-x} x^y$ for $x > 0$ (treating $y$ as fixed). This is the kernel of a $\text{Gamma}(y + 1, 1)$ distribution. So $X \mid Y = y \sim \text{Gamma}(y + 1, 1)$.

(c) The Gibbs sampler alternates:

  1. Given current $(x^{(t)}, y^{(t)})$, draw $y^{(t+1)} \sim \text{Poisson}(x^{(t)})$.
  2. Draw $x^{(t+1)} \sim \text{Gamma}(y^{(t+1)} + 1, 1)$.

Both conditionals are standard distributions that are easy to sample from, making this a natural fit for Gibbs sampling. The marginal distribution of $Y$ is $p(y) = \int_0^\infty e^{-x} x^y / y! \, dx = \Gamma(y+1)/(y! \cdot 1) = 1$... which suggests $p(x,y)$ is improper as stated. To make it proper, we can add a prior like $p(x) = e^{-x}$, giving $X \mid Y \sim \text{Gamma}(y+1, 2)$. $\square$

Exercise 9: Effective Sample Size

An MCMC chain of length $N = 10{,}000$ has autocorrelation $\rho_k \approx 0.95^k$ at lag $k$. (a) Compute the effective sample size $N_{\text{eff}} = N / (1 + 2\sum_{k=1}^\infty \rho_k)$. (b) How long should you run the chain to get $N_{\text{eff}} = 1{,}000$? (c) If a different sampler achieves $\rho_k \approx 0.5^k$, what is its $N_{\text{eff}}$ for the same $N = 10{,}000$?

Show Solution

(a) $\sum_{k=1}^\infty \rho_k = \sum_{k=1}^\infty 0.95^k = \frac{0.95}{1 - 0.95} = 19$.

$N_{\text{eff}} = \frac{10{,}000}{1 + 2 \times 19} = \frac{10{,}000}{39} \approx 256$.

So 10,000 correlated samples are worth only about 256 independent samples.

(b) We need $N_{\text{eff}} = N/39 = 1{,}000$, so $N = 39{,}000$.

(c) $\sum_{k=1}^\infty 0.5^k = \frac{0.5}{1-0.5} = 1$.

$N_{\text{eff}} = \frac{10{,}000}{1 + 2} = 3{,}333$.

The faster-mixing sampler ($\rho = 0.5$ vs $\rho = 0.95$) is 13 times more efficient. This illustrates why mixing rate is critical for MCMC performance.

Exercise 10: Self-Normalized Importance Sampling

You want to compute $E_p[X]$ where $p(x) \propto e^{-x^4}$ (an unnormalized density on $\mathbb{R}$). Using proposal $q = \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$: (a) Write the self-normalized importance sampling estimator. (b) Show that the unnormalized weights are $\tilde{w}(x) = \exp(-x^4 + x^2/2)$. (c) For which values of $x$ are the weights largest? Is this a good or bad proposal? Explain.

Show Solution

(a) Draw $x_1, \ldots, x_N \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. The self-normalized IS estimator is:

$$\hat{\mu} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N x_i \tilde{w}(x_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^N \tilde{w}(x_i)}, \quad \tilde{w}(x) = \frac{\tilde{p}(x)}{q(x)}$$

(b) $\tilde{w}(x) = \frac{e^{-x^4}}{(2\pi)^{-1/2}e^{-x^2/2}} \propto e^{-x^4 + x^2/2}$ (dropping the constant $(2\pi)^{1/2}$, which cancels in the ratio).

(c) $\tilde{w}(x)$ is maximized where $-x^4 + x^2/2$ is maximized. Taking the derivative: $-4x^3 + x = 0$, so $x = 0$ or $x = \pm 1/2$. The maximum is at $x = \pm 1/2$ with $\tilde{w} = e^{-1/16 + 1/8} = e^{1/16}$.

This is actually a good proposal because the target $p(x) \propto e^{-x^4}$ has lighter tails than the Gaussian (its tails decay as $e^{-x^4}$ vs $e^{-x^2/2}$). This means the weights $\tilde{w}(x)$ are bounded and well-behaved—no sample will have an enormous weight. The effective sample size will be close to $N$. (If the situation were reversed—heavy-tailed target with light-tailed proposal—we would get catastrophic weight degeneracy.)

Exercise 11: Proving Detailed Balance for Metropolis-Hastings

Give a rigorous proof that the Metropolis-Hastings transition kernel satisfies detailed balance with respect to $\pi$. Specifically: (a) Write the full transition kernel $T(x' \mid x)$ including both the acceptance and rejection components. (b) Show that $\pi(x)T(x'|x) = \pi(x')T(x|x')$ for $x' \neq x$. (c) Explain why detailed balance implies $\pi$ is a stationary distribution.

Show Solution

(a) The MH transition kernel is:

$$T(x' \mid x) = q(x' \mid x) \alpha(x', x) + \left(1 - \int q(y \mid x)\alpha(y, x) \, dy\right)\delta(x' - x)$$

where $\alpha(x', x) = \min\!\left(1, \frac{\pi(x')q(x \mid x')}{\pi(x)q(x' \mid x)}\right)$ and $\delta$ is the Dirac delta.

(b) For $x' \neq x$, the delta component vanishes. We need to show:

$$\pi(x) q(x'|x) \alpha(x',x) = \pi(x') q(x|x') \alpha(x, x')$$

Without loss of generality, assume $\pi(x')q(x|x') \geq \pi(x)q(x'|x)$. Then:

  • $\alpha(x', x) = 1$ (the move from $x$ to $x'$ is always accepted)
  • $\alpha(x, x') = \frac{\pi(x)q(x'|x)}{\pi(x')q(x|x')} \leq 1$

LHS: $\pi(x) q(x'|x) \cdot 1 = \pi(x) q(x'|x)$

RHS: $\pi(x') q(x|x') \cdot \frac{\pi(x)q(x'|x)}{\pi(x')q(x|x')} = \pi(x) q(x'|x)$ $\checkmark$

The case $\pi(x')q(x|x') < \pi(x)q(x'|x)$ is symmetric. In both cases, both sides equal $\min(\pi(x)q(x'|x), \pi(x')q(x|x'))$.

(c) Integrating detailed balance over $x$:

$$\int \pi(x) T(x'|x) \, dx = \int \pi(x') T(x|x') \, dx = \pi(x') \underbrace{\int T(x|x') \, dx}_{=1} = \pi(x')$$

So $\pi T = \pi$, meaning $\pi$ is a stationary distribution of the chain. $\square$

Exercise 12: Optimal Importance Sampling Proposal

Prove that for estimating $I = E_p[g(X)]$ with $g(X) \geq 0$, the variance-minimizing importance sampling proposal is $q^*(x) = g(x) p(x) / I$. Show that this proposal achieves zero variance. Then explain the paradox: if $q^*$ requires knowing $I$ (the quantity we want to estimate), how is this result useful in practice?

Show Solution

Setup: The IS estimator is $\hat{I} = \frac{1}{N}\sum g(x_i) w(x_i)$ where $w = p/q$ and $x_i \sim q$.

Variance of a single term: $\text{Var}_q[g(X)w(X)] = E_q[(g(X)w(X))^2] - I^2 = \int \frac{g(x)^2 p(x)^2}{q(x)} dx - I^2$.

Minimizing over $q$: We minimize $\int \frac{g(x)^2 p(x)^2}{q(x)} dx$ subject to $\int q(x) dx = 1$. By the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality (or Lagrange multipliers):

$$\int \frac{g(x)^2 p(x)^2}{q(x)} dx \geq \left(\int g(x) p(x) dx\right)^2 = I^2$$

with equality when $q(x) \propto g(x) p(x)$. Normalizing: $q^*(x) = g(x) p(x) / I$.

Zero variance: With $q = q^*$, each sample gives $g(x_i) w(x_i) = g(x_i) \frac{p(x_i)}{q^*(x_i)} = g(x_i) \frac{p(x_i)}{g(x_i) p(x_i) / I} = I$. Every sample returns exactly $I$, so the variance is zero.

Resolving the paradox: While $q^*$ itself requires knowing $I$, this result is useful because:

  1. It tells us the shape of a good proposal: concentrate mass where $|g(x)|p(x)$ is large.
  2. We can design parametric families $q_\theta$ that approximate $q^*$ and optimize $\theta$ (e.g., cross-entropy method, adaptive IS).
  3. It provides a lower bound on achievable variance, useful for benchmarking.
  4. In sequential settings, we can use a rough estimate of $I$ from previous rounds to improve the proposal iteratively.

Exercise 13: Hamiltonian Monte Carlo Derivation

Consider a target $\pi(\theta) \propto e^{-U(\theta)}$ with auxiliary momentum $p \sim \mathcal{N}(0, M)$. (a) Show that the joint distribution $\pi(\theta, p) \propto e^{-H(\theta,p)}$ marginalizes to $\pi(\theta)$. (b) Show that the leapfrog integrator is volume-preserving (the Jacobian determinant is 1). (c) Derive the MH acceptance probability for the HMC proposal and explain why it is close to 1 when the step size is small.

Show Solution

(a) $H(\theta, p) = U(\theta) + \frac{1}{2}p^T M^{-1} p$, so:

$$\pi(\theta, p) \propto e^{-H} = e^{-U(\theta)} \cdot e^{-\frac{1}{2}p^T M^{-1} p}$$

Marginalizing over $p$:

$$\int \pi(\theta, p) \, dp \propto e^{-U(\theta)} \underbrace{\int e^{-\frac{1}{2}p^T M^{-1} p} dp}_{= (2\pi)^{d/2}|M|^{1/2}} \propto e^{-U(\theta)} = \pi(\theta) \; \checkmark$$

(b) One leapfrog step: $p \gets p - \frac{\epsilon}{2}\nabla U$, $\theta \gets \theta + \epsilon M^{-1}p$, $p \gets p - \frac{\epsilon}{2}\nabla U$.

Each substep is a shear transformation. The Jacobian of $p \gets p - (\epsilon/2)\nabla U(\theta)$ (with $\theta$ fixed) is the identity (lower triangular with 1s on diagonal). The Jacobian of $\theta \gets \theta + \epsilon M^{-1}p$ (with $p$ fixed) is also identity. Composing: $|\det J| = 1 \cdot 1 \cdot 1 = 1$. $\square$

(c) Let $(\theta^*, p^*)$ be the leapfrog endpoint. The HMC proposal is $(\theta^*, -p^*)$ (momentum flip for reversibility). Since the transformation is volume-preserving and reversible:

$$\alpha = \min\!\left(1, \frac{\pi(\theta^*, p^*)}{\pi(\theta, p)}\right) = \min\!\left(1, e^{-H(\theta^*,p^*) + H(\theta,p)}\right) = \min\!\left(1, e^{-\Delta H}\right)$$

Exact Hamiltonian dynamics conserves $H$, so $\Delta H = 0$ and $\alpha = 1$. The leapfrog introduces discretization error $\Delta H = O(\epsilon^2)$ per step, or $O(L\epsilon^3)$ total (by the symplectic property). For small $\epsilon$, $|\Delta H| \ll 1$ and $\alpha \approx 1$.

Exercise 14: Rao-Blackwellization

Let $(X, Y)$ have joint distribution $\pi$ and suppose we run a Gibbs sampler producing pairs $(x_i, y_i)$. To estimate $E_\pi[h(X)]$, compare: (i) the standard estimator $\hat{\mu}_1 = \frac{1}{N}\sum h(x_i)$, and (ii) the Rao-Blackwellized estimator $\hat{\mu}_2 = \frac{1}{N}\sum E[h(X) \mid Y = y_i]$. Prove that $\text{Var}(\hat{\mu}_2) \leq \text{Var}(\hat{\mu}_1)$, with equality iff $h(X)$ is a function of $Y$ alone.

Show Solution

Both estimators are unbiased (by iterated expectation): $E[\hat{\mu}_2] = E[E[h(X)|Y]] = E[h(X)] = E[\hat{\mu}_1]$.

For a single sample, define $g(Y) = E[h(X)|Y]$. By the law of total variance:

$$\text{Var}(h(X)) = \text{Var}(E[h(X)|Y]) + E[\text{Var}(h(X)|Y)]$$ $$= \text{Var}(g(Y)) + E[\text{Var}(h(X)|Y)]$$

Since $E[\text{Var}(h(X)|Y)] \geq 0$:

$$\text{Var}(g(Y)) \leq \text{Var}(h(X))$$

This is the variance of a single term. For the sample means (accounting for the $1/N$ factor and autocorrelation structure, which is the same for both since the $y_i$'s are from the same chain):

More carefully, for MCMC samples the inequality still holds because the Rao-Blackwellized function $g(Y_i) = E[h(X)|Y_i]$ has both lower marginal variance AND the same autocorrelation structure (since it is a function of the same chain). Therefore:

$$\text{Var}(\hat{\mu}_2) = \frac{1}{N^2}\sum_{i,j}\text{Cov}(g(Y_i), g(Y_j)) \leq \frac{1}{N^2}\sum_{i,j}\text{Cov}(h(X_i), h(X_j)) = \text{Var}(\hat{\mu}_1)$$

Equality holds iff $\text{Var}(h(X)|Y) = 0$ a.s., meaning $h(X)$ is determined by $Y$, i.e., $h(X) = g(Y)$ a.s. $\square$

Practical implication: Whenever you have a Gibbs sampler with tractable conditional expectations, always use the Rao-Blackwellized estimator—it is guaranteed to have lower variance at no extra computational cost.

Exercise 15: Langevin Dynamics and Score Functions

The (unadjusted) Langevin algorithm generates samples via $\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \frac{\epsilon}{2}\nabla \log \pi(\theta_t) + \sqrt{\epsilon} \, z_t$ where $z_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$. (a) Show that as $\epsilon \to 0$, the continuous-time limit is the Langevin SDE $d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\nabla \log \pi(\theta)\,dt + dW_t$, whose stationary distribution is $\pi$. (b) The term $\nabla \log \pi(\theta)$ is called the score function. Explain its connection to score-based diffusion models in generative AI. (c) Why does the Metropolis-adjusted Langevin algorithm (MALA) add an acceptance step, and what does this correct?

Show Solution

(a) The discrete update $\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \frac{\epsilon}{2}\nabla\log\pi(\theta_t) + \sqrt{\epsilon}\,z_t$ is the Euler-Maruyama discretization of:

$$d\theta_t = \frac{1}{2}\nabla\log\pi(\theta_t)\,dt + dW_t$$

This is the overdamped Langevin SDE. Its Fokker-Planck equation has stationary solution $\pi(\theta)$ because the drift $\frac{1}{2}\nabla\log\pi = \frac{1}{2}\frac{\nabla\pi}{\pi}$ and diffusion coefficient $\frac{1}{2}$ satisfy the detailed balance condition for the Fokker-Planck equation:

$$\frac{1}{2}\nabla\log\pi(\theta)\cdot\pi(\theta) + \frac{1}{2}\nabla\pi(\theta) = \nabla\pi(\theta)$$

which equals $\frac{1}{2}\nabla \cdot [\pi(\theta) \cdot 1]$ (the divergence of the probability flux equals zero at stationarity). $\square$

(b) In score-based diffusion models (Song et al., 2021), a neural network $s_\theta(x, t) \approx \nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ is trained to estimate the score of the noised data distribution at each noise level $t$. The reverse diffusion process uses this estimated score to denoise:

$$dx = [-\frac{1}{2}\beta(t)x + \beta(t)s_\theta(x,t)]\,dt + \sqrt{\beta(t)}\,d\bar{W}_t$$

This is exactly a Langevin diffusion (with time-varying drift) that transports the noise distribution toward the data distribution. The connection is direct: sampling from a generative model = running Langevin dynamics with a learned score function.

(c) The unadjusted Langevin algorithm (ULA) has discretization bias: for finite $\epsilon$, its stationary distribution is $\pi_\epsilon \neq \pi$, with bias $O(\epsilon)$. MALA treats the Langevin step as a MH proposal:

$$q(\theta'|\theta) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(\theta + \frac{\epsilon}{2}\nabla\log\pi(\theta), \; \epsilon I\right)$$

and accepts with probability $\alpha = \min(1, \pi(\theta')q(\theta|\theta')/(\pi(\theta)q(\theta'|\theta)))$. This corrects the discretization error, ensuring the chain converges to exactly $\pi$ (not $\pi_\epsilon$). The cost is occasional rejections, but for well-tuned $\epsilon$ the acceptance rate is high (optimal $\approx 57\%$ in high dimensions). MALA achieves $O(d^{1/3})$ scaling vs $O(d)$ for random walk MH. $\square$
