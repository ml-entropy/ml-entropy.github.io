07. Probability Inequalities

In most real-world problems, computing exact probabilities is impossible or intractable. Probability inequalities give us bounds—guaranteed upper or lower limits on probabilities—using only limited information such as the mean, variance, or boundedness of a random variable. These tools are the backbone of statistical learning theory, concentration of measure, and algorithm analysis.

Why Inequalities?

Suppose you know only that a non-negative random variable $X$ has mean $\mu = 100$. Can you say anything about $P(X \geq 1000)$? Without knowing the full distribution, you cannot compute this exactly—but you can bound it. Markov's inequality will tell you $P(X \geq 1000) \leq 1/10$.

Probability inequalities answer a fundamental question: how much can a random variable deviate from its expected behavior? This question arises everywhere:

  • Tail bounds: What is the probability that a random variable takes an unusually large (or small) value? Critical for risk analysis and algorithm design.
  • Concentration of measure: When you average many independent random variables, the result concentrates tightly around the mean. How tightly?
  • Generalization bounds: In machine learning, how much can the test error differ from the training error? This is fundamentally a tail-bound question.
  • Sample complexity: How many samples do you need to estimate a parameter to a given accuracy with a given confidence level?

The inequalities in this tutorial form a hierarchy of increasing strength and specificity. Markov uses only the mean; Chebyshev adds variance; Chernoff and Hoeffding exploit moment generating functions and independence. The more you know about your random variable, the tighter the bound you can obtain.

Markov's Inequality

Markov's inequality is the simplest and most general tail bound. It requires only that $X$ be non-negative with a finite mean.

$$\text{If } X \geq 0 \text{ and } a > 0, \quad P(X \geq a) \leq \frac{E[X]}{a}$$

Proof (one line!):

Since $X \geq 0$, we have $E[X] = \int_0^{\infty} x \, f(x)\,dx \geq \int_a^{\infty} x \, f(x)\,dx \geq a \int_a^{\infty} f(x)\,dx = a \cdot P(X \geq a)$. Dividing both sides by $a$ gives the result. $\square$

Interpretation: If your average salary is \$50,000, then at most 1% of people earn \$5,000,000 or more. The mean limits how much probability mass can sit far in the tail.

Example: Let $X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$ with $E[X] = 1/\lambda$. Markov says $P(X \geq a) \leq \frac{1}{\lambda a}$. The exact answer is $e^{-\lambda a}$, which is much smaller for large $a$. This illustrates that Markov is loose but universally applicable.

Tightness: Markov's inequality is tight—there exist distributions that achieve equality. Consider $X$ that equals $a$ with probability $\mu/a$ and $0$ otherwise, where $E[X] = \mu$. Then $P(X \geq a) = \mu/a$ exactly.

Chebyshev's Inequality

When you know the variance in addition to the mean, you can get a much tighter bound. Chebyshev's inequality bounds the probability of deviating from the mean by $k$ standard deviations.

$$P(|X - \mu| \geq k\sigma) \leq \frac{1}{k^2}$$

Equivalently, for any $t > 0$:

$$P(|X - \mu| \geq t) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{t^2}$$

Proof from Markov: Let $Y = (X - \mu)^2$. Then $Y \geq 0$ and $E[Y] = \sigma^2$. Apply Markov's inequality to $Y$ with threshold $t^2$:

$$P(|X - \mu| \geq t) = P((X - \mu)^2 \geq t^2) = P(Y \geq t^2) \leq \frac{E[Y]}{t^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{t^2} \quad \square$$

Interpretation: Regardless of the distribution's shape, at most 25% of the probability can lie beyond 2 standard deviations from the mean, at most 11% beyond 3 standard deviations, and at most $1/k^2$ beyond $k$ standard deviations.

Comparison with the normal distribution: For a Gaussian, $P(|X - \mu| \geq 2\sigma) \approx 0.046$, which is much smaller than Chebyshev's bound of $0.25$. Chebyshev is distribution-free, so it must accommodate the worst case.

One-sided Chebyshev (Cantelli's inequality): If we only care about one tail:

$$P(X - \mu \geq t) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma^2 + t^2}$$

This is tighter than simply halving the two-sided bound. The proof uses the substitution $Y = X - \mu + c$ for an optimal $c$, then applies Markov to $Y^2$.

Jensen's Inequality

Jensen's inequality relates the expectation of a function to the function of an expectation. It is one of the most frequently used inequalities in probability, information theory, and optimization.

$$\text{If } g \text{ is convex:} \quad E[g(X)] \geq g(E[X])$$
$$\text{If } g \text{ is concave:} \quad E[g(X)] \leq g(E[X])$$

Proof sketch (convex case): By definition of convexity, for any point $x_0$ there exists a supporting hyperplane (tangent line) $\ell(x) = g(x_0) + g'(x_0)(x - x_0)$ such that $g(x) \geq \ell(x)$ for all $x$. Set $x_0 = E[X]$:

$$E[g(X)] \geq E[\ell(X)] = E[g(E[X]) + g'(E[X])(X - E[X])] = g(E[X]) + g'(E[X]) \cdot 0 = g(E[X]) \quad \square$$

Key applications:

  • AM-GM inequality: Since $\log$ is concave, $E[\log X] \leq \log E[X]$, which gives $(x_1 x_2 \cdots x_n)^{1/n} \leq \frac{x_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_n}{n}$.
  • Variance is non-negative: Since $x^2$ is convex, $E[X^2] \geq (E[X])^2$, hence $\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 \geq 0$.
  • KL divergence is non-negative: $D_{\text{KL}}(P \| Q) = E_P\left[\log \frac{P}{Q}\right] \geq 0$ follows from Jensen applied to $-\log$.
  • Log-sum inequality: For non-negative $a_i, b_i$: $\sum a_i \log \frac{a_i}{b_i} \geq \left(\sum a_i\right) \log \frac{\sum a_i}{\sum b_i}$.
  • EM algorithm: The Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) in variational inference is derived directly from Jensen's inequality applied to $\log p(x)$.

When does equality hold? For a strictly convex $g$, equality in Jensen's inequality holds if and only if $X$ is a constant (i.e., $\text{Var}(X) = 0$), or $g$ is affine on the support of $X$.

Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality bounds the correlation between two random variables and is fundamental in linear algebra, statistics, and functional analysis.

$$(E[XY])^2 \leq E[X^2] \cdot E[Y^2]$$

Equivalently, $|E[XY]| \leq \sqrt{E[X^2]} \cdot \sqrt{E[Y^2]}$, i.e., the inner product of two random variables is bounded by the product of their $L^2$ norms.

Proof: For any $t \in \mathbb{R}$, $E[(X + tY)^2] \geq 0$ since it is an expectation of a non-negative quantity. Expanding:

$$E[X^2] + 2t \, E[XY] + t^2 E[Y^2] \geq 0$$

This is a quadratic in $t$ that is always non-negative, so its discriminant must be non-positive: $(2 E[XY])^2 - 4 E[X^2] E[Y^2] \leq 0$, which gives $(E[XY])^2 \leq E[X^2] E[Y^2]$. $\square$

Important consequence—correlation bound: For any random variables $X, Y$ with finite variances, the correlation coefficient satisfies $|\rho(X, Y)| \leq 1$. This follows directly from Cauchy-Schwarz applied to the centered variables $X - E[X]$ and $Y - E[Y]$.

Application in ML: The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality is used to prove that the cosine similarity between any two vectors lies in $[-1, 1]$, which underlies many similarity metrics in NLP and recommendation systems.

Chernoff Bound

The Chernoff bound is the most powerful general-purpose tail bound. It uses the moment generating function (MGF) to achieve exponentially tight tail bounds. This is a vast improvement over Markov (polynomial in $1/a$) and Chebyshev ($1/t^2$).

$$P(X \geq a) \leq \min_{t > 0} \, e^{-ta} \, E[e^{tX}] = \min_{t > 0} \, e^{-ta} \, M_X(t)$$

Derivation: For any $t > 0$, $X \geq a$ if and only if $e^{tX} \geq e^{ta}$ (since $e^{t \cdot}$ is monotone increasing). Apply Markov to the non-negative random variable $e^{tX}$:

$$P(X \geq a) = P(e^{tX} \geq e^{ta}) \leq \frac{E[e^{tX}]}{e^{ta}} = e^{-ta} M_X(t)$$

Since this holds for all $t > 0$, we optimize over $t$ to get the tightest bound. $\square$

Example: Sum of Bernoulli random variables. Let $X = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ where $X_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$ are independent. Then $E[X] = np$, and for $a = (1+\delta)np$:

$$P(X \geq (1+\delta)np) \leq \left(\frac{e^{\delta}}{(1+\delta)^{(1+\delta)}}\right)^{np}$$

This bound decreases exponentially in $n$, showing that the sum concentrates sharply around its mean as $n$ grows.

Why "exponential tightness" matters: For large deviations, polynomial bounds like Chebyshev ($\sim 1/n$) are far too loose. Chernoff gives bounds like $e^{-cn}$, which is essentially zero for large $n$. This is why Chernoff bounds are the tool of choice in algorithm analysis, information theory, and statistical learning theory.

Left tail: The same technique works for the lower tail: for $t < 0$, $P(X \leq a) \leq e^{-ta} M_X(t)$, and we minimize over $t < 0$.

Hoeffding's Inequality

Hoeffding's inequality is the workhorse of statistical learning theory. It applies to sums of bounded, independent random variables and gives explicit, easy-to-use exponential tail bounds.

Hoeffding's Lemma: If $a \leq X \leq b$ and $E[X] = 0$, then for all $t > 0$:

$$E[e^{tX}] \leq \exp\!\left(\frac{t^2(b-a)^2}{8}\right)$$

Hoeffding's Inequality: Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be independent random variables with $a_i \leq X_i \leq b_i$. Let $\bar{X} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ and $\mu = E[\bar{X}]$. Then:

$$P(|\bar{X} - \mu| \geq t) \leq 2\exp\!\left(-\frac{2n^2 t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n (b_i - a_i)^2}\right)$$

In the common case where all $X_i \in [a, b]$:

$$P(|\bar{X} - \mu| \geq t) \leq 2\exp\!\left(-\frac{2nt^2}{(b-a)^2}\right)$$

Proof outline:

  1. Start with $P(\bar{X} - \mu \geq t) = P\left(\sum_i (X_i - \mu_i) \geq nt\right)$.
  2. Apply the Chernoff technique: for any $s > 0$, $P\left(\sum_i (X_i - \mu_i) \geq nt\right) \leq e^{-snt} \prod_i E[e^{s(X_i - \mu_i)}]$ (independence!).
  3. Apply Hoeffding's Lemma to each factor: $E[e^{s(X_i - \mu_i)}] \leq e^{s^2(b_i - a_i)^2/8}$.
  4. Combine: $P \leq \exp\!\left(-snt + \frac{s^2}{8}\sum_i(b_i - a_i)^2\right)$.
  5. Optimize over $s$: set $s^* = \frac{4nt}{\sum_i(b_i - a_i)^2}$ to get the result.
  6. Apply the same argument to $-\bar{X}$ for the other tail, then union-bound the two tails. $\square$

Sample complexity consequence: If $X_i \in [0,1]$ are i.i.d., to ensure $P(|\bar{X} - \mu| \geq \epsilon) \leq \delta$, we need:

$$n \geq \frac{\ln(2/\delta)}{2\epsilon^2}$$

For example, to estimate a probability to within $\pm 0.01$ with 95% confidence, you need $n \geq \frac{\ln 40}{2 \cdot 0.0001} \approx 18{,}445$ samples.

Union Bound (Boole's Inequality)

The union bound is deceptively simple but extraordinarily useful. It says that the probability of at least one of several events occurring is at most the sum of their individual probabilities.

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^n A_i\right) \leq \sum_{i=1}^n P(A_i)$$

Proof: This follows immediately from the inclusion-exclusion principle by dropping all the negative terms (which only decrease the sum): $P(\bigcup A_i) = \sum P(A_i) - \sum_{i < j} P(A_i \cap A_j) + \cdots \leq \sum P(A_i)$. $\square$

When to use: The union bound is the tool you reach for when you want to argue that "nothing goes wrong" across many events simultaneously. In ML, this is essential for:

  • Uniform convergence: Bounding $P(\exists h \in \mathcal{H}: |\hat{R}(h) - R(h)| \geq \epsilon)$ by $\sum_h P(|\hat{R}(h) - R(h)| \geq \epsilon)$.
  • Multiple hypothesis testing: If you run 100 tests at significance level 0.05, the probability of at least one false positive is at most $100 \times 0.05 = 5$ (trivially bounded by 1). The Bonferroni correction sets each test to level $\alpha/n$ to control the family-wise error rate.
  • Algorithm analysis: Showing that a randomized algorithm succeeds with high probability by enumerating all failure modes and bounding each.

Strength and weakness: The union bound is tight when events are nearly disjoint (low overlap). It becomes very loose when events are highly correlated.

Bonferroni Inequalities

The Bonferroni inequalities generalize the union bound by using higher-order terms from the inclusion-exclusion formula. They provide alternating upper and lower bounds.

Recall the full inclusion-exclusion formula:

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^n A_i\right) = \sum_{k=1}^n (-1)^{k+1} S_k, \quad \text{where } S_k = \sum_{1 \leq i_1 < \cdots < i_k \leq n} P(A_{i_1} \cap \cdots \cap A_{i_k})$$

The Bonferroni inequalities state that truncating this alternating sum at an odd index gives an upper bound, and truncating at an even index gives a lower bound:

$$\text{Upper (odd } m \text{):} \quad P\!\left(\bigcup A_i\right) \leq \sum_{k=1}^m (-1)^{k+1} S_k$$
$$\text{Lower (even } m \text{):} \quad P\!\left(\bigcup A_i\right) \geq \sum_{k=1}^m (-1)^{k+1} S_k$$

Special cases:

  • $m = 1$ (upper): $P(\bigcup A_i) \leq S_1 = \sum P(A_i)$ — this is the union bound.
  • $m = 2$ (lower): $P(\bigcup A_i) \geq S_1 - S_2 = \sum P(A_i) - \sum_{i
  • $m = 3$ (upper): $P(\bigcup A_i) \leq S_1 - S_2 + S_3$, a tighter upper bound.

Each additional term of inclusion-exclusion produces a tighter bound, alternating between over- and under-estimation. In practice, going beyond $m = 2$ or $m = 3$ is rare because computing higher-order intersection probabilities is usually difficult.

Concentration Inequalities in ML

The inequalities we have studied form the mathematical foundation of statistical learning theory. Here we connect them to core ML concepts.

PAC Learning Bounds

In the Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) framework, we want to find a hypothesis $h$ whose true risk $R(h) = P(h(X) \neq Y)$ is close to optimal. Using only the empirical risk $\hat{R}(h)$ computed from $n$ training samples:

$$P\!\left(\sup_{h \in \mathcal{H}} |R(h) - \hat{R}(h)| \geq \epsilon\right) \leq 2|\mathcal{H}| \exp(-2n\epsilon^2)$$

This combines Hoeffding's inequality (for each fixed $h$) with the union bound (over all $h \in \mathcal{H}$). It tells us that with $n = O\!\left(\frac{1}{\epsilon^2}\left(\log|\mathcal{H}| + \log \frac{1}{\delta}\right)\right)$ samples, the empirical risk uniformly approximates the true risk.

VC Dimension Connection

For infinite hypothesis classes, $|\mathcal{H}| = \infty$ and the simple union bound over hypotheses fails. The Vapnik-Chervonenkis (VC) dimension $d_{\text{VC}}$ provides a finite measure of hypothesis class complexity. The key result replaces $\log |\mathcal{H}|$ with $d_{\text{VC}} \log(n/d_{\text{VC}})$:

$$P\!\left(\sup_{h \in \mathcal{H}} |R(h) - \hat{R}(h)| \geq \epsilon\right) \leq 4 \, \Pi_{\mathcal{H}}(2n) \, e^{-n\epsilon^2/8}$$

where $\Pi_{\mathcal{H}}(n)$ is the growth function, bounded by $\Pi_{\mathcal{H}}(n) \leq \left(\frac{en}{d_{\text{VC}}}\right)^{d_{\text{VC}}}$ by the Sauer-Shelah lemma.

Sample Complexity

Solving for $n$ in the PAC bound gives us the sample complexity—the number of examples needed to learn. For a finite hypothesis class:

$$n \geq \frac{1}{2\epsilon^2}\left(\ln|\mathcal{H}| + \ln\frac{2}{\delta}\right)$$

This is logarithmic in $|\mathcal{H}|$, meaning that even for exponentially large hypothesis classes, the sample complexity grows only linearly in the number of parameters.

Regularization and Complexity Control

The generalization bounds above reveal a fundamental trade-off: a larger hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ can fit the training data better (lower empirical risk) but has a larger complexity term (wider gap between empirical and true risk). Regularization in ML (e.g., L2 penalty, dropout, early stopping) can be understood as controlling the effective hypothesis class complexity to tighten these concentration bounds.

Summary and Comparison

Each inequality has its own assumptions, strength, and domain of applicability. The following table provides a quick reference.

Inequality Requires Bound Type Tightness
Markov $X \geq 0$, finite $E[X]$ $O(1/a)$ Loose; tight in worst case
Chebyshev Finite mean, variance $O(1/t^2)$ Better; tight for 2-point dist.
Chernoff Finite MGF $e^{-\Theta(t)}$ Exponentially tight
Hoeffding Bounded, independent RVs $e^{-\Theta(nt^2)}$ Excellent for bounded sums
Jensen Convex/concave function Expectation inequality Tight when $X$ is constant
Cauchy-Schwarz Finite second moments Product bound on $E[XY]$ Tight when $X \propto Y$
Union Bound Any events $\leq \sum P(A_i)$ Tight for disjoint events

Figure 3. Comparison of tail bound decay rates. Chernoff bounds decay exponentially, vastly outperforming Markov ($1/a$) and Chebyshev ($1/a^2$) for large deviations.

Practical guidelines for choosing an inequality:

  • Know only the mean and $X \geq 0$? Use Markov.
  • Know the mean and variance? Use Chebyshev.
  • Have bounded, independent random variables? Use Hoeffding.
  • Can compute the MGF? Use Chernoff for the tightest bound.
  • Need to bound a union of events? Use the union bound, or Bonferroni if you can compute pairwise intersections.
  • Have a convex/concave function of an expectation? Use Jensen.
  • Bounding a covariance or inner product? Use Cauchy-Schwarz.

1. Verifying Markov and Chebyshev Inequalities

import numpy as np

def verify_markov_chebyshev(dist_name, samples, true_mean, true_var):
    """
    Empirically verify Markov and Chebyshev bounds.

    Parameters:
        dist_name: name of the distribution (for display)
        samples: array of samples from the distribution
        true_mean: E[X]
        true_var: Var(X)
    """
    n = len(samples)
    true_std = np.sqrt(true_var)

    print(f"=== {dist_name} ===")
    print(f"  E[X] = {true_mean:.4f}, Var(X) = {true_var:.4f}, Std(X) = {true_std:.4f}")
    print(f"  n = {n:,} samples\n")

    # Markov's inequality: P(X >= a) <= E[X] / a
    print("  Markov's Inequality (X >= 0 required):")
    for mult in [2, 5, 10]:
        a = mult * true_mean
        markov_bound = 1.0 / mult  # E[X] / a = E[X] / (mult * E[X])
        empirical = np.mean(samples >= a)
        print(f"    a = {mult}*E[X] = {a:.2f}: "
              f"Markov <= {markov_bound:.4f}, "
              f"Empirical = {empirical:.4f}")

    print()

    # Chebyshev's inequality: P(|X - mu| >= k*sigma) <= 1/k^2
    print("  Chebyshev's Inequality:")
    for k in [1, 2, 3, 5]:
        t = k * true_std
        chebyshev_bound = min(1.0, 1.0 / k**2)
        empirical = np.mean(np.abs(samples - true_mean) >= t)
        print(f"    k = {k}: "
              f"Chebyshev <= {chebyshev_bound:.4f}, "
              f"Empirical = {empirical:.4f}")
    print()

2. Jensen's Inequality Demonstration

import numpy as np

def demonstrate_jensen(samples, func, func_name, is_convex=True):
    """
    Demonstrate Jensen's inequality: E[g(X)] vs g(E[X]).

    For convex g: E[g(X)] >= g(E[X])
    For concave g: E[g(X)] <= g(E[X])
    """
    mean_X = np.mean(samples)
    E_gX = np.mean(func(samples))
    g_EX = func(mean_X)

    inequality_type = ">=" if is_convex else "<="
    satisfied = E_gX >= g_EX if is_convex else E_gX <= g_EX

    print(f"g(x) = {func_name} ({'convex' if is_convex else 'concave'})")
    print(f"  E[g(X)] = {E_gX:.6f}")
    print(f"  g(E[X]) = {g_EX:.6f}")
    print(f"  E[g(X)] {inequality_type} g(E[X])? {satisfied}")
    print(f"  Gap: E[g(X)] - g(E[X]) = {E_gX - g_EX:.6f}")
    print()

3. Chernoff Bound for Sum of Bernoullis

import numpy as np
from scipy import optimize

def chernoff_bernoulli_sum(n, p, a):
    """
    Compute the Chernoff bound for P(S_n >= a) where
    S_n = X_1 + ... + X_n, X_i ~ Bernoulli(p).

    The MGF of S_n is M(t) = (1 - p + p*e^t)^n.
    Chernoff bound: P(S_n >= a) <= min_{t>0} e^{-ta} * M(t)
    """
    mu = n * p

    if a <= mu:
        return 1.0  # Bound is trivially 1

    def bound_func(t):
        """The Chernoff bound as a function of t > 0."""
        log_bound = -t * a + n * np.log(1 - p + p * np.exp(t))
        return np.exp(log_bound)

    # Optimize over t > 0
    result = optimize.minimize_scalar(bound_func, bounds=(1e-10, 20),
                                       method='bounded')
    return result.fun

import numpy as np

def hoeffding_bound(n, t, a=0, b=1):
    """
    Hoeffding's bound for P(|X_bar - mu| >= t)
    where X_i in [a, b] are iid.

    P(|X_bar - mu| >= t) <= 2 * exp(-2*n*t^2 / (b-a)^2)
    """
    return 2 * np.exp(-2 * n * t**2 / (b - a)**2)


def verify_hoeffding(n_values, t, dist_sampler, true_mean, a=0, b=1,
                     dist_name="", n_trials=50_000):
    """
    Compare Hoeffding bound with empirical tail probability
    for different sample sizes.
    """

    print(f"True mean = {true_mean:.4f}, Range = [{a}, {b}]")
    print(f"Deviation threshold t = {t}\n")

    print(header)

By Jensen's inequality for convex functions:

Therefore $\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 \geq 0$. $\square$

Answer: Apply Jensen to the convex function $g(x) = x^2$.

Exercise 18: Jensen and KL Divergence

Let $P$ and $Q$ be two probability distributions over the same finite sample space. Using Jensen's inequality, prove that the KL divergence $D_{\text{KL}}(P \| Q) \geq 0$, where:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(P \| Q) = \sum_x P(x) \ln \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$$
Show Solution

We can write:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(P \| Q) = -\sum_x P(x) \ln \frac{Q(x)}{P(x)} = -E_P\!\left[\ln \frac{Q(X)}{P(X)}\right]$$

Since $\ln$ is concave, by Jensen's inequality:

$$E_P\!\left[\ln \frac{Q(X)}{P(X)}\right] \leq \ln\!\left(E_P\!\left[\frac{Q(X)}{P(X)}\right]\right)$$

Now compute the expectation on the right:

$$E_P\!\left[\frac{Q(X)}{P(X)}\right] = \sum_x P(x) \cdot \frac{Q(x)}{P(x)} = \sum_x Q(x) = 1$$

Therefore:

$$E_P\!\left[\ln \frac{Q(X)}{P(X)}\right] \leq \ln(1) = 0$$ $$D_{\text{KL}}(P \| Q) = -E_P\!\left[\ln \frac{Q(X)}{P(X)}\right] \geq 0$$

Answer: $D_{\text{KL}}(P \| Q) \geq 0$ with equality if and only if $P = Q$ a.s. This is known as Gibbs' inequality. $\square$

Exercise 21: Prove Cantelli's Inequality

Prove the one-sided Chebyshev inequality (Cantelli's inequality): For a random variable $X$ with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, show that for any $t > 0$:

$$P(X - \mu \geq t) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma^2 + t^2}$$
Show Solution

Without loss of generality, let $\mu = 0$ (replace $X$ with $X - \mu$). We want to show $P(X \geq t) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma^2 + t^2}$.

Step 1: For any $c > 0$, note that $X \geq t$ implies $X + c \geq t + c > 0$, so $(X + c)^2 \geq (t + c)^2$.

Step 2: Since $(X + c)^2 \geq 0$ always, Markov gives:

$$P(X \geq t) = P((X+c)^2 \geq (t+c)^2) \leq \frac{E[(X+c)^2]}{(t+c)^2}$$

Step 3: Expand the numerator: $E[(X+c)^2] = E[X^2] + 2cE[X] + c^2 = \sigma^2 + 0 + c^2 = \sigma^2 + c^2$.

So $P(X \geq t) \leq \frac{\sigma^2 + c^2}{(t + c)^2}$.

Step 4: Minimize over $c > 0$. Take derivative and set to 0:

$$\frac{d}{dc}\frac{\sigma^2 + c^2}{(t+c)^2} = \frac{2c(t+c)^2 - 2(t+c)(\sigma^2 + c^2)}{(t+c)^4} = 0$$ $$2c(t+c) = 2(\sigma^2 + c^2) \implies ct = \sigma^2 \implies c^* = \sigma^2/t$$

Step 5: Substitute $c^* = \sigma^2/t$:

$$\frac{\sigma^2 + \sigma^4/t^2}{(t + \sigma^2/t)^2} = \frac{\sigma^2(t^2 + \sigma^2)/t^2}{(t^2 + \sigma^2)^2/t^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{t^2 + \sigma^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma^2 + t^2}$$

$\square$

Exercise 22: Hoeffding's Lemma

Prove Hoeffding's Lemma: If $a \leq X \leq b$ and $E[X] = 0$, then for all $s > 0$:

$$E[e^{sX}] \leq \exp\!\left(\frac{s^2(b-a)^2}{8}\right)$$

Hint: Use the convexity of $e^{sx}$ to write $e^{sX} \leq \frac{b - X}{b-a}e^{sa} + \frac{X - a}{b-a}e^{sb}$, then take expectations and apply $\ln(1+u) \leq u$.

Show Solution

Step 1 (Convexity bound): Since $e^{sx}$ is convex and $X \in [a, b]$, writing $X = \frac{b-X}{b-a}\cdot a + \frac{X-a}{b-a}\cdot b$:

$$e^{sX} \leq \frac{b - X}{b-a}e^{sa} + \frac{X - a}{b-a}e^{sb}$$

Step 2 (Take expectations): Using $E[X] = 0$:

$$E[e^{sX}] \leq \frac{b}{b-a}e^{sa} + \frac{-a}{b-a}e^{sb}$$

Let $p = \frac{-a}{b-a}$ and $h = s(b-a)$, so $1-p = \frac{b}{b-a}$. Then:

$$E[e^{sX}] \leq (1-p)e^{-ph} + pe^{(1-p)h} = e^{L(h)}$$

where $L(h) = -ph + \ln(1 - p + pe^h)$.

Step 3 (Bound $L(h)$): Note $L(0) = 0$ and $L'(0) = 0$. By Taylor's theorem with remainder, $L(h) = \frac{h^2}{2}L''(\xi)$ for some $\xi \in [0, h]$.

We have $L''(h) = \frac{pe^h(1-p+pe^h) - p^2e^{2h}}{(1-p+pe^h)^2} = \frac{pe^h(1-p)}{(1-p+pe^h)^2}$.

Setting $u = \frac{pe^h}{1-p+pe^h} \in (0,1)$, we get $L''(h) = u(1-u) \leq 1/4$ (since $u(1-u) \leq 1/4$ for all $u$).

Step 4: Therefore $L(h) \leq \frac{h^2}{8} = \frac{s^2(b-a)^2}{8}$, giving:

$$E[e^{sX}] \leq \exp\!\left(\frac{s^2(b-a)^2}{8}\right) \quad \square$$

Exercise 24: PAC Learning Bound

Consider a finite hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ with $|\mathcal{H}| = m$. Each hypothesis $h \in \mathcal{H}$ has true error $R(h) = P(h(X) \neq Y)$ and empirical error $\hat{R}(h) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}[h(x_i) \neq y_i]$ on $n$ i.i.d. samples. Show that:

$$P\!\left(\exists h \in \mathcal{H}: |R(h) - \hat{R}(h)| \geq \epsilon\right) \leq 2m \exp(-2n\epsilon^2)$$

and derive the sample complexity $n(\epsilon, \delta)$ needed so that the above probability is at most $\delta$.

Show Solution

Step 1 (Hoeffding for a single hypothesis): For a fixed $h$, the indicators $Z_i = \mathbf{1}[h(x_i) \neq y_i]$ are i.i.d. Bernoulli with $E[Z_i] = R(h)$, and $Z_i \in [0, 1]$. By Hoeffding's inequality:

$$P(|R(h) - \hat{R}(h)| \geq \epsilon) \leq 2\exp(-2n\epsilon^2)$$

Step 2 (Union bound):

$$P(\exists h \in \mathcal{H}: |R(h) - \hat{R}(h)| \geq \epsilon) = P\!\left(\bigcup_{h \in \mathcal{H}} \{|R(h) - \hat{R}(h)| \geq \epsilon\}\right)$$ $$\leq \sum_{h \in \mathcal{H}} P(|R(h) - \hat{R}(h)| \geq \epsilon) \leq m \cdot 2\exp(-2n\epsilon^2) = 2m\exp(-2n\epsilon^2)$$

Step 3 (Sample complexity): Set $2m\exp(-2n\epsilon^2) \leq \delta$:

$$\exp(-2n\epsilon^2) \leq \frac{\delta}{2m}$$ $$-2n\epsilon^2 \leq \ln\frac{\delta}{2m}$$ $$n \geq \frac{1}{2\epsilon^2}\ln\frac{2m}{\delta} = \frac{1}{2\epsilon^2}\left(\ln m + \ln\frac{2}{\delta}\right)$$

Answer: $n(\epsilon, \delta) = \frac{1}{2\epsilon^2}\left(\ln|\mathcal{H}| + \ln\frac{2}{\delta}\right)$.

Key insight: sample complexity is logarithmic in $|\mathcal{H}|$, so even exponentially large hypothesis classes (e.g., $|\mathcal{H}| = 2^d$ for $d$ parameters) require only $O(d/\epsilon^2)$ samples. $\square$

Exercise 25: Tight Example for Chebyshev

Construct a distribution for which Chebyshev's inequality is tight. That is, find a random variable $X$ with $E[X] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2$ such that for some specific $k > 1$:

$$P(|X - \mu| \geq k\sigma) = \frac{1}{k^2}$$

Verify that your construction works and explain why this is essentially the unique tight example.

Show Solution

Construction: Let $X$ take three values:

$$X = \begin{cases} \mu - k\sigma & \text{with probability } \frac{1}{2k^2} \\ \mu & \text{with probability } 1 - \frac{1}{k^2} \\ \mu + k\sigma & \text{with probability } \frac{1}{2k^2} \end{cases}$$

Verification of mean:

$$E[X] = (\mu - k\sigma)\frac{1}{2k^2} + \mu\left(1 - \frac{1}{k^2}\right) + (\mu + k\sigma)\frac{1}{2k^2}$$ $$= \mu\left(\frac{1}{2k^2} + 1 - \frac{1}{k^2} + \frac{1}{2k^2}\right) + k\sigma\left(-\frac{1}{2k^2} + \frac{1}{2k^2}\right) = \mu \cdot 1 + 0 = \mu \checkmark$$

Verification of variance:

$$E[(X - \mu)^2] = (k\sigma)^2 \cdot \frac{1}{2k^2} + 0 + (k\sigma)^2 \cdot \frac{1}{2k^2} = k^2\sigma^2 \cdot \frac{1}{k^2} = \sigma^2 \checkmark$$

Verification of tightness:

$$P(|X - \mu| \geq k\sigma) = P(X = \mu - k\sigma) + P(X = \mu + k\sigma) = \frac{1}{2k^2} + \frac{1}{2k^2} = \frac{1}{k^2} \checkmark$$

Any mass at a distance greater than $k\sigma$ would increase the variance beyond $\sigma^2$ without increasing $P(|X - \mu| \geq k\sigma)$, and any mass at a smaller distance would not contribute to the tail. Thus the three-point distribution above (or its variants with mass only on one side) is essentially the unique extremal example. $\square$

$$E[X] \geq a \cdot E[\mathbf{1}_{X \geq a}] = a \cdot P(X \geq a)$$

and explain the connection to Hoeffding's inequality.

$$|f(x_1, \ldots, x_i, \ldots, x_n) - f(x_1, \ldots, x_i', \ldots, x_n)| \leq c_i$$

If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent, prove McDiarmid's inequality:

$$P(f(X_1, \ldots, X_n) - E[f] \geq t) \leq \exp\!\left(-\frac{2t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n c_i^2}\right)$$

Hint: Use a martingale decomposition and Hoeffding's lemma on each increment.

Show Solution

Step 1 (Doob martingale): Define $Z_0 = E[f]$, and for $k = 1, \ldots, n$:

$$Z_k = E[f(X_1, \ldots, X_n) \mid X_1, \ldots, X_k]$$

Then $Z_n = f(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ and $\{Z_k\}$ is a martingale. Let $D_k = Z_k - Z_{k-1}$.

Step 2 (Bound each increment): We have $E[D_k \mid X_1, \ldots, X_{k-1}] = 0$. By the bounded differences property, $Z_k$ can change by at most $c_k$ when we reveal $X_k$, so $D_k \in [a_k, b_k]$ with $b_k - a_k \leq c_k$.

$$P(f - E[f] \geq t) = P\!\left(\sum_{k=1}^n D_k \geq t\right) = P\!\left(e^{s\sum D_k} \geq e^{st}\right) \leq e^{-st} E\!\left[e^{s\sum D_k}\right]$$

$$E\!\left[e^{s\sum D_k}\right] = E\!\left[e^{s\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}D_k} \cdot E[e^{sD_n} \mid X_1, \ldots, X_{n-1}]\right]$$

$$E[e^{sD_n} \mid X_1, \ldots, X_{n-1}] \leq e^{s^2 c_n^2/8}$$

$$E\!\left[e^{s\sum D_k}\right] \leq \exp\!\left(\frac{s^2}{8}\sum_{k=1}^n c_k^2\right)$$

$$P(f - E[f] \geq t) \leq \exp\!\left(-\frac{2t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n c_i^2}\right) \quad \square$$

Exercise 29: Symmetrization and Rademacher Complexity

$$E\!\left[\sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \left(E[f(X)] - \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n f(X_i)\right)\right] \leq 2\, E\!\left[\sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \epsilon_i f(X_i)\right]$$

$$E\!\left[\sup_f \left(E[f] - \frac{1}{n}\sum_i f(X_i)\right)\right] = E\!\left[\sup_f \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \left(E_{X_i'}[f(X_i')] - f(X_i)\right)\right]$$

$$\leq E_{X, X'}\!\left[\sup_f \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \left(f(X_i') - f(X_i)\right)\right]$$

$$E_{X, X'}\!\left[\sup_f \frac{1}{n}\sum_i (f(X_i') - f(X_i))\right] = E_{\epsilon, X, X'}\!\left[\sup_f \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \epsilon_i(f(X_i') - f(X_i))\right]$$

$$P\!\left(g - E[g] \geq t\right) \leq \exp\!\left(-\frac{2t^2}{n \cdot (1/n)^2}\right) = \exp(-2nt^2)$$

Exercise 30: Bernstein's Inequality

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be independent zero-mean random variables with $|X_i| \leq M$ almost surely. Prove Bernstein's inequality:

$$P\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \geq t\right) \leq \exp\!\left(-\frac{t^2/2}{\sum_{i=1}^n E[X_i^2] + Mt/3}\right)$$

Hint: Use the bound $E[e^{sX_i}] \leq \exp\!\left(\frac{s^2 E[X_i^2]}{2(1 - s M/3)}\right)$ for $0 < s < 3/M$, which follows from bounding higher moments using $E[|X_i|^k] \leq \frac{k!}{2} E[X_i^2] M^{k-2}$.

$$E[e^{sX_i}] = 1 + \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \frac{s^k E[X_i^k]}{k!}$$

$$E[e^{sX_i}] \leq 1 + \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \frac{s^k E[X_i^2] M^{k-2}}{k!} \leq 1 + \frac{s^2 E[X_i^2]}{2} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(sM)^k}{3^k}$$

$$E[e^{sX_i}] \leq 1 + \frac{s^2 E[X_i^2]}{2(1 - sM/3)} \leq \exp\!\left(\frac{s^2 E[X_i^2]}{2(1 - sM/3)}\right)$$

$$P\!\left(\sum X_i \geq t\right) \leq e^{-st} \prod_{i=1}^n E[e^{sX_i}] \leq \exp\!\left(-st + \frac{s^2 v}{2(1 - sM/3)}\right)$$

$$g(s^*) = -\frac{t^2}{v + Mt/3} + \frac{t^2 v}{2(v + Mt/3)^2 \cdot \left(1 - \frac{Mt}{3(v + Mt/3)}\right)}$$

$$g(s^*) = -\frac{t^2}{v + Mt/3} + \frac{t^2 v}{2(v + Mt/3)^2} \cdot \frac{v + Mt/3}{v} = -\frac{t^2}{v + Mt/3} + \frac{t^2}{2(v + Mt/3)}$$ $$= -\frac{t^2}{2(v + Mt/3)} = -\frac{t^2/2}{v + Mt/3}$$

Therefore:

$$P\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \geq t\right) \leq \exp\!\left(-\frac{t^2/2}{\sum_{i=1}^n E[X_i^2] + Mt/3}\right) \quad \square$$

Key insight: Bernstein interpolates between two regimes. For small $t$ (relative to $v/M$), the bound behaves like $e^{-t^2/(2v)}$ (sub-Gaussian, similar to Hoeffding). For large $t$, it behaves like $e^{-3t/(2M)}$ (sub-exponential). This makes Bernstein tighter than Hoeffding when the variance $v$ is much smaller than the worst case.

Key Concept

Probability inequalities let us bound probabilities without computing exact distributions. They trade generality for tightness: the more you know about a random variable (mean, variance, boundedness, MGF), the tighter the bound you can obtain.

Prerequisites

  • Expectation and variance
  • Convexity of functions
  • Moment generating functions (for Chernoff)
  • Independence of random variables

Key Formulas

Markov: $P(X \geq a) \leq \frac{E[X]}{a}$

Chebyshev: $P(|X - \mu| \geq k\sigma) \leq \frac{1}{k^2}$

Jensen: $E[g(X)] \geq g(E[X])$ for convex $g$

Chernoff: $P(X \geq a) \leq \min_{t>0} e^{-ta} M_X(t)$

Hoeffding: $P(|\bar{X} - \mu| \geq t) \leq 2e^{-2nt^2/(b-a)^2}$
