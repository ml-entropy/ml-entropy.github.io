The Chain Rule

If $y = f(u)$ and $u = g(x)$, then:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$$

This simple rule, applied recursively, is the entire basis of backpropagation.

Multivariate Chain Rule

If $y$ depends on $u_1, \ldots, u_n$ and each $u_i$ depends on $x$: $$\frac{\partial y}{\partial x} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\partial y}{\partial u_i} \cdot \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x}$$

Computational Graphs

A neural network is a computational graph—a directed acyclic graph where nodes are operations and edges carry values.

Forward pass: Compute outputs from inputs, left to right.
Backward pass: Compute gradients from outputs to inputs, right to left.

Key Insight

Each node only needs to know its local gradient—how its output changes with respect to its inputs. The chain rule combines these local gradients into global gradients.

Forward and Backward Pass

Consider a simple network: $L = (wx + b - y)^2$

Forward Pass
1
$z_1 = wx$ (multiplication)
2
$z_2 = z_1 + b$ (addition)
3
$z_3 = z_2 - y$ (subtraction)
4
$L = z_3^2$ (square)
Backward Pass
1
$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_3} = 2z_3$
2
$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_2} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_3} \cdot 1 = 2z_3$
3
$\frac{\partial L}{\partial b} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_2} \cdot 1 = 2z_3$
4
$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_2} \cdot \frac{\partial z_1}{\partial w} = 2z_3 \cdot x = 2(wx + b - y) \cdot x$

Gradients Through Layers

For a layer $\mathbf{y} = \sigma(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b})$:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{W}} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{y}} \odot \sigma'(\mathbf{z}) \cdot \mathbf{x}^T$$
$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{W}^T \left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{y}} \odot \sigma'(\mathbf{z})\right)$$

where $\odot$ is element-wise multiplication and $\sigma'$ is the activation derivative.

Vanishing and Exploding Gradients

In deep networks, gradients get multiplied through many layers:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{W}_1} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{y}_n} \cdot \prod_{i=2}^{n} \mathbf{W}_i \cdot \sigma'(\mathbf{z}_i) \cdot \ldots$$
The Problem

If $|\sigma'| < 1$ consistently (like sigmoid), gradients vanish exponentially.
If $\|\mathbf{W}_i\| > 1$ consistently, gradients explode exponentially.
Either way, early layers don't learn properly.

Solutions

  • ReLU: $\sigma'(z) = 1$ for $z > 0$ — no vanishing!
  • Residual connections: Gradients can flow directly through skip connections
  • Batch normalization: Keeps activations in good range
  • Careful initialization: Xavier/He initialization maintains variance
  • Gradient clipping: Caps gradient magnitude to prevent explosion

07. Backpropagation

Backpropagation is just the chain rule applied systematically. Understanding it deeply reveals why certain architectures train better and how gradients flow through networks.
