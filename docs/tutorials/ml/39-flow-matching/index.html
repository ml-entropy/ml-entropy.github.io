From Discrete to Continuous Flows

A continuous normalizing flow (CNF) defines a velocity field $v_\theta(x, t)$ that describes how points move through space:

$$\frac{dx(t)}{dt} = v_\theta(x(t), t), \quad t \in [0, 1]$$

At $t=0$: samples start as noise, $x(0) \sim p_0$ (typically $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$). At $t=1$: samples arrive at data, $x(1) \sim p_1 \approx p_{data}$.

The River Analogy

Every point in space has a velocity at each time $t$. Place a particle (sample) at time 0, and it follows the velocity field to time 1. The collection of all particles transforms the noise distribution into the data distribution.

The continuity equation governs how probability density evolves:

$$\frac{\partial p_t(x)}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (p_t(x) \cdot v_t(x)) = 0$$

This is conservation of probability — probability is neither created nor destroyed, just moved around.

The Problem with Training CNFs

Training a CNF requires solving ODEs forward and backward (for gradients), each involving dozens to hundreds of neural network evaluations. This is extremely slow.

The Key Question

Can we learn the velocity field $v_\theta$ without solving any ODEs during training? Yes! This is exactly what flow matching achieves.

Conditional Flow Matching — The Breakthrough

For each data point $x_1$, define a simple conditional path from noise $x_0$ to data $x_1$ via linear interpolation:

$$x_t = (1 - t) \cdot x_0 + t \cdot x_1$$

The conditional velocity field is trivially: $u_t(x | x_1) = x_1 - x_0$

The Training Algorithm
1
Sample $x_1$ from data, $x_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$, $t \sim U(0,1)$
2
Compute $x_t = (1-t) x_0 + t x_1$
3
Target velocity: $x_1 - x_0$
4
Loss: $\|v_\theta(x_t, t) - (x_1 - x_0)\|^2$

It's just a regression problem! No ODE solving, no adversarial training, no ELBO.

Why Does This Work?

Theorem (Lipman et al., 2023): The gradient of the conditional flow matching loss equals the gradient of the full flow matching loss in expectation: $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_{CFM}(\theta) = \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_{FM}(\theta)$. Optimizing the simple conditional loss is equivalent to optimizing the intractable marginal loss.

Probability Paths

Linear Interpolation (Optimal Transport Path):

$$x_t = (1 - t) x_0 + t x_1$$

Variance-Preserving Path (Diffusion-Style):

$$x_t = \sqrt{1 - t^2} \cdot x_0 + t \cdot x_1$$

General Gaussian Path: $x_t = \alpha_t \cdot x_1 + \sigma_t \cdot x_0$ where $\alpha_0 = 0, \alpha_1 = 1$ and $\sigma_0 = 1, \sigma_1 = 0$.

Why Linear Paths Are Best

Linear interpolation corresponds to optimal transport — the minimum-cost way to move probability mass. Straighter trajectories are easier to learn, require fewer ODE steps at inference, and produce better sample quality.

Sampling (Inference)

At inference time, we solve the ODE using the Euler method:

$$x_{t+\Delta t} = x_t + v_\theta(x_t, t) \cdot \Delta t$$

Because of the straight OT paths, even Euler with $N=10\text{-}20$ steps gives good results. Diffusion models typically need 50-1000 steps!

Connection to Diffusion Models

Diffusion models learn the score function $s_\theta(x, t) = \nabla_x \log p_t(x)$. Flow matching directly learns the velocity field $v_\theta(x, t)$. Under the variance-preserving path, the CFM loss is equivalent (up to time-dependent weighting) to the denoising score matching loss:

$$\mathcal{L}_{DSM} = \mathbb{E}_{t, x_0, x_1}\left[\|\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) - \epsilon\|^2\right]$$

The difference is purely in parameterization: predicting velocity vs. predicting noise vs. predicting score.

Rectified Flows and Impact

Rectified flows take the OT idea further: given a trained flow matching model, straighten the learned trajectories by retraining. After 2-3 iterations, a single-step ODE can produce reasonable samples!

Flow matching has become the backbone of state-of-the-art models:

  • Stable Diffusion 3 (2024) — uses rectified flow matching
  • Flux (2024) — flow matching with improved architecture
  • Sora (2024) — video generation with flow matching

From the information-theoretic perspective: the velocity field is the gradient of information transport, the OT path is the minimum entropy production path, and the flow moves probability mass along the geodesic in Wasserstein space.

38. Flow Matching

Flow matching learns a velocity field that transports noise into data using a simple regression loss. Linear interpolation paths correspond to optimal transport, enabling high-quality generation in just 5-20 steps.
