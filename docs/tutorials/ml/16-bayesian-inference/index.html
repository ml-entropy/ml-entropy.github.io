16. Bayesian Inference

Moving from point estimates to reasoning with uncertainty. We derive Bayes' theorem for parameters, work through the Beta-Binomial conjugate pair in full detail, connect MAP estimation to regularization, and understand why the posterior predictive distribution is the principled way to make predictions.

Why Bayesian Inference?

In frequentist statistics and maximum likelihood estimation (MLE), we treat parameters as fixed but unknown quantities. The goal is to find the single "best" set of parameters $\hat{\theta}$ that maximizes $P(\mathcal{D}|\theta)$. This gives us a point estimate: a single number, with no notion of uncertainty.

But what happens when data is scarce, noisy, or ambiguous? A point estimate can be dangerously overconfident. Consider:

  • Medicine: You flip a new drug's trial coin 3 times and see 3 successes. MLE says the success rate is 100%. Would you bet your life on that?
  • Autonomous driving: A neural network has never seen a particular road scenario. It outputs a confident "turn left." How much should you trust it?
  • Active learning: You have a limited budget to label data. Which data points are most informative? You need to know where your model is uncertain.
The Bayesian Paradigm

Instead of seeking a single "best" parameter $\hat{\theta}$, maintain a distribution over all possible parameters $P(\theta|\mathcal{D})$.

Before data: Express prior beliefs as $P(\theta)$
Observe data: Update beliefs using Bayes' theorem
After data: The posterior $P(\theta|\mathcal{D})$ captures both the best estimate and the uncertainty around it

Analogy: Imagine you're trying to estimate the temperature outside. A frequentist looks at the thermometer and says "it's 22.3°C." A Bayesian says "I'm fairly confident it's between 21° and 24°, most likely around 22°, but I wouldn't be shocked if it were 20°." The Bayesian answer is richer: it tells you not just the estimate but how much to trust it.

Bayes' Theorem for Parameters

The mathematical foundation of Bayesian inference is Bayes' Theorem, applied to a set of parameters $\theta$ and observed data $\mathcal{D}$.

Deriving Bayes' Theorem
1
Start from the product rule of probability. The joint distribution $P(\theta, \mathcal{D})$ can be decomposed in two ways: $$P(\theta, \mathcal{D}) = P(\mathcal{D} | \theta) \cdot P(\theta) = P(\theta | \mathcal{D}) \cdot P(\mathcal{D})$$
2
Solve for the posterior $P(\theta | \mathcal{D})$: $$P(\theta | \mathcal{D}) = \frac{P(\mathcal{D} | \theta) \cdot P(\theta)}{P(\mathcal{D})}$$
3
Expand the evidence (also called the marginal likelihood): $$P(\mathcal{D}) = \int P(\mathcal{D}|\theta) P(\theta) \, d\theta$$

This integrates the likelihood over all possible parameter values, weighted by the prior. It ensures the posterior integrates to 1.

Each term has a distinct role:

  • $\color{orange}P(\theta)$: The Prior. Our belief about parameters before seeing any data. Encodes domain knowledge, previous experiments, or deliberate ignorance.
  • $\color{green}P(\mathcal{D} | \theta)$: The Likelihood. How probable is the observed data, given these specific parameters? This is the same function that MLE maximizes.
  • $\color{blue}P(\theta | \mathcal{D})$: The Posterior. Our updated belief about parameters after seeing the data. This is the goal of Bayesian inference.
  • $\color{red}P(\mathcal{D})$: The Evidence. A normalizing constant. It doesn't depend on $\theta$, so it often cancels out or can be ignored. But computing it exactly requires the intractable integral above.
The Proportionality Shortcut

Since $P(\mathcal{D})$ doesn't depend on $\theta$, we often write: $$P(\theta | \mathcal{D}) \propto P(\mathcal{D} | \theta) \cdot P(\theta)$$ Read this as: "The posterior is proportional to the likelihood times the prior." This is sufficient whenever we can recognize the functional form of the posterior (as with conjugate priors) or when we only need the mode (as with MAP estimation).

The Coin Flip: A Complete Worked Example

The best way to internalize Bayesian inference is to work through a complete example. Suppose we have a coin with unknown bias $\theta = P(\text{heads})$ and we want to estimate $\theta$ from observed flips.

Setting Up the Model

The Beta-Binomial Model

Likelihood: Each flip is a Bernoulli trial. After $N$ flips with $k$ heads: $$P(\mathcal{D}|\theta) = \binom{N}{k} \theta^k (1-\theta)^{N-k}$$

Prior: We model our prior belief about $\theta \in [0, 1]$ with the Beta distribution: $$P(\theta) = \text{Beta}(\theta; \alpha, \beta) = \frac{\theta^{\alpha-1}(1-\theta)^{\beta-1}}{B(\alpha, \beta)}$$ where $B(\alpha, \beta) = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}$ is the Beta function (a normalizing constant).

Interpretation of $\alpha$ and $\beta$: Think of $\alpha - 1$ as "imaginary prior heads" and $\beta - 1$ as "imaginary prior tails." The prior $\text{Beta}(1, 1)$ is uniform (no prior knowledge). $\text{Beta}(10, 10)$ expresses a strong belief that the coin is fair. $\text{Beta}(2, 5)$ expresses a belief that the coin is biased toward tails.

Computing the Posterior

Beta-Binomial Posterior Derivation
1
Write down the posterior using Bayes' rule: $$P(\theta|\mathcal{D}) \propto P(\mathcal{D}|\theta) \cdot P(\theta)$$
2
Substitute the likelihood and prior: $$P(\theta|\mathcal{D}) \propto \underbrace{\theta^k (1-\theta)^{N-k}}_{\text{Binomial likelihood}} \cdot \underbrace{\theta^{\alpha-1}(1-\theta)^{\beta-1}}_{\text{Beta prior}}$$

We dropped $\binom{N}{k}$ and $B(\alpha,\beta)$ because they are constants that don't depend on $\theta$.

3
Combine exponents: $$P(\theta|\mathcal{D}) \propto \theta^{(\alpha + k) - 1} (1-\theta)^{(\beta + N - k) - 1}$$
4
Recognize the kernel of a Beta distribution: $$\boxed{P(\theta|\mathcal{D}) = \text{Beta}(\alpha + k, \, \beta + N - k)}$$

The posterior is Beta with updated parameters: add the observed heads to $\alpha$ and the observed tails to $\beta$. The prior "pseudo-counts" simply combine with the real counts. This is conjugacy in action.

A Concrete Example

Suppose we start with a mild prior $\text{Beta}(2, 2)$ (slightly favoring fairness) and observe 7 heads out of 10 flips.

Quantity Value Interpretation
Prior $\text{Beta}(2, 2)$ Mild belief the coin is roughly fair
Data $k=7, \, N=10$ 7 heads in 10 flips
MLE $\hat{\theta} = 7/10 = 0.70$ Frequentist point estimate
Posterior $\text{Beta}(9, 5)$ $\alpha' = 2+7=9, \; \beta' = 2+3=5$
Posterior mean $\frac{9}{9+5} = 0.643$ Shrunk toward 0.5 by the prior
Posterior mode (MAP) $\frac{9-1}{9+5-2} = 0.667$ Between MLE (0.70) and prior mean (0.50)
Key Insight: The Prior as Regularization

The MLE says $\theta = 0.70$, but the Bayesian posterior mean is $0.643$ — it has been pulled toward the prior mean of 0.5. This "shrinkage" prevents overfitting to small samples. With more data, the likelihood dominates and the prior's influence fades. The posterior mean for $\text{Beta}(\alpha + k, \beta + N - k)$ is $\frac{\alpha + k}{\alpha + \beta + N}$, which approaches $\frac{k}{N}$ (the MLE) as $N \to \infty$.

Conjugate Priors

The Beta-Binomial pair above worked out beautifully: the posterior had the same functional form as the prior. This is not a coincidence — it's the defining property of conjugate priors.

Conjugate Prior

A prior $P(\theta)$ is conjugate to a likelihood $P(\mathcal{D}|\theta)$ if the posterior $P(\theta|\mathcal{D})$ belongs to the same family of distributions as the prior. The Bayesian update reduces to simply updating the hyperparameters.

The most important conjugate pairs in practice:

Likelihood Conjugate Prior Posterior Use Case
Bernoulli / Binomial Beta($\alpha, \beta$) Beta($\alpha+k, \beta+N-k$) Coin flips, click rates
Poisson($\lambda$) Gamma($\alpha, \beta$) Gamma($\alpha+\sum x_i, \beta+N$) Event counts, arrival rates
Normal (known $\sigma^2$) Normal($\mu_0, \sigma_0^2$) Normal($\mu_N, \sigma_N^2$) Measurement estimation
Multinomial Dirichlet($\boldsymbol{\alpha}$) Dirichlet($\boldsymbol{\alpha} + \mathbf{c}$) Categorical data, topic models
Normal (known $\mu$) Inverse-Gamma($\alpha, \beta$) Inverse-Gamma($\alpha', \beta'$) Estimating variance

The Gaussian-Gaussian Case

The Normal-Normal conjugate pair is especially important and reveals the mechanics of Bayesian updating clearly.

Posterior for Normal Likelihood with Normal Prior
1
Setup: We observe $N$ data points $x_1, \ldots, x_N$ from $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with known $\sigma^2$, and place a prior $\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)$.
2
Posterior: $\mu | \mathcal{D} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_N, \sigma_N^2)$ where: $$\mu_N = \frac{\frac{1}{\sigma_0^2}\mu_0 + \frac{N}{\sigma^2}\bar{x}}{\frac{1}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{N}{\sigma^2}}, \qquad \sigma_N^2 = \frac{1}{\frac{1}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{N}{\sigma^2}}$$
3
Interpretation: The posterior mean $\mu_N$ is a precision-weighted average of the prior mean $\mu_0$ and the sample mean $\bar{x}$. Precision $= 1/\text{variance}$. Whichever source is more precise (lower variance) gets more weight.

As $N \to \infty$, $\mu_N \to \bar{x}$ and $\sigma_N^2 \to 0$ — the posterior concentrates on the MLE, and the prior is "washed out" by data.

Limitations of Conjugate Priors

Conjugate priors are mathematically elegant but restrictive. They require the prior to have a specific parametric form dictated by the likelihood, which may not match our actual beliefs. For complex models (neural networks, hierarchical models), no conjugate prior exists. This is where approximate inference methods become essential: Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) for sampling-based approximation and Variational Inference for optimization-based approximation.

Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) Estimation

Full Bayesian inference maintains the entire posterior distribution. But sometimes we want a single "best" parameter estimate that still incorporates the prior. The MAP estimate is the mode (peak) of the posterior.

MAP Estimation and Its Connection to Regularization
1
Definition: $$\theta_{MAP} = \arg\max_\theta P(\theta|\mathcal{D}) = \arg\max_\theta \frac{P(\mathcal{D}|\theta)P(\theta)}{P(\mathcal{D})}$$
2
Since $P(\mathcal{D})$ doesn't depend on $\theta$, and $\log$ is monotonic: $$\theta_{MAP} = \arg\max_\theta \left[\log P(\mathcal{D}|\theta) + \log P(\theta)\right]$$
3
Now choose a Gaussian prior: $P(\theta) = \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_p^2 I)$ $$\log P(\theta) = -\frac{\|\theta\|^2}{2\sigma_p^2} + \text{const}$$
4
Substituting: $$\theta_{MAP} = \arg\max_\theta \left[\log P(\mathcal{D}|\theta) - \frac{1}{2\sigma_p^2}\|\theta\|^2\right]$$

Equivalently (negating to minimize):

$$\theta_{MAP} = \arg\min_\theta \left[\underbrace{-\log P(\mathcal{D}|\theta)}_{\text{NLL loss}} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{2\sigma_p^2}\|\theta\|^2}_{\text{L2 regularization}}\right]$$
5
Setting $\lambda = \frac{1}{2\sigma_p^2}$: $$\boxed{\theta_{MAP} = \arg\min_\theta \left[-\log P(\mathcal{D}|\theta) + \lambda\|\theta\|^2\right]}$$

This is exactly the L2-regularized MLE (weight decay)! A tighter prior (smaller $\sigma_p^2$) corresponds to stronger regularization (larger $\lambda$).

The Bayesian View of Regularization

This derivation reveals a deep connection:

  • MLE = MAP with a uniform (improper) prior — no regularization
  • L2 regularization (weight decay) = MAP with a Gaussian prior centered at zero
  • L1 regularization (Lasso) = MAP with a Laplace prior centered at zero

Every time you use weight decay in a neural network, you are implicitly performing MAP estimation with a Gaussian prior. The regularization strength $\lambda$ encodes how strongly you believe the weights should be small.

The Posterior Predictive Distribution

MAP and MLE give point estimates, but the truly Bayesian way to make predictions is to integrate over all parameter values, weighted by their posterior probability. This is the posterior predictive distribution.

Posterior Predictive Distribution

For a new data point $x^*$, the predictive distribution is:

$$P(x^* | \mathcal{D}) = \int P(x^* | \theta) \, P(\theta | \mathcal{D}) \, d\theta$$

Instead of plugging in a single $\hat{\theta}$, we let every possible $\theta$ "vote" on the prediction, weighted by how plausible it is given the data.

Why is this better? Consider two extreme scenarios:

  • Confident posterior (lots of data): The posterior $P(\theta|\mathcal{D})$ is sharply peaked. Nearly all the probability mass is near $\hat{\theta}$. The predictive distribution is essentially $P(x^*|\hat{\theta})$ — same as MLE prediction.
  • Uncertain posterior (little data): The posterior is broad. Many different $\theta$ values contribute to the prediction. The result is a wider predictive distribution — the model honestly says "I don't know."

Coin Flip Predictive Example

Continuing our coin example with posterior $\text{Beta}(9, 5)$: what is the probability that the next flip is heads?

Predictive Probability of Heads
1
$$P(x^* = H | \mathcal{D}) = \int_0^1 \underbrace{\theta}_{P(H|\theta)} \cdot \underbrace{P(\theta|\mathcal{D})}_{\text{Beta}(9,5)} \, d\theta$$
2
This is just the expected value of $\theta$ under the posterior: $$= \mathbb{E}_{\text{Beta}(9,5)}[\theta] = \frac{9}{9+5} = \frac{9}{14} \approx 0.643$$

Compare: MLE would give $0.70$ (overconfident). The Bayesian prediction is more conservative, hedging toward 0.5 due to limited data.

Sequential Bayesian Updating

One of the most elegant properties of Bayesian inference is that it is sequential. Today's posterior becomes tomorrow's prior. If we observe data in batches $\mathcal{D}_1, \mathcal{D}_2, \ldots$, we can update incrementally:

$$\underbrace{P(\theta|\mathcal{D}_1)}_{\text{posterior}_1 = \text{prior}_2} \xrightarrow{\text{observe } \mathcal{D}_2} P(\theta|\mathcal{D}_1, \mathcal{D}_2) \xrightarrow{\text{observe } \mathcal{D}_3} \cdots$$

The result is identical to processing all the data at once. This is because Bayes' theorem is a consequence of the product rule of probability, which is associative.

Proof of Sequential Equivalence
1
After $\mathcal{D}_1$: $$P(\theta|\mathcal{D}_1) \propto P(\mathcal{D}_1|\theta) P(\theta)$$
2
After $\mathcal{D}_2$ (using $P(\theta|\mathcal{D}_1)$ as prior): $$P(\theta|\mathcal{D}_1, \mathcal{D}_2) \propto P(\mathcal{D}_2|\theta) \cdot P(\theta|\mathcal{D}_1) \propto P(\mathcal{D}_2|\theta) \cdot P(\mathcal{D}_1|\theta) \cdot P(\theta)$$
3
If $\mathcal{D}_1$ and $\mathcal{D}_2$ are conditionally independent given $\theta$: $$P(\mathcal{D}_2|\theta) \cdot P(\mathcal{D}_1|\theta) = P(\mathcal{D}_1, \mathcal{D}_2|\theta)$$

So $P(\theta|\mathcal{D}_1, \mathcal{D}_2) \propto P(\mathcal{D}_1, \mathcal{D}_2|\theta) P(\theta)$ — the same as processing all data at once.

This property is invaluable for online learning and streaming data. Each new observation refines the posterior without needing to reprocess all historical data. The posterior naturally sharpens as evidence accumulates, reflecting growing confidence.

The Computational Challenge

The theory above is beautiful but hides a serious practical obstacle: computing the posterior is generally intractable. The evidence integral

$$P(\mathcal{D}) = \int P(\mathcal{D}|\theta) P(\theta) \, d\theta$$

can only be computed in closed form for conjugate models. For anything more complex — neural networks, hierarchical models, mixture models — the integral has no analytical solution.

The Curse of Dimensionality

A modern neural network might have millions of parameters. The evidence integral is over all of them simultaneously. Even a modest model with 100 parameters, evaluated on a grid with just 10 points per dimension, would require $10^{100}$ evaluations — more than the number of atoms in the observable universe.

Numerical integration (quadrature) fails catastrophically in high dimensions. We need fundamentally different approaches.

Two major families of approximate inference have emerged:

Method Approach Strengths Weaknesses
MCMC Draw correlated samples from the posterior Asymptotically exact Slow, hard to diagnose convergence
Variational Inference Approximate the posterior with a simpler distribution via optimization Fast, scalable, works with SGD Biased, may underestimate uncertainty

The next tutorial covers Variational Inference in depth, showing how to turn the intractable integral into an optimization problem — the key idea behind VAEs, modern Bayesian deep learning, and much more.

Bayesian Neural Networks

Applying Bayesian inference to neural networks means placing a prior over the weights $\mathbf{w}$ and computing (or approximating) the posterior $P(\mathbf{w}|\mathcal{D})$.

$$P(\mathbf{w}|\mathcal{D}) = \frac{P(\mathcal{D}|\mathbf{w})P(\mathbf{w})}{P(\mathcal{D})} = \frac{\prod_{i=1}^N P(y_i|x_i, \mathbf{w}) \cdot P(\mathbf{w})}{\int \prod_{i=1}^N P(y_i|x_i, \mathbf{w}) \cdot P(\mathbf{w}) \, d\mathbf{w}}$$

Predictions integrate over weight uncertainty:

$$P(y^*|x^*, \mathcal{D}) = \int P(y^*|x^*, \mathbf{w}) P(\mathbf{w}|\mathcal{D}) \, d\mathbf{w}$$

In practice, the weight-space posterior is massively high-dimensional and multi-modal, so exact computation is impossible. Common approximations include:

  • MC Dropout: Use dropout at test time and average multiple stochastic forward passes. This can be shown to approximate a specific variational posterior.
  • Variational BNN: Learn a Gaussian distribution $q(\mathbf{w}) = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ over each weight by maximizing the ELBO (covered in Tutorial 17).
  • Deep Ensembles: Train multiple networks with different initializations. While not strictly Bayesian, this captures functional diversity effectively.
  • Laplace Approximation: Fit a Gaussian to the posterior at the MAP estimate using the Hessian of the loss — a second-order Taylor expansion of $\log P(\theta|\mathcal{D})$.
Why Uncertainty Matters in Deep Learning

Standard neural networks output a single prediction with no calibrated uncertainty. When given an out-of-distribution input (e.g., a cat image to a digit classifier), they can be confidently wrong. Bayesian approaches produce wider predictive distributions for unfamiliar inputs, signaling "I don't know." This is critical for safety-sensitive applications: medical diagnosis, autonomous vehicles, and active learning systems that must decide which data to label next.

Beta-Binomial Conjugate Model

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# --- Beta-Binomial Bayesian Inference ---

# Prior: Beta(alpha, beta)
alpha_prior, beta_prior = 2, 2

# Observed data: 7 heads out of 10 flips
n_heads, n_tails = 7, 3

# Posterior: Beta(alpha + heads, beta + tails)
alpha_post = alpha_prior + n_heads
beta_post = beta_prior + n_tails

print(f"Prior:     Beta({alpha_prior}, {beta_prior})")
print(f"Data:      {n_heads} heads, {n_tails} tails")
print(f"Posterior: Beta({alpha_post}, {beta_post})")

# Compute key statistics
prior_dist = stats.beta(alpha_prior, beta_prior)
post_dist = stats.beta(alpha_post, beta_post)

print(f"\nPrior mean:     {prior_dist.mean():.4f}")
print(f"Posterior mean: {post_dist.mean():.4f}")
print(f"MLE:            {n_heads / (n_heads + n_tails):.4f}")

# MAP estimate: (alpha - 1) / (alpha + beta - 2)
map_estimate = (alpha_post - 1) / (alpha_post + beta_post - 2)
print(f"MAP estimate:   {map_estimate:.4f}")

# 95% Credible Interval
ci_low, ci_high = post_dist.ppf(0.025), post_dist.ppf(0.975)
print(f"95% CI:         [{ci_low:.4f}, {ci_high:.4f}]")

# --- Visualization ---
theta = np.linspace(0, 1, 500)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))
ax.plot(theta, prior_dist.pdf(theta), '--', color='gray',
        linewidth=2, label=f'Prior: Beta({alpha_prior},{beta_prior})')
ax.plot(theta, post_dist.pdf(theta), '-', color='#3498db',
        linewidth=2.5, label=f'Posterior: Beta({alpha_post},{beta_post})')

# Show MLE
ax.axvline(n_heads/(n_heads+n_tails), color='#e74c3c',
           linestyle=':', linewidth=1.5, label=f'MLE = {n_heads/(n_heads+n_tails):.2f}')

# Shade credible interval
mask = (theta >= ci_low) & (theta <= ci_high)
ax.fill_between(theta[mask], post_dist.pdf(theta[mask]),
                alpha=0.2, color='#3498db', label='95% Credible Interval')

ax.set_xlabel(r'$\theta$ (probability of heads)')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$P(\theta)$')
ax.set_title('Bayesian Update: Prior → Posterior')
ax.legend()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Sequential Bayesian Updating

def sequential_update(data_batches, alpha_0=2, beta_0=2):
    """
    Demonstrate sequential Bayesian updating.
    Each batch updates the posterior, which becomes
    the prior for the next batch.

    Args:
        data_batches: list of (n_heads, n_tails) tuples
        alpha_0, beta_0: initial prior parameters
    """
    alpha, beta = alpha_0, beta_0
    history = [(alpha, beta, 0, 'Prior')]

    total_heads, total_tails = 0, 0
    for i, (h, t) in enumerate(data_batches):
        alpha += h
        beta += t
        total_heads += h
        total_tails += t
        history.append((alpha, beta, total_heads + total_tails,
                        f'After batch {i+1}'))

    # Verify: same as single batch update
    alpha_batch = alpha_0 + total_heads
    beta_batch = beta_0 + total_tails
    assert alpha == alpha_batch and beta == beta_batch, \
        "Sequential and batch updates must agree!"

    # Plot the evolution
    theta = np.linspace(0, 1, 500)
    fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))

    colors = plt.cm.Reds(np.linspace(0.2, 0.9, len(history)))
    for (a, b, n, label), color in zip(history, colors):
        dist = stats.beta(a, b)
        style = '--' if n == 0 else '-'
        ax.plot(theta, dist.pdf(theta), style, color=color,
                linewidth=2, label=f'{label}: Beta({a},{b}), N={n}')

    ax.set_xlabel(r'$\theta$')
    ax.set_ylabel(r'$P(\theta | \mathcal{D})$')
    ax.set_title('Sequential Bayesian Updating')
    ax.legend(fontsize=9)
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()

    return history

# Example: observe data in 4 batches
batches = [(3, 1), (2, 1), (4, 2), (5, 1)]  # (heads, tails)
history = sequential_update(batches)

MAP vs MLE: Regularization Connection

def compare_mle_map(true_theta=0.6, prior_strength_values=[0.1, 1, 10, 100]):
    """
    Show how MAP shrinks toward the prior as prior strength increases,
    demonstrating the regularization effect.
    """
    np.random.seed(42)
    sample_sizes = [5, 10, 20, 50, 100, 500]

    fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(14, 5))

    # Left: MLE vs MAP for different N
    ax = axes[0]
    for strength in prior_strength_values:
        alpha_p, beta_p = strength, strength  # Symmetric prior
        map_estimates = []
        for N in sample_sizes:
            data = np.random.binomial(1, true_theta, N)
            k = data.sum()
            # MAP for Beta-Binomial
            a_post = alpha_p + k
            b_post = beta_p + (N - k)
            map_est = (a_post - 1) / (a_post + b_post - 2)
            map_estimates.append(map_est)

        ax.plot(sample_sizes, map_estimates, 'o-',
                label=f'MAP (prior strength={strength})')

    # MLE line
    mle_estimates = []
    for N in sample_sizes:
        data = np.random.binomial(1, true_theta, N)
        mle_estimates.append(data.mean())
    ax.plot(sample_sizes, mle_estimates, 's--', color='black',
            label='MLE', linewidth=2)
    ax.axhline(true_theta, color='green', linestyle=':', label=f'True θ={true_theta}')
    ax.set_xlabel('Sample size N')
    ax.set_ylabel('Estimate of θ')
    ax.set_title('MAP vs MLE: Effect of Prior Strength')
    ax.legend(fontsize=8)

    # Right: Posterior width vs N
    ax = axes[1]
    widths_bayes = []
    for N in sample_sizes:
        data = np.random.binomial(1, true_theta, N)
        k = data.sum()
        post = stats.beta(2 + k, 2 + N - k)
        ci = post.ppf(0.975) - post.ppf(0.025)
        widths_bayes.append(ci)

    ax.plot(sample_sizes, widths_bayes, 'o-', color='#3498db',
            linewidth=2, label='95% CI width')
    ax.set_xlabel('Sample size N')
    ax.set_ylabel('Width of 95% credible interval')
    ax.set_title('Uncertainty Decreases with Data')
    ax.legend()

    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()

compare_mle_map()

Gaussian-Gaussian Conjugate Update

def gaussian_bayesian_update(data, mu_0, sigma_0_sq, sigma_sq):
    """
    Bayesian update for Normal likelihood with Normal prior.

    Args:
        data: observed data points
        mu_0: prior mean
        sigma_0_sq: prior variance
        sigma_sq: known observation variance
    Returns:
        mu_N, sigma_N_sq: posterior mean and variance
    """
    N = len(data)
    x_bar = np.mean(data)

    # Precision-weighted combination
    precision_prior = 1.0 / sigma_0_sq
    precision_data = N / sigma_sq

    sigma_N_sq = 1.0 / (precision_prior + precision_data)
    mu_N = sigma_N_sq * (precision_prior * mu_0 + precision_data * x_bar)

    return mu_N, sigma_N_sq


# Example: estimating the mean of a Normal distribution
np.random.seed(42)
true_mu = 5.0
sigma_sq = 4.0  # Known variance

# Prior: N(0, 10)
mu_0, sigma_0_sq = 0.0, 10.0

# Observe data sequentially
all_data = np.random.normal(true_mu, np.sqrt(sigma_sq), 50)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 4))
for idx, N in enumerate([1, 5, 50]):
    data = all_data[:N]
    mu_N, sigma_N_sq = gaussian_bayesian_update(data, mu_0, sigma_0_sq, sigma_sq)

    x = np.linspace(-5, 12, 500)
    prior = stats.norm(mu_0, np.sqrt(sigma_0_sq))
    posterior = stats.norm(mu_N, np.sqrt(sigma_N_sq))

    ax = axes[idx]
    ax.plot(x, prior.pdf(x), '--', color='gray', label='Prior')
    ax.plot(x, posterior.pdf(x), '-', color='#3498db', linewidth=2,
            label=f'Posterior (N={N})')
    ax.axvline(true_mu, color='green', linestyle=':', label=f'True μ={true_mu}')
    ax.axvline(np.mean(data), color='red', linestyle=':',
               alpha=0.7, label=f'MLE={np.mean(data):.2f}')
    ax.set_title(f'N = {N} observations')
    ax.set_xlabel('μ')
    ax.legend(fontsize=8)

plt.suptitle('Gaussian-Gaussian Bayesian Update', fontsize=14)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Exercises & Progress

Test your understanding of Bayesian inference concepts, from basic intuition to mathematical derivations.

Easy 1. Prior, Likelihood, Posterior

A factory produces widgets. Historically, 5% are defective. You test 20 widgets and find 3 defective ones.

  1. What is a reasonable prior distribution for the defect rate $\theta$?
  2. What is the likelihood function?
  3. If you use a $\text{Beta}(2, 38)$ prior (mean = 0.05), what is the posterior distribution?
  4. Compare the posterior mean to the MLE. Which is closer to the historical rate?
Easy 2. Sequential vs Batch

Starting with a $\text{Beta}(1,1)$ prior, you observe the following coin flips in sequence: H, H, T, H, T.

  1. Write down the posterior after each flip (5 posteriors total).
  2. Verify that the final posterior equals what you would get from a single batch update with all 5 observations.
  3. What is the posterior mean after all 5 flips? How does it compare to the MLE?
Medium 3. MAP and Regularization

Show mathematically that MAP estimation with a zero-mean Laplace prior

$$P(\theta) \propto \exp\left(-\frac{|\theta|}{\lambda}\right)$$

is equivalent to MLE with L1 (Lasso) regularization. What does this imply about the sparsity of the MAP solution?

Medium 4. Gaussian Posterior Derivation

Derive the Gaussian-Gaussian posterior in full. Given:

  • Likelihood: $x_i | \mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ for $i = 1, \ldots, N$ (known $\sigma^2$)
  • Prior: $\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)$

Show that $\mu | \mathcal{D} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_N, \sigma_N^2)$ and derive the formulas for $\mu_N$ and $\sigma_N^2$. Verify that:

  1. As $N \to \infty$, $\mu_N \to \bar{x}$ (the MLE)
  2. As $\sigma_0^2 \to \infty$ (vague prior), $\mu_N \to \bar{x}$
  3. As $\sigma^2 \to \infty$ (very noisy data), $\mu_N \to \mu_0$
Hard 5. Posterior Predictive for Beta-Binomial

After observing $k$ heads in $N$ coin flips with a $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ prior, the posterior is $\text{Beta}(\alpha + k, \beta + N - k)$.

  1. Derive the posterior predictive probability that the next $m$ flips yield exactly $j$ heads. Show that this follows a Beta-Binomial distribution: $$P(j | m, \mathcal{D}) = \binom{m}{j} \frac{B(\alpha' + j, \, \beta' + m - j)}{B(\alpha', \beta')}$$ where $\alpha' = \alpha + k$ and $\beta' = \beta + N - k$.
  2. Show that the predictive variance is larger than what you'd get from plugging in the MAP estimate. Why?
Hard 6. Bayesian Model Comparison

You have two hypotheses for a coin:

  • $\mathcal{M}_1$: The coin is fair ($\theta = 0.5$)
  • $\mathcal{M}_2$: The coin has unknown bias ($\theta \sim \text{Beta}(1,1)$)

You observe 7 heads in 10 flips.

  1. Compute the marginal likelihood $P(\mathcal{D} | \mathcal{M}_1)$ for the fair coin model.
  2. Compute $P(\mathcal{D} | \mathcal{M}_2)$ using the Beta-Binomial marginal likelihood.
  3. Compute the Bayes factor $BF_{21} = P(\mathcal{D}|\mathcal{M}_2) / P(\mathcal{D}|\mathcal{M}_1)$ and interpret it.

Hint: For model $\mathcal{M}_2$, $P(\mathcal{D}|\mathcal{M}_2) = \binom{N}{k} \frac{B(\alpha+k, \beta+N-k)}{B(\alpha, \beta)}$
