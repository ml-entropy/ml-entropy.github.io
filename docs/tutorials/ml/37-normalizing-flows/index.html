The Density Estimation Problem

Given data $\{x_1, ..., x_N\}$, we want to find a probability density $p_\theta(x)$ that assigns high probability to observed data, can generate new samples, and allows evaluating $p_\theta(x)$ for any $x$. Normalizing flows achieve all three — unique among deep generative models.

Change of Variables: The Foundation

One Dimension: If $x$ has density $p_X(x)$ and $y = f(x)$ is a monotonic differentiable function, then:

$$p_Y(y) = p_X(f^{-1}(y)) \cdot \left|\frac{df^{-1}}{dy}\right|$$

When $f$ stretches a region of space, the same probability spreads over a larger region, so density decreases. When $f$ compresses, density increases.

Multiple Dimensions: For $\mathbf{y} = f(\mathbf{x})$ where $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is a diffeomorphism:

$$p_Y(\mathbf{y}) = p_X(f^{-1}(\mathbf{y})) \cdot \left|\det\left(\frac{\partial f^{-1}}{\partial \mathbf{y}}\right)\right|$$
The Jacobian Determinant

The Jacobian determinant $|\det(J)|$ measures the local volume change factor:
$|\det(J)| > 1$: expansion (density decreases)
$|\det(J)| < 1$: compression (density increases)
$|\det(J)| = 1$: volume-preserving

Flow Composition

Compose many simple invertible transformations:

$$\mathbf{x} = f_K \circ f_{K-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1(\mathbf{z}_0)$$

where $\mathbf{z}_0 \sim p_0(\mathbf{z}_0)$ is the base distribution (typically $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$).

The log-likelihood decomposes beautifully:

$$\log p(\mathbf{x}) = \log p_0(\mathbf{z}_0) - \sum_{k=1}^{K} \log \left|\det\left(\frac{\partial f_k}{\partial \mathbf{z}_{k-1}}\right)\right|$$
Three Operations

Training: Maximize the log-likelihood over flow parameters
Sampling: Draw $\mathbf{z}_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$, push forward through $f_K \circ \cdots \circ f_1$
Density evaluation: Map $\mathbf{x}$ backward through $f_1^{-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_K^{-1}$, compute the formula

Coupling Layers (RealNVP)

The breakthrough idea that makes flows practical. Split the input: $\mathbf{x} = [\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2]$. Transform:

$$\mathbf{y}_1 = \mathbf{x}_1 \quad \text{(identity)}$$ $$\mathbf{y}_2 = \mathbf{x}_2 \odot \exp(s(\mathbf{x}_1)) + t(\mathbf{x}_1)$$

where $s$ and $t$ are arbitrary neural networks (scale and translate).

Why Coupling Layers Are Brilliant
1
Triangular Jacobian: The Jacobian is lower-triangular, so the determinant is the product of diagonal entries
2
Cheap determinant: $\det(J) = \exp(\sum_i s_i(\mathbf{x}_1))$ — this is $O(n)$ instead of $O(n^3)$!
3
Easy inverse: $\mathbf{x}_2 = (\mathbf{y}_2 - t(\mathbf{y}_1)) \odot \exp(-s(\mathbf{y}_1))$
4
Expressive: $s$ and $t$ can be arbitrarily complex neural networks

To transform ALL dimensions, alternate which half is transformed across layers.

Autoregressive Flows

Masked Autoregressive Flow (MAF):

$$y_i = x_i \cdot \exp(\alpha_i) + \mu_i \quad \text{where} \quad \mu_i, \alpha_i = \text{NN}(x_1, ..., x_{i-1})$$

Each dimension depends only on previous dimensions — lower-triangular Jacobian — $O(n)$ determinant.

Speed Trade-off

MAF: fast density evaluation, slow sampling (sequential)
IAF (Inverse Autoregressive Flow): fast sampling, slow density evaluation

Continuous Normalizing Flows

Instead of discrete transformation steps, use an ODE:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{z}(t)}{dt} = f_\theta(\mathbf{z}(t), t)$$

The log-density evolves according to the instantaneous change of variables:

$$\frac{d \log p(\mathbf{z}(t))}{dt} = -\text{tr}\left(\frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial \mathbf{z}(t)}\right)$$

No restriction on architecture, memory-efficient (adjoint method), but requires an ODE solver. This connects directly to flow matching (next tutorial).

Training and Information Theory

Flows are trained by directly maximizing the log-likelihood — no adversarial training, no ELBO, no approximations:

$$\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \left[\log p_0(f^{-1}_\theta(x_i)) + \log\left|\det\left(\frac{\partial f^{-1}_\theta}{\partial x_i}\right)\right|\right]$$

Maximizing likelihood is equivalent to minimizing forward KL divergence $D_{KL}(p_{data} \| p_\theta)$, which is mode-covering.

From the information-theoretic perspective: the base distribution is maximum entropy (Gaussian), flow transformations are structured removal of entropy, and training finds the transformation that makes data look most Gaussian when mapped backward. The flow literally "normalizes" complex distributions back to the Gaussian — hence the name.

37. Normalizing Flows

Normalizing flows transform simple distributions into complex ones using invertible functions, tracking density changes via the Jacobian determinant. Coupling layers make this computationally tractable with exact likelihood computation.
