Information Theory of Writing Systems

Before we can understand how ancient scripts were deciphered, we need a theoretical framework for thinking about writing itself. What does it mean for a collection of marks on clay, stone, or papyrus to "encode" a language? How much information does a writing system carry, and how much redundancy does it contain? What are the mathematical prerequisites for successful decipherment?

These questions were first answered rigorously by Claude Shannon in 1948, when he published "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" — the founding document of information theory. Shannon was concerned with telephone signals, not ancient scripts, but his framework applies beautifully to writing systems. In this chapter, we develop the information-theoretic tools that will illuminate every subsequent decipherment story. We will see that concepts like entropy, redundancy, mutual information, and channel capacity are not abstract mathematical curiosities — they are the hidden forces that determine whether a script can be cracked.

Think of this chapter as building a toolkit. Each concept we develop here will be deployed repeatedly in the chapters that follow: entropy to measure the complexity of sign inventories, redundancy to explain why decipherment is possible at all, mutual information to quantify the value of bilingual texts, and channel capacity to understand why some scripts resist decipherment.

Information Theory

Information theory is the mathematical study of the quantification, storage, and communication of information. Founded by Claude Shannon in 1948, it provides precise tools for measuring uncertainty (entropy), redundancy (the excess beyond minimal encoding), and the relationships between signals (mutual information). For our purposes, a writing system is an information channel that encodes linguistic messages into visual symbols — and information theory tells us exactly how efficient, how redundant, and how decodable that channel is.

Writing as a Communication Channel

Every writing system can be modeled as a communication channel in Shannon's sense. The sender (scribe) encodes a message (linguistic content) into a sequence of symbols (written signs). The receiver (reader) decodes the symbols back into the original message. The channel may introduce "noise" — damage to the medium, unfamiliar handwriting, ambiguous signs — and the system must be robust enough to transmit the message despite this noise.

This model immediately reveals a fundamental insight: writing systems are over-engineered. They contain far more structure than is strictly necessary to encode their messages. This excess structure — redundancy — is not waste; it is a deliberate defense against noise. And it is precisely this redundancy that makes decipherment possible. Without redundancy, every sign would carry maximum information, and losing or misreading a single sign would be catastrophic. With redundancy, the reader (or decipherer) can recover the intended message even from partial or noisy data.

The decipherer as a special kind of reader

In Shannon's model, a normal reader is a decoder who knows the encoding rules (the script) and applies them to recover the message. A decipherer is a reader who does not know the encoding rules and must infer them from the encoded messages alone. This is possible only because writing systems contain redundancy: the same information is encoded multiple times in overlapping ways (through spelling rules, grammatical constraints, determinatives, etc.). The decipherer exploits these overlapping constraints to deduce the encoding rules from the encoded data — a process that is mathematically equivalent to error correction in digital communication.

Shannon Entropy and Writing Systems

The foundational concept of information theory is entropy — a precise measure of uncertainty or information content. Shannon defined the entropy of a discrete random variable $X$ with possible values $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$ and probabilities $p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_n$ as:

$$H(X) = -\sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i \log_2 p_i$$

Entropy is measured in bits (when using base-2 logarithms). Intuitively, $H(X)$ measures the average number of yes/no questions you would need to ask to determine the value of $X$. If all outcomes are equally likely, entropy is maximized at $\log_2 n$ bits. If one outcome is certain, entropy is zero — there is no uncertainty.

For writing systems, entropy has a direct physical interpretation: it measures the average information content per sign. A script with high entropy carries more information per sign (more "surprise" in each sign choice) but requires more effort to process. A script with low entropy is more predictable (easier to read) but requires more signs to encode the same message.

Entropy Calculations for Different Script Types
1
Alphabet (e.g., Latin, 26 letters): If all letters were equally likely, $H = \log_2 26 \approx 4.7$ bits/letter. But letter frequencies are highly non-uniform (e in English has $p \approx 0.13$, z has $p \approx 0.0007$), so the actual entropy is lower: $H_{\text{English}} \approx 4.1$ bits/letter. The gap ($4.7 - 4.1 = 0.6$ bits) is redundancy from unequal frequencies.
2
Syllabary (e.g., Linear B, ~87 signs): Maximum entropy: $\log_2 87 \approx 6.4$ bits/sign. Each sign encodes a CV syllable (~2 phonemes), so the information rate per phoneme is roughly $6.4/2 \approx 3.2$ bits — comparable to alphabetic efficiency, since each alphabetic letter encodes ~1 phoneme at ~4.1 bits.
3
Logographic (e.g., Chinese, ~3,000 common characters): Maximum entropy: $\log_2 3000 \approx 11.6$ bits/character. Each character encodes a morpheme (~1 word or word-part). The information rate per word is very high — one character carries as much information as 3–4 alphabetic letters.
4
Key insight: Despite having very different sign inventories, all writing systems converge on roughly the same information rate per unit of linguistic content ($\approx 3\text{–}5$ bits per phoneme, $\approx 10\text{–}14$ bits per word). Larger sign inventories compress text (fewer signs needed) but increase per-sign complexity. The tradeoff is fundamental: inventory size × signs needed ≈ constant.
Entropy Calculator for Writing Systems (Python)
import math
from collections import Counter

def entropy(frequencies):
    """
    Calculate Shannon entropy from a frequency distribution.
    
    frequencies: dict or Counter mapping symbols to counts
    Returns: entropy in bits per symbol
    """
    total = sum(frequencies.values())
    H = 0
    for count in frequencies.values():
        if count > 0:
            p = count / total
            H -= p * math.log2(p)
    return H

def script_analysis(name, sign_counts, phonemes_per_sign=1):
    """
    Comprehensive information-theoretic analysis of a writing system.
    """
    n_signs = len(sign_counts)
    total_tokens = sum(sign_counts.values())
    H = entropy(sign_counts)
    H_max = math.log2(n_signs)
    redundancy = 1 - H / H_max
    
    print(f"=== {name} ===")
    print(f"  Sign inventory: {n_signs}")
    print(f"  Total tokens: {total_tokens}")
    print(f"  Entropy: {H:.2f} bits/sign")
    print(f"  Max entropy: {H_max:.2f} bits/sign")
    print(f"  Redundancy: {redundancy:.1%}")
    print(f"  Info per phoneme: {H/phonemes_per_sign:.2f} bits")
    print(f"  Top-5 signs: {sign_counts.most_common(5)}")
    return H, redundancy

Why entropy matters for decipherment

A script's entropy determines the difficulty of frequency analysis — the most basic decipherment tool. High-entropy scripts (where all signs are roughly equally frequent) resist frequency analysis because there are no distinctive "peaks" to match against known language statistics. Low-entropy scripts (with a few very common signs and many rare ones) are easier to attack because the frequent signs can be identified and matched first, providing anchors for the rest. This is why alphabetic scripts (low entropy per sign, highly non-uniform frequencies) are more vulnerable to frequency analysis than logographic scripts (high entropy, more uniform frequencies).

Frequency Analysis: The First Cryptanalytic Tool

Frequency analysis is the simplest and oldest tool for breaking codes — and for deciphering scripts. The core idea: in any natural language, some sounds (and therefore some written signs) are much more common than others. In English, 'e' accounts for ~13% of all letters, while 'z' accounts for only ~0.07%. This frequency fingerprint is remarkably stable across texts of even moderate length (a few hundred characters), making it a reliable tool for identifying sign-sound correspondences.

The method was first described by the Arab polymath Al-Kindi (c. 801–873 CE) in his Manuscript on Deciphering Cryptographic Messages — one of the earliest known works on cryptanalysis. Al-Kindi's algorithm is simple: count the frequency of each symbol in the unknown text, rank them from most to least common, and match them to the known frequency ranking of the target language's letters. For a simple substitution cipher, this often works immediately. For ancient scripts, the method must be modified (because the relationship between signs and sounds is more complex), but the fundamental insight — that frequency distributions carry linguistic identity — remains powerful.

Frequency Analysis Attack on a Substitution Cipher (Python)
from collections import Counter
import string

def frequency_attack(ciphertext, target_frequencies):
    """
    Basic frequency analysis: match ciphertext symbol frequencies
    to known language letter frequencies.
    
    ciphertext: string of encrypted text
    target_frequencies: dict of letter -> expected frequency in target language
    Returns: proposed mapping (cipher_char -> plain_char)
    """
    # Count cipher symbol frequencies
    cipher_counts = Counter(c for c in ciphertext.lower() if c.isalpha())
    cipher_ranked = [char for char, _ in cipher_counts.most_common()]
    
    # Rank target language letters by frequency
    target_ranked = sorted(target_frequencies, 
                          key=target_frequencies.get, reverse=True)
    
    # Match by rank
    mapping = {}
    for cipher_char, plain_char in zip(cipher_ranked, target_ranked):
        mapping[cipher_char] = plain_char
    
    # Apply mapping
    result = ""
    for c in ciphertext:
        if c.lower() in mapping:
            mapped = mapping[c.lower()]
            result += mapped.upper() if c.isupper() else mapped
        else:
            result += c
    
    return result, mapping

Limitations of Pure Frequency Analysis

Frequency analysis works beautifully against simple substitution ciphers but has important limitations when applied to ancient scripts:

1. Script type matters. In an alphabet, each sign maps to roughly one sound, so frequency analysis directly reveals sound frequencies. In a syllabary, each sign maps to a CV pair, so frequency analysis reveals syllable frequencies (less diagnostic). In a logographic system, frequency analysis reveals word frequencies (which depend more on text content than on language structure).

2. Polyvalence and homophony. Many scripts have signs with multiple readings (polyvalence) or multiple signs for the same sound (homophony). These many-to-many relationships scramble the clean frequency signal that simple substitution preserves.

3. Corpus size. Reliable frequency estimates require a minimum corpus size. For an alphabet with 26 signs, about 200 characters suffice. For a syllabary with 87 signs, roughly 2,000 characters are needed. For a logographic system with 3,000 characters, tens of thousands of tokens are required. Many undeciphered scripts have corpora far below these thresholds.

How many tokens do you need for reliable frequency estimates?

A useful rule of thumb: to estimate a sign's frequency to within ±1% with 95% confidence, you need approximately $n \approx p(1-p)/0.01^2 \approx 2{,}500/p$ tokens, where $p$ is the sign's true frequency. For a common sign ($p = 0.05$), this requires ~500 tokens. For a rare sign ($p = 0.002$), you need ~12,500 tokens. The Indus Valley corpus (~18,000 tokens for ~417 signs) provides only ~43 tokens per sign on average — far too few for reliable frequency analysis of the rarer signs. This is one reason the Indus script remains undeciphered.

Redundancy: The Decipherer's Best Friend

Redundancy is the single most important concept for understanding decipherment. In information theory, redundancy measures how much of a message's structure is "extra" — beyond the minimum needed to encode the raw content. Shannon showed that English text has roughly 50% redundancy: you can remove half the letters from a typical English sentence and still reconstruct the original. This means half the information in written English is "free" — recoverable from context.

$$R = 1 - \frac{H(\text{actual})}{H(\text{maximum})}$$

where $H(\text{actual})$ is the true entropy of the text and $H(\text{maximum})$ is the entropy if all symbols were equally likely and independent.

Redundancy arises from multiple sources in writing systems:

  • Frequency imbalance: Some signs are much more common than others (contributes ~10–15% redundancy).
  • Sequential constraints: Certain sign combinations are far more likely than others — "th" is common in English, "qx" is essentially impossible (contributes ~20–25% redundancy).
  • Morphological patterns: Word structure rules constrain which signs can appear in which positions (contributes ~10–15% redundancy).
  • Semantic/syntactic constraints: Grammar and meaning further constrain word sequences (contributes ~10–15% redundancy).
The "Shannon game" — measuring redundancy experimentally

Shannon devised an elegant experiment to measure English redundancy: show a human subject a partially revealed text and ask them to guess the next letter. Skilled guessers can predict the next letter correctly about 75% of the time. Since a random guess among 26 letters would succeed only 3.8% of the time, this demonstrates that English carries enormous redundancy — most of each letter's identity is predictable from context. Shannon estimated that the true information rate of English is about 1.0–1.5 bits per letter, compared to the theoretical maximum of 4.7 bits — implying 68–79% redundancy. This is the redundancy that makes decipherment possible: a decipherer can reconstruct the encoding rules because most of the "information" in a text is determined by the rules themselves, not by the message content.

Why Redundancy Enables Decipherment
1
Consider a script with $n$ signs. The decipherer must determine each sign's reading — a total of $n \times \log_2 v$ bits of unknown information, where $v$ is the number of possible readings per sign.
2
Each token (sign occurrence) in the corpus provides some constraint on the mapping. If the text has redundancy $R$, then each token provides approximately $H_{\max} \times R$ bits of structural information — information that constrains the mapping rather than the message content.
3
Decipherment is possible when the total structural information in the corpus exceeds the unknown mapping information: $T \times H_{\max} \times R \geq n \times \log_2 v$, where $T$ is the corpus size in tokens.
4
Rearranging: the minimum corpus size for decipherment is $T_{\min} = \frac{n \times \log_2 v}{H_{\max} \times R}$. For an alphabet ($n = 26, v = 26, H_{\max} = 4.7, R = 0.5$): $T_{\min} \approx \frac{26 \times 4.7}{4.7 \times 0.5} \approx 52$ characters. For a syllabary ($n = 87, v = 87$): $T_{\min} \approx 530$ signs. For a logographic system ($n = 700, v = 700$): $T_{\min} \approx 6{,}500$ signs.
Minimum Corpus Size Calculator (Python)
import math

def min_corpus_for_decipherment(n_signs, vocab_size=None, 
                                 max_entropy=None, redundancy=0.5):
    """
    Estimate the minimum corpus size needed for decipherment.
    
    n_signs: number of distinct signs in the script
    vocab_size: number of possible readings (default = n_signs)
    max_entropy: bits per sign at maximum (default = log2(n_signs))
    redundancy: estimated text redundancy (0 to 1)
    """
    if vocab_size is None:
        vocab_size = n_signs
    if max_entropy is None:
        max_entropy = math.log2(n_signs)
    
    # Information needed to determine the mapping
    I_mapping = n_signs * math.log2(vocab_size)
    
    # Information per token from redundancy
    I_per_token = max_entropy * redundancy
    
    # Minimum corpus
    T_min = I_mapping / I_per_token
    
    print(f"Script: {n_signs} signs, {vocab_size} possible readings")
    print(f"Mapping information needed: {I_mapping:.0f} bits")
    print(f"Structural info per token: {I_per_token:.2f} bits")
    print(f"Minimum corpus: {T_min:.0f} tokens")
    print(f"  (with safety factor 3x: ~{T_min * 3:.0f} tokens)")
    return T_min

Sign Inventories and Script Classification

One of the most powerful diagnostics for an unknown script is simply counting the signs. The number of distinct signs immediately constrains what type of script it must be:

  • 15–35 signs → Alphabet (Latin, Greek, Arabic, Hebrew). Each sign represents roughly one phoneme.
  • 40–100 signs → Syllabary (Linear B, Cherokee, Japanese kana). Each sign represents a CV syllable.
  • 100–500 signs → Logosyllabic (Maya, Egyptian). Mix of logograms and syllabic signs.
  • 500+ signs → Logographic (Chinese, Sumerian). Most signs represent whole words or morphemes.

This classification is not arbitrary — it reflects a deep mathematical constraint. An alphabet needs only as many signs as there are phonemes (typically 15–35 in human languages). A syllabary needs $C \times V$ signs for $C$ consonants and $V$ vowels (typically 40–100). A logographic system needs a sign for each word or morpheme (hundreds to thousands). The sign count reveals the encoding strategy of the script.

The Indus script sign count puzzle

The Indus Valley script has approximately 417 distinct signs. This places it squarely in the "logosyllabic" zone — similar to Egyptian hieroglyphs (~700) or Maya glyphs (~800) but smaller. However, the controversy around the Indus script hinges partly on whether all 417 signs are truly distinct or whether some are variants of the same sign. If many are variants, the true count might be lower (~200), pushing toward "syllabary." If all are distinct, it suggests a mixed logosyllabic system. This ambiguity in sign counting illustrates a general problem: the diagnostic power of sign counts depends on correct sign identification, which is itself part of the decipherment problem.

Conditional Entropy and Bigram Analysis

The entropy $H(X)$ measures the average uncertainty of a single sign in isolation. But signs in a text are not independent — each sign's identity is partly predicted by the signs around it. Conditional entropy $H(X_n | X_{n-1})$ measures the remaining uncertainty of a sign after you have seen the previous sign:

$$H(X_n | X_{n-1}) = -\sum_{i,j} p(x_i, x_j) \log_2 p(x_j | x_i)$$

The difference $H(X) - H(X_n | X_{n-1})$ is the mutual information between adjacent signs — the amount of information each sign provides about the next. This quantity is always non-negative (a consequence of the data processing inequality) and is typically 15–30% of $H(X)$ for natural language texts.

Conditional entropy is a powerful diagnostic for several reasons:

  1. Script detection: Writing systems have distinctive conditional entropy signatures (ratio $H(X_n|X_{n-1})/H(X_n) \approx 0.7\text{–}0.85$) that distinguish them from non-linguistic symbol systems (~0.95–1.0).
  2. Language identification: Different languages have different bigram statistics, even when written in the same script.
  3. Decipherment aid: High mutual information between sign pairs constrains which signs can be adjacent, reducing the search space for correct readings.
Bigram Analysis and Conditional Entropy (Python)
import math
from collections import Counter, defaultdict

def bigram_analysis(text):
    """
    Compute bigram frequencies, conditional entropy,
    and mutual information for a text.
    """
    # Count unigrams and bigrams
    unigrams = Counter(text)
    bigrams = Counter(zip(text[:-1], text[1:]))
    total_uni = sum(unigrams.values())
    total_bi = sum(bigrams.values())
    
    # Unigram entropy H(X)
    H_uni = -sum((c/total_uni) * math.log2(c/total_uni) 
                  for c in unigrams.values())
    
    # Bigram entropy H(X_n, X_{n-1})
    H_bi = -sum((c/total_bi) * math.log2(c/total_bi) 
                 for c in bigrams.values())
    
    # Conditional entropy H(X_n | X_{n-1}) = H(X_n, X_{n-1}) - H(X_{n-1})
    H_cond = H_bi - H_uni
    
    # Mutual information I(X_n; X_{n-1}) = H(X_n) - H(X_n | X_{n-1})
    MI = H_uni - H_cond
    
    # Diagnostic ratio
    ratio = H_cond / H_uni if H_uni > 0 else 1.0
    
    print(f"Unigram entropy H(X): {H_uni:.3f} bits")
    print(f"Conditional entropy H(X|prev): {H_cond:.3f} bits")
    print(f"Mutual information I(X;prev): {MI:.3f} bits")
    print(f"Conditional ratio: {ratio:.3f}")
    print(f"  → {'Writing system' if 0.65 < ratio < 0.90 else 'Possibly non-linguistic'}")
    
    # Top predictive bigrams (highest MI contribution)
    print(f"\nTop 5 most predictive bigrams:")
    bigram_mi = {}
    for (a, b), count in bigrams.items():
        p_ab = count / total_bi
        p_a = unigrams[a] / total_uni
        p_b = unigrams[b] / total_uni
        mi_contribution = p_ab * math.log2(p_ab / (p_a * p_b))
        bigram_mi[(a, b)] = mi_contribution
    
    for bg, mi in sorted(bigram_mi.items(), key=lambda x: -x[1])[:5]:
        print(f"  '{bg[0]}{bg[1]}': MI contribution = {mi:.4f} bits")
    
    return H_uni, H_cond, MI

bigram_analysis(sample)

Mutual Information and Bilingual Texts

The concept of mutual information becomes especially powerful when we consider bilingual texts — inscriptions that contain the same content in two or more languages/scripts. The Rosetta Stone (Greek + Demotic + Hieroglyphic), the Behistun inscription (Old Persian + Elamite + Babylonian), and numerous other bilinguals have been the key enablers of decipherment throughout history.

Why are bilinguals so valuable? Because they create a direct channel of mutual information between a known and an unknown script. If the same message appears in both scripts, every feature of the known text provides information about the unknown text — word boundaries, proper names, content structure, sign frequencies, and more.

$$I(\text{Unknown}; \text{Known}) = H(\text{Unknown}) - H(\text{Unknown} | \text{Known})$$

A perfect bilingual (exact parallel text) provides $I = H(\text{Unknown})$ — complete information. In practice, bilinguals are never perfectly parallel (different languages express the same content differently), so $I < H(\text{Unknown})$. But even partial bilinguals provide enormous leverage.

The Value of Proper Names in Bilinguals
1
Proper names are the most valuable elements in a bilingual because they are language-independent anchors. The name "Ptolemy" must appear in both the Greek and hieroglyphic sections of the Rosetta Stone. Identifying this correspondence reveals the phonetic values of the hieroglyphic signs that spell the name.
2
Each proper name of length $k$ phonemes provides approximately $k \times \log_2 s$ bits of information, where $s$ is the script's sign inventory. For the Rosetta Stone ($s \approx 700$): "Ptolemy" ($k \approx 7$) provides $7 \times \log_2 700 \approx 65$ bits. "Cleopatra" ($k \approx 10$) provides $\approx 93$ bits.
3
The Rosetta Stone contains approximately 15 identifiable proper names. Total information from names: $\approx 15 \times 60 = 900$ bits. A full Egyptian sign-to-sound mapping requires $\approx 700 \times \log_2 700 \approx 6{,}600$ bits. Names alone provide only $\approx 14\%$ of the needed information — but they provide the crucial anchors that bootstrap the rest.
4
The remaining $\approx 86\%$ comes from: context within the bilingual (~30%), frequency analysis (~15%), morphological patterns (~20%), and comparison with known languages like Coptic (~21%). Each source independently constrains the mapping, and their combined effect is sufficient for decipherment.
Not all bilinguals are created equal

The information value of a bilingual depends on several factors: (1) Length — longer bilinguals provide more constraints. (2) Parallelism — exact translations (like the Rosetta Stone) are more valuable than loose paraphrases. (3) Name density — bilinguals rich in proper names provide more phonetic anchors. (4) Script complexity — a bilingual is more valuable when the unknown script is complex (more signs to determine). The Behistun inscription is arguably the most valuable bilingual ever found: it is long (~1,100+ lines across three scripts), highly parallel, and rich in names (~38 distinct names) — providing enough information to crack three scripts simultaneously.

A Practical Information-Theoretic Framework

We can now synthesize the concepts developed in this chapter into a practical framework for analyzing any decipherment problem. The framework asks four questions:

Question 1: What is the sign inventory? Count the signs to classify the script type (alphabet, syllabary, logosyllabic, logographic). This immediately constrains the encoding strategy and the information content per sign.

Question 2: What is the corpus size? Measure the total number of sign tokens. Compare against the minimum corpus size for the script type. If the corpus is too small, decipherment may be informationally impossible regardless of method.

Question 3: What external information is available? Bilinguals? Known language? Historical context? Each source provides bits of constraint that reduce the search space.

Question 4: What is the information deficit? Calculate $\Delta I = I_{\text{required}} - I_{\text{available}}$. If $\Delta I > 0$, decipherment requires new evidence. If $\Delta I \leq 0$, the data is sufficient and the challenge is computational/conceptual.

Complete Decipherment Feasibility Analyzer (Python)
import math

def decipherment_feasibility(name, n_signs, corpus_tokens, avg_text_len,
                              has_bilingual=False, bilingual_length=0,
                              language_known=False, related_language=False):
    """
    Comprehensive feasibility analysis for deciphering a script.
    Outputs: information required, available, deficit, and verdict.
    """
    # Step 1: Script type classification
    if n_signs < 40:
        script_type = "Alphabet"
        vocab = n_signs
    elif n_signs < 100:
        script_type = "Syllabary"
        vocab = n_signs
    elif n_signs < 500:
        script_type = "Logosyllabic"
        vocab = int(n_signs * 1.5)  # polyvalence multiplier
    else:
        script_type = "Logographic"
        vocab = int(n_signs * 2)
    
    H_max = math.log2(n_signs)
    
    # Step 2: Information required
    I_required = n_signs * math.log2(vocab)
    
    # Step 3: Information available
    # From corpus (depends on text length → context richness)
    if avg_text_len < 5:
        r = 0.08   # very short texts
    elif avg_text_len < 20:
        r = 0.25   # medium texts
    elif avg_text_len < 100:
        r = 0.40   # long texts
    else:
        r = 0.50   # very long texts
    
    I_corpus = corpus_tokens * r
    I_bilingual = bilingual_length * math.log2(vocab) if has_bilingual else 0
    I_language = I_required * 0.3 if language_known else (I_required * 0.15 if related_language else 0)
    
    I_available = I_corpus + I_bilingual + I_language
    deficit = I_required - I_available
    
    # Verdict
    if deficit <= 0:
        verdict = "FEASIBLE — data sufficient for decipherment"
    elif deficit < I_required * 0.2:
        verdict = "MARGINAL — possible with clever analysis"
    elif deficit < I_required * 0.5:
        verdict = "DIFFICULT — needs additional evidence"
    else:
        verdict = "LIKELY IMPOSSIBLE — major new evidence required"
    
    print(f"=== {name} ===")
    print(f"Script type: {script_type} ({n_signs} signs)")
    print(f"Corpus: {corpus_tokens:,} tokens, avg text length: {avg_text_len}")
    print(f"")
    print(f"I_required:  {I_required:>8,.0f} bits")
    print(f"I_corpus:    {I_corpus:>8,.0f} bits")
    print(f"I_bilingual: {I_bilingual:>8,.0f} bits")
    print(f"I_language:  {I_language:>8,.0f} bits")
    print(f"I_available: {I_available:>8,.0f} bits")
    print(f"DEFICIT:     {deficit:>8,.0f} bits ({max(0, deficit/I_required*100):.0f}%)")
    print(f"→ {verdict}")
    return deficit

The Information-Theoretic Limits of Decipherment

Our framework reveals a profound truth: decipherment has hard information-theoretic limits. No amount of cleverness can extract information that is not present in the data. If the corpus is too small, the texts too short, or the script too complex relative to available evidence, decipherment is mathematically impossible — not merely difficult, but impossible in a rigorous, provable sense.

This insight reframes the problem of undeciphered scripts. The question is not "why haven't we been clever enough to crack the Indus script?" but rather "does the available data contain enough information for any solution to be uniquely determined?" For the Indus script, the answer appears to be no — the information deficit is real, not an artifact of insufficient effort.

However, the limits also offer hope. They tell us exactly what kind of evidence would close the gap: a bilingual of specific length, a corpus expansion of specific size, or a language identification of specific confidence. Decipherment research can be guided by information theory — pursuing the evidence types that provide the most bits per unit of archaeological effort.

The three eras of decipherment

Looking across all the case studies we will explore in this course, decipherment methods fall into three eras: (1) Bilingual era (pre-1950) — Egyptian, cuneiform, and other scripts cracked using multilingual inscriptions. The bilingual provides a direct information bridge. (2) Structural era (1950–2000) — Linear B and Maya cracked using internal structural analysis (frequency, distribution, grammatical patterns). No bilingual needed, but a candidate language helps enormously. (3) Computational era (2000–present) — Algorithms and AI applied to both deciphered and undeciphered scripts. The computer searches vast hypothesis spaces that no human could explore. Each era exploits information from different sources, but the underlying mathematics — Shannon's information theory — connects them all.

What this chapter means for the rest of the course

Every concept introduced in this chapter will recur throughout the decipherment stories that follow. When Champollion uses the Rosetta Stone, he is exploiting mutual information between parallel texts. When Kober analyzes Linear B triplets, she is exploiting conditional entropy in inflection patterns. When Knorozov reads Maya glyphs, he is exploiting the redundancy of phonetic complements. And when modern algorithms attack undeciphered scripts, they are computing information deficits to assess feasibility. Information theory is not just a mathematical framework — it is the hidden language of all decipherment.

Key Takeaways: Information Theory of Writing Systems

Writing systems are communication channels — they encode linguistic messages into visual symbols, with redundancy protecting against noise. For decipherment, our ignorance of the encoding is the noise.

Shannon entropy $H = -\sum p_i \log_2 p_i$ measures the information content per sign. Alphabets: ~4.1 bits/letter. Syllabaries: ~6.4 bits/sign. Logographic: ~11+ bits/character. Despite these differences, all scripts converge on ~3–5 bits per phoneme.

Redundancy (~50% for English) is the decipherer's essential resource. It arises from frequency imbalance, sequential constraints, morphological patterns, and semantic context. Without redundancy, decipherment would be impossible.

The minimum corpus for decipherment scales with sign inventory size: ~50 tokens for alphabets, ~500 for syllabaries, ~6,500 for logographic systems. Many undeciphered scripts fall below these thresholds.

The information deficit $\Delta I = I_{\text{required}} - I_{\text{available}}$ determines decipherment feasibility. When $\Delta I > 0$, no method can succeed — new evidence is needed. This framework explains both past successes and current stalemates.

🟢 1. Define Shannon Entropy +

State the formula for Shannon entropy and explain in your own words what it measures.

Shannon entropy is $H = -\sum_i p_i \log_2 p_i$ bits/symbol. It measures the average information content (or unpredictability) of a symbol drawn from a distribution. High entropy = high unpredictability = more information per symbol. Low entropy = high predictability = less information per symbol.

🟢 2. Maximum Entropy for an Alphabet +

What is the maximum possible entropy for a 26-letter alphabet, and when is it achieved?

Maximum entropy is $H_{max} = \log_2 26 \approx 4.70$ bits/symbol. It is achieved when all 26 letters are equally probable (each appears with probability 1/26). English is below this maximum because letters have unequal frequencies.

🟢 3. Frequency Analysis and Why It Works +

Explain in two or three sentences why frequency analysis can crack a simple substitution cipher.

A substitution cipher replaces each letter with another letter one-to-one. Because the substitution is fixed, the frequency distribution of the ciphertext mirrors the frequency distribution of the plaintext language. The most common ciphertext symbol must correspond to the most common plaintext letter (e.g., 'E' in English), giving an immediate foothold.

🟢 4. Script Classification by Sign Count +

A newly discovered script has 85 distinct signs. What type of writing system does this suggest?

85 signs falls in the syllabary range (roughly 40–120 signs). This suggests the script is a syllabary, where each sign represents a consonant-vowel syllable. Examples in this range include Linear B (~87 signs) and the Japanese syllabaries Hiragana and Katakana (~46 signs each).

🟢 5. What Is Redundancy? +

Define linguistic redundancy and explain why it helps decipherers.

Redundancy is $R = 1 - H/H_{max}$. It measures the proportion of text that is statistically predictable. High redundancy means each new symbol is partly predictable from context. For decipherers, redundancy creates constraints: if you correctly identify some symbols, the surrounding context helps identify others. English's ~75% redundancy means most text is recoverable even from partial information.

🟢 6. Al-Kindi's Contribution +

Who was Al-Kindi and what was his key insight about language and cipher analysis?

Al-Kindi (c. 801–873 CE) was an Arab polymath who wrote the first known treatise on cryptanalysis. His key insight: natural language has a fixed frequency distribution for its letters/symbols. A simple substitution cipher preserves this distribution, just relabeled. By matching cipher frequencies to known language frequencies, you can break the cipher without knowing the key.

🟢 7. Entropy Calculation +

Calculate the entropy of a 2-symbol system where symbol A occurs 90% of the time and symbol B occurs 10% of the time. Compare it to the maximum entropy.

$H = -0.9 \log_2 0.9 - 0.1 \log_2 0.1 \approx 0.152 + 0.332 = 0.469$ bits/symbol. Maximum for 2 symbols is $\log_2 2 = 1$ bit/symbol. This system has only 47% of maximum entropy — it is very predictable (A appears 9 times out of 10).

🟢 8. Mutual Information in Plain Language +

Explain what mutual information means in the context of a bilingual inscription.

Mutual information $I(X;Y)$ measures how much knowing one text reduces your uncertainty about the other. For a bilingual inscription, the two texts encode the same semantic content in different scripts, so $I(X;Y)$ is high. Knowing the Greek text of the Rosetta Stone reduces your uncertainty about the hieroglyphic text, because the same content (a royal decree) is expressed in both.

🟢 9. Why Are Proper Nouns Useful? +

Why do proper nouns play such an important role in decipherment?

Proper nouns (names of people and places) must be spelled phonetically in any script — they cannot be paraphrased. In a bilingual text, a name like 'Ptolemy' appears in both scripts as a phonetic approximation of the same name. This provides a direct mapping between known phonetic values (from the known script) and unknown values (from the unknown script), acting as a 'known plaintext' anchor.

🟢 10. Zipf's Law and Writing +

State Zipf's law and explain how it applies to writing systems.

Zipf's law states that in a corpus of natural language, the frequency of a word is inversely proportional to its frequency rank: the $n$-th most common word appears approximately $1/n$ as often as the most common word. This creates a highly skewed distribution where a few words dominate the corpus. For writing, this means a small number of signs/words appear very frequently while most appear rarely — exactly the pattern that frequency analysis exploits.

🟡 11. Entropy Comparison Across Languages +

English has about 4.2 bits/character entropy while a language with a 100-character script might have higher character-level entropy. Does higher character entropy make a script harder to decipher? Explain the factors involved.

Not necessarily. Higher entropy per character means less predictability per character, but decipherment depends on total information and redundancy, not just per-character entropy. A large character inventory with high entropy may still have high redundancy if the language has strong grammatical patterns. What matters is whether the corpus provides enough total bits of constraint, i.e., corpus_size × H × (1 - R) > log₂(k!), where k is the sign inventory size. A 100-sign syllabary with 90% redundancy could be easier to decipher than a 30-sign alphabet with low redundancy.

🟡 12. Bigram Matrix Analysis +

You analyze a script and build a bigram frequency matrix. You find that signs A, B, C are always followed by one of signs X, Y, Z, and vice versa. What might this tell you about the script's structure?

This suggests a CV (consonant-vowel) syllabary structure. Signs A, B, C likely share the same consonant but differ in vowel (or vice versa), and signs X, Y, Z form a complementary set. In a pure CV syllabary, you would expect strong bigram patterns: a sign ending in vowel V₁ is typically followed by a sign starting with consonant C₁, so the bigram matrix clusters along phonological lines. This type of analysis was central to Ventris's grid method for Linear B.

🟡 13. Information Content of the Rosetta Stone +

The Rosetta Stone has ~1450 lines of Greek text and ~1419 hieroglyphic signs. Estimate the mutual information available for decipherment, assuming English-like entropy (4.2 bits/character) and 75% redundancy.

Available mutual information ≈ corpus size × entropy × (1 − redundancy) = 1419 × 4.2 × 0.25 ≈ 1490 bits. For a 700-sign hieroglyphic system, we need log₂(700!) ≈ 6600 bits to fully specify the mapping. So the Rosetta Stone alone was theoretically insufficient to fully decipher hieroglyphs — and indeed, Champollion needed additional inscriptions (Philae obelisk, Abu Simbel cartouches) to confirm the full system. The Stone was a crucial anchor, not a complete solution.

🟡 14. Designing an Alphabet for Minimum Decipherability +

If you wanted to design a writing system that would be maximally difficult to decipher (for a future civilization), what properties would it have? Analyze from an information-theoretic perspective.

To maximize decipherment difficulty: (1) Minimize redundancy — use a compressed encoding where each symbol carries maximum information with minimum statistical correlation to neighbors. (2) Use variable sign meaning — polyvalent signs where the same glyph means different things in different contexts. (3) Avoid proper nouns or use obfuscated phonetic representations. (4) Mix encoding types (some signs logographic, some syllabic, some alphabetic) without consistent rules. (5) Minimize the corpus size. This combination eliminates frequency analysis foothold (low redundancy), removes proper-noun anchors, and makes structural inference impossible without context.

🟡 15. Conditional Entropy in Inflected Languages +

An inflected language (like Latin or Ancient Greek) has grammatical endings that vary systematically with case, number, and gender. How would this affect the bigram conditional entropy H(X₂|X₁) in a syllabic script recording such a language?

Inflected languages create strong positional patterns: word-final signs are constrained to a small set of inflectional endings. This lowers H(X₂|X₁) at word boundaries — the last sign of a word strongly predicts certain possible endings, and the grammatical role predicts which paradigm. In Kober's analysis of Linear B, she found groups of words ('triplets') where three words shared the same stem signs but had three different pair-ending patterns, showing the three case endings for masculine nouns. This is exactly the signature of low conditional entropy at word-final position.

🟡 16. Estimating Minimum Corpus Size +

A newly discovered script has 50 distinct signs. Assuming it is a syllabary with ~60% redundancy and ~5 bits/sign entropy, calculate the minimum corpus size needed to theoretically constrain the full decipherment.

We need at least log₂(50!) ≈ log₂(3.04 × 10⁶⁴) ≈ 214 bits of constraint. Each sign provides H × (1−R) = 5 × 0.4 = 2 bits of effective constraint. Minimum corpus = 214 / 2 = 107 signs. In practice, due to sparse coverage of rare signs and the difficulty of extracting all available information, you'd need at least 10× this: ~1000 signs or more. This is well within reach for most syllabaries.

🟡 17. Why Logographic Scripts Are Harder +

Explain why logographic scripts (like early Sumerian) are generally harder to decipher than alphabets, using information-theoretic reasoning.

A logographic script with k=500 signs has log₂(500!) ≈ 4000 bits of mapping space to constrain, versus log₂(30!) ≈ 108 bits for a 30-sign alphabet. The corpus must provide proportionally more information. Additionally, logograms have low positional redundancy (a logogram for 'king' can appear anywhere in a sentence), reducing the contextual constraints available. Polysemy (one sign with multiple meanings) and homophony (multiple signs with the same sound) add further ambiguity. Without a related known language or explicit glosses, the mapping space is simply too large relative to typical corpus sizes.

🟡 18. The Role of Determinatives +

Many ancient scripts (Egyptian hieroglyphs, Sumerian cuneiform) use 'determinatives' — silent signs that classify the semantic category of the word they accompany. From an information-theoretic perspective, do determinatives make scripts easier or harder to decipher?

Determinatives make scripts easier to decipher. They add semantic redundancy: a determinative for 'city' following a word tells you the word is a place name, even before you know its phonetic value. This semantic tagging creates strong contextual constraints that reduce H(word|context). For a decipherer, determinatives cluster words into semantic categories, allowing systematic analysis: 'all words with the seated-man determinative are masculine personal names,' etc. This was crucial in hieroglyphic decipherment — the determinative for 'king' marked royal names even before cartouches were understood.

🟡 19. Practical Frequency Analysis +

You have a corpus of 500 signs in an unknown script with 30 distinct signs. The most frequent sign appears 85 times. The expected frequency for equal distribution would be 500/30 ≈ 16.7. Calculate the information this provides about the script's entropy.

The observed frequency of the top sign is 85/500 = 0.17 (17%). If all signs were equal, p = 1/30 ≈ 0.033. The actual entropy with one sign at 0.17 and the remaining 29 uniformly sharing the rest: p_rest = (1-0.17)/29 ≈ 0.029. H ≈ −0.17 log₂(0.17) − 29 × 0.029 log₂(0.029) ≈ 0.466 + 29 × 0.172 ≈ 0.466 + 4.99 ≈ 5.45 bits. Compare to H_max = log₂(30) ≈ 4.91 bits. Wait — note that when one sign dominates, H is LOWER than H_max. H ≈ 4.37 bits in practice for this uneven distribution. Redundancy ≈ 1 − 4.37/4.91 ≈ 11%, suggesting the script is relatively efficient (not highly redundant at first glance, though bigram analysis would reveal more).

🟡 20. Comparing Decipherment Strategies +

Compare the strategies available for decipherment when (a) you have a bilingual text, (b) you know the language family but have no bilingual text, and (c) you have neither. Analyze the information-theoretic constraints in each case.

(a) Bilingual text: Mutual information I(X;Y) directly constrains the mapping. Proper nouns provide character-level alignment. This is the richest case — the Rosetta Stone scenario. (b) Known language family: You have strong phonological priors. Sound correspondence laws constrain the phonetic values of signs. Cognate detection can identify loan words or grammatical words that match the known family. This reduces the effective search space substantially. (c) Neither: You are limited to internal structural analysis — frequency, bigram, positional constraints. You can classify the script type and identify paradigmatic patterns (like Kober's triplets), but you cannot assign phonetic values without external anchors. Decipherment in this case requires either a serendipitous discovery or advances in computational methods that can bootstrap from typological universals.

🔴 21. Information-Theoretic Bound on Undecipherability +

The Indus Valley script has approximately 5000 inscriptions with an average length of 5 signs, using ~400 distinct signs. Prove that this corpus is theoretically insufficient to uniquely determine the decipherment, even with perfect statistical analysis.

Total corpus: ~25,000 signs. For a 400-sign system, the mapping space is log₂(400!) ≈ 2900 bits. Each sign provides at most H_max = log₂(400) ≈ 8.6 bits of information, but with typical linguistic redundancy (~70%), effective information per sign ≈ 8.6 × 0.3 ≈ 2.6 bits. Total available information: 25,000 × 2.6 = 65,000 bits. This seems sufficient, BUT: (1) The corpus consists of 5000 separate short inscriptions, so long-range context is unavailable. (2) Average inscription length of 5 signs means we cannot observe bigram patterns reliably for rare signs. (3) With no external constraint (no bilingual text, unknown language), we have no prior to anchor the mapping. The effective degrees of freedom remain high. Furthermore, if many inscriptions are single-word seals (labels), the positional statistics collapse to first/last sign distributions only, providing far fewer constraints than a continuous text. The Farmer-Sproat-Witzel argument (2004) that the Indus corpus may not even be linguistic writing is consistent with this analysis.

🔴 22. Deriving the Relationship Between Script Type and Information Rate +

Derive a relationship between the number of signs k in a writing system and the expected information rate in bits per morpheme, assuming a language with V distinct vocabulary items and m morphemes per word on average.

Consider a language with vocabulary size V and average word length m morphemes. Each morpheme must be encodable by the script. For an alphabet with phoneme inventory P: each morpheme of average length ℓ phonemes requires ℓ signs. Information per morpheme: ℓ × log₂(P) bits maximum. For a syllabary: each morpheme of ℓ syllables requires ℓ signs, but log₂(k) bits per sign where k ≈ P²/2 (number of CV combinations). For a logography: each morpheme gets 1 sign (k ≈ V), so log₂(V) bits per morpheme in 1 sign. Information rate per morpheme: alphabet gives ℓ × log₂(P), syllabary gives ℓ × log₂(P²/2) ≈ ℓ(2log₂P − 1), logography gives log₂(V). The logography is most efficient per morpheme (fewest signs) but requires the most symbols to memorize. Scripts evolve toward the minimum k that maintains sufficient phonological specificity — syllabaries are a natural equilibrium for CV-heavy languages.

🔴 23. Designing a Decipherment Algorithm +

Design a complete algorithm for computationally deciphering an unknown abjad (consonantal alphabet) given: an unknown corpus U, a candidate target language L with known statistics, and the assumption of a one-to-one sign mapping. State the objective function, search strategy, and convergence criterion.

Objective function: Given mapping f: U_signs → L_consonants, maximize P(f | corpus) ∝ P(corpus | f) × P(f). P(corpus | f) is computed by applying f to get 'decoded' text and measuring its likelihood under a character n-gram language model of L. P(f) is the prior (uniform for one-to-one mappings, or linguistically informed for known phonological typology). Search strategy: (1) Initialize with frequency-rank matching (most common U sign → most common L consonant). (2) Apply iterative hill climbing: for each pair of sign mappings (f(a), f(b)), compute the log-likelihood improvement from swapping them; accept improvements. (3) Add simulated annealing to escape local optima: accept swaps with probability exp(ΔLL/T) where T decreases over iterations. (4) Run multiple restarts from random initializations. Convergence criterion: No swap among all n(n-1)/2 pairs improves log-likelihood by more than ε = 0.001. Validation: Check that decoded text matches known typological properties (consonant cluster distribution, word length distribution, etc.). This is essentially the Snyder et al. (2010) approach for Ugaritic.

🔴 24. Entropy of Mixed Writing Systems +

Consider a script that uses two types of signs: 200 logograms (each representing a whole word) and 50 syllabic signs. A typical text uses 60% logographic signs and 40% syllabic signs. Calculate the entropy of this script and discuss how this mixed system affects decipherment difficulty.

For the logographic component: if 200 logograms are used uniformly in 60% of positions, their entropy contribution is 0.6 × log₂(200) ≈ 0.6 × 7.64 = 4.58 bits. For syllabic component: 50 syllabic signs in 40% of positions → 0.4 × log₂(50) ≈ 0.4 × 5.64 = 2.26 bits. Combined entropy: ~6.84 bits/sign maximum (actual lower due to unequal frequencies). Decipherment difficulty: The two subsystems interact. Syllabic signs help identify phonetic values, but they must be distinguished from logograms first. If determinatives mark which signs are logograms, this disambiguation is easy. If not, you have a mixture model to untangle. The effective degrees of freedom for decipherment are the product of the two subsystem mapping spaces: log₂(200! × 50!) ≈ log₂(200!) + log₂(50!) ≈ 1600 + 214 = 1814 bits to constrain. A corpus of ~2000 signs would theoretically suffice, but only if the two subsystems can be separated.

🔴 25. Proving the Necessity of External Constraints +

Prove that for any script with k ≥ 26 signs and a corpus of N signs, there always exists an alternative decipherment that achieves the same unigram frequency match as the correct decipherment. What does this imply for frequency analysis alone?

Proof: Given correct mapping f*, any permutation π of the target alphabet symbols gives an alternative mapping f' = π ∘ f*. Since π is a bijection on the target alphabet, the frequency of f'(x) for each source sign x equals the frequency of f*(x). The unigram frequency distribution of the decoded text under f' is identical to that under f* (just with symbol labels permuted). Therefore, unigram frequency matching alone CANNOT distinguish f* from any of its k! permutations. Implication: Frequency analysis alone (unigram level) cannot achieve a unique decipherment — it provides necessary but not sufficient constraints. To uniquely constrain the mapping, you need either (1) higher-order statistics (bigrams, trigrams) that are not permutation-invariant because language models are not exchangeable, or (2) external constraints (known proper nouns, bilingual anchors, phonological priors). This is why Champollion needed proper names (cartouches), why Ventris needed the grid method (bigram structure), and why computational methods use language models rather than frequency tables.

🔴 26. Channel Capacity of Ancient Scripts +

Model a clay tablet cuneiform inscription as a noisy channel: the sender was the original scribe, the receiver is the modern scholar. Physical damage destroys 20% of signs uniformly at random. Calculate the channel capacity and the maximum data recovery rate.

Model the channel as a binary erasure channel with erasure probability ε = 0.20. The capacity of a binary erasure channel is C = 1 − ε = 0.80 bits per channel use. For a cuneiform sign with entropy H ≈ 7 bits/sign (for ~128 sign system), the effective channel capacity is C_eff = 0.80 × 7 = 5.6 bits/sign. Shannon's theorem guarantees that data can be recovered at rates up to C_eff with arbitrarily small error probability — IF appropriate error-correcting codes are used. Natural language's 70% redundancy acts as an implicit error-correcting code: with H_eff ≈ 7 × 0.3 = 2.1 bits/sign of real information, and a channel capacity of 5.6 bits/sign, there is a comfortable margin. The maximum erasure fraction that language redundancy alone can correct is ε_max = 1 − H_info/H_total = 1 − 2.1/7 = 0.70. So natural cuneiform text can theoretically survive up to 70% erasure and still be recoverable — explaining why badly damaged tablets can still be read by experts.

🔴 27. Information Theory and the Farmer-Sproat-Witzel Debate +

The Farmer-Sproat-Witzel (2004) paper argues that the Indus Valley 'script' may not be writing at all, but rather proto-writing or non-linguistic symbols. Design an information-theoretic test that could help resolve this question, and describe its limitations.

Test design: Compute the conditional entropy H(sign_{n+1} | sign_n) for the Indus corpus. For true writing: H(sign_{n+1} | sign_n) << H(sign_{n+1}) — consecutive signs are statistically dependent as in language. For non-linguistic symbol systems (e.g., heraldic emblems, owner marks): signs may appear independently, giving H(sign_{n+1} | sign_n) ≈ H(sign_{n+1}). Additionally compute: (1) Type-token ratio: linguistic texts have Zipfian distributions; random labels may not. (2) Positional entropy: in writing, sign entropy varies by position (lower at beginnings/ends due to grammatical constraints); random sequences have uniform positional entropy. (3) Long-range correlations: compute mutual information I(sign_i; sign_j) for j-i up to 10; linguistic texts show slow decay while random sequences show sharp cutoff. Limitations: (1) Short inscription length (~5 signs average) makes statistical tests unreliable for individual inscriptions. (2) Seals may be labels (single words/names) rather than continuous text, exhibiting weaker sequential statistics even if linguistic. (3) Multiple sign usage patterns (short seals vs longer copper tablets) may reflect different text genres. The test can suggest a direction but cannot definitively prove or disprove linguistic status with the current corpus.

🔴 28. Mutual Information Estimation from Bilingual Texts +

You have a bilingual text with 2000 characters of known language A and 1800 signs of unknown script B, representing the same content. Describe how you would estimate the mutual information I(A;B) and use it to extract phonetic correspondences.

Step 1: Align the texts. Use known word boundaries in A to hypothesize word boundaries in B. For each word in A with frequency f_A(w), find the corresponding position in B and compute co-occurrence counts of B sign sequences with each A word. Step 2: Build a co-occurrence matrix M where M[b_seq][a_word] counts how often B-sequence b_seq aligns with A-word a_word. Step 3: Estimate I(A;B) = H(A) + H(B) − H(A,B). Compute H(A) from A word frequencies, H(B) from B sequence frequencies, H(A,B) from joint frequencies. High I(A;B) confirms the texts are translations of each other. Step 4: Use the co-occurrence matrix to extract correspondences. Apply EM (Expectation-Maximization): E-step: estimate P(b_sign | a_char) from current alignments; M-step: update sign-character alignments to maximize P(B|A). Iterate to convergence. Validated when: (1) high-frequency A words consistently align with specific B sequences, (2) proper noun A words align with phonetically reasonable B sign sequences, (3) the induced language model for B's 'decoded' text has low perplexity under a model of A's language family.

🔴 29. Typological Universals as Decipherment Priors +

Linguistic typology has identified universal tendencies in human languages (e.g., Greenberg's universals, frequency distributions of consonants/vowels). How can these universals be formalized as Bayesian priors for a computational decipherment algorithm?

Formalization: Let f: S → P be the mapping from script signs to phonemes. The Bayesian prior P(f) should encode: (1) Phoneme frequency prior: P(f maps sign s to phoneme p) ∝ frequency(p in world languages). High-frequency phonemes (a, i, u, n, s, t, k) should be assigned to common signs. (2) Syllable structure prior: if the script is a syllabary and the target language type is CV, P(f) rewards mappings where common syllables (CV combinations) are assigned to the most frequent signs. (3) Phonological clustering prior: signs that appear in similar positional distributions are likely phonologically similar (same manner of articulation, same vowel). P(f) rewards mappings where positionally similar signs map to phonologically proximate phonemes. (4) Markedness prior: unmarked phonemes (voiced stops, central vowels) should be more common than marked ones (ejectives, retroflex). Implementation: Use a hierarchical Bayesian model where the prior is a mixture of typological language profiles. The posterior P(f | corpus) ∝ P(corpus | f) × P(f) is sampled via MCMC. Limitation: Priors based on modern language typology may not reflect ancient languages, introducing systematic bias — particularly for language isolates or extinct branches.

🔴 30. The Computational Complexity of Decipherment +

Show that finding the maximum-likelihood decipherment mapping f*: S → L (from script signs S to target language L) is NP-hard by reduction to a known NP-hard problem. What does this imply for practical decipherment algorithms?

Reduction from the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) or, more directly, from the problem of finding the maximum-weight matching in a bipartite graph under a non-linear objective. Formal sketch: Given a corpus of sign bigrams with frequencies f(s_i, s_j), and a target language with bigram probabilities P(c_i, c_j), the decipherment objective is to find a bijection f: S → L maximizing Σ f(s_i, s_j) × log P(f(s_i), f(s_j)). This is the Quadratic Assignment Problem (QAP): place k items in k locations to maximize quadratic interactions. QAP is NP-hard. Implication for practice: (1) Exhaustive search is infeasible for k > 15. (2) Local search (hill climbing) converges to local optima, not global. (3) The practical algorithms (simulated annealing, genetic algorithms, EM) provide approximate solutions with no guarantee of optimality. (4) With good initializations (frequency-rank matching, typological priors), local search often finds the correct solution for small scripts (k ≤ 30), explaining the success of Snyder et al. (2010) on Ugaritic (30 signs). For larger scripts (k = 87 for Linear B, k = 700 for hieroglyphs), the problem is too hard for pure computation and requires human expertise to provide structural insights that prune the search space.
