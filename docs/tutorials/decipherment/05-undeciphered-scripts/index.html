Undeciphered Scripts

Despite the spectacular successes of the last two centuries — Egyptian, cuneiform, Linear B, Maya — many ancient writing systems remain unread. These undeciphered scripts represent some of the most tantalizing intellectual puzzles in human knowledge: entire civilizations whose written records exist but cannot be understood. In some cases, we can physically hold a message from 4,000 years ago in our hands, see the individual signs clearly, and yet have no idea what it says.

Why do some scripts resist decipherment while others yield? This chapter examines the three most famous undeciphered scripts — the Indus Valley script, Linear A, and Rongorongo — through an information-theoretic lens. We will develop a formal framework for analyzing why decipherment fails, quantifying the information deficit that separates readable from unreadable scripts. The analysis reveals that decipherment is not merely a matter of cleverness — it requires sufficient information, and when that information doesn't exist, no amount of brilliance can compensate.

Information deficit

The information deficit of an undeciphered script is the gap between the information available to would-be decipherers and the information needed to uniquely determine the sign-to-meaning/sound mapping. If $I_{\text{available}} < I_{\text{needed}}$, the system is underdetermined — multiple consistent decipherments exist, and no purely analytical method can select among them. The deficit $\Delta I = I_{\text{needed}} - I_{\text{available}}$ quantifies how far we are from a solution and what kind of new evidence would close the gap.

The Indus Valley Script

The Indus Valley civilization (c. 2600–1900 BCE) — also called the Harappan civilization — was one of the three great Bronze Age civilizations, alongside Egypt and Mesopotamia. Its cities (Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, Dholavira) show sophisticated urban planning, standardized weights and measures, and extensive trade networks. Its writing system appears on over 4,000 inscribed objects — mostly small sealstone stamps, plus some pottery, copper tablets, and a few other objects.

And yet, after a century of effort, the Indus script remains undeciphered. The reasons are instructive:

Why the Indus Script Resists

Challenge 1: Extremely short texts. The average Indus inscription is only 4.6 signs long. The longest known inscription contains just 26 signs. Compare this with the Rosetta Stone (~1,400 hieroglyphic signs) or the Behistun inscription (~1,200 lines). Short texts provide minimal context and minimal statistical data for frequency analysis or pattern recognition.

Challenge 2: No bilingual. No Indus-bilingual inscription has ever been found. There is no Rosetta Stone, no Behistun, no parallel text in a known language. Without external semantic anchors, decipherers must rely entirely on internal analysis — and the inscriptions are too short for internal analysis to yield definitive results.

Challenge 3: Unknown language. We don't know what language the Indus people spoke. Candidates include Proto-Dravidian, an early Indo-Aryan language, a language isolate, or something else entirely. Without a candidate language to test readings against, there is no linguistic prior to constrain the decipherment.

Challenge 4: Uncertain sign count. Estimates of the number of distinct signs range from ~400 to ~600, depending on whether certain variations are counted as separate signs or variants of one sign. This range spans the boundary between "large syllabary" and "small logography," leaving even the type of writing system in dispute.

Information Deficit of the Indus Script
1
Information needed: With ~400 signs, full decipherment requires determining each sign's reading. Minimum: $400 \times \log_2 400 \approx 400 \times 8.6 = 3440$ bits (assuming each sign maps to a unique meaning/sound from a space of 400).
2
Information available from corpus: Total corpus: $\approx 4000$ texts $\times$ 4.6 signs/text $= 18{,}400$ sign tokens. At $\log_2 400 = 8.6$ bits per sign, raw data: $18{,}400 \times 8.6 \approx 158{,}000$ bits. But most of this is message content, not system information. The useful structural information (frequencies, bigram statistics, positional patterns): $\approx 400 \times 8.6 + 400^2 \times 0.01 \approx 5040$ bits.
3
Structural information ($\approx 5040$ bits) exceeds needed information ($\approx 3440$ bits) — so in principle, enough data exists. But the critical missing piece is the semantic anchor: structural analysis can determine which signs belong to the same category and which patterns exist, but cannot determine what the categories mean (which sounds or words they represent).
4
The semantic grounding deficit: without a bilingual or known language, the mapping from structural classes to actual readings requires $\approx 2000+$ bits of external information that simply does not exist. This is the true information deficit: $\Delta I \approx 2000$ bits. Closing this gap requires either: (a) finding a bilingual, (b) identifying the language, or (c) discovering much longer texts with identifiable content (names, dates, formulas).
Is the Indus script even writing?

Some scholars (notably Steve Farmer, Richard Sproat, and Michael Witzel) have argued that the Indus "script" may not be writing at all — that the short sign sequences on seals may be non-linguistic symbols (like heraldic devices or trademarks) rather than encodings of language. The evidence is genuinely ambiguous: the texts are too short for standard statistical tests of linguistic structure to be decisive. If the Indus symbols are non-linguistic, then "decipherment" in the traditional sense is impossible — there is no language to decode. This remains one of the most contentious debates in archaeology.

Linear A: So Close, Yet So Far

Linear A is the writing system of the Minoan civilization of Crete, used from roughly 1800–1450 BCE. It is the direct ancestor of Linear B — many Linear A signs have clear visual equivalents in Linear B. And since Linear B has been deciphered, one might expect Linear A to yield quickly. It has not.

The problem is that while we can read many Linear A signs phonetically (by borrowing their Linear B values), the resulting sounds do not form words in any known language. Linear B writes Greek; Linear A writes something else — the unknown Minoan language. We can transliterate Linear A texts (produce approximate sound sequences), but we cannot understand them. It is as if you could read Arabic script aloud but didn't know Arabic — you could produce sounds without comprehension.

Transliteration without understanding

Linear A illustrates a crucial distinction in decipherment: script decipherment (determining the sound values of signs) vs. language decipherment (understanding the meaning of the resulting words). Linear A's script is largely deciphered — we know approximately how to pronounce most signs. But the language remains undeciphered because Minoan has no known relatives. This distinction maps to information theory: script decipherment requires $\sim \log_2(N!)$ bits to fix the sign-sound mapping, while language decipherment requires an entire linguistic model — orders of magnitude more information.

Rongorongo: The Isolated Script

Rongorongo is the writing system of Easter Island (Rapa Nui), first documented by European missionaries in the 1860s. It is one of very few scripts invented independently in the Pacific, and its existence on such a small, isolated island is remarkable. Approximately 26 inscribed wooden objects survive, containing a total of about 14,000 glyphs organized into ~120 distinct sign types.

Rongorongo faces extreme decipherment challenges: the corpus is tiny, no bilingual exists, the language (Old Rapanui) is poorly attested, and — uniquely — the script is written in reverse boustrophedon: alternate lines run in opposite directions, and the reader must rotate the tablet 180° at the end of each line.

A General Framework: When Is Decipherment Possible?

Drawing on the successes and failures examined throughout this course, we can formalize the conditions for successful decipherment:

Decipherment Feasibility Conditions
1
Condition 1 — Sufficient corpus size: The corpus must contain enough sign tokens ($T$) to establish reliable frequency distributions and co-occurrence patterns. Minimum: $T > 10 \times N$ where $N$ is the sign inventory size. For $N = 400$: $T > 4000$ tokens. The Indus corpus ($\approx 18{,}400$ tokens) meets this. Rongorongo ($\approx 14{,}000$ tokens) also meets this. Both have sufficient structural data.
2
Condition 2 — External semantic anchor: Either a bilingual text, a known related language, or identifiable content (e.g., known historical events, predictable formulas) must provide semantic grounding. This is the critical bottleneck for all undeciphered scripts. The information from a bilingual: $I_{\text{bilingual}} \propto L \times \log_2 V$ where $L$ = text length and $V$ = vocabulary size.
3
Condition 3 — Correct structural hypothesis: The decipherer must correctly identify the script type (alphabet, syllabary, logography, or mixed). An incorrect hypothesis wastes all analytical effort. The information cost of testing $k$ structural hypotheses: $\log_2 k$ bits — trivial compared to the decipherment itself, but the social cost of a wrong hypothesis held as orthodoxy (cf. Thompson on Maya) can be enormous.
4
Condition 4 — Language identification or model: The underlying language must be known, or a closely related language must exist to serve as a model. For Indus (unknown language family) and Rongorongo (poorly attested Old Rapanui), this condition fails. For Linear A (Minoan — no known relatives), this is the primary obstacle.
Could new discoveries change everything?

For each undeciphered script, there is a "dream discovery" that would likely enable decipherment: (1) For Indus: a bilingual inscription — even a short one with known names — would provide the semantic anchor; alternatively, a much longer Indus text (100+ signs) would enable statistical analysis to determine the script type definitively. (2) For Linear A: discovering a language related to Minoan — perhaps preserved in place-names or loanwords — would complete the decipherment. (3) For Rongorongo: a surviving oral tradition that matches specific text passages would provide content anchors. In each case, the needed information is specific and quantifiable — decipherment awaits not cleverness but data.

Key Takeaways: Undeciphered Scripts

Decipherment is an information problem. Success requires sufficient total information from: corpus statistics, external anchors (bilinguals/known content), and linguistic models (known or related languages).

The Indus script faces a multi-dimensional deficit: short texts, no bilingual, unknown language, and uncertain script type. Structural data exists but cannot be semantically grounded.

Linear A is partially deciphered at the script level (borrowing Linear B phonetic values) but blocked at the language level — Minoan has no known relatives.

Rongorongo faces extreme isolation: small corpus, no bilingual, and poor documentation of the underlying language.

The information deficit can be quantified: for each script, the gap $\Delta I = I_{\text{needed}} - I_{\text{available}}$ measures how much new evidence would be needed. For the Indus script, $\Delta I \approx 2000$ bits of semantic grounding information.

🟢 Easy

🟢1. How many Indus Valley inscriptions exist?+

Q: Give approximate statistics on the Indus Valley corpus.

~5000 inscriptions found, mostly on stamp seals. Average inscription length is ~5 signs. Total corpus: ~25,000 signs. ~400–600 distinct signs identified.

🟢2. The Farmer-Sproat-Witzel argument+

Q: What did Farmer, Sproat, and Witzel argue about the Indus script in 2004?

They argued that the Indus "script" may not be true writing at all, but proto-writing (owner marks, heraldic emblems) without linguistic phonetic structure.

🟢3. Linear A and transliteration+

Q: Can Linear A be transliterated? Explain the paradox.

Yes, Linear A shares signs with Linear B, whose values are known. You can transliterate to sounds. But the underlying language is unknown, so the sounds make no sense.

🟢4. Rongorongo statistics+

Q: How many rongorongo objects survive?

~24 surviving wooden objects (tablets and staff). ~120 base glyph types. Total glyphs: <500 at most.

🟢5. Reverse boustrophedon+

Q: Describe rongorongo's writing direction.

Reverse boustrophedon: lines alternate left-to-right and right-to-left. Every other line is upside down (requires 180° tablet rotation). Unique to rongorongo.

🟢6. Proto-Elamite date+

Q: When and where was Proto-Elamite used?

Proto-Elamite was used ~3100–2700 BCE in ancient Elam (southwestern Iran). ~1600 tablets found, mostly administrative.

🟢7. Why is Proto-Elamite promising?+

Q: List reasons Proto-Elamite is best for computational decipherment.

(1) Largest corpus of undeciphered glyphs (~160,000). (2) Numeric system known. (3) Administrative content predictable. (4) Related language exists (Elamite proper).

🟢8. Corpus fragmentation problem+

Q: Why does Indus corpus fragmentation make decipherment harder despite having 25,000 signs?

Corpus is 5000 separate seals. Long-range patterns (bigrams, trigrams) cannot be observed across seal boundaries. Each seal provides only local context. Total bits appear sufficient but are structurally insufficient.

🟢9. Linear A corpus size+

Q: How many Linear A documents exist and what is total sign count?

~1400 documents with ~7000 sign occurrences total. ~300 distinct signs. This is marginal corpus size for a 300-sign system.

🟢10. Why rongorongo corpus insufficient+

Q: Why does rongorongo's small corpus make decipherment nearly impossible?

Only ~24 objects = <500 glyphs total. With ~120 glyph types, even with correct hypothesis, rare glyphs might never appear. Underdetermined mapping space.

🟡 Medium

🟡11. Information-theoretic analysis of Indus+

Q: Calculate: For 400-sign logosyllabic system, how many bits needed? How many bits in Indus corpus? What is the limiting factor if quantity appears sufficient?

Needed: log₂(400!) ≈ 2900 bits. Available: 25,000 signs × 2.6 bits/sign ≈ 65,000 bits. Apparent sufficiency masked by fragmentation (no cross-seal bigrams) and unknown language (no prior to validate).

🟡12. Linear A phonetic feasibility+

Q: Linear A has 7000 glyphs from ~1400 docs. A 300-sign syllabary needs ~1700 bits. Enough information for phonetic identification?

Available: 7000 × 2.5 ≈ 17,500 bits (sufficient quantity). But underlying language unknown. Phonetic transliteration ≠ semantic translation. You could have phonetics perfect but language still unidentified. This is Linear A's unique challenge.

🟡13. Conditions for successful decipherment+

Q: List four necessary conditions for decipherment. Check each against Indus, Linear A, and Proto-Elamite.

Conditions: (1) Sufficient corpus. (2) Known/identifiable language. (3) Connected text. (4) Observable structure. Indus: fails (2)(3)(4). Linear A: fails (2), partial (3)(4). Proto-Elamite: partial (2)(3)(4), excellent (1).

🟡14. Why Rosetta Stone enabled Egyptian+

Q: Rosetta Stone is a bilingual. Explain why this solved the "known language" requirement for Egyptian.

Bilingual provides matching Greek+Egyptian texts. Greek is known, so we know the semantic content. Each Egyptian sign/group is anchored to Greek words—solving language identification without needing prior knowledge.

🟡15. Semantic vs syntactic structure+

Q: Can you have syntactic structure without semantic understanding? Give an example from linguistics.

Yes. You can recognize patterns (Subject-Verb-Object ordering, prefix/suffix morphology) without knowing meaning. Example: unintelligible foreign language—you hear structure but don't understand. This is Linear A: phonetically transliterable but semantically opaque.

🟡16. Proto-Elamite numerical anchors+

Q: Why does Proto-Elamite's known numerical system help decipherment of non-numerical signs?

Numbers provide fixed reference points. If you know a sign represents "5" or "10", adjacent signs in inventory contexts can be hypothesized as quantity+commodity pairs. This structural anchor constrains the search space for other signs.

🟡17. Rongorongo cultural loss+

Q: Why was knowledge of rongorongo lost? What information deficit did this create?

Rapa Nui population devastated by slave raids and disease. No fluent readers survived. This created an information barrier (no native speaker) that no corpus analysis can overcome—like having written code but no native speaker to validate meaning.

🟡18. Is Indus a writing system?+

Q: What does Farmer-Sproat-Witzel mean by "proto-writing" vs "writing"? How does this affect decipherment prospects?

Proto-writing: marks with administrative/heraldic function but no linguistic structure (owner marks, seals, emblems). Writing: encodes language phonetically/morphologically. If Indus is proto-writing, decipherment is impossible—there's nothing to decode. This remains controversial.

🔴 Hard

🔴21. Comparative information-theoretic analysis+

Q: Compare Indus, Linear A, Proto-Elamite corpus sufficiency. For each, calculate: (1) bits needed for sign mapping. (2) bits in corpus. (3) structural sufficiency (fragmentation, context). (4) Conclusion on feasibility.

Indus: (1) ~2900 bits. (2) ~65,000 bits. (3) Fails—fragmented, no cross-seal context. (4) Infeasible. Linear A: (1) ~1700 bits. (2) ~17,500 bits. (3) Partial—documentwise connected, but unknown language blocks semantic validation. (4) Partial—phonetics possible, translation blocked. Proto-Elamite: (1) ~5000 bits. (2) ~400,000+ bits. (3) Good—connected tablets, predictable inventory content. (4) Feasible with computational assistance.

🔴22. Cross-modal decipherment problem+

Q: Linear A is readable but untranslatable. Design a thought experiment: suppose archaeologists discover Modern Minoan (a living descendant of Linear A's language). What would this enable? What would it NOT enable?

Would enable: Semantic validation of Linear A texts, word-by-word translation, morphological analysis. Would NOT enable: Retroactive phonetic assignment (Modern Minoan phonology might differ from ancient). But it would provide semantic anchors to validate computational decipherment hypotheses. This is why language recovery is more valuable than phonetic recovery alone.

🔴23. Criterion for writing vs proto-writing+

Q: Propose a linguistic criterion to distinguish "true writing" (encodes language) from "proto-writing" (marks without language encoding). Apply to Indus and cuneiform tokens.

Criterion: True writing shows productivity (new sign combinations produce new meanings), grammatical marking (morphology), and redundancy/predictability patterns consistent with linguistic structure. Proto-writing shows fixed meaning (owner marks stay owner marks) and no productive grammar. Indus: no strong evidence of productivity or morphology (pro-proto-writing). Cuneiform tokens: evolved into true writing by adding logograms and phonetic signs (transition point clear). This criterion is testable via corpus statistics.

🔴24. Computational rescue for undeciphered scripts+

Q: Describe a complete computational pipeline for Proto-Elamite that would exploit its advantages (corpus size, administrative context, related language) over Indus or Linear A. What are the limits of this pipeline?

Pipeline: (1) Corpus enrichment—segment tablets via ML. (2) Numeric anchor—use known numerals as fixed reference points. (3) Language model—train on reconstructed Elamite+related languages. (4) Cognate search—identify inventory items (sheep, barley, etc.) via semantic predictability. (5) Constraint satisfaction—solve for signs that maximize: linguistic plausibility × numeric consistency × inventory coherence. (6) Iterative refinement—use EM to co-optimize. Limits: (a) If underlying language is too different from descendants, cognate matching fails. (b) If numerical system is wrong, entire pipeline derails. (c) Rare signs still underdetermined. (d) No guarantee of phonetic accuracy—only semantic coherence.

🔴25. Why algorithms cannot create missing information+

Q: Prove or refute: "A sufficiently clever algorithm can decipher Rongorongo (500 glyphs, 120 signs, no known language) by discovering hidden patterns."

Refute. Rongorongo problem is information-theoretic, not computational. With <500 glyphs and ~120 sign types, many signs will never appear. Rare signs' values are fundamentally underdetermined—no algorithm can constrain them without external information (cognate language, bilingual, recovered speaker knowledge). Pattern discovery can only exploit structure present in the data. No hidden pattern can generate information that isn't there. Algorithm can at best narrow hypotheses; it cannot eliminate indeterminacy.

🔴26. Indus script Bayesian formulation+

Q: Formulate a Bayesian model for Indus decipherment. Identify: prior P(mapping), likelihood P(corpus|mapping), and explain what makes this problem intractable.

Prior P(M): phonetic plausibility over 400 signs. Likelihood P(C|M): product of probabilities of observed sign sequences under a language model. Intractable because: (1) Unknown language makes language model impossible. (2) Fragmented corpus (5000 separate seals) provides almost no cross-seal bigrams for likelihood estimation. (3) Possible non-linguistic nature of corpus means no valid linguistic likelihood exists. Bayesian framework is sound but requires information (language) that we don't have.

🔴27. Cognate discovery in Proto-Elamite+

Q: Proto-Elamite (~3100 BCE) and Elamite proper (~2000 BCE) are ~1000 years apart. Propose a method to identify cognates between them despite sound changes and morphological evolution. What are failure modes?

Method: (1) Alignment algorithms on Elamite proper word list. (2) Sound change inference—learn systematic phonological shifts (p→f, t→s, etc.). (3) Score Proto-Elamite sign sequences for cognate likelihood. (4) Identify high-confidence matches. Failure modes: (a) Sound changes too extreme to detect. (b) Morphology completely restructured. (c) Semantic drift (words changed meaning). (d) Borrowing and replacements obscure cognacy. Mitigation: Use multiple related languages; identify most robust cognate patterns; accept partial matches.

🔴28. Information deficit vs computational power+

Q: Argue: Can Moore's Law (exponential computational growth) ever close the gap for undeciphered scripts? Or is the barrier fundamentally information-theoretic?

Fundamentally information-theoretic. Moore's Law enables faster search over the same solution space. But if the solution space has a symmetry (multiple equally plausible solutions with same likelihood), computation cannot break it. Example: Indus with unknown language—there are ~(400!)^(number of seals) possible solutions. Computational power cannot reduce this; only external information (language identification, bilingual text) can. Answer: No amount of computation closes an information deficit. The bottleneck is the data and external constraints, not the CPU.

🔴29. Decipherment success criteria+

Q: Define success for decipherment at three levels: (1) Structural (identify sign inventory + rules), (2) Phonetic (assign sounds), (3) Semantic (understand meaning). For each undeciphered script, which levels are achievable?

(1) Indus: Structure possibly not linguistic (FSW debate). (2) Linear A: Structural yes, phonetic yes (via Linear B cognates), semantic no (unknown language). (3) Rongorongo: Structure uncertain (too few examples), phonetic impossible (corpus too small), semantic impossible (language lost). (4) Proto-Elamite: Structure yes, phonetic partial, semantic possible with cognate matching. Full success requires all three levels; scripts fail at different stages.

🔴30. Future decipherments+

Q: For each undeciphered script, identify what single new discovery (artifact, bilingual text, related language) would most accelerate decipherment. Explain.

Indus: Bilingual in known language (Vedic Sanskrit cognate, Dravidian connection) would anchor meanings—equivalent to finding Indo-European Rosetta Stone. Linear A: Modern Minoan descendant language (if it exists) enables semantic validation, or clear syllabic patterns via larger corpus. Rongorongo: Recovered native speaker or written description (though unlikely). Proto-Elamite: Expanded related-language corpus (Akkadian/Old Persian trade records) enables cognate linking. Each discovery targets the specific information barrier: language ID for Indus/Linear A, corpus for Rongorongo, linguistic relatives for Proto-Elamite.
