Undeciphered Scripts: The Frontiers of Decipherment

Not every ancient writing system has yielded its secrets. Several scripts remain partially or wholly undeciphered — tantalizing puzzles that continue to challenge linguists, archaeologists, and computational scientists. These undeciphered scripts are not failures of modern scholarship; they represent genuinely harder problems, where the conditions that enabled past decipherments (bilinguals, known languages, sufficient text) are absent. Studying what makes these scripts resistant to decipherment reveals, in negative, the information-theoretic prerequisites for successful decipherment.

In this chapter, we examine three major undeciphered (or partially deciphered) scripts: the Indus Valley script, Linear A, and Rongorongo. For each, we analyze what information is available, what is missing, and what would be needed to crack them. We also formalize the concept of an information deficit — the gap between available evidence and the information required for decipherment — and show how to calculate it.

Information deficit

The information deficit of an undeciphered script is the difference between the information required for full decipherment and the information available from existing evidence. Formally: $\Delta I = I_{\text{required}} - I_{\text{available}}$. When $\Delta I > 0$, decipherment is impossible from existing data alone — new evidence (bilinguals, longer texts, related languages) is needed. When $\Delta I \leq 0$, the data is sufficient in principle, and the barrier is computational or conceptual rather than evidential.

The Indus Valley Script

The Indus Valley civilization (c. 2600–1900 BCE) — also called the Harappan civilization — was one of the world's great early urban societies, flourishing in what is now Pakistan and northwestern India. It left behind approximately 4,000 inscribed objects bearing roughly 400–450 distinct signs. The inscriptions appear on seals, tablets, pottery, and other objects.

The Indus script is the most debated undeciphered script in the world. Its decipherment is blocked by several compounding problems:

1. Extremely short texts. The average Indus inscription is only 4.6 signs long. The longest known inscription contains only about 26 signs. There are no long texts — no narratives, no administrative records, no literary compositions. The total corpus amounts to roughly 18,000 sign occurrences — less text than a single newspaper article.

2. Unknown language. We do not know what language the Indus people spoke. Candidates include an early Dravidian language, an Indo-Aryan language, a language isolate, or something entirely unknown. Without a candidate language, there is no "Ventris moment" — no hypothesis to test.

3. No bilingual. There is no Indus "Rosetta Stone" — no inscription in both Indus script and a known script/language.

Is the Indus script even writing?

A controversial 2004 paper by Steve Farmer, Richard Sproat, and Michael Witzel argued that the Indus script may not be writing at all — that it might be a system of non-linguistic symbols (like heraldic emblems or property marks) rather than an encoding of language. Their key argument: the inscriptions are too short to encode linguistic content meaningfully. This claim triggered fierce debate. Defenders of the "writing" interpretation point to: (a) consistent sign ordering rules (positional syntax), (b) conditional entropy patterns matching known writing systems, and (c) the presence of apparent grammatical markers. The debate remains unresolved and highlights a fundamental question: how much data is needed to distinguish writing from non-writing?

Information Deficit Calculation for the Indus Script
1
Information required: To decipher a script with $n = 417$ signs, we need to assign a reading to each sign. If each reading is selected from a vocabulary of $v$ possible values, the total information required is $I_{\text{req}} = n \times \log_2 v$. For a logosyllabic system with $v \approx 500$ possible readings: $I_{\text{req}} = 417 \times \log_2 500 \approx 3{,}740$ bits.
2
Information available from corpus: The corpus contains $T \approx 18{,}000$ sign tokens in $D \approx 4{,}000$ inscriptions. The mutual information between context and sign identity provides decipherment evidence. For texts of average length $\bar{L} = 4.6$, the contextual information per sign is severely limited: $I_{\text{context}} \approx T \times r$, where $r$ is the bits of useful constraint per token. With such short texts, $r \approx 0.1$ bits/token, giving $I_{\text{available}} \approx 1{,}800$ bits.
3
Information deficit: $\Delta I = 3{,}740 - 1{,}800 = 1{,}940$ bits. The corpus provides only about 48% of the information needed for decipherment. The remaining 52% must come from external sources: bilinguals, language identification, or dramatically more text.
4
What would close the gap? A bilingual inscription of ~200 signs would provide $\approx 200 \times \log_2 500 \approx 1{,}800$ bits — just enough. Alternatively, finding ~4,000 more inscription objects (doubling the corpus) with longer texts would provide the necessary contextual constraints. Neither seems likely given the archaeological record.
Information Deficit Calculator (Python)
import math

def information_deficit(n_signs, vocab_size, corpus_tokens, avg_text_length, 
                        has_bilingual=False, bilingual_length=0):
    """
    Calculate the information deficit for deciphering a script.
    
    Returns deficit in bits (positive = insufficient data, 
    negative = data theoretically sufficient).
    """
    # Information required
    I_required = n_signs * math.log2(vocab_size)
    
    # Information from corpus (contextual constraints)
    # Short texts provide less constraint per token
    if avg_text_length < 5:
        r = 0.1  # very short texts: minimal context
    elif avg_text_length < 20:
        r = 0.3  # medium texts: moderate context
    else:
        r = 0.5  # long texts: rich context
    
    I_corpus = corpus_tokens * r
    
    # Information from bilingual (if available)
    I_bilingual = bilingual_length * math.log2(vocab_size) if has_bilingual else 0
    
    I_available = I_corpus + I_bilingual
    deficit = I_required - I_available
    
    print(f"Information required: {I_required:.0f} bits")
    print(f"Information from corpus: {I_corpus:.0f} bits")
    print(f"Information from bilingual: {I_bilingual:.0f} bits")
    print(f"Total available: {I_available:.0f} bits")
    print(f"DEFICIT: {deficit:.0f} bits ({deficit/I_required*100:.0f}% shortfall)")
    
    return deficit

# Indus Valley: large deficit

information_deficit(87, 85, 30000, 8.0)

Linear A: So Close, Yet So Far

Linear A is the writing system of the Minoan civilization of Crete, used from roughly 1800–1450 BCE. It is the direct ancestor of Linear B — many Linear A signs are visually similar to their Linear B counterparts — but Linear A writes an unknown language (Minoan), while Linear B writes Greek. This is the cruelest irony in decipherment: we can read Linear A signs (by applying Linear B sound values to similar-looking signs), but we cannot understand what the texts say, because the underlying Minoan language is unknown.

The situation is like receiving a phone call in perfect audio quality but in a language you have never heard: the signal is clear, but the message is opaque.

Reading vs. understanding: a critical distinction

Decipherment has two levels: (1) reading — assigning sound values to signs (knowing that 𐀒 = ko), and (2) understanding — knowing what the resulting words mean (knowing that ko-no-so = Knossos). For Linear B, both levels are achieved because the language is Greek. For Linear A, level 1 is partially achieved (by analogy with Linear B), but level 2 is blocked because no one knows what language the Minoans spoke. A few Minoan words can be guessed from context (numbers, commodity names visible from ideograms), but the grammar and vocabulary remain unknown.

How much Linear A text exists?

The Linear A corpus is small but not tiny: approximately 1,500 inscriptions containing about 7,400 sign tokens. Most are short administrative records (inventory lists, offering tallies), similar in format to Linear B tablets. A few longer texts exist, including some ritual or dedicatory inscriptions. The corpus is sufficient for structural analysis but insufficient for language identification without external evidence. Efforts to identify the language have included comparisons with Semitic, Anatolian, and various other language families, but none has produced a convincing match.

Rongorongo: Writing at the Edge of the World

Rongorongo is a system of glyphs carved on wooden tablets from Easter Island (Rapa Nui). Only about 26 objects bearing Rongorongo survive, containing roughly 14,000 sign tokens from an inventory of approximately 120 basic signs (with many allographic variants bringing the total to ~600). The script is written in reverse boustrophedon — alternating lines are read in opposite directions, with the signs flipped upside down on alternate lines.

Rongorongo is deeply mysterious. It may be one of the very few cases of independent invention of writing — developed without influence from any other writing system. If so, it would be one of only four or five independent origins of writing in human history. However, some scholars argue it was inspired by the sight of European writing (specifically, the Spanish Treaty of Annexation signed by Rapa Nui chiefs in 1770), which would make it a case of stimulus diffusion — the concept of writing being transmitted without the specific system.

Why Rongorongo may be forever undecipherable

The prospects for Rongorongo decipherment are bleak: (1) only 26 objects survive — most were destroyed in the 19th century as "pagan artifacts" by Christian missionaries; (2) all literate Rongorongo readers were killed or enslaved in the Peruvian slave raids of 1862–63, destroying the living tradition; (3) the language of the texts is unknown — it may be an early form of Rapanui, or an older Polynesian language, or something unrelated; (4) there is no bilingual text. The combination of tiny corpus, destroyed reading tradition, and unknown language creates an information deficit that may be insurmountable.

What Would Crack These Scripts?

For each undeciphered script, we can identify the most impactful type of new evidence that would close the information deficit:

Indus Valley: what would help most

Best case: A bilingual inscription (Indus + Mesopotamian cuneiform). Indus seals have been found in Mesopotamia, suggesting trade contacts — a bilingual is not impossible. Even a short bilingual (~50 signs) with identifiable proper names would provide enough anchor points to bootstrap phonetic readings. Second best: Identifying the language family. If we knew the Indus language was Dravidian (the most popular hypothesis), the vocabulary and grammar of Proto-Dravidian could serve as a prior, potentially reducing the search space by 50–80%. Most realistic: Finding longer texts. The current corpus is dominated by seal inscriptions (labels), but other text types (letters, records) may exist in undiscovered archives.

Linear A: what would help most

The key obstacle is the language, not the script. Since Linear A signs can be approximately read using Linear B values, the bottleneck is understanding the resulting Minoan words. The most impactful discovery would be a clear language identification — finding a modern or historically attested language that matches Minoan vocabulary and grammar patterns visible in the texts. Alternatively, a bilingual (Minoan + Egyptian or Minoan + Semitic) would be transformative. Some scholars have proposed Semitic connections based on a few plausible word matches, but the evidence remains inconclusive.

Rongorongo: what would help most

More tablets. Rongorongo's longest texts are actually reasonably long (500+ signs), but the tiny number of objects (26) means little cross-validation is possible. If even 10 more tablets were found — perhaps preserved in private collections or archaeological contexts — the additional data might be sufficient, especially if the language turns out to be Old Rapanui (which is documented). The most promising computational approach is testing whether Rongorongo encodes known Rapanui chants or genealogies that are preserved in the oral tradition.

Computational Approaches to Undeciphered Scripts

Modern computational methods offer new hope for undeciphered scripts. Several approaches have been applied or proposed:

Zipf's Law Test for Writing Systems (Python)
import math
from collections import Counter

def zipf_test(sign_frequencies, script_name):
    """
    Test whether a sign frequency distribution follows Zipf's law,
    which is characteristic of natural language writing systems.
    
    Non-linguistic symbol systems (heraldry, road signs) typically
    do NOT follow Zipf's law.
    """
    # Sort frequencies in descending order
    sorted_freqs = sorted(sign_frequencies.values(), reverse=True)
    n = len(sorted_freqs)
    total = sum(sorted_freqs)
    
    # Calculate Zipf exponent via linear regression on log-log plot
    log_ranks = [math.log(i+1) for i in range(n)]
    log_freqs = [math.log(f/total) for f in sorted_freqs]
    
    # Simple linear regression
    n_pts = len(log_ranks)
    sum_x = sum(log_ranks)
    sum_y = sum(log_freqs)
    sum_xy = sum(x*y for x, y in zip(log_ranks, log_freqs))
    sum_xx = sum(x*x for x in log_ranks)
    
    slope = (n_pts * sum_xy - sum_x * sum_y) / (n_pts * sum_xx - sum_x**2)
    
    print(f"{script_name}:")
    print(f"  Signs: {n}, Tokens: {total}")
    print(f"  Zipf exponent: {-slope:.2f}")
    
    if -1.5 < slope < -0.8:
        print(f"  → Consistent with natural language (writing system)")
    elif slope > -0.5:
        print(f"  → Too flat — may not be linguistic")
    else:
        print(f"  → Too steep — unusual distribution")
    
    return -slope

# exponent ≈ 1.0 — consistent with writing, contra Farmer et al.
Conditional Entropy as a Writing System Diagnostic
1
The conditional entropy $H(X_n | X_{n-1})$ — the uncertainty of a sign given the previous sign — is a powerful diagnostic. Writing systems have $H(X_n | X_{n-1})$ significantly lower than $H(X_n)$ (the unconditional entropy), because linguistic text has sequential structure.
2
For known writing systems: $H(X_n | X_{n-1}) / H(X_n) \approx 0.7\text{–}0.85$. This ratio — the conditional entropy ratio — reflects the degree of sequential predictability. English text: ~0.75. Chinese: ~0.82. Non-linguistic symbol sequences (DNA, random marks): ~0.95–1.0.
3
For the Indus script, careful analysis yields $H(X_n | X_{n-1}) / H(X_n) \approx 0.78$ — squarely in the writing system range. This is strong evidence that the Indus script encodes language, despite the Farmer-Sproat-Witzel challenge.
4
However, this test cannot distinguish between full writing (encoding complete utterances) and proto-writing (encoding concepts or names in a structured but non-linguistic way). Some proto-writing systems may also exhibit sequential structure due to cultural conventions about symbol ordering.
AI and the future of undeciphered scripts

Recent advances in machine learning — particularly large language models and unsupervised translation — have renewed hope for undeciphered scripts. In 2019, Jiaming Luo et al. at MIT demonstrated that their algorithm could automatically decipher Ugaritic and Linear B from raw corpus data, without bilingual texts. The key idea: if you can learn the structure of an unknown language's writing system and match it to a known related language's structure, you can transfer knowledge across the gap. For the Indus script, this would require identifying the correct related language — still the fundamental unknown. But if the language is indeed Dravidian, modern computational methods might close the information deficit that has stymied human decipherers for a century.

Boustrophedon

Boustrophedon (Greek: "ox-turning") is a writing direction where alternate lines run in opposite directions — left-to-right, then right-to-left, then left-to-right again, like an ox plowing a field. Rongorongo uses a variant called reverse boustrophedon, where the signs on alternate lines are also rotated 180°, so the reader must turn the tablet upside down for every other line. Early Greek inscriptions also used boustrophedon before standardizing on left-to-right. The system is ergonomically efficient — the eye never needs to jump back to the start of a line — but adds complexity to the encoding.

Key Takeaways: Undeciphered Scripts

The information deficit formalizes why some scripts resist decipherment: $\Delta I = I_{\text{required}} - I_{\text{available}}$. When the deficit is positive, no amount of cleverness can compensate — new evidence is needed.

The Indus Valley script has the largest deficit (~52%): too many signs (~417), too short texts (avg 4.6 signs), no bilingual, unknown language. The debate over whether it is even writing highlights the limits of statistical diagnostics.

Linear A can be partly read (via Linear B sign values) but not understood — the Minoan language remains unknown. This illustrates that reading ≠ understanding: script decipherment and language identification are separate problems.

Rongorongo may represent independent invention of writing — one of only ~5 such cases in history. Its tiny corpus (26 objects) and destroyed reading tradition create a possibly insurmountable barrier.

Computational diagnostics (Zipf's law, conditional entropy) confirm that the Indus script and Linear A exhibit statistical properties consistent with linguistic writing — but cannot resolve the decipherment alone.

AI-assisted decipherment offers new hope, particularly for scripts where a related language can be identified: unsupervised structure-matching algorithms have already succeeded on Ugaritic and Linear B.

🟢 Easy

🟢1. How many Indus Valley inscriptions exist?+

Q: Give approximate statistics on the Indus Valley corpus.

~5000 inscriptions found, mostly on stamp seals. Average inscription length is ~5 signs. Total corpus: ~25,000 signs. ~400–600 distinct signs identified.

🟢2. The Farmer-Sproat-Witzel argument+

Q: What did Farmer, Sproat, and Witzel argue about the Indus script in 2004?

They argued that the Indus "script" may not be true writing at all, but proto-writing (owner marks, heraldic emblems) without linguistic phonetic structure.

🟢3. Linear A and transliteration+

Q: Can Linear A be transliterated? Explain the paradox.

Yes, Linear A shares signs with Linear B, whose values are known. You can transliterate to sounds. But the underlying language is unknown, so the sounds make no sense.

🟢4. Rongorongo statistics+

Q: How many rongorongo objects survive?

~24 surviving wooden objects (tablets and staff). ~120 base glyph types. Total glyphs: <500 at most.

🟢5. Reverse boustrophedon+

Q: Describe rongorongo's writing direction.

Reverse boustrophedon: lines alternate left-to-right and right-to-left. Every other line is upside down (requires 180° tablet rotation). Unique to rongorongo.

🟢6. Proto-Elamite date+

Q: When and where was Proto-Elamite used?

Proto-Elamite was used ~3100–2700 BCE in ancient Elam (southwestern Iran). ~1600 tablets found, mostly administrative.

🟢7. Why is Proto-Elamite promising?+

Q: List reasons Proto-Elamite is best for computational decipherment.

(1) Largest corpus of undeciphered glyphs (~160,000). (2) Numeric system known. (3) Administrative content predictable. (4) Related language exists (Elamite proper).

🟢8. Corpus fragmentation problem+

Q: Why does Indus corpus fragmentation make decipherment harder despite having 25,000 signs?

Corpus is 5000 separate seals. Long-range patterns (bigrams, trigrams) cannot be observed across seal boundaries. Each seal provides only local context. Total bits appear sufficient but are structurally insufficient.

🟢9. Linear A corpus size+

Q: How many Linear A documents exist and what is total sign count?

~1400 documents with ~7000 sign occurrences total. ~300 distinct signs. This is marginal corpus size for a 300-sign system.

🟢10. Why rongorongo corpus insufficient+

Q: Why does rongorongo's small corpus make decipherment nearly impossible?

Only ~24 objects = <500 glyphs total. With ~120 glyph types, even with correct hypothesis, rare glyphs might never appear. Underdetermined mapping space.

🟡 Medium

🟡11. Information-theoretic analysis of Indus+

Q: Calculate: For 400-sign logosyllabic system, how many bits needed? How many bits in Indus corpus? What is the limiting factor if quantity appears sufficient?

Needed: log₂(400!) ≈ 2900 bits. Available: 25,000 signs × 2.6 bits/sign ≈ 65,000 bits. Apparent sufficiency masked by fragmentation (no cross-seal bigrams) and unknown language (no prior to validate).

🟡12. Linear A phonetic feasibility+

Q: Linear A has 7000 glyphs from ~1400 docs. A 300-sign syllabary needs ~1700 bits. Enough information for phonetic identification?

Available: 7000 × 2.5 ≈ 17,500 bits (sufficient quantity). But underlying language unknown. Phonetic transliteration ≠ semantic translation. You could have phonetics perfect but language still unidentified. This is Linear A's unique challenge.

🟡13. Conditions for successful decipherment+

Q: List four necessary conditions for decipherment. Check each against Indus, Linear A, and Proto-Elamite.

Conditions: (1) Sufficient corpus. (2) Known/identifiable language. (3) Connected text. (4) Observable structure. Indus: fails (2)(3)(4). Linear A: fails (2), partial (3)(4). Proto-Elamite: partial (2)(3)(4), excellent (1).

🟡14. Why Rosetta Stone enabled Egyptian+

Q: Rosetta Stone is a bilingual. Explain why this solved the "known language" requirement for Egyptian.

Bilingual provides matching Greek+Egyptian texts. Greek is known, so we know the semantic content. Each Egyptian sign/group is anchored to Greek words—solving language identification without needing prior knowledge.

🟡15. Semantic vs syntactic structure+

Q: Can you have syntactic structure without semantic understanding? Give an example from linguistics.

Yes. You can recognize patterns (Subject-Verb-Object ordering, prefix/suffix morphology) without knowing meaning. Example: unintelligible foreign language—you hear structure but don't understand. This is Linear A: phonetically transliterable but semantically opaque.

🟡16. Proto-Elamite numerical anchors+

Q: Why does Proto-Elamite's known numerical system help decipherment of non-numerical signs?

Numbers provide fixed reference points. If you know a sign represents "5" or "10", adjacent signs in inventory contexts can be hypothesized as quantity+commodity pairs. This structural anchor constrains the search space for other signs.

🟡17. Rongorongo cultural loss+

Q: Why was knowledge of rongorongo lost? What information deficit did this create?

Rapa Nui population devastated by slave raids and disease. No fluent readers survived. This created an information barrier (no native speaker) that no corpus analysis can overcome—like having written code but no native speaker to validate meaning.

🟡18. Is Indus a writing system?+

Q: What does Farmer-Sproat-Witzel mean by "proto-writing" vs "writing"? How does this affect decipherment prospects?

Proto-writing: marks with administrative/heraldic function but no linguistic structure (owner marks, seals, emblems). Writing: encodes language phonetically/morphologically. If Indus is proto-writing, decipherment is impossible—there's nothing to decode. This remains controversial.

🔴21. Comparative information-theoretic analysis+

Q: Compare Indus, Linear A, Proto-Elamite corpus sufficiency. For each, calculate: (1) bits needed for sign mapping. (2) bits in corpus. (3) structural sufficiency (fragmentation, context). (4) Conclusion on feasibility.

Indus: (1) ~2900 bits. (2) ~65,000 bits. (3) Fails—fragmented, no cross-seal context. (4) Infeasible. Linear A: (1) ~1700 bits. (2) ~17,500 bits. 🔴22. Cross-modal decipherment problem+

Q: Linear A is readable but untranslatable. Design a thought experiment: suppose archaeologists discover Modern Minoan (a living descendant of Linear A's language). What would this enable? What would it NOT enable?

Would enable: Semantic validation of Linear A texts, word-by-word translation, morphological analysis. Would NOT enable: Retroactive phonetic assignment (Modern Minoan phonology might differ from ancient). But it would provide semantic anchors to validate computational decipherment hypotheses. This is why language recovery is more valuable than phonetic recovery alone.

🔴23. Criterion for writing vs proto-writing+

Q: Propose a linguistic criterion to distinguish "true writing" (encodes language) from "proto-writing" (marks without language encoding). Apply to Indus and cuneiform tokens.

Criterion: True writing shows productivity (new sign combinations produce new meanings), grammatical marking (morphology), and redundancy/predictability patterns consistent with linguistic structure. Proto-writing shows fixed meaning (owner marks stay owner marks) and no productive grammar. Indus: no strong evidence of productivity or morphology (pro-proto-writing). Cuneiform tokens: evolved into true writing by adding logograms and phonetic signs (transition point clear). This criterion is testable via corpus statistics.

🔴24. Computational rescue for undeciphered scripts+

Q: Describe a complete computational pipeline for Proto-Elamite that would exploit its advantages (corpus size, administrative context, related language) over Indus or Linear A. What are the limits of this pipeline?

Pipeline: (1) Corpus enrichment—segment tablets via ML. (2) Numeric anchor—use known numerals as fixed reference points. (3) Language model—train on reconstructed Elamite+related languages. (4) Cognate search—identify inventory items (sheep, barley, etc.) via semantic predictability. (5) Constraint satisfaction—solve for signs that maximize: linguistic plausibility × numeric consistency × inventory coherence. (6) Iterative refinement—use EM to co-optimize. Limits: (a) If underlying language is too different from descendants, cognate matching fails. (b) If numerical system is wrong, entire pipeline derails. (c) Rare signs still underdetermined. (d) No guarantee of phonetic accuracy—only semantic coherence.

🔴25. Why algorithms cannot create missing information+

Q: Prove or refute: "A sufficiently clever algorithm can decipher Rongorongo (500 glyphs, 120 signs, no known language) by discovering hidden patterns."

Refute. Rongorongo problem is information-theoretic, not computational. With <500 glyphs and ~120 sign types, many signs will never appear. Rare signs' values are fundamentally underdetermined—no algorithm can constrain them without external information (cognate language, bilingual, recovered speaker knowledge). Pattern discovery can only exploit structure present in the data. No hidden pattern can generate information that isn't there. Algorithm can at best narrow hypotheses; it cannot eliminate indeterminacy.

🔴26. Indus script Bayesian formulation+

Q: Formulate a Bayesian model for Indus decipherment. Identify: prior P(mapping), likelihood P(corpus|mapping), and explain what makes this problem intractable.

Prior P(M): phonetic plausibility over 400 signs. Likelihood P(C|M): product of probabilities of observed sign sequences under a language model. Intractable because: (1) Unknown language makes language model impossible. (2) Fragmented corpus (5000 separate seals) provides almost no cross-seal bigrams for likelihood estimation. (3) Possible non-linguistic nature of corpus means no valid linguistic likelihood exists. Bayesian framework is sound but requires information (language) that we don't have.

🔴27. Cognate discovery in Proto-Elamite+

Q: Proto-Elamite (~3100 BCE) and Elamite proper (~2000 BCE) are ~1000 years apart. Propose a method to identify cognates between them despite sound changes and morphological evolution. What are failure modes?

Method: (1) Alignment algorithms on Elamite proper word list. (2) Sound change inference—learn systematic phonological shifts (p→f, t→s, etc.). (3) Score Proto-Elamite sign sequences for cognate likelihood. (4) Identify high-confidence matches. Failure modes: (a) Sound changes too extreme to detect. (b) Morphology completely restructured. (c) Semantic drift (words changed meaning). (d) Borrowing and replacements obscure cognacy. Mitigation: Use multiple related languages; identify most robust cognate patterns; accept partial matches.

🔴28. Information deficit vs computational power+

Q: Argue: Can Moore's Law (exponential computational growth) ever close the gap for undeciphered scripts? Or is the barrier fundamentally information-theoretic?

Fundamentally information-theoretic. Moore's Law enables faster search over the same solution space. But if the solution space has a symmetry (multiple equally plausible solutions with same likelihood), computation cannot break it. Example: Indus with unknown language—there are ~(400!)^(number of seals) possible solutions. Computational power cannot reduce this; only external information (language identification, bilingual text) can. Answer: No amount of computation closes an information deficit. The bottleneck is the data and external constraints, not the CPU.

🔴29. Decipherment success criteria+

Q: Define success for decipherment at three levels: (1) Structural (identify sign inventory + rules), (2) Phonetic (assign sounds), (3) Semantic (understand meaning). For each undeciphered script, which levels are achievable?

(1) Indus: Structure possibly not linguistic (FSW debate). (2) Linear A: Structural yes, phonetic yes (via Linear B cognates), semantic no (unknown language). (3) Rongorongo: Structure uncertain (too few examples), phonetic impossible (corpus too small), semantic impossible (language lost). (4) Proto-Elamite: Structure yes, phonetic partial, semantic possible with cognate matching. Full success requires all three levels; scripts fail at different stages.

🔴30. Future decipherments+

Q: For each undeciphered script, identify what single new discovery (artifact, bilingual text, related language) would most accelerate decipherment. Explain.

Indus: Bilingual in known language (Vedic Sanskrit cognate, Dravidian connection) would anchor meanings—equivalent to finding Indo-European Rosetta Stone. Each discovery targets the specific information barrier: language ID for Indus/Linear A, corpus for Rongorongo, linguistic relatives for Proto-Elamite.
