Linear B & the Decipherment of Mycenaean Greek

The decipherment of Linear B is one of the greatest intellectual achievements of the twentieth century. Unlike the Rosetta Stone or Behistun inscription, there was no bilingual text — no convenient bridge between known and unknown. Instead, the script was cracked through pure structural analysis: the systematic study of sign frequencies, distributions, and combinatorial patterns. The heroes of this story are Alice Kober (1906–1950), whose meticulous analytical work laid the foundation, and Michael Ventris (1922–1956), who built on Kober's insights to achieve the final breakthrough in 1952.

Linear B was used by the Mycenaean civilization of Bronze Age Greece, roughly 1400–1200 BCE. The tablets, found primarily at Knossos (Crete) and Pylos (mainland Greece), record palace inventories, offerings, personnel lists, and economic transactions — the bureaucratic records of a centralized palace economy. They were preserved by a historical accident: the palaces burned, and the fires hardened clay tablets that were never meant to be permanent records.

For our information-theoretic thread, Linear B is the ideal case study. Its decipherment required no external knowledge of the underlying language — only the statistical structure of the texts themselves. The decipherers exploited the same patterns that Shannon identified in his mathematical theory of communication: redundancy, frequency distributions, positional constraints, and combinatorial regularity.

Linear B: a syllabic script for Mycenaean Greek

Linear B uses approximately 87 syllabic signs (each representing a consonant-vowel pair like ka, ro, ti) plus about 100 logograms (ideograms representing commodities like wheat, oil, bronze, man, woman). The syllabic signs spell out words phonetically; the logograms serve as shorthand in accounting contexts. This mixed system — phonetic core plus logographic supplements — is a common pattern in ancient writing, reflecting an information-theoretic tradeoff between encoding efficiency (logograms) and generality (syllabic spelling).

Alice Kober: The Grid Method

Alice Kober was a classicist at Brooklyn College who devoted her career to the systematic analysis of Linear B. Working without computers, funding, or institutional support, she hand-catalogued every sign occurrence on every known tablet — eventually filling over 180,000 index cards stored in cigarette cartons. Her approach was groundbreaking: she treated the script as a formal system and sought structural patterns without assuming anything about the underlying language.

Kober's greatest discovery was that Linear B words showed systematic inflection patterns. She identified groups of words that shared the same beginning (stem) but differed in their endings — exactly what you would expect in an inflected language where nouns change form for case, number, or gender. She called these "triplets" because the same word appeared in three different forms.

The critical insight was this: if two signs appear as alternative endings for the same grammatical form across different words, those signs must share the same vowel. Why? In a syllabary, each sign represents a consonant-vowel pair (CV). Grammatical endings typically change the consonant but keep the vowel constant (e.g., nominative -os, -as keep the vowel of the stem but add different endings). This meant Kober could group signs by their vowel without knowing what any sign sounded like.

The Kober Grid

Kober organized her findings into a grid where rows represent shared consonants and columns represent shared vowels. She couldn't fill in the actual phonetic values — she deliberately refused to guess — but the structure of the grid was established purely from distributional analysis:

Reconstructing Kober's Grid from Triplet Analysis (Python)
from collections import defaultdict

def find_triplets(tablets):
    """
    Identify inflection triplets: groups of words sharing
    a stem but differing in their final 1-2 signs.
    """
    # Build index: stem (first N-1 signs) -> list of endings
    stem_to_endings = defaultdict(list)
    for tablet in tablets:
        for word in tablet.words:
            if len(word.signs) >= 3:
                stem = tuple(word.signs[:-1])
                ending = word.signs[-1]
                stem_to_endings[stem].append(ending)
    
    # Find stems with exactly 3 distinct endings (triplets)
    triplets = []
    for stem, endings in stem_to_endings.items():
        unique_endings = set(endings)
        if len(unique_endings) == 3:
            triplets.append((stem, sorted(unique_endings)))
    
    return triplets

def build_kober_grid(triplets):
    """
    From triplets, build a consonant-vowel grid.
    Signs that appear in the same 'slot' across different triplets
    share the same vowel.
    """
    # vowel_groups[slot_position] = set of signs sharing that vowel
    vowel_groups = defaultdict(set)
    
    for stem, endings in triplets:
        for slot_idx, ending_sign in enumerate(endings):
            vowel_groups[slot_idx].add(ending_sign)
    
    # consonant_groups: signs appearing as alternatives in
    # the same stem position share a consonant
    consonant_groups = defaultdict(set)
    for stem, endings in triplets:
        last_stem_sign = stem[-1]
        # The stem sign and endings in form 3 may share a consonant
        # (the stem changes its last sign in form 3)
        consonant_groups[last_stem_sign].update(endings)
    
    print("=== Kober Grid (abstract) ===")
    print(f"Vowel columns identified: {len(vowel_groups)}")
    for col, signs in vowel_groups.items():
        print(f"  Vowel {col}: signs {signs}")
    print(f"Consonant rows identified: {len(consonant_groups)}")
    
    return vowel_groups, consonant_groups

# Kober identified 5 vowel columns and ~15 consonant rows
# without assigning ANY phonetic values — pure structure!
Why Kober refused to guess phonetic values

Many of Kober's contemporaries (including Ventris himself, early on) tried to guess what language Linear B concealed — Etruscan, Basque, a pre-Greek language — and then forced phonetic values to match. They all failed spectacularly. Kober understood that premature language identification was the cardinal error. By staying purely structural — cataloguing patterns without interpretation — she built a framework that was correct regardless of what the language turned out to be. Her method was essentially Bayesian: she refused to commit to a prior (language hypothesis) and instead accumulated evidence (structural patterns) that any correct hypothesis would have to explain. This made her grid robust — when Ventris finally identified the language as Greek, Kober's structural grid mapped perfectly onto the Greek vowel-consonant system.

Sign Count Diagnostic: Syllabary Detection
1
The number of distinct signs in a writing system is a powerful diagnostic of its type. Alphabets: 20–40 signs. Syllabaries: 40–100 signs. Logographic systems: 200+ signs. Linear B has ~87 syllabic signs — squarely in the syllabary range.
2
For a CV syllabary, the sign count equals $C \times V$ where $C$ is the number of consonants and $V$ the number of vowels. Linear B's 87 syllabic signs suggest approximately $C \approx 17, V \approx 5$ (since $17 \times 5 = 85 \approx 87$).
3
This is consistent with Greek phonology: 5 vowels (a, e, i, o, u) and ~17 consonant series. The sign count alone — without reading a single sign — correctly predicted the vowel-consonant structure of the underlying language.
4
Entropy per sign: $H = \log_2 87 \approx 6.4$ bits. For comparison: an English letter carries $\log_2 26 \approx 4.7$ bits, but a Linear B syllable encodes approximately two phonemes, so the information rate per phoneme is $6.4/2 \approx 3.2$ bits — comparable to alphabetic efficiency.

Ventris: The Final Breakthrough

Michael Ventris was a remarkable figure — an architect by profession, with no academic position in linguistics or classics, who worked on Linear B as an obsessive hobby from the age of 14. Building on Kober's grid, he made the decisive leap in June 1952: he tested the hypothesis that Linear B wrote an early form of Greek.

This was a bold guess that most scholars considered absurd. The tablets predated Homer by 700 years, and the conventional wisdom held that Greeks did not arrive in the Aegean until much later. But Ventris had a specific method for testing it: he used the place names.

Ventris's key reasoning:

  1. Tablets from Knossos (Crete) should contain Cretan place names.
  2. Some of these places survived into classical Greek: Knossos (Κνωσός), Amnisos (Ἀμνισός), Tylissos (Τύλισσος), Phaistos (Φαιστός).
  3. If Linear B writes Greek, these names should be recognizable when the correct phonetic values are assigned.
  4. Kober's grid constrains which signs share vowels/consonants — this dramatically reduces the search space for valid assignments.

The Place Name Test

Ventris's test was elegant. He identified sign sequences on the Knossos tablets that appeared in contexts suggesting place names (e.g., following words meaning "from" or appearing in lists of locations). He then tried to read them as known Cretan place names using his tentative phonetic assignments:

Worked Example: Reading "Knossos" in Linear B

Step 1: A frequently occurring sign sequence on Knossos tablets: 𐀒-𐀜-𐀰. It appears in administrative contexts as a location name.

Step 2: Ventris's tentative grid assigns: 𐀒 = ko, 𐀜 = no, 𐀰 = so.

Step 3: Reading: ko-no-so = Knossos (Κνωσός). The match is exact.

Step 4: Cross-check: Another sequence 𐀀-𐀖-𐀛-𐀰 at the same site reads a-mi-ni-so = Amnisos, the port of Knossos. Another: 𐀶-𐀪-𐀰 = tu-ri-so = Tylissos, a nearby town.

Step 5: Three independent place names, all reading correctly as Greek — the probability of this being coincidence is astronomically small. Each name confirms ~6 sign values. Three names give ~18 confirmed signs, nearly 20% of the syllabary.

Ventris's Place Name Verification (Python)
import math

def test_phonetic_assignment(grid, sign_sequences, known_places):
    """
    Test a phonetic grid assignment against known place names.
    
    grid: dict mapping sign -> (consonant, vowel) pair
    sign_sequences: list of sign sequences found in place-name contexts
    known_places: list of known ancient place names in the region
    """
    matches = []
    
    for signs in sign_sequences:
        # Convert signs to syllables using the grid
        syllables = [grid.get(s, '??') for s in signs]
        reading = '-'.join(f"{c}{v}" for c, v in syllables)
        
        # Check against known places
        for place in known_places:
            if matches_place_name(reading, place):
                matches.append((signs, reading, place))
                print(f"  MATCH: {reading} → {place}")
    
    # Statistical significance
    # Probability of one random match: ~1/500 (for 87-sign syllabary)
    # Probability of k independent matches: (1/500)^k
    if matches:
        p_chance = (1/500) ** len(matches)
        bits_evidence = -math.log2(p_chance)
        print(f"\n{len(matches)} matches found")
        print(f"P(chance) = {p_chance:.2e}")
        print(f"Evidence: {bits_evidence:.1f} bits")
    
    return matches

def matches_place_name(syllabic_reading, greek_name):
    """Check if a syllabic rendering matches a Greek place name,
    accounting for Linear B's spelling conventions:
    - Final consonants dropped (Greek -os -> Linear B -o)
    - Consonant clusters split with extra vowels
    - No distinction between l/r, p/b, t/d in some series
    """
    # Simplified matching logic
    normalized = syllabic_reading.replace('-', '')
    return normalized.lower().startswith(greek_name[:4].lower())

The spelling rules that almost derailed decipherment

Linear B's syllabary has a significant limitation: every sign is a consonant+vowel pair (CV), but Greek has many consonant clusters (kn-, mn-, str-) and word-final consonants (-s, -n). Linear B handles this by inserting silent vowels: "Knossos" (Greek Κνωσός) becomes ko-no-so (not *kno-sos). Final consonants are simply dropped: Greek wanaks ("king") becomes wa-na-ka. These spelling conventions made the script seem more different from Greek than it actually was — leading many scholars to reject the Greek hypothesis before Ventris proved it. Understanding these systematic sound-to-sign mapping rules is essential for reading Linear B texts.

The Information Content of Linear B

The spelling conventions of Linear B have interesting information-theoretic consequences. By dropping final consonants and inserting silent vowels, Linear B loses information compared to a faithful transcription of Greek. How much?

Information Loss in Linear B Spelling
1
Greek has approximately $H_{\text{Greek}} \approx 4.5$ bits per phoneme. With an average word length of 6 phonemes, a typical Greek word carries $\approx 27$ bits of information.
2
Linear B drops final consonants (~10% of phonemes) and neutralizes certain distinctions (l/r, voiced/voiceless). This removes approximately $0.8$ bits per phoneme on average. For a 6-phoneme word: $\Delta I \approx 6 \times 0.8 = 4.8$ bits lost.
3
Retained information: $27 - 4.8 = 22.2$ bits per word, a fidelity of $\approx 82\%$. The "missing" 18% must be reconstructed by the reader using context (grammatical expectations, semantic plausibility, etc.).
4
This 82% fidelity is sufficient for administrative records (highly formulaic, limited vocabulary) but would be problematic for literature or legal texts. This may explain why Linear B was used only for palace inventories — the system's information loss was tolerable only for predictable, stereotyped content.
Why Linear B was never used for literature

The thousands of Linear B tablets recovered are exclusively administrative: inventories of grain, oil, livestock, personnel, and offerings. Not a single literary or religious text has been found. This is partly explained by the information-theoretic limitation above: with 18% information loss, reading Linear B requires strong contextual expectations. Palace records — "10 jars of oil from location X" — are predictable enough to read despite the lossy encoding. Poetry or narrative, with much lower predictability, would be extremely difficult to read in Linear B spelling. The Greeks eventually solved this problem by adopting the Phoenician alphabet, which could encode Greek with near-zero information loss.

Kober, Ventris, and the Ethics of Credit

Alice Kober died in 1950, just two years before Ventris's breakthrough. She was 43 years old, likely of stomach cancer, and had been working under conditions of severe material deprivation — teaching a full course load at Brooklyn College while pursuing research without funding or leave. Her 180,000 index cards represented perhaps 10,000 hours of labor.

For decades, the decipherment story was told as "Ventris cracked Linear B" — a solo genius narrative. Only in recent years, through the work of historian Margalit Fox and others, has Kober's foundational role been properly recognized. Her grid method provided the essential structural framework without which Ventris's phonetic assignments would have been impossible. The decipherment was not the work of one mind but a relay: Kober's rigorous structural analysis passed the baton to Ventris's brilliant phonetic intuition.

The scientific method in action

The Kober-Ventris collaboration (though they never formally collaborated) illustrates a fundamental pattern in scientific discovery: structural analysis before interpretation. Kober established what the data must look like (the grid structure) without committing to what it means (the phonetic values). Ventris then tested specific hypotheses against this structural framework. This two-phase approach — structure first, semantics second — is exactly how modern computational approaches work: first learn the statistical structure of a corpus, then map that structure to meaning.

Confirmation from Pylos tablet Ta 641

The most dramatic confirmation of Ventris's decipherment came from a newly excavated tablet from Pylos, which neither Ventris nor anyone else had seen. Carl Blegen, who was excavating Pylos, wrote to Ventris in 1952 that a new tablet (Ta 641) listed vessels with descriptions. When Ventris applied his grid, the descriptions read perfectly as Greek words for types of vessels: ti-ri-po = "tripod" (Greek tripos), ti-ri-po-de = "tripods" (dual form tripode). The tablet even included ideograms showing vessels with 3 legs, 4 handles, etc. — matching the deciphered text exactly. This was the "blind test" that proved the decipherment beyond doubt.

Simulating the Decipherment Search Space (Python)
import math
from itertools import permutations

def decipherment_search_space(n_signs=87, n_consonants=17, n_vowels=5):
    """
    Calculate the search space for assigning phonetic values
    to Linear B signs, with and without Kober's grid constraints.
    """
    # Without any constraints: assign 87 signs to 85 CV pairs
    # This is essentially 85! / (85-87)! ... but simplified:
    unconstrained = math.factorial(87)  # permutations of sign values
    
    # With Kober's grid: signs are pre-grouped into
    # 5 vowel columns and ~17 consonant rows
    # Now we only need to:
    #   1. Assign 5 vowels to 5 columns: 5! = 120
    #   2. Assign 17 consonants to 17 rows: 17! = 355,687,428,096,000
    constrained = math.factorial(5) * math.factorial(17)
    
    # With place-name hints (3 names = ~15 known sign values):
    # Reduces effective unknowns by ~15 signs
    # Remaining: assign ~2 vowels and ~10 consonants
    with_names = math.factorial(2) * math.factorial(10)
    
    print(f"Unconstrained search space: ~10^{math.log10(unconstrained):.0f}")

    print(f"Reduction from grid + names: {unconstrained/with_names:.1e}×")

    H_unconstrained = math.log2(unconstrained)

CV Syllabary

A CV (consonant-vowel) syllabary is a writing system where each sign represents exactly one consonant followed by one vowel: ka, no, ti, etc. Linear B is the prototypical example, with ~87 signs covering ~17 consonant series × 5 vowels. CV syllabaries are highly regular — their grid structure can be inferred purely from distributional analysis, as Kober demonstrated. They are common in languages with simple syllable structure (Japanese kana is another example) but awkward for languages with complex consonant clusters (like Greek), requiring spelling conventions to handle clusters and final consonants.

Why didn't computers help decipher Linear B?

When Ventris made his breakthrough in 1952, electronic computers existed but were barely accessible. The first stored-program computer (Manchester Baby) had run only 4 years earlier. Could a computer have solved Linear B faster? In principle, yes — the grid+place-name search space of $\sim 10^7$ is trivially searchable by brute force. But the real challenge was not searching the space — it was defining the search space correctly. Kober's insight about vowel-consonant grouping and Ventris's hypothesis that the language was Greek were the creative leaps that no 1950s computer could have made. Even today, AI can search efficiently but struggles to formulate the right hypothesis space — the hardest part of decipherment.

The Pylos Archive: A Bronze Age Bureaucracy

The single most important source of Linear B texts is the archive found at the Palace of Nestor at Pylos (southwestern Greece), excavated by Carl Blegen beginning in 1939. On his very first day of digging, Blegen discovered the "Archive Room" — a small chamber containing over 600 clay tablets, all inscribed in Linear B. The tablets had been accidentally preserved when the palace burned around 1200 BCE: the fire baked the clay tablets (which were originally meant to be temporary records, reused each year), making them nearly indestructible.

The Pylos tablets turned out to be primarily administrative records: inventories of goods, lists of personnel, records of land holdings, livestock counts, offerings to gods, and allocations of raw materials to craftspeople. They reveal a centralized palace economy of remarkable sophistication: the Mycenaean palace tracked individual workers by name, recorded the exact quantities of grain, oil, wine, and wool moving through its storerooms, and maintained detailed records of military equipment — chariots, wheels, armor, and weapons.

For decipherment, the Pylos archive was invaluable because its administrative nature imposed strong constraints on possible readings. An inventory tablet lists items and quantities: the number signs are recognizable (based on the decimal system), the commodity signs are pictographic (grain, oil, wine are represented by standardized ideograms), and the text portions must be nouns (item names) and personal names. This predictable structure provided cribs — partially known content that constrained the phonetic values of the syllabic signs.

Alice Kober and the "Triplets": Foundation for Ventris

The unsung heroine of Linear B decipherment is Alice Kober (1906–1950), an American classicist who died just two years before Ventris announced his solution. Kober's methodological contributions were indispensable, yet she received little recognition during her lifetime and for decades after.

Kober's crucial discovery was what she called "triplets" — groups of three words that shared the same root but had different endings. She identified these by pure pattern analysis, without knowing what language the script encoded. In modern terms, she discovered inflectional morphology through distributional analysis alone — a remarkable achievement equivalent to discovering that a language has case endings purely by examining the statistical patterns of sign sequences.

From the triplets, Kober deduced that the signs at the beginning of related words (the root) must share phonetic features, while the differing signs at the end (the inflection) must represent grammatical endings. She constructed a "grid" showing which signs shared consonants (appearing in the same column) and which shared vowels (appearing in the same row) — even though she didn't know what the actual consonants and vowels were. This abstract grid of relationships was the scaffolding that Ventris later filled in with specific phonetic values.

Kober's grid: structure without substance

Kober's grid is a beautiful example of structural decipherment — identifying relationships between signs without knowing their values. She showed that if two signs appear in the same position within different triplets, they must share either a consonant or a vowel. By cross-referencing enough triplets, she built a matrix of "same consonant" and "same vowel" constraints. In information-theoretic terms, each constraint eliminates possibilities from the solution space: if signs A and B share a consonant, and signs A and C share a vowel, then the possible values of A are constrained by both B's consonant and C's vowel — dramatically narrowing the options. Kober's grid didn't solve the puzzle, but it reduced the search space from astronomical to manageable — the essential precondition for Ventris's breakthrough.

The Mycenaean economy revealed

Once Linear B was deciphered, the tablets revealed a Bronze Age palace economy of surprising sophistication. The palace of Pylos directly controlled the labor of thousands of workers — men, women, and children — assigning them to specific tasks: textile production, metalworking, farming, herding, and religious service. The tablets record individual workers by name, track their rations (bread, figs, wine), and monitor their output. The level of bureaucratic detail rivals that of much later civilizations: the palace knew exactly how much flax each spinner was expected to process and how much bronze each smith received. This revelation transformed our understanding of Mycenaean Greece: far from the heroic warrior society depicted in Homer, the reality was a highly organized, centrally planned economy — more akin to a planned economy than the free-trading mercantile society that classical Greece would later become.

Ventris's Grid Method: A Systematic Attack

Michael Ventris's approach to Linear B was distinguished by its systematic rigor. Rather than guessing at individual sign values (as many amateur decipherers attempted), Ventris built a comprehensive framework — the famous syllabic grid — that encoded all the structural relationships between signs before assigning any specific phonetic values. This approach, which he developed in his "Work Notes" distributed to colleagues between 1951–1952, is a masterclass in constrained optimization: reduce the degrees of freedom first, then solve the remaining system.

The grid was organized with consonants on one axis and vowels on the other. Ventris didn't know which consonant or vowel each row/column represented — he only knew that signs in the same row shared a consonant, and signs in the same column shared a vowel. This knowledge came from Kober's triplet analysis and from his own systematic comparison of sign alternations in different grammatical contexts.

The key constraints Ventris used to fill in the grid included: (1) Alternation patterns: words that appeared in both a "base" form and an inflected form, where only the final syllable changed, revealed which signs shared consonants. (2) Frequency analysis: the most common signs were likely to represent the most common CV syllables in the target language. (3) Word-initial and word-final statistics: in most languages, certain syllables are much more common at the beginning or end of words than in the middle. (4) The Kober triplets: three-form paradigms that revealed consistent ending patterns, directly identifying "same consonant, different vowel" relationships.

Constraint Propagation in the Syllabic Grid
1
With ~87 syllabic signs and a CV structure (~15 consonants × ~5 vowels = 75 slots), the unconstrained mapping space is $\frac{87!}{(87-75)!} \approx 10^{140}$. This is astronomically large — brute force is impossible even with modern computers.
2
Each "same consonant" constraint (placing two signs in the same row) eliminates a factor of ~15 from the search space ($\log_2 15 \approx 3.9$ bits of constraint). Ventris identified approximately 50 such constraints from Kober's triplets and his own analysis, providing $\approx 195$ bits of constraint.
3
Each "same vowel" constraint (placing two signs in the same column) provides another $\log_2 5 \approx 2.3$ bits. With approximately 30 such constraints, this adds $\approx 69$ bits. Total structural constraints: ~264 bits.
4
The mapping requires $\approx 75 \times \log_2 87 \approx 480$ bits to specify completely. The 264 bits of structural constraint reduce the effective search space from $10^{140}$ to roughly $2^{480-264} = 2^{216} \approx 10^{65}$ — still enormous, but the grid structure means these constraints propagate: once a few anchor values are established, the rest cascade. When Ventris identified the place names "ko-no-so" (Knossos) and "a-mi-ni-so" (Amnisos), the shared signs propagated through the grid, fixing dozens of values simultaneously.
Syllabic Grid Constraint Solver (Python)
import random

class SyllabicGrid:
    """
    Simplified model of Ventris's syllabic grid.
    Demonstrates how constraints propagate to reduce
    the search space.
    """
    def __init__(self, consonants, vowels, signs):
        self.C = consonants  # e.g., ['k','t','p','n','m','r','s','d','j','w']
        self.V = vowels      # e.g., ['a','e','i','o','u']
        self.signs = signs   # sign IDs
        self.grid = {}       # (c_idx, v_idx) -> sign
        self.constraints = []
    
    def add_same_consonant(self, sign_a, sign_b):
        """Signs A and B share a consonant (same row)."""
        self.constraints.append(('same_c', sign_a, sign_b))
    
    def add_same_vowel(self, sign_a, sign_b):
        """Signs A and B share a vowel (same column)."""
        self.constraints.append(('same_v', sign_a, sign_b))
    
    def solve_with_anchors(self, anchors):
        """
        Given anchor values {sign: (consonant, vowel)},
        propagate through constraints to fill grid.
        """
        known = dict(anchors)
        changed = True
        iterations = 0
        
        while changed:
            changed = False
            iterations += 1
            for ctype, sa, sb in self.constraints:
                if sa in known and sb not in known:
                    c_a, v_a = known[sa]
                    if ctype == 'same_c':
                        # sb has same consonant, unknown vowel
                        known[sb] = (c_a, '?')
                        changed = True
                    elif ctype == 'same_v':
                        known[sb] = ('?', v_a)
                        changed = True
                elif sb in known and sa not in known:
                    c_b, v_b = known[sb]
                    if ctype == 'same_c':
                        known[sa] = (c_b, '?')
                        changed = True
                    elif ctype == 'same_v':
                        known[sa] = ('?', v_b)
                        changed = True
                # If both known, refine partial values
                elif sa in known and sb in known:
                    c_a, v_a = known[sa]
                    c_b, v_b = known[sb]
                    if ctype == 'same_c' and c_a != '?' and c_b == '?':
                        known[sb] = (c_a, v_b)
                        changed = True
                    if ctype == 'same_v' and v_a != '?' and v_b == '?':
                        known[sb] = (c_b, v_a)
                        changed = True
        
        print(f"Propagation: {iterations} iterations")
        print(f"Known signs: {len(known)} / {len(self.signs)}")
        fully_known = sum(1 for c, v in known.values() 
                          if c != '?' and v != '?')
        print(f"Fully resolved: {fully_known}")
        return known

# Demo: small grid

    ['k','t','n','r','s'], ['a','e','i','o','u'],

The verification moment: "ko-no-so" = Knossos

On June 1, 1952, Ventris tested his tentative grid by applying it to actual tablet texts. When he substituted his phonetic values into a frequently occurring sign group from Knossos tablets, the result read ko-no-so — obviously "Knossos," the very site where the tablets were found. A second common group read a-mi-ni-so — Amnisos, the harbor town of Knossos mentioned in Homer's Odyssey. A third read tu-li-so — Tylissos, another Cretan town known from Greek sources. Three independent place names, all correctly spelled, all from the same grid. The probability of this occurring by chance is vanishingly small: with ~75 possible syllable values per sign position, three correct multi-sign readings gives a probability of roughly $(1/75)^{10} \approx 10^{-19}$. Ventris knew immediately that the script wrote Greek — and the grid was correct.

What Linear B Revealed: Mycenaean Greek

The decipherment of Linear B revealed that the language of Mycenaean Greece was an early form of Greek — pushing the documented history of the Greek language back by roughly 700 years (from ~800 BCE to ~1450 BCE). The tablets documented a dialect that was recognizably ancestral to Classical Greek but with archaic features: different vowel qualities, preserved labiovelals (sounds like *kʷ that later split into p, t, or k in different environments), and grammatical forms that had disappeared by the time of Homer.

Some of the most illuminating discoveries included: Religious vocabulary — the tablets mention gods already familiar from Classical Greek religion (Zeus, Hera, Poseidon, Hermes, Athena, Artemis, Dionysus) but in archaic name forms, proving these deities were worshipped over a millennium before the Classical period. Social structure — a complex hierarchy of kings (wanax), military leaders (lawagetas), and local governors (basileus — which later became the standard Greek word for "king," suggesting a democratic demotion of the title). Economic data — detailed records of agricultural production, textile manufacturing, metal distribution, and military equipment that reveal a palace-centered redistributive economy far more bureaucratic than the heroic warrior society of Homeric legend.

Linear B's limitations as a script for Greek

Linear B was not designed for Greek — it was adapted from Linear A (which wrote an unknown, non-Greek language) with minimal modification. As a result, it was a poor fit for Greek phonology. The script cannot distinguish voiced, voiceless, and aspirated consonants (pa, ba, and pha are all written the same way); it cannot write consonant clusters (the word stathmos "stable" becomes ta-to-mo); and word-final consonants are omitted (making many words ambiguous). These limitations meant that the same spelling could represent multiple different Greek words — a source of ongoing scholarly debate about specific readings. The script's unsuitability for Greek is itself evidence that it was borrowed from a non-Greek tradition: a script designed from scratch for Greek would never have these deficiencies.

Key Takeaways: Linear B Decipherment

Kober's grid method exploited inflection patterns to group signs by shared vowels and consonants — without knowing any phonetic values. This reduced the search space from $10^{132}$ to $10^{17}$ — a breathtaking 115 orders of magnitude.

The sign-count diagnostic (87 signs → CV syllabary, $C \approx 17, V \approx 5$) correctly predicted the script's structure before a single sign was read.

Ventris's place-name test used known Cretan locations (Knossos, Amnisos, Tylissos) to anchor phonetic values, reducing the space to $\sim 10^7$ — humanly searchable.

Linear B's spelling conventions (dropping final consonants, inserting silent vowels) cause ~18% information loss — tolerable for formulaic records but insufficient for literature, explaining the script's exclusively administrative use.

The Kober-Ventris relay illustrates the two-phase pattern: structural analysis (Kober) before phonetic interpretation (Ventris) — a methodology that anticipates modern computational approaches.

🟡11. Kober's structural insight+

Q: Kober identified triplets without knowing phonetic values. How did she extract linguistic structure?

By analyzing patterns: if three sequences share common portions, they likely represent inflected forms. Different portions would indicate grammatical affixes. Example: if sequ-ence1 = ABC, sequence2 = ABD, sequence3 = ABE, the common part AB is likely a root; C, D, E are case/number markers.

🟡12. Grid constraint satisfaction+

Q: Ventris's grid has syllables as rows and phonetic values as columns. How does filling in known values constrain remaining entries?

Once one syllable-phoneme pairing is established (e.g., sign X = "pa"), whenever sign X appears, it contributes phonetic "pa" to that word. If pattern matching suggests related words (triplets), their phonetic patterns must be compatible. Constraints propagate: one assignment narrows possibilities for adjacent signs and related patterns.

🟡13. Greek as target language+

Greek grammar is known (from Classical Greek and historical linguistics). Ventris could validate assignments against Greek phonotactics and morphology. 🟡14. Chadwick's validation+

Q: John Chadwick, a Classicist, worked with Ventris on validation. What linguistic knowledge did Chadwick provide?

Chadwick knew Classical Greek, historical linguistics, and Greek place names/royal names. He recognized patterns in Ventris's decoded text that matched known Greek terms. 🟡15. Information content of triplets+

Q: From an information-theoretic view, how much information do triplets provide without knowing phonetics?

Triplets reveal grammar structure (e.g., 3 case forms = nominative/accusative/genitive). Each triplet position constrains meaning without phonetics. With N triplets showing distinct patterns, you can deduce: (1) Morpheme boundaries. (2) Inflectional categories (gender, case, number). (3) Relative sign frequencies. Typically: one triplet ≈ 30 bits of structural information (specifies inflection, case, position). Multiple triplets ≈ 100+ bits, enabling grammar reconstruction.

🟡16. Semantic validation advantage+

Semantic validation provides external ground truth—decoded text is correct if it meaningfully describes physical context. This is much stronger than linguistic plausibility alone. 🟡17. Logogram + syllabic hybrid+

Linear B encodes Greek (known language); Linear A encodes unknown language. Knowing target language (Greek) enabled Ventris to validate assignments. Linear A lacks this luxury—underlying language is unknown, making validation almost impossible regardless of structural analysis. Known language was decisive advantage.

🟡19. Greek as validation anchor+

Q: Why was Greek language knowledge crucial for Linear B decipherment?

Ventris could validate assignments against Greek phonotactics, morphology, known place names. Linear A (unknown language) lacked this validation—no way to check assignments. Known target language = essential external constraint.

Predictable endings constrain syllabic assignments. Triplet analysis predicts expected case forms; morphological structure narrows phonetic space. Expected patterns validate hypothesis.

🔴 Hard

🔴21. Structural decipherment via triplets+

Q: Formalize Kober's triplet method as a pattern discovery algorithm. Given a corpus, describe steps to extract morphological structure without phonetics.

Algorithm: (1) Enumerate all multi-word sequences (trigrams, quadgrams, etc.). (2) Cluster sequences by shared subsequences (substring similarity). (3) For each cluster, identify invariant and variable portions. (4) Label invariant portion as "root," variables as "affixes." (5) Group similar affix patterns to identify inflectional categories (case, gender, number). (6) Build morphological model. Output: Grammar rules without phonetics. Example: If three sequences are ABC, ABD, ABE (B shared), B is likely a root; C, D, E are affixes. This extracts structure purely from surface patterns.

🔴22. Ventris grid formalized+

Q: Formalize Ventris's grid as a constraint satisfaction problem. Variables = syllable-phoneme pairs. Define constraints and objective function.

Variables: sign_i ↔ syllable_j for each Linear B sign. Domain: each sign maps to one of ~90 syllables. Constraints: (1) Bijectivity—one sign, one syllable. (2) Linguistic—decoded sequences produce Greek-like phonotactics. (3) Morphological—affixes match Greek morphology. (4) Semantic—decoded text fits logogram context. (5) Kober patterns—triplet morphological rules satisfied. Objective: Maximize constraint satisfaction. Solution: Hypothesis + constraint propagation + validation. Ventris implicitly solved this CSP by hand—modern approach would use SAT solver or EM.

🔴23. Greek phonotactics as prior+

Q: Design a language model prior P(syllable sequence | Greek grammar) for Linear B decipherment. How would violations of Greek phonotactics penalize hypotheses?

Prior: For each sequence of decoded syllables, compute probability under Greek language model. Greek allows: (1) CV syllables (ba, ki, po). (2) No consonant clusters at word start (except geminates). (3) Specific vowel sequences (ai, ei, oi allowed; others not). (4) Morphological patterns (genitive -o, nominative -s, etc.). Penalty: If hypothesis produces non-Greek phonotactic structure (e.g., "ptzo" cluster unattested in Greek), assign low prior probability. Correctly decoded Greek sequences assign high probability. This prior constrains Ventris's search space dramatically: thousands of hypotheses are rejected immediately as linguistically implausible.

🔴24. Tripod semantic validation+

Q: Design a semantic validation algorithm for Linear B: given decoded text and logogram context (tripod picture, pitcher pictures), verify correctness.

Algorithm: (1) Parse decoded text—extract nouns, adjectives, numbers. (2) Normalize to English (tripod, pitcher, handle, four). (3) Extract context—count objects depicted, their types, descriptions. (4) Match: does decoded text accurately describe the depicted scene? (5) Score: semantic overlap between decoded and depicted. (6) Threshold: if overlap > 90%, decoded text is valid; else, reject hypothesis. Example: Decoded "tripod with four handles" vs. picture showing tripod + 4 handles ✓ validated. This validation proved Tripod Tablet: Ventris's decipherment matched depicted objects perfectly.

🔴25. Why Mycenaean Greek+

Q: Linear B decipherment revealed Linear B encodes Mycenaean Greek (~1450–1100 BCE). Why is this linguistically significant? How does it relate to Classical Greek?

Significance: Linear B is earliest written Greek (500+ years before Homer). Mycenaean Greek shows: (1) Early Greek inflectional system (cases, genders preserved). (2) Archaic vocabulary (different from Classical). (3) Different phonological evolution (loss of laryngeal sounds, vowel shifting). It bridges Indo-European historical linguistics and Classical Greek. Mycenaean reveals language evolution: Mycenaean → Classical → Modern. Linear B decipherment enabled historical language study—understanding Greek's own history.

🔴26. Chadwick-Ventris methodology+

Q: Ventris (engineer, non-linguist) + Chadwick (Classicist) achieved decipherment together. Analyze their complementary skills: Why is this team composition effective?

Ventris: Strong in pattern recognition, logical reasoning, constraint satisfaction (engineering background). Grid method = systematic hypothesis testing. Chadwick: Expert in Greek language, history, place names, cognates. Validates Ventris's phonetic assignments against linguistic knowledge. Complementarity: Ventris generates hypotheses (structural analysis); Chadwick validates (linguistic expertise). Neither alone suffices—Ventris needed linguistic expertise; Chadwick needed systematic method (not just intuition). This illustrates collaboration: specialists + generalists together solve harder problems than either alone.

🔴27. Linear B uniqueness+

Q: Compare Linear B decipherment to Egyptian, Cuneiform, Ugaritic. What made Linear B unique? What was the common method?

Linear B unique: (1) First decipherment via pattern analysis (Kober) rather than bilingual text. (2) Enabled by large corpus of similar tablets (administrative texts all follow patterns). Common: All required linguistic knowledge (Greek for Linear B; Persian for Cuneiform; Coptic for Egyptian). All exploited structural redundancy: logograms, determinatives, phonetic complements, or known word forms. All combined logical deduction + pattern matching + validation. Linear B shows pattern analysis alone (without bilingual) can decipher if corpus is large and language is known.

🔴28. Why syllabary for Linear B+

Q: Why did Mycenaean Greek use syllabic writing (Linear B) instead of alphabet? What changed to make alphabet adopt?

Hypothesis: (1) Linear B adapted from Minoan (Linear A), which was syllabic. (2) Mycenaean Greeks adopted existing technology (cultural transmission). (3) Syllabary sufficed for administrative needs (inventory, personnel lists). (4) Later (Dark Ages collapse), Linear B was abandoned. When Greeks emerged from Dark Ages (~800 BCE), they borrowed Phoenician abjad (consonants only) and adapted it—adding vowel signs to create first true alphabet. Why alphabet over syllabary: (1) Alphabet simpler to learn (~24 signs vs ~90). (2) Better for diverse languages and new words (phonetic productivity). (3) Cultural prestige (Phoenician trade contact). The transition from syllabary → alphabet reflects linguistic innovation, not inherent superiority—both work, but alphabet won through historical contingency.

🔴29. Information flow in decipherment+

Q: Map information flow in Linear B decipherment: Kober triplets → Ventris grid → Chadwick validation → Tripod Tablet. How does each step refine knowledge?

Flow: (1) Kober triplets: Extract morphological structure (30-50 bits structural info). (2) Ventris grid: Constrain phonetic assignments (estimate each sign ≈ log₂(90) ≈ 6.5 bits; corpus of 5000 tablets ≈ 32,500 bits available). (3) Chadwick validation: Filter hypotheses using Greek language model (eliminates non-Greek phonotactics). (4) Tripod Tablet: Semantic validation (single tablet ≈ 200 bits of semantic info confirming correctness). Each step reduces hypothesis space: Kober eliminates ~70% (structure only); Ventris explores ~1000 candidates; Chadwick filters to top ~10; Tripod confirms #1. Cumulative information = decipherment success.

🔴30. Syllabic limitations for written Greek+

Q: Linear B syllabary has ~90 syllables, roughly one per Greek CV and V combination. Design a compression algorithm: how many signs are minimally needed to write Greek phonetically? How does this compare to alphabet?

Analysis: Greek has ~24 consonants × ~6 vowels ≈ 144 CV combinations. But many are rare or unattested. Phonologically productive: ~60-80 common CV syllables + ~7 vowels = ~90 syllables. Minimal syllabary: ~70 signs (realistic). Alphabet: ~24 letters (consonants) + ~7 vowel marks ≈ 31 signs. Comparison: Syllabary 70 vs Alphabet 31 = alphabet ~2x more compact per sign. But alphabet requires phonetic awareness (understanding consonants, vowels, their separate identity). Syllabary is more holistic (each sign = whole syllable, intuitive). Tradeoff: Alphabet is efficient (fewer signs, easier to extend); Syllabary is natural (matches human phonetic perception). Linear B chose the natural path; Greek later chose the efficient path.
