Linear B — Kober & Ventris

The decipherment of Linear B — the script of the Mycenaean Greeks, used between roughly 1450 and 1200 BCE — stands as perhaps the most intellectually elegant of all decipherments. Unlike Egyptian or cuneiform, there was no bilingual text. There was no known related language. The corpus was small (a few thousand tablets), fragmentary, and limited almost entirely to bureaucratic inventories. The script had been known since Arthur Evans excavated the palace of Knossos in 1900, but for half a century it resisted all attempts at reading.

The breakthrough came from an unlikely pair: Alice Kober, a Brooklyn classics professor who developed the structural analytical framework, and Michael Ventris, a young British architect with no formal linguistic training who synthesized Kober's insights into a complete solution. Together — though Kober died before seeing the result — they demonstrated that Linear B wrote an early form of Greek, a conclusion so shocking that many scholars initially refused to believe it.

This chapter is the story of how rigorous structural analysis can crack a script even when the underlying language is completely unknown. It is, in many ways, the purest test of the information-theoretic approach to decipherment.

Linear B: what it is and isn't

Linear B is a syllabic script with about 87 phonetic signs, each representing a consonant-vowel (CV) pair or a pure vowel. It also uses ~100 logograms for common commodities (wheat, oil, bronze, horses). The tablets are clay, sun-dried (preserved accidentally when the palaces burned), and record palace inventories — how many jars of olive oil, how many sheep assigned to which shepherd, how many bronze ingots issued to which smith. There is virtually no literature, religion, or narrative — only bureaucracy. Linear B is related to, but distinct from, Linear A (the still-undeciphered script of Minoan Crete) and completely unrelated to cuneiform or hieroglyphs.

The Structural Clues: What Can Be Deduced Without Reading

Before anyone could read Linear B, scholars could deduce a great deal about its structure just by analyzing the patterns of signs. This is internal analysis — extracting information from the text itself, without any external anchor. The key observations were:

Clue 1: Sign Count Implies Syllabary

The Linear B corpus contains approximately 87 distinct phonetic signs used in running text. This number is immediately diagnostic:

  • An alphabet uses 20–30 signs (each sign = one sound). Linear B has too many signs for an alphabet.
  • A logography uses thousands of signs (each sign = one word/morpheme). Linear B has far too few signs for a logography.
  • A syllabary uses 50–200 signs (each sign = one syllable). The count of ~87 fits perfectly.

This observation, first made clearly by Alice Kober, narrowed the structural hypothesis enormously. If Linear B is syllabic, then each sign represents a syllable — most likely a consonant-vowel (CV) pair. With perhaps 15 consonants and 5 vowels, we'd expect ~75 CV signs plus a few vowel-only signs: $15 \times 5 + 5 = 80$. The actual count of 87 is a remarkably close match.

Sign Count as a Script-Type Diagnostic
1
Let $N$ be the number of distinct signs in a script. We can classify script types by $N$: alphabets have $N \in [20, 35]$, syllabaries have $N \in [40, 200]$, logographies have $N > 500$.
2
For a CV syllabary: $N = C \times V + V_{\text{pure}}$, where $C$ = number of consonants, $V$ = number of vowel distinctions, $V_{\text{pure}}$ = pure vowel signs. This gives an expected range of $(10 \times 4 + 4) = 44$ to $(20 \times 7 + 7) = 147$ signs.
3
Linear B has $N = 87$. The information per sign in a syllabary: $\log_2 87 \approx 6.4$ bits. Compare: alphabet $\log_2 26 \approx 4.7$ bits, logography $\log_2 5000 \approx 12.3$ bits. The syllabary is intermediate in information density — each sign carries more information than a letter but less than a logogram.
4
For $C = 15, V = 5$: predicted $N = 15 \times 5 + 5 = 80$. Observed $N = 87$. The small surplus ($+7$) likely represents signs for rare or complex syllables (e.g., initial clusters like "pte-"). The match is strong evidence for a CV syllabary with ~15 consonant series and ~5 vowel columns.

Clue 2: Systematic Alternation at Word Endings

Kober's most brilliant observation was identifying patterns of systematic alternation in sign groups. She noticed that certain words appeared in multiple forms that differed only in their final one or two signs. For example, a word might appear as:

  • Sign-A Sign-B Sign-C Sign-D
  • Sign-A Sign-B Sign-E Sign-F
  • Sign-A Sign-B Sign-G

where the first two signs remained constant but the endings varied. Kober recognized this as inflection — the grammatical modification of words through suffixes, exactly as occurs in Latin (dominus, dominum, domini) or Greek (anthropos, anthropon, anthropou). The crucial implication: the language behind Linear B has grammatical case inflection.

Kober's "triplets" — the key structural insight

Kober organized her observations into what she called "triplets" — sets of three related word forms that she believed represented the same word in three grammatical cases (e.g., nominative, genitive, dative). She published these in a landmark 1946 paper, showing that the alternation patterns were systematic — the same pattern of ending-changes recurred across many different word roots. This systematicity proved that the endings were grammatical (not semantic) and that the script was phonetic (not logographic). A logographic script cannot show inflectional endings, because logograms encode meaning, not sound. Only a phonetic script can capture the sound-changes that inflection produces.

Clue 3: Signs Share Vowels Across Rows

Kober went further. She noticed that certain pairs of signs seemed to share a vowel while differing in their consonant. In a CV syllabary, the sign for ka and the sign for ta share the vowel a. If inflectional endings consistently replace certain sign pairs (like replacing so with si-jo), and if the first sign of the new pair (si) shares a consonant with the replaced sign (so) but has a different vowel, then we can deduce which signs share consonants and which share vowels — even without knowing what the consonants or vowels are.

This was the foundation of what Ventris would later call the grid method: arranging all 87 signs into a two-dimensional grid where rows share a consonant and columns share a vowel. If the grid is correct, then a single identification (e.g., this sign = ta) immediately identifies an entire row (all signs beginning with t-) and an entire column (all signs ending in -a).

The Grid Method: Structural Constraints Before Phonetic Values
1
A CV syllabary with $C$ consonants and $V$ vowels has $C \times V$ signs (ignoring pure vowels). Assigning phonetic values to all signs requires determining $C + V$ identities (which row = which consonant, which column = which vowel), not $C \times V$ independent identities.
2
Information needed without grid: $\log_2((C \times V)!) \approx 87 \times 6.4 \approx 557$ bits (assigning each sign independently). Information needed with grid: $\log_2(C!) + \log_2(V!) \approx \log_2(15!) + \log_2(5!) \approx 40.2 + 6.9 = 47.1$ bits.
3
Reduction: $557 - 47.1 = 509.9$ bits of information provided by the grid structure alone. The grid provides $\frac{509.9}{557} \approx 91.5\%$ of the information needed for full decipherment!
4
Once the grid is constructed, identifying just one sign (e.g., a sign known to be "ta") determines its entire row ($t$-row: $ta, te, ti, to, tu$) and its entire column ($a$-column: $da, ja, ka, \ldots, wa$). A single identification propagates through the grid, multiplying its information by a factor of $C + V - 1$.

Ventris: Filling in the Grid

Michael Ventris (1922–1956) began working on Linear B as a teenager, when he heard Arthur Evans lecture. During World War II, he served as a navigator-bombardier in the RAF, spending his spare time on the script. After the war, he pursued architecture but continued his Linear B research in his evenings, communicating with other scholars through a remarkable series of "Work Notes" that he circulated by mail.

Ventris built directly on Kober's structural framework. His key innovation was to systematically construct the vowel-consonant grid using statistical analysis of sign distribution. He tracked which signs appeared in which positions (word-initial, word-final, before/after specific other signs) and used these distributions to group signs that likely shared consonants or vowels.

The Place Name Test

In June 1952, Ventris tried a crucial experiment. Tablets from Knossos (Crete) ought to contain Cretan place names. If he could guess which sign-groups represented well-known Cretan cities — Knossos, Amnisos, Tylissos, Phaistos — he could test whether the resulting phonetic values were self-consistent and compatible with the grid.

The experiment worked spectacularly. Assigning the city names yielded phonetic values that were not only internally consistent but also produced recognizable Greek words when applied to other sign-groups. The word for "total" turned out to read to-so — archaic Greek tosson (τόσσον). The word for "boy/child" read ko-wo — Greek kouros/kowos (κοῦρος). The word preceding horse logograms read po-ro — Greek polos (πῶλος, "foal").

Why Greek was such a surprise

Nearly all scholars had assumed the Linear B language was not Greek. Evans himself believed it was "Minoan" — the language of the pre-Greek civilization of Crete. Even Ventris initially worked under this assumption. The idea that Greeks had been literate as early as 1400 BCE — centuries before Homer, whom tradition called the first Greek writer — seemed absurd. But the evidence was overwhelming: once the phonetic values were assigned, Greek words poured out of every tablet. This is a powerful reminder that assumptions about the identity of the language can be the biggest obstacle to decipherment.

The Tripod Tablet: Definitive Confirmation

The most dramatic confirmation came from a newly excavated tablet from Pylos (on the Greek mainland, not Crete). This tablet, which Ventris had never seen, listed vessels alongside logograms showing their shapes — tripod cauldrons, goblets with varying numbers of handles. When Ventris applied his decipherment, the word next to the tripod logogram read ti-ri-po-de — Greek tripode (τρίποδε, "tripod," dual form). The word next to a four-handled vessel read qe-to-ro-we — Greek qetrōwes (four-eared/four-handled).

This was decisive. The probability that a wrong decipherment would produce not just Greek-sounding words, but Greek words that correctly described the objects depicted by the accompanying logograms, is astronomically low.

Statistical Confidence from the Tripod Tablet
1
The tripod tablet contains 5 distinct words paired with logograms showing vessel types. For each word, the decoded Greek meaning matches the depicted object.
2
Under a random decipherment, each word has probability $p$ of accidentally matching its logogram's meaning. With a Greek vocabulary of $\approx 50{,}000$ words, and $\approx 20$ plausible vessel-related words, $p \approx 20/50000 = 0.0004$ per word.
3
Probability of all 5 matching by chance: $p^5 = (0.0004)^5 \approx 10^{-17}$. This is $10^{17}$-to-1 odds against — far exceeding any reasonable significance threshold.
4
In information-theoretic terms: $-\log_2(10^{-17}) \approx 56.5$ bits of evidence for the decipherment. For comparison, a DNA fingerprint match in forensics provides $\approx 43$ bits ($1$ in $10^{13}$). The tripod tablet alone is more conclusive than DNA evidence.

Alice Kober: The Unsung Hero

Alice Kober (1906–1950) deserves far more recognition than she has historically received. Working as a classics professor at Brooklyn College with no research funding, she hand-filed over 180,000 data entries on cigarette cartons (she couldn't afford index cards), systematically cataloging every sign occurrence, every sign sequence, and every contextual pattern in the entire Linear B corpus.

Her contributions were foundational: the triplet analysis that proved inflection, the insight that signs could be organized into a grid, and the demonstration that internal structural analysis could extract information even without knowing the language. She died of cancer in 1950, just two years before Ventris completed the decipherment. There is no doubt that without Kober's framework, Ventris could not have succeeded.

The information-theoretic lesson of Linear B

Linear B's decipherment teaches a profound lesson: structural analysis can extract most of the information needed for decipherment even without any external anchor. Kober and Ventris derived the grid structure — providing ~91.5% of the needed information — purely from internal patterns. The remaining ~8.5% (the actual phonetic identities) came from the inspired guess of Cretan place names. Compare this with Egyptian, where the bilingual Rosetta Stone was essential from the start. Linear B shows that when a script has enough internal structure (inflection, systematic sign distribution), the text itself contains most of the key to its own decipherment.

Key Takeaways: Linear B

Sign count (~87) immediately identified Linear B as a syllabary — too many signs for an alphabet, too few for a logography.

Kober's triplets proved the language had grammatical inflection, and that signs could be organized into a consonant × vowel grid. This structural insight provided ~91.5% of the information needed for decipherment.

Ventris's grid method reduced the problem from assigning 87 independent signs (~557 bits) to identifying ~15 consonant rows and ~5 vowel columns (~47 bits).

The place name test provided the phonetic seed — actual sound values — that brought the abstract grid to life, revealing Greek beneath the script.

The tripod tablet provided $10^{17}$-to-1 statistical confirmation — more conclusive than DNA evidence — by matching decoded Greek words to depicted objects on a tablet Ventris had never seen.

Q: How many syllabic signs and logograms does Linear B have?

~90 syllabic signs (CV and V syllables) + ~100 logograms (commodities, animals, people).

🟢3. CV syllables+

Q: What does CV mean in Linear B syllabic structure?

CV = Consonant-Vowel. Linear B uses syllabic signs where each represents a syllable: CV (ba, ki, po), V (a, e, i), etc.

🟢4. Alice Kober's contribution+

Q: Who was Alice Kober and what was her key discovery?

Kober identified recurring three-word sequences (triplets) in Linear B. She hypothesized these were inflected forms of the same word, revealing structural patterns.

🟢5. Triplet method+

Q: Explain the triplet method: what pattern does it identify?

Triplet method: Three similar sequences appear, likely representing inflected forms (masculine, feminine, neuter; or singular, plural, dual). By analyzing recurring patterns, linguists deduce inflectional rules without knowing phonetic values.

🟢6. Ventris's grid+

Q: Describe Ventris's grid method.

Grid with syllables on one axis, potential phonetic values on the other. Ventris systematically explored assignments, using linguistic constraints (Greek grammar plausibility) to narrow hypotheses.

🟢7. Linear B discovery context+

Q: When was Linear B used (date range)? In which civilization?

Linear B was used ~1450–1100 BCE in Bronze Age Mycenaean Greece.

🟢8. Tripod tablet significance+

Q: Why was the Tripod Tablet crucial for validating the Linear B decipherment?

The decoded text described tripods and pitchers, matching the physical objects depicted. Semantic coherence between deciphered text and depicted objects proved the decipherment was correct.

🟢9. Ventris's hypothesis+

Q: What was Ventris's key hypothesis about Linear B?

Ventris hypothesized that Linear B encoded Greek—an assumption that proved crucial and correct. This enabled validation against known Greek grammar.

🟢10. Syllabic vs alphabetic advantage+

Q: What is one disadvantage of syllabic writing (Linear B) compared to alphabetic writing?

Words must fit syllabic boundaries, making some spellings awkward. Alphabet is more flexible and requires fewer signs per word on average.

🟡 Medium

🟡11. Kober's structural insight+

Q: Kober identified triplets without knowing phonetic values. How did she extract linguistic structure?

By analyzing patterns: if three sequences share common portions, they likely represent inflected forms. Different portions would indicate grammatical affixes. Example: if sequ-ence1 = ABC, sequence2 = ABD, sequence3 = ABE, the common part AB is likely a root; C, D, E are case/number markers.

🟡12. Grid constraint satisfaction+

Q: Ventris's grid has syllables as rows and phonetic values as columns. How does filling in known values constrain remaining entries?

Once one syllable-phoneme pairing is established (e.g., sign X = "pa"), whenever sign X appears, it contributes phonetic "pa" to that word. If pattern matching suggests related words (triplets), their phonetic patterns must be compatible. Constraints propagate: one assignment narrows possibilities for adjacent signs and related patterns.

🟡13. Greek as target language+

Q: Why was Ventris's assumption that Linear B encodes Greek so powerful?

Greek grammar is known (from Classical Greek and historical linguistics). Ventris could validate assignments against Greek phonotactics and morphology. If assignment produces non-Greek-like phonetic sequences or violates Greek morphology, reject it. This linguistic prior dramatically constrained the search space.

🟡14. Chadwick's validation+

Q: John Chadwick, a Classicist, worked with Ventris on validation. What linguistic knowledge did Chadwick provide?

Chadwick knew Classical Greek, historical linguistics, and Greek place names/royal names. He recognized patterns in Ventris's decoded text that matched known Greek terms. Example: place names decoded as Knossos (Cretan site), Thebes, etc. This linguistic validation confirmed Ventris's phonetic assignments.

🟡15. Information content of triplets+

Q: From an information-theoretic view, how much information do triplets provide without knowing phonetics?

Triplets reveal grammar structure (e.g., 3 case forms = nominative/accusative/genitive). Each triplet position constrains meaning without phonetics. With N triplets showing distinct patterns, you can deduce: (1) Morpheme boundaries. (2) Inflectional categories (gender, case, number). (3) Relative sign frequencies. Typically: one triplet ≈ 30 bits of structural information (specifies inflection, case, position). Multiple triplets ≈ 100+ bits, enabling grammar reconstruction.

🟡16. Semantic validation advantage+

Q: The Tripod Tablet provided semantic validation: decoded text matched depicted objects. Why is this validation method so powerful?

Semantic validation provides external ground truth—decoded text is correct if it meaningfully describes physical context. This is much stronger than linguistic plausibility alone. Without semantic validation, multiple phonetic assignments might produce grammatically valid (but wrong) Greek. Semantic validation eliminates such ambiguity.

🟡17. Logogram + syllabic hybrid+

Q: Linear B uses both logograms and syllabic signs. How does this hybrid encoding help decipherment?

Logograms are semantic anchors—pictures of tripods, pitchers, animals. When decoded, context (tripod picture + decoded "tripod" text) confirms correctness. Syllabic signs encode pronunciation. Together: logograms fix semantic content; syllabic signs reveal phonetics. Neither alone; combination enables validation.

🟡18. Why Linear B preceded Linear A+

Q: Linear B (Mycenaean, 1450–1100 BCE) deciphered before Linear A (Minoan, 1800–1450 BCE). Why?

Linear B encodes Greek (known language); Linear A encodes unknown language. Knowing target language (Greek) enabled Ventris to validate assignments. Linear A lacks this luxury—underlying language is unknown, making validation almost impossible regardless of structural analysis. Known language was decisive advantage.

🟡19. Greek as validation anchor+

Q: Why was Greek language knowledge crucial for Linear B decipherment?

Ventris could validate assignments against Greek phonotactics, morphology, known place names. Linear A (unknown language) lacked this validation—no way to check assignments. Known target language = essential external constraint.

🟡20. Greek morphological templates+

Q: Greek has regular inflections (genitive -o, nominative -s). How do templates help?

Predictable endings constrain syllabic assignments. Triplet analysis predicts expected case forms; morphological structure narrows phonetic space. Expected patterns validate hypothesis.

🔴 Hard

🔴21. Structural decipherment via triplets+

Q: Formalize Kober's triplet method as a pattern discovery algorithm. Given a corpus, describe steps to extract morphological structure without phonetics.

Algorithm: (1) Enumerate all multi-word sequences (trigrams, quadgrams, etc.). (2) Cluster sequences by shared subsequences (substring similarity). (3) For each cluster, identify invariant and variable portions. (4) Label invariant portion as "root," variables as "affixes." (5) Group similar affix patterns to identify inflectional categories (case, gender, number). (6) Build morphological model. Output: Grammar rules without phonetics. Example: If three sequences are ABC, ABD, ABE (B shared), B is likely a root; C, D, E are affixes. This extracts structure purely from surface patterns.

🔴22. Ventris grid formalized+

Q: Formalize Ventris's grid as a constraint satisfaction problem. Variables = syllable-phoneme pairs. Define constraints and objective function.

Variables: sign_i ↔ syllable_j for each Linear B sign. Domain: each sign maps to one of ~90 syllables. Constraints: (1) Bijectivity—one sign, one syllable. (2) Linguistic—decoded sequences produce Greek-like phonotactics. (3) Morphological—affixes match Greek morphology. (4) Semantic—decoded text fits logogram context. (5) Kober patterns—triplet morphological rules satisfied. Objective: Maximize constraint satisfaction. Solution: Hypothesis + constraint propagation + validation. Ventris implicitly solved this CSP by hand—modern approach would use SAT solver or EM.

🔴23. Greek phonotactics as prior+

Q: Design a language model prior P(syllable sequence | Greek grammar) for Linear B decipherment. How would violations of Greek phonotactics penalize hypotheses?

Prior: For each sequence of decoded syllables, compute probability under Greek language model. Greek allows: (1) CV syllables (ba, ki, po). (2) No consonant clusters at word start (except geminates). (3) Specific vowel sequences (ai, ei, oi allowed; others not). (4) Morphological patterns (genitive -o, nominative -s, etc.). Penalty: If hypothesis produces non-Greek phonotactic structure (e.g., "ptzo" cluster unattested in Greek), assign low prior probability. Correctly decoded Greek sequences assign high probability. This prior constrains Ventris's search space dramatically: thousands of hypotheses are rejected immediately as linguistically implausible.

🔴24. Tripod semantic validation+

Q: Design a semantic validation algorithm for Linear B: given decoded text and logogram context (tripod picture, pitcher pictures), verify correctness.

Algorithm: (1) Parse decoded text—extract nouns, adjectives, numbers. (2) Normalize to English (tripod, pitcher, handle, four). (3) Extract context—count objects depicted, their types, descriptions. (4) Match: does decoded text accurately describe the depicted scene? (5) Score: semantic overlap between decoded and depicted. (6) Threshold: if overlap > 90%, decoded text is valid; else, reject hypothesis. Example: Decoded "tripod with four handles" vs. picture showing tripod + 4 handles ✓ validated. This validation proved Tripod Tablet: Ventris's decipherment matched depicted objects perfectly.

🔴25. Why Mycenaean Greek+

Q: Linear B decipherment revealed Linear B encodes Mycenaean Greek (~1450–1100 BCE). Why is this linguistically significant? How does it relate to Classical Greek?

Significance: Linear B is earliest written Greek (500+ years before Homer). Mycenaean Greek shows: (1) Early Greek inflectional system (cases, genders preserved). (2) Archaic vocabulary (different from Classical). (3) Different phonological evolution (loss of laryngeal sounds, vowel shifting). It bridges Indo-European historical linguistics and Classical Greek. Mycenaean reveals language evolution: Mycenaean → Classical → Modern. Linear B decipherment enabled historical language study—understanding Greek's own history.

🔴26. Chadwick-Ventris methodology+

Q: Ventris (engineer, non-linguist) + Chadwick (Classicist) achieved decipherment together. Analyze their complementary skills: Why is this team composition effective?

Ventris: Strong in pattern recognition, logical reasoning, constraint satisfaction (engineering background). Grid method = systematic hypothesis testing. Chadwick: Expert in Greek language, history, place names, cognates. Validates Ventris's phonetic assignments against linguistic knowledge. Complementarity: Ventris generates hypotheses (structural analysis); Chadwick validates (linguistic expertise). Neither alone suffices—Ventris needed linguistic expertise; Chadwick needed systematic method (not just intuition). This illustrates collaboration: specialists + generalists together solve harder problems than either alone.

🔴27. Linear B uniqueness+

Q: Compare Linear B decipherment to Egyptian, Cuneiform, Ugaritic. What made Linear B unique? What was the common method?

Linear B unique: (1) First decipherment via pattern analysis (Kober) rather than bilingual text. (2) Enabled by large corpus of similar tablets (administrative texts all follow patterns). Common: All required linguistic knowledge (Greek for Linear B; Persian for Cuneiform; Coptic for Egyptian). All exploited structural redundancy: logograms, determinatives, phonetic complements, or known word forms. All combined logical deduction + pattern matching + validation. Linear B shows pattern analysis alone (without bilingual) can decipher if corpus is large and language is known.

🔴28. Why syllabary for Linear B+

Q: Why did Mycenaean Greek use syllabic writing (Linear B) instead of alphabet? What changed to make alphabet adopt?

Hypothesis: (1) Linear B adapted from Minoan (Linear A), which was syllabic. (2) Mycenaean Greeks adopted existing technology (cultural transmission). (3) Syllabary sufficed for administrative needs (inventory, personnel lists). (4) Later (Dark Ages collapse), Linear B was abandoned. When Greeks emerged from Dark Ages (~800 BCE), they borrowed Phoenician abjad (consonants only) and adapted it—adding vowel signs to create first true alphabet. Why alphabet over syllabary: (1) Alphabet simpler to learn (~24 signs vs ~90). (2) Better for diverse languages and new words (phonetic productivity). (3) Cultural prestige (Phoenician trade contact). The transition from syllabary → alphabet reflects linguistic innovation, not inherent superiority—both work, but alphabet won through historical contingency.

🔴29. Information flow in decipherment+

Q: Map information flow in Linear B decipherment: Kober triplets → Ventris grid → Chadwick validation → Tripod Tablet. How does each step refine knowledge?

Flow: (1) Kober triplets: Extract morphological structure (30-50 bits structural info). (2) Ventris grid: Constrain phonetic assignments (estimate each sign ≈ log₂(90) ≈ 6.5 bits; corpus of 5000 tablets ≈ 32,500 bits available). (3) Chadwick validation: Filter hypotheses using Greek language model (eliminates non-Greek phonotactics). (4) Tripod Tablet: Semantic validation (single tablet ≈ 200 bits of semantic info confirming correctness). Each step reduces hypothesis space: Kober eliminates ~70% (structure only); Ventris explores ~1000 candidates; Chadwick filters to top ~10; Tripod confirms #1. Cumulative information = decipherment success.

🔴30. Syllabic limitations for written Greek+

Q: Linear B syllabary has ~90 syllables, roughly one per Greek CV and V combination. Design a compression algorithm: how many signs are minimally needed to write Greek phonetically? How does this compare to alphabet?

Analysis: Greek has ~24 consonants × ~6 vowels ≈ 144 CV combinations. But many are rare or unattested. Phonologically productive: ~60-80 common CV syllables + ~7 vowels = ~90 syllables. Minimal syllabary: ~70 signs (realistic). Alphabet: ~24 letters (consonants) + ~7 vowel marks ≈ 31 signs. Comparison: Syllabary 70 vs Alphabet 31 = alphabet ~2x more compact per sign. But alphabet requires phonetic awareness (understanding consonants, vowels, their separate identity). Syllabary is more holistic (each sign = whole syllable, intuitive). Tradeoff: Alphabet is efficient (fewer signs, easier to extend); Syllabary is natural (matches human phonetic perception). Linear B chose the natural path; Greek later chose the efficient path.
