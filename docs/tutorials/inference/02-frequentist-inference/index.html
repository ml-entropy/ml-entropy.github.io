02. Frequentist Inference

Sampling distributions, MLE, confidence intervals, and hypothesis tests from a repeated-sampling view.

In This Chapter

We develop the frequentist paradigm from the ground up: parameters are fixed, data are random, and procedures are judged by their long-run frequency properties. You will learn to construct estimators, evaluate them by bias, consistency, and efficiency, derive maximum likelihood estimates, build confidence intervals, and carry out hypothesis tests — all from the repeated-sampling perspective.

The Big Picture

Frequentist inference treats parameters as fixed but unknown constants and treats data as random variables generated by a probability model that depends on those parameters. This is the defining philosophical commitment of the frequentist paradigm: the parameter $\theta$ that governs the data-generating process has a single true value — it is not itself a random variable, and we never assign it a probability distribution. All of the randomness in the problem lives in the data. Different samples drawn from the same population will produce different estimates, different confidence intervals, and different test statistics, and it is the behavior of these procedures across the space of possible samples that defines their quality.

Because the parameter is fixed, it makes no sense in this framework to ask "What is the probability that $\theta$ lies in some interval?" Instead, we ask a procedural question: "If I applied this estimation or testing procedure to every possible sample I could draw, how often would it give me the right answer?" This shift from belief about parameters to long-run performance of procedures is what distinguishes frequentist inference from Bayesian inference. A frequentist does not claim to know what $\theta$ is after observing data; rather, the frequentist deploys a method that is guaranteed to behave well in the long run.

The central question of frequentist inference can be stated plainly:

If I repeated this experiment many times, each time collecting new data and applying the same procedure, how would my estimator, interval, or test behave across all those repetitions?

This repeated-sampling framework has profound practical consequences. It means that "good" procedures are those with desirable frequency properties — small bias, low variance, correct coverage probability, and controlled error rates — averaged over all possible datasets that the true parameter could generate. The frequentist does not need to specify prior beliefs about the parameter, which can be both a strength (objectivity, regulatory acceptability) and a weakness (inability to incorporate existing knowledge). Understanding when this tradeoff works in your favor is one of the most important skills a practitioner can develop.

Before we develop the technical machinery, let us set the terminology straight. The parameter space $\Theta$ is the set of all possible values the parameter could take. A statistical model is a family of distributions $\{p(x;\theta) : \theta \in \Theta\}$, one for each value of the parameter. The observed data $x_1, \dots, x_n$ is a realization of the random sample $X_1, \dots, X_n$. An inferential procedure is any rule that maps data to a conclusion (an estimate, an interval, a decision). The frequentist evaluates this procedure by imagining all the datasets that could have been generated — the "ensemble" of possible experiments — and asking how the procedure performs on average across that ensemble.

The frequentist paradigm dominates much of applied statistics, from clinical trials and A/B testing to quality control in manufacturing. Regulatory bodies like the FDA and EMA require frequentist error-rate guarantees for drug approval. The framework's emphasis on controlling Type I errors (false positives) at pre-specified levels provides a rigorous, reproducible standard that is difficult to achieve with purely subjective methods. At the same time, the frequentist approach has well-known limitations — particularly in small-sample settings, high-dimensional problems, and situations where strong prior information is available — that motivate the Bayesian alternative discussed in the next chapter.

Historically, the frequentist framework was shaped by three towering figures in the early 20th century. Ronald Fisher (1890–1962) introduced maximum likelihood estimation, the concept of sufficiency, and the use of p-values as measures of evidence against a hypothesis. Jerzy Neyman (1894–1981) and Egon Pearson (1895–1980) formalized hypothesis testing as a decision procedure with controlled error rates, introducing the concepts of Type I and Type II errors, power, and the Neyman–Pearson Lemma. The modern practice of frequentist inference is an (often uneasy) blend of Fisher's evidential approach and the Neyman–Pearson decision-theoretic approach — a fact that explains some of the philosophical tensions practitioners encounter when interpreting p-values and confidence intervals.

Formal Setup

The standard frequentist setup assumes:

  • A parametric family of distributions $\{p(x;\theta) : \theta \in \Theta\}$ indexed by a parameter $\theta$ in a parameter space $\Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$.
  • A random sample $X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} p(x;\theta_0)$, where $\theta_0$ is the true (fixed, unknown) parameter value.
  • The goal is to use the observed data $x_1, \dots, x_n$ to learn about $\theta_0$ — through estimation (point estimates, confidence intervals) or testing (hypothesis tests).

Everything in frequentist inference flows from this setup. The key philosophical commitment is that $\theta_0$ is a fixed constant, and all probability statements are about the random data $X$, not about $\theta_0$.

Intuition

A frequentist confidence interval is like a manufacturing guarantee on a procedure, not a statement about any particular product. Imagine a factory that produces rulers. The factory guarantees that 95% of rulers it manufactures are accurate to within 0.1 mm. If you pick up one ruler from the shelf, you don't know whether this particular ruler is accurate — it either is or it isn't. But you know that the manufacturing process produces accurate rulers 95% of the time. A 95% confidence interval works the same way: the procedure of constructing the interval captures the true parameter 95% of the time, but any specific interval either contains $\theta$ or it does not.

This is subtle but essential. Consider tossing a coin whose true probability of heads is $p = 0.7$, though you don't know this value. You collect 100 tosses, compute $\hat{p}$, and build a confidence interval. That interval is now a fixed pair of numbers — say, $[0.62, 0.78]$. The true $p = 0.7$ is either inside that interval or it isn't; there is nothing random left. The 95% refers to the fact that if you repeated the entire experiment — 100 new tosses, new estimate, new interval — then 95 out of every 100 such intervals would contain $p = 0.7$. The guarantee is about the procedure's long-run hit rate, not about any single interval.

Another useful analogy is a weather forecast. When a meteorologist says there is a "30% chance of rain," a frequentist interpretation means: among all days with similar atmospheric conditions for which the model predicted 30%, roughly 30% of those days actually experienced rain. The statement is about the calibration of the forecasting procedure over many similar situations, not about this particular day in isolation. Frequentist inference applies the same logic to parameter estimation and hypothesis testing.

The frequentist perspective also illuminates the concept of calibration. A well-calibrated procedure is one whose stated error rates match its actual error rates. A 95% confidence interval procedure is well-calibrated if it truly covers the parameter 95% of the time. A test at level $\alpha = 0.05$ is well-calibrated if it rejects true nulls at most 5% of the time. Calibration is an empirically verifiable property — you can assess it by simulation, by meta-analysis of published studies, or by cross-validation. This is one of the great strengths of the frequentist framework: its guarantees are, in principle, falsifiable.

Why does this matter in practice? Because confusing the procedure's coverage probability with a posterior belief about the parameter leads to systematic misinterpretation of statistical results. A researcher who says "there is a 95% probability that the treatment effect lies in $[0.5, 1.3]$" is making a Bayesian statement, not a frequentist one. The frequentist statement is: "I used a procedure that, in 95% of experiments like this one, produces an interval containing the true treatment effect." This distinction becomes especially important in policy and regulatory settings, where the strength of evidence must be stated precisely.

Common Mistake

Do not say "there is a 95% probability that $\theta$ is in the interval." That is a Bayesian posterior statement. The frequentist statement is: "The procedure that generated this interval has a 95% coverage rate." Once the data are observed and the interval is computed, $\theta$ is either in the interval or not — there is no remaining randomness to assign a probability to.

To make this concrete, imagine running the same experiment 20 times, each time constructing a 95% confidence interval. You would expect about 19 of those intervals to contain $\theta$ and about 1 to miss. If you happened to see only the one that missed, you might draw a wrong conclusion — but you would have no way to know. This is the frequentist's fundamental bargain: you cannot guarantee correctness for any particular experiment, but you can guarantee a long-run error rate. In fields like clinical medicine, where the same type of study is repeated many times across different drugs and conditions, this long-run guarantee is genuinely meaningful.

A helpful mental model is the "confidence interval machine." Think of a machine that takes in data and outputs an interval. The machine has been calibrated so that 95% of the intervals it produces contain the true parameter. You feed your data into the machine and get one interval out. You trust the machine because of its calibration, not because of any property of this particular interval. Bayesian inference, by contrast, works like a different machine that also outputs beliefs — but that machine requires you to first tell it your prior beliefs. When reasonable people disagree about priors, the Bayesian machine gives different outputs; the frequentist machine always gives the same output for the same data.

A concrete coverage demonstration. Suppose the true mean is $\mu = 50$, the population standard deviation is $\sigma = 10$, and we draw samples of size $n = 25$. Each 95% CI has the form $\bar{x} \pm 1.96 \times 10/\sqrt{25} = \bar{x} \pm 3.92$. Here are five hypothetical experiments:

But across thousands of experiments, about 95% of intervals will cover $\mu = 50$. This is exactly the kind of guarantee that frequentist inference provides.

Core Objects

The frequentist framework is built on a small number of precisely defined mathematical objects. Mastering these objects — and understanding the relationships between them — is the key to fluency in frequentist inference. Everything that follows (properties of estimators, MLE, confidence intervals, hypothesis tests) is expressed in terms of these building blocks.

Estimator

Definition: Estimator

An estimator is a function of the observed data — and only the data — that produces a numerical guess for an unknown parameter. Formally, given a random sample $X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n$, an estimator is any function $\hat{\theta} = T(X_1, \dots, X_n)$ that does not depend on the unknown parameter $\theta$.

The key insight is that because the data are random, the estimator is itself a random variable. Before you collect the data, $\hat{\theta}$ has a distribution — it could take on many different values depending on which sample you happen to draw. After you collect the data, $\hat{\theta}$ becomes a single number called an estimate. The distinction between "estimator" (a random variable, a function of random inputs) and "estimate" (a realized number, the output of that function applied to a specific dataset) is fundamental. Much of frequentist theory is about the properties of the estimator-as-random-variable, evaluated across all possible datasets. This distinction is crucial: when we say "the sample mean is unbiased," we are making a statement about the estimator $\bar{X}$ (a random variable), not about any particular estimate $\bar{x}$ (a number).

Some common estimators you will encounter repeatedly:

  • Sample mean: $\bar{X} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ — estimates the population mean $\mu$.
  • Sample variance: $S^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X})^2$ — estimates the population variance $\sigma^2$. Notice the $n-1$ denominator, which is chosen to make this estimator unbiased.
  • Sample proportion: $\hat{p} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ where $X_i \in \{0, 1\}$ — estimates a Bernoulli probability $p$.
  • Maximum likelihood estimator (MLE): the value of $\theta$ that maximizes the likelihood of the observed data. We discuss this in detail below.

Not every function of the data is a good estimator. You could define $\hat{\theta} = 42$ regardless of the data — this is technically an estimator, but it ignores the data entirely and is useless unless the true parameter happens to be 42. You could define $\hat{\theta} = X_1$ — this uses only the first observation and throws away the rest. It is valid but wasteful. The properties of good estimators (unbiasedness, consistency, efficiency) discussed below give us principled criteria for choosing among the infinitely many possible estimators.

Sampling Distribution

Definition: Sampling Distribution

The sampling distribution of an estimator $\hat{\theta}$ is the probability distribution of $\hat{\theta}$ induced by the randomness in the data. It describes the set of values $\hat{\theta}$ would take if you repeated the experiment infinitely many times, each time drawing a new sample from the same population and computing $\hat{\theta}$ anew.

The sampling distribution is the engine of frequentist inference. Every frequentist statement — about bias, about variance, about confidence intervals, about p-values — is derived from the sampling distribution of an estimator or test statistic. If you understand the sampling distribution, you understand the behavior of the procedure.

Consider a concrete example. Suppose you draw $n = 25$ observations from a $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ population with $\mu = 10$ and $\sigma = 3$. The sample mean $\bar{X}$ is an estimator of $\mu$. Its sampling distribution is:

$$\bar{X} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(10, \frac{9}{25}\right) = \mathcal{N}(10, 0.36)$$

This tells us that if you repeated the experiment many times, the values of $\bar{X}$ would cluster around 10 with a standard deviation of $\sqrt{0.36} = 0.6$. Most estimates would fall within $10 \pm 1.2$. The standard deviation of the sampling distribution is called the standard error — it quantifies how much variability you expect in your estimate from one sample to the next. The standard error decreases as $n$ grows, at rate $1/\sqrt{n}$, which is why larger samples give more precise estimates.

Let us derive the variance of the sample mean to see why the $1/\sqrt{n}$ rate holds. For i.i.d. observations with $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2$:

$$\text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \text{Var}\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right) = \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i) = \frac{1}{n^2}\cdot n\sigma^2 = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$$

The second step uses the fact that variance of a sum of independent random variables equals the sum of their variances. The standard error is therefore $\text{SE}(\bar{X}) = \sigma/\sqrt{n}$. To halve the standard error, you must quadruple the sample size — a rule of diminishing returns that has profound implications for study design and resource allocation.

For many estimators, the exact sampling distribution is unknown or intractable. In such cases, we rely on asymptotic results — approximations that become accurate as $n \to \infty$. The most important of these is the Central Limit Theorem (CLT), which tells us that for any estimator that is an average of independent terms, the sampling distribution is approximately normal for large $n$. Formally, if $X_1, \dots, X_n$ are i.i.d. with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, then:

$$\frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, 1) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

This is why the normal distribution appears so ubiquitously in frequentist inference: not because we believe the data are normal, but because the estimator's sampling distribution is approximately normal for large samples, regardless of the shape of the underlying population distribution. The CLT applies to sums of any i.i.d. random variables with finite variance — uniform, exponential, Poisson, or any other distribution.

A closely related concept is sufficiency. A statistic $T(X)$ is sufficient for $\theta$ if the conditional distribution of the data given $T$ does not depend on $\theta$. Intuitively, $T$ captures all the information in the data about $\theta$ — once you know $T$, the rest of the data is just noise. For example, when estimating the mean of a normal distribution, the sample mean $\bar{X}$ is sufficient: knowing $\bar{X}$ tells you everything the data can tell you about $\mu$. Sufficient statistics allow us to reduce the data without losing any information, and the best estimators are typically functions of sufficient statistics (this is the content of the Rao–Blackwell theorem).

Properties of Good Estimators

Given that there are infinitely many possible estimators for any parameter, how do we choose? Frequentist theory provides a set of desirable properties that serve as a scorecard. No single property is sufficient on its own — an estimator can be unbiased but wildly imprecise, or biased but converge beautifully to the truth as the sample grows. The art of estimation lies in balancing these criteria for the problem at hand.

More formally, frequentist decision theory evaluates estimators using a loss function $L(\theta, \hat{\theta})$ that measures how bad it is to estimate $\theta$ by $\hat{\theta}$. The most common choice is squared error loss, $L(\theta, \hat{\theta}) = (\hat{\theta} - \theta)^2$. The risk of an estimator is its expected loss: $R(\theta, \hat{\theta}) = E_\theta[L(\theta, \hat{\theta})]$. Under squared error loss, the risk equals the mean squared error (MSE):

$$\text{MSE}(\hat{\theta}) = E[(\hat{\theta} - \theta)^2] = \text{Bias}(\hat{\theta})^2 + \text{Var}(\hat{\theta})$$

This bias-variance decomposition is one of the most important formulas in statistics and machine learning. It reveals that the total error of an estimator has two sources: systematic error (bias) and random error (variance). Minimizing MSE requires balancing both. An estimator with zero bias but enormous variance can have higher MSE than one with moderate bias but very low variance. This tradeoff is at the heart of regularization techniques like ridge regression and LASSO.

Unbiasedness

Definition: Unbiasedness

An estimator $\hat{\theta}$ is unbiased for $\theta$ if its expected value equals the true parameter value for every possible $\theta$:

$$E_\theta[\hat{\theta}] = \theta \quad \text{for all } \theta$$

The bias of an estimator is $\text{Bias}(\hat{\theta}) = E[\hat{\theta}] - \theta$. An unbiased estimator has zero bias.

Unbiasedness means that if you could repeat the experiment infinitely many times and average all the estimates, the average would equal the true parameter. In any single experiment, the estimate might be too high or too low, but there is no systematic tendency in either direction. Think of it like a marksman: an unbiased marksman's shots are centered on the bullseye, even if individual shots scatter around it. A biased marksman's shots cluster around a point that is systematically off-center.

Let us prove that the sample mean $\bar{X}$ is unbiased for the population mean $\mu$. Suppose $X_1, \dots, X_n$ are i.i.d. with $E[X_i] = \mu$. Then:

$$E[\bar{X}] = E\!\left[\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right] = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n E[X_i] = \frac{1}{n} \cdot n\mu = \mu$$

The proof uses only the linearity of expectation — no assumptions about normality or any particular distribution shape. This is a beautifully general result: the sample mean is always unbiased for the population mean, regardless of the underlying distribution, as long as the mean exists.

However, unbiasedness alone is not enough. Consider estimating the population mean $\mu$ from a sample of size $n = 100$. The estimator $\hat{\mu} = X_1$ (just use the first observation) is unbiased — $E[X_1] = \mu$ — but it throws away 99 observations. The sample mean $\bar{X}$ is also unbiased, but it uses all the data and has much lower variance. Both are unbiased, but one is clearly better. This motivates the next properties.

Another subtlety: transformations of unbiased estimators are generally not unbiased. If $\hat{\theta}$ is unbiased for $\theta$, it does not follow that $g(\hat{\theta})$ is unbiased for $g(\theta)$, unless $g$ is a linear function. This is a consequence of Jensen's inequality: for a convex function $g$, $E[g(\hat{\theta})] \geq g(E[\hat{\theta}]) = g(\theta)$. For example, $S^2$ is unbiased for $\sigma^2$, but $S = \sqrt{S^2}$ is not unbiased for $\sigma$ — it systematically underestimates the standard deviation, though the bias is small for large $n$.

Common Mistake

Do not assume that unbiased estimators are always preferable. Sometimes a slightly biased estimator with much lower variance gives better overall performance. The mean squared error $\text{MSE}(\hat{\theta}) = \text{Bias}^2 + \text{Var}(\hat{\theta})$ captures this tradeoff. Ridge regression, for example, deliberately introduces bias to achieve a dramatic reduction in variance, often resulting in lower MSE than the unbiased OLS estimator in high-dimensional settings.

Why does sample variance use $n-1$? A natural estimator of $\sigma^2$ would be $\tilde{S}^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i - \bar{X})^2$, but this is biased. To see why, note that $\bar{X}$ is closer to the data points than $\mu$ is (because $\bar{X}$ minimizes the sum of squared deviations), so $\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2 < \sum(X_i - \mu)^2$ on average. The bias calculation gives:

$$E\!\left[\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i - \bar{X})^2\right] = \frac{n-1}{n}\sigma^2$$

Dividing by $n-1$ instead of $n$ corrects this bias: $E[S^2] = E\!\left[\frac{1}{n-1}\sum(X_i-\bar{X})^2\right] = \sigma^2$. The intuition is that estimating $\mu$ from the data "uses up" one degree of freedom, leaving only $n-1$ independent pieces of information for estimating $\sigma^2$. This is the origin of the Bessel correction and the concept of degrees of freedom.

Consistency

Definition: Consistency

An estimator $\hat{\theta}_n$ is consistent for $\theta$ if it converges in probability to the true value as the sample size grows:

$$\hat{\theta}_n \xrightarrow{p} \theta \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

Formally, for every $\epsilon > 0$: $\lim_{n \to \infty} P(|\hat{\theta}_n - \theta| > \epsilon) = 0$.

Consistency is arguably more important than unbiasedness for practical purposes. An estimator is consistent if, given enough data, it will get arbitrarily close to the truth. This is a minimal requirement: if an estimator does not converge to the right answer as you collect more and more data, something is fundamentally wrong with it. The Law of Large Numbers guarantees that the sample mean is consistent for the population mean — this is perhaps the most important convergence result in all of statistics.

Consistency and unbiasedness are logically independent properties. An estimator can be unbiased but inconsistent. For example, consider $\hat{\mu} = X_1$ regardless of sample size. This estimator is unbiased ($E[X_1] = \mu$), but its variance $\text{Var}(X_1) = \sigma^2$ does not decrease with $n$ — it never gets more precise, so it is not consistent. Conversely, an estimator can be biased for every finite $n$ but consistent. The sample variance with the "wrong" denominator, $\tilde{S}^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$, has $E[\tilde{S}^2] = \frac{n-1}{n}\sigma^2 \neq \sigma^2$, so it is biased, but the bias $\frac{-\sigma^2}{n} \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$, and the estimator converges to $\sigma^2$, making it consistent.

A useful sufficient condition for consistency: if $\text{Bias}(\hat{\theta}_n) \to 0$ and $\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_n) \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$, then $\hat{\theta}_n$ is consistent. This follows from Chebyshev's inequality. For any $\epsilon > 0$:

$$P(|\hat{\theta}_n - \theta| > \epsilon) \leq P(|\hat{\theta}_n - E[\hat{\theta}_n]| > \epsilon - |\text{Bias}|) \leq \frac{\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_n)}{(\epsilon - |\text{Bias}|)^2} \to 0$$

For the sample mean: $\text{Bias}(\bar{X}) = 0$ and $\text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \sigma^2/n \to 0$, so $P(|\bar{X} - \mu| > \epsilon) \leq \sigma^2/(n\epsilon^2) \to 0$, confirming consistency. This is essentially the proof of the Weak Law of Large Numbers.

There is also a stronger form called strong consistency, which requires $\hat{\theta}_n \to \theta$ almost surely (i.e., with probability 1, not just in probability). The Strong Law of Large Numbers guarantees that the sample mean is strongly consistent. The distinction between weak and strong consistency rarely matters in practice — if an estimator is weakly consistent, it is good enough for most applications — but strong consistency provides a more satisfying guarantee that the estimator will converge to the truth, not merely that it is likely to.

Efficiency

Definition: Efficiency and the Cramér–Rao Bound

Among all unbiased estimators of $\theta$, the one with the smallest variance is called the minimum-variance unbiased estimator (MVUE). The Cramér–Rao lower bound provides a floor on how small the variance of any unbiased estimator can be:

$$\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}) \geq \frac{1}{I(\theta)}$$

where $I(\theta) = E\!\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\log p(X;\theta)\right)^2\right]$ is the Fisher information. An unbiased estimator that achieves this bound is called efficient.

The Cramér–Rao bound is one of the most elegant results in all of statistics. It says that the precision of any unbiased estimator is fundamentally limited by how much information the data carry about the parameter. If the likelihood function is very peaked (high Fisher information), then the data are very "informative" about $\theta$, and an efficient estimator can achieve very low variance. If the likelihood is flat (low Fisher information), then no unbiased estimator can be very precise — the data simply do not contain enough information.

Let us compute the Cramér–Rao bound for the mean of a normal distribution. If $X_1, \dots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2$ known, the log-likelihood for a single observation is:

$$\log p(x_i; \mu) = -\frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{(x_i - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}$$

The score (derivative of log-likelihood) for one observation is:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial \mu}\log p(x_i; \mu) = \frac{x_i - \mu}{\sigma^2}$$

The Fisher information for one observation is:

$$I_1(\mu) = E\!\left[\left(\frac{x_i - \mu}{\sigma^2}\right)^2\right] = \frac{1}{\sigma^4}\,E[(X_i - \mu)^2] = \frac{1}{\sigma^4}\cdot\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}$$

For $n$ independent observations, the total Fisher information is $I_n(\mu) = n \cdot I_1(\mu) = n/\sigma^2$. The Cramér–Rao lower bound on the variance of any unbiased estimator of $\mu$ is therefore:

$$\text{Var}(\hat{\mu}) \geq \frac{1}{I_n(\mu)} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$$

Since $\text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \sigma^2/n$ exactly, the sample mean achieves the Cramér–Rao bound — it is the most efficient unbiased estimator of the normal mean. No other unbiased estimator can have lower variance. This is a powerful and specific guarantee.

The concept of relative efficiency allows us to compare two estimators. If $\hat{\theta}_1$ and $\hat{\theta}_2$ are both unbiased estimators of $\theta$, their relative efficiency is $e(\hat{\theta}_1, \hat{\theta}_2) = \text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_2)/\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_1)$. A relative efficiency greater than 1 means $\hat{\theta}_1$ is more efficient (lower variance). For example, when estimating the center of a symmetric distribution, the sample mean and the sample median are both unbiased, but their relative efficiency depends on the distribution shape. For a normal distribution, the mean is about 57% more efficient than the median ($e \approx \pi/2$). For heavy-tailed distributions like the Cauchy, the roles reverse — the median is far more efficient because the mean is dominated by extreme observations.

Another example: Cramér–Rao bound for the Bernoulli parameter. For $X \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$, the log-likelihood is $\log p(x;p) = x\log p + (1-x)\log(1-p)$. The score is:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial p}\log p(x;p) = \frac{x}{p} - \frac{1-x}{1-p} = \frac{x - p}{p(1-p)}$$

The Fisher information for one observation is $I_1(p) = E\!\left[\frac{(X-p)^2}{p^2(1-p)^2}\right] = \frac{p(1-p)}{p^2(1-p)^2} = \frac{1}{p(1-p)}$. For $n$ observations, $I_n(p) = \frac{n}{p(1-p)}$, so the Cramér–Rao bound is $\text{Var}(\hat{p}) \geq \frac{p(1-p)}{n}$. Since $\text{Var}(\hat{p}) = \text{Var}(\bar{X}) = p(1-p)/n$ for the sample proportion, the MLE $\hat{p} = \bar{X}$ is efficient. Notice how the bound depends on $p$: estimation is hardest (highest variance) when $p = 0.5$ (maximum uncertainty) and easiest when $p$ is near 0 or 1.

Asymptotic Normality

Definition: Asymptotic Normality

An estimator $\hat{\theta}_n$ is asymptotically normal if, after proper centering and scaling, it converges in distribution to a normal random variable:

$$\sqrt{n}\,(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, V)$$

where $V$ is the asymptotic variance. Equivalently, for large $n$, $\hat{\theta}_n$ is approximately $\mathcal{N}(\theta, V/n)$.

Asymptotic normality is the workhorse property that makes frequentist inference practical for large samples. Even when we do not know the exact sampling distribution of an estimator, we can often show that it is approximately normal for large $n$. This is not a coincidence — it is a deep consequence of the Central Limit Theorem. Any estimator that can be expressed as a smooth function of sample averages will typically inherit asymptotic normality.

The practical payoff is enormous. If $\hat{\theta}_n$ is approximately $\mathcal{N}(\theta, V/n)$, then we can immediately construct an approximate confidence interval: $\hat{\theta}_n \pm z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{V/n}$, where $z_{\alpha/2}$ is the appropriate quantile of the standard normal. We can also build test statistics: $Z = \sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta_0)/\sqrt{V}$ is approximately $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ under $H_0: \theta = \theta_0$. This is why normal-based confidence intervals and z-tests appear everywhere in practice — they are the default tools enabled by asymptotic normality.

The maximum likelihood estimator (MLE), under regularity conditions, is asymptotically normal with asymptotic variance equal to the inverse Fisher information: $V = 1/I_1(\theta)$. This means the MLE is asymptotically efficient — it achieves the Cramér–Rao bound in the limit. This remarkable fact, combined with the MLE's consistency and invariance properties, explains why maximum likelihood is the default estimation method in statistics and machine learning.

Sketch of the proof of MLE asymptotic normality. The key idea is a Taylor expansion of the score function around the true parameter $\theta_0$. The MLE satisfies the score equation $\ell'(\hat{\theta}) = 0$. Expanding around $\theta_0$:

$$0 = \ell'(\hat{\theta}) \approx \ell'(\theta_0) + \ell''(\theta_0)(\hat{\theta} - \theta_0)$$

Solving for $\hat{\theta} - \theta_0$:

$$\hat{\theta} - \theta_0 \approx -\frac{\ell'(\theta_0)}{\ell''(\theta_0)} = \frac{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n s(\theta_0; X_i)}{-\frac{1}{n}\ell''(\theta_0)}$$

The numerator is an average of i.i.d. score terms with mean zero and variance $I_1(\theta_0)$, so by the CLT, $\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum s(\theta_0;X_i) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, I_1(\theta_0))$. The denominator converges in probability to $I_1(\theta_0)$ by the Law of Large Numbers. Combining these via Slutsky's theorem gives $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta} - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, 1/I_1(\theta_0))$. This proof sketch reveals why the Fisher information appears as the asymptotic variance: the MLE's precision is ultimately limited by the variability of the score function, which is exactly the Fisher information.

In practice, consistency is the most essential (an estimator that does not converge to the truth is fundamentally broken), while efficiency is the most aspirational (achieving the Cramér–Rao bound is the gold standard for precision).

Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) is the single most important estimation method in statistics and machine learning. The idea is disarmingly simple: given the data you actually observed, find the parameter value that makes those data most probable. Among all possible values of $\theta$, choose the one under which the observed sample was most likely to have occurred. This is not a philosophical principle but a practical recipe — and it turns out to have extraordinarily good theoretical properties.

Definition: Maximum Likelihood Estimator

Given i.i.d. observations $x_1, \dots, x_n$ from a model $p(x;\theta)$, the likelihood function is the joint density viewed as a function of $\theta$:

$$L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^n p(x_i \mid \theta)$$

The log-likelihood is $\ell(\theta) = \log L(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^n \log p(x_i \mid \theta)$. The MLE is:

$$\hat{\theta}_{\text{MLE}} = \arg\max_\theta \, \ell(\theta)$$

We work with the log-likelihood rather than the likelihood itself for two reasons. First, the log transforms products into sums, making calculus much easier — differentiating a sum is far simpler than differentiating a product. Second, in practice, the likelihood involves products of many small probabilities, which can underflow to zero on a computer; the log-likelihood avoids this numerical issue by working with sums of log-probabilities instead. Since the logarithm is a strictly increasing function, the maximizer of $L(\theta)$ and $\ell(\theta)$ are the same — maximizing one is equivalent to maximizing the other.

Worked example: MLE for the Gaussian mean. Suppose $X_1, \dots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2$ known, and we want to find the MLE of $\mu$. This is the simplest and most important MLE derivation — it illustrates the general procedure that applies to all parametric models.

The log-likelihood is:

$$\ell(\mu) = \sum_{i=1}^n \log p(x_i; \mu) = \sum_{i=1}^n \left[-\frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{(x_i - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right]$$

Dropping the constant terms that do not depend on $\mu$:

$$\ell(\mu) = -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \mu)^2 + \text{const}$$

To maximize, take the derivative with respect to $\mu$ and set it to zero:

$$\frac{d\ell}{d\mu} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \mu) = 0$$

Solving:

$$\sum_{i=1}^n x_i - n\mu = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \hat{\mu}_{\text{MLE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i = \bar{x}$$

The MLE of the Gaussian mean is simply the sample mean — an intuitive and satisfying result. We can verify that this is indeed a maximum (not a minimum) by checking the second derivative: $d^2\ell/d\mu^2 = -n/\sigma^2 < 0$, confirming concavity. Notice that the derivation did not require $\sigma^2$ to be known for the formula to work — the MLE of $\mu$ is $\bar{x}$ regardless of $\sigma^2$.

Worked example: MLE for a Bernoulli parameter. Suppose $X_1, \dots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Bernoulli}(p)$, where each $X_i \in \{0, 1\}$. We observe $k = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i$ successes. The log-likelihood is:

$$\ell(p) = \sum_{i=1}^n \big[x_i \log p + (1-x_i)\log(1-p)\big] = k\log p + (n-k)\log(1-p)$$

Taking the derivative and setting it to zero:

$$\frac{d\ell}{dp} = \frac{k}{p} - \frac{n-k}{1-p} = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad k(1-p) = (n-k)p \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \hat{p}_{\text{MLE}} = \frac{k}{n}$$

The MLE of a Bernoulli probability is the sample proportion — exactly the intuitive estimator. If you observe 73 heads in 100 coin flips, the MLE is $\hat{p} = 0.73$. The second derivative $d^2\ell/dp^2 = -k/p^2 - (n-k)/(1-p)^2 < 0$ confirms this is a maximum.

An important caveat: MLE can be biased. When both $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ are unknown in the Gaussian model, the MLE of $\sigma^2$ turns out to be $\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{MLE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i - \bar{X})^2$, which uses $n$ in the denominator rather than $n-1$. This estimator is biased: $E[\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{MLE}}] = \frac{n-1}{n}\sigma^2 < \sigma^2$. The MLE systematically underestimates the variance. This is a concrete illustration of the fact that the MLE's desirable properties (consistency, efficiency, normality) are all asymptotic — for finite samples, the MLE can be biased. The bias vanishes as $n \to \infty$, since $\frac{n-1}{n} \to 1$, so the MLE of $\sigma^2$ is still consistent and asymptotically efficient despite being biased.

Why MLE Matters

The MLE has a constellation of desirable properties that make it the default choice in most estimation problems:

  • Consistency: Under mild regularity conditions, $\hat{\theta}_{\text{MLE}} \xrightarrow{p} \theta_0$ as $n \to \infty$, where $\theta_0$ is the true parameter value.
  • Asymptotic efficiency: The MLE achieves the Cramér–Rao lower bound asymptotically. No other consistent estimator can have lower asymptotic variance.
  • Asymptotic normality: $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_{\text{MLE}} - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, I(\theta_0)^{-1})$, where $I(\theta_0)$ is the Fisher information. This immediately gives us confidence intervals and test statistics.
  • Invariance: If $\hat{\theta}$ is the MLE of $\theta$, then $g(\hat{\theta})$ is the MLE of $g(\theta)$ for any function $g$. This means you can reparameterize freely without re-deriving the estimator.

These properties are asymptotic — they hold as $n \to \infty$. For small samples, the MLE can be biased, and the normal approximation may be poor. In practice, "large enough $n$" depends on the complexity of the model and the smoothness of the likelihood. For simple models like the Gaussian mean, $n = 30$ is often sufficient. For complex models like neural networks, you may need thousands or millions of observations before asymptotic theory provides a reasonable approximation. A useful rule of thumb: the more parameters the model has, the more data you need before asymptotics kick in.

The "regularity conditions" mentioned above are worth understanding, at least informally. They require that: (1) the parameter space is an open set (the true parameter is not on a boundary), (2) the support of the distribution does not depend on $\theta$ (ruling out, e.g., $\text{Uniform}(0, \theta)$), (3) the log-likelihood is sufficiently smooth (at least three continuous derivatives), and (4) the Fisher information is finite and positive. When these conditions are violated — as they are for mixture models, models with boundary parameters, or non-regular families — the MLE may still be useful, but its asymptotic distribution can be non-normal, and the Cramér–Rao bound may not apply. These edge cases are important in advanced work.

The MLE also has a deep connection to information theory: maximizing the log-likelihood is equivalent to minimizing the Kullback–Leibler divergence between the empirical distribution of the data and the model family. To see why, note that maximizing $\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \log p(x_i;\theta)$ is equivalent to maximizing the expected log-likelihood under the empirical distribution $\hat{p}$. Adding and subtracting $\sum \log \hat{p}(x_i)$ shows this is the same as minimizing:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(\hat{p}\;\|\;p_\theta) = \sum_x \hat{p}(x)\log\frac{\hat{p}(x)}{p(x;\theta)}$$

So the MLE finds the model distribution closest to the data in the sense of KL divergence — a beautiful unification of estimation and information theory that we explore further below.

Confidence Intervals

Definition: Confidence Interval

A $100(1-\alpha)\%$ confidence interval for a parameter $\theta$ is a random interval $C(X) = [L(X), U(X)]$ — where both endpoints are functions of the random data $X$ — that satisfies the coverage property:

$$P_\theta\!\big(\theta \in C(X)\big) \geq 1 - \alpha \quad \text{for all } \theta$$

The probability is taken over the random data $X$, not over $\theta$. The parameter $\theta$ is fixed; the interval $C(X)$ is random.

The coverage property is a statement about the procedure, not about any single interval. Before you collect data, the interval $C(X)$ is random — its endpoints depend on the sample you will draw. The coverage property guarantees that, across all possible samples, at least $100(1-\alpha)\%$ of the resulting intervals will contain the true $\theta$. Once you collect your data and compute the interval, it becomes a pair of fixed numbers, and $\theta$ either lies inside or it does not. There is no remaining probability to assign.

The general recipe for constructing a confidence interval involves three steps: (1) find a pivotal quantity — a function of the data and the parameter whose distribution is completely known; (2) use the known distribution to sandwich the pivotal quantity between two quantiles with the desired probability; (3) algebraically rearrange to isolate the parameter. This pivotal method is the most common approach and produces intervals with exact coverage when a suitable pivot exists.

Worked example: constructing a confidence interval for a Gaussian mean. Suppose $X_1, \dots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2$ known. We want a 95% confidence interval for $\mu$.

Step 1: Identify a pivotal quantity. A pivotal quantity is a function of the data and the parameter whose distribution does not depend on any unknown parameters. Here:

$$Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

This is pivotal because $Z$ follows a standard normal distribution regardless of the values of $\mu$ and $\sigma$ (since $\sigma$ is known).

Step 2: Use the known distribution to set probability bounds. We know that $P(-1.96 \leq Z \leq 1.96) = 0.95$ from standard normal tables. Therefore:

$$P\!\left(-1.96 \leq \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \leq 1.96\right) = 0.95$$

Step 3: Solve for $\mu$ to get the interval. Rearranging the inequality inside the probability:

$$P\!\left(\bar{X} - 1.96\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \leq \mu \leq \bar{X} + 1.96\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right) = 0.95$$

Therefore, the 95% confidence interval is:

$$C(X) = \left[\bar{X} - 1.96\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}},\;\; \bar{X} + 1.96\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right]$$

Numerical example. Suppose you measure $n = 36$ light bulb lifetimes (in hours) and obtain $\bar{x} = 1200$, with known population standard deviation $\sigma = 120$. The 95% CI is:

$$1200 \pm 1.96 \cdot \frac{120}{\sqrt{36}} = 1200 \pm 1.96 \cdot 20 = 1200 \pm 39.2 = [1160.8, \; 1239.2]$$

You can report: "A 95% confidence interval for the mean lifetime is [1160.8, 1239.2] hours." This means that if you repeated the experiment many times, about 95% of such intervals would contain the true mean lifetime.

Confidence intervals can also be one-sided. A one-sided 95% lower confidence bound for $\mu$ takes the form $[\bar{X} - z_{0.05} \cdot \sigma/\sqrt{n}, +\infty)$, and provides 95% confidence that $\mu$ exceeds the lower bound. This is useful when you only care about establishing a minimum (or maximum) value — for example, a manufacturer might want to guarantee that mean product lifetime is at least $L$ hours.

When $\sigma$ is unknown — which is the usual case in practice — you replace it with the sample standard deviation $S$ and use the $t$-distribution instead of the normal. The interval becomes $\bar{X} \pm t_{n-1,\alpha/2} \cdot S/\sqrt{n}$, where $t_{n-1,\alpha/2}$ is the critical value from a $t$-distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom. For large $n$, the $t$-distribution is nearly indistinguishable from the standard normal, so the two methods give almost identical results.

The tradeoff between confidence level and interval width. There is a fundamental tension between how confident you want to be and how precise your interval can be. Higher confidence requires a wider interval: a 99% CI uses $z_{0.005} = 2.576$ instead of $1.96$, producing an interval roughly 32% wider. Conversely, a 90% CI uses $z_{0.05} = 1.645$ and is about 16% narrower. The only way to increase confidence without widening the interval is to increase the sample size. Doubling $n$ shrinks the interval width by a factor of $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41$, which is the fundamental $1/\sqrt{n}$ rate of convergence for averages.

You might wonder: why not always use 99.99% confidence? The answer is that extremely wide intervals become uninformative. A 99.99% CI for the drug effect might be $[-10, 16.4]$ mmHg, which tells you almost nothing. The choice of confidence level reflects a tradeoff between the procedure's reliability (coverage) and its informativeness (width). In practice, 95% is a convention, not a law of nature — different fields and applications may warrant different levels.

The bootstrap: confidence intervals without distributional assumptions. When the exact or asymptotic sampling distribution of your estimator is unknown or unreliable, the bootstrap provides a powerful alternative. The idea, due to Bradley Efron (1979), is to approximate the sampling distribution by resampling from the observed data. You draw $B$ bootstrap samples (each of size $n$, sampled with replacement from your data), compute the estimator on each bootstrap sample, and use the distribution of these $B$ bootstrap estimates to construct a confidence interval. For example, the "percentile bootstrap" CI uses the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the bootstrap distribution as the interval endpoints. The bootstrap is a genuinely frequentist method — it estimates the sampling distribution directly — and it works for a vast range of statistics where analytical formulas are unavailable.

Sample size determination from the CI formula. Confidence intervals also provide a natural way to determine sample sizes. Suppose you want a 95% CI for a mean with a margin of error no larger than $E$. Setting $1.96 \cdot \sigma/\sqrt{n} \leq E$ and solving gives:

$$n \geq \left(\frac{1.96\,\sigma}{E}\right)^2$$

For the light bulb example, if you want the margin of error to be at most 10 hours (instead of 39.2), you need $n \geq (1.96 \times 120/10)^2 = (23.52)^2 \approx 554$ bulbs. This is a four-fold decrease in margin of error from $n = 36$, requiring a roughly $15\times$ increase in sample size — again illustrating the $1/\sqrt{n}$ rate.

Common Mistake

Incorrect: "There is a 95% probability that $\mu$ lies in $[1160.8, 1239.2]$." This is a Bayesian posterior probability statement and requires a prior distribution on $\mu$.

Correct: "The interval $[1160.8, 1239.2]$ was constructed by a procedure that captures the true $\mu$ in 95% of repeated experiments." The distinction matters because it clarifies what the "95%" refers to — the procedure's long-run success rate, not a degree of belief about this particular interval.

Duality between confidence intervals and hypothesis tests. There is a beautiful correspondence between confidence intervals and hypothesis tests: a $100(1-\alpha)\%$ confidence interval for $\theta$ contains exactly those parameter values $\theta_0$ for which the test of $H_0: \theta = \theta_0$ at level $\alpha$ would not be rejected. In other words, the confidence interval is the set of "plausible" parameter values — those that are compatible with the observed data at the chosen significance level. This duality means that you can construct a confidence interval by inverting a family of tests, and vice versa. It also means that if a 95% CI for a treatment effect does not contain zero, then the two-sided test of $H_0: \text{effect} = 0$ at $\alpha = 0.05$ would reject — the two statements are logically equivalent.

Hypothesis Testing

Hypothesis testing is the frequentist framework for making binary decisions based on data. The logic is indirect and often counter-intuitive: you start by assuming the thing you want to disprove (the null hypothesis), then ask whether the data are extreme enough to cast doubt on that assumption. If the data would be very unlikely under the null, you reject it. If not, you fail to reject — which is not the same as accepting it.

Definition: Hypothesis Test

A hypothesis test consists of four components:

  • Null hypothesis $H_0$: a default claim about the parameter (e.g., $\mu = 0$, "no effect").
  • Alternative hypothesis $H_1$: the claim you are trying to find evidence for (e.g., $\mu \neq 0$).
  • Test statistic: a function of the data that measures the discrepancy between the data and $H_0$.
  • Rejection region: the set of test statistic values for which you reject $H_0$, chosen to control the Type I error rate (the probability of rejecting $H_0$ when it is true) at level $\alpha$.

There are two types of errors in hypothesis testing, and they trade off against each other:

  • Type I error (false positive): Rejecting $H_0$ when it is actually true. The probability of this is $\alpha$, called the significance level. Typical choices are $\alpha = 0.05$ or $\alpha = 0.01$.
  • Type II error (false negative): Failing to reject $H_0$ when $H_1$ is actually true. The probability of this is $\beta$. The power of the test is $1 - \beta$ — the probability of correctly rejecting $H_0$ when $H_1$ holds.

Reducing $\alpha$ (being more cautious about false positives) increases $\beta$ (more false negatives), and vice versa. The only way to reduce both is to increase the sample size $n$, which makes the test statistic more sensitive to departures from $H_0$.

The logic of hypothesis testing is analogous to proof by contradiction in mathematics. You assume $H_0$ is true, derive what the data should look like under that assumption, and then check whether the actual data are compatible with that prediction. If the data are wildly incompatible with $H_0$ (the test statistic falls in the rejection region), you "reject" $H_0$ — not because you have proved $H_1$, but because the data provide strong evidence against $H_0$. If the data are compatible with $H_0$, you "fail to reject" — which means the data do not provide sufficient evidence to abandon $H_0$, not that $H_0$ has been confirmed. The asymmetry between rejection and failure to reject is a defining feature of the Neyman–Pearson framework.

In the courtroom analogy, $H_0$ is "the defendant is innocent" and $H_1$ is "the defendant is guilty." The significance level $\alpha$ corresponds to the standard of proof — how much evidence is needed to convict. A verdict of "not guilty" does not mean "innocent"; it means the prosecution failed to meet the burden of proof. Similarly, failure to reject $H_0$ does not mean $H_0$ is true — it means the data did not provide strong enough evidence against it at the chosen $\alpha$ level.

p-value

Definition: p-value

The p-value is the probability, computed under the null hypothesis $H_0$, of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the value actually observed:

$$p\text{-value} = P_{H_0}(T \geq t_{\text{obs}})$$

(for a one-sided test; for two-sided, use $P_{H_0}(|T| \geq |t_{\text{obs}}|)$). A small p-value means the observed data are unlikely under $H_0$, providing evidence against $H_0$.

The p-value answers a very specific question: "If the null hypothesis were true, how surprising would my data be?" It does not answer the questions most people actually want answered. Understanding what the p-value is and is not is essential for sound statistical practice. The p-value can be thought of as a measure of compatibility between the data and $H_0$: a large p-value means the data are perfectly compatible with $H_0$, while a small p-value indicates tension. However, a small p-value does not tell you why the data are incompatible — the discrepancy could be due to the alternative hypothesis being true, or due to model misspecification, or due to other issues like selection bias.

A complete z-test example. A pharmaceutical company claims that a new drug lowers systolic blood pressure by $\mu_0 = 0$ mmHg (i.e., the drug has no effect). You recruit $n = 64$ patients, administer the drug, and measure the change in blood pressure. You observe a sample mean decrease of $\bar{x} = 3.2$ mmHg with a known population standard deviation $\sigma = 10$ mmHg. Test at $\alpha = 0.05$.

Step 1: State the hypotheses.

$$H_0: \mu = 0 \quad \text{(no effect)} \qquad H_1: \mu \neq 0 \quad \text{(some effect)}$$

Step 2: Choose and compute the test statistic. Under $H_0$, $\bar{X} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2/n)$, so:

$$Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} = \frac{3.2 - 0}{10/\sqrt{64}} = \frac{3.2}{1.25} = 2.56$$

Step 3: Determine the rejection region. For a two-sided test at $\alpha = 0.05$, we reject $H_0$ if $|Z| > z_{0.025} = 1.96$. Since $|2.56| = 2.56 > 1.96$, we reject $H_0$.

Step 4: Compute the p-value. The p-value is $P(|Z| \geq 2.56) = 2 \cdot P(Z \geq 2.56) = 2 \cdot 0.0052 = 0.0104$. Since $0.0104 < 0.05$, this confirms our rejection of $H_0$.

Step 5: Interpret. There is statistically significant evidence at the 5% level that the drug affects blood pressure. The estimated effect is a decrease of 3.2 mmHg. Note that statistical significance does not automatically imply clinical significance — a 3.2 mmHg decrease might or might not be medically meaningful, depending on the context. We should also report the confidence interval: $3.2 \pm 1.96 \times 1.25 = [0.75, 5.65]$ mmHg, which gives a range of plausible effect sizes and is often more informative than the p-value alone.

One-sided vs. two-sided tests. In the example above, we used a two-sided test ($H_1: \mu \neq 0$), which detects effects in either direction. If we had prior scientific reason to believe the drug can only lower blood pressure, we could use a one-sided test: $H_0: \mu \leq 0$ vs. $H_1: \mu > 0$. The one-sided p-value would be $P(Z \geq 2.56) = 0.0052$, exactly half the two-sided value. One-sided tests are more powerful for detecting effects in the specified direction, but they cannot detect effects in the opposite direction, no matter how large. The choice between one-sided and two-sided should be made before looking at the data, based on scientific considerations — not chosen after the fact to make the p-value smaller.

Common Mistake

The p-value is not any of the following:

  • Not the probability that $H_0$ is true. That would require a prior probability on $H_0$, which is a Bayesian concept.
  • Not the probability that the result occurred "by chance." The p-value assumes $H_0$ is true and asks about the data; it does not compute the probability that $H_0$ generated the data.
  • Not a measure of effect size. A tiny, practically meaningless effect can produce a very small p-value if $n$ is large enough. Conversely, a large, important effect can produce a non-significant p-value if $n$ is too small.

Always report effect sizes and confidence intervals alongside p-values to give a complete picture of your findings.

Power and sample size. The power of a test is $1 - \beta = P(\text{reject } H_0 \mid H_1 \text{ is true})$. In our blood pressure example, suppose the true effect is $\mu = 3$ mmHg. Then under $H_1$, $\bar{X} \sim \mathcal{N}(3, \sigma^2/n) = \mathcal{N}(3, 100/64)$, and we reject when $|Z| > 1.96$. The test statistic under $H_1$ has distribution $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(3/1.25, 1) = \mathcal{N}(2.4, 1)$. The power is $P(|Z| > 1.96 \mid Z \sim \mathcal{N}(2.4,1)) \approx 0.80$. This means there is about an 80% chance of detecting a true 3 mmHg effect with $n = 64$ patients — a common benchmark for adequate power.

Power increases with three factors: larger sample size $n$ (which shrinks the standard error), larger true effect size $\mu$ (which pushes the test statistic further from zero), and larger significance level $\alpha$ (which widens the rejection region, at the cost of more false positives). Power analysis — computing the sample size needed to detect a clinically meaningful effect with adequate power — is a crucial part of study design that should be done before collecting data, not after.

The Neyman–Pearson Lemma and optimal tests. A natural question is: among all tests at significance level $\alpha$, which one has the highest power? For testing a simple null $H_0: \theta = \theta_0$ against a simple alternative $H_1: \theta = \theta_1$, the Neyman–Pearson Lemma provides the definitive answer. The most powerful test rejects $H_0$ when the likelihood ratio exceeds a threshold:

$$\text{Reject } H_0 \text{ when } \frac{L(\theta_1)}{L(\theta_0)} = \frac{p(x_1,\dots,x_n;\theta_1)}{p(x_1,\dots,x_n;\theta_0)} > k$$

where $k$ is chosen so that $P_{\theta_0}(\text{reject}) = \alpha$. This elegant result says that the likelihood ratio is the optimal test statistic — it extracts the maximum possible evidence from the data for distinguishing $\theta_1$ from $\theta_0$. The likelihood ratio test generalizes to composite hypotheses and is the foundation for many standard tests in applied statistics, including the $t$-test, $F$-test, and chi-squared test for contingency tables.

Effect sizes and practical significance. While p-values tell you whether an effect is statistically distinguishable from zero, effect sizes tell you how large the effect is. Common standardized effect sizes include Cohen's $d$ (the difference in means divided by the pooled standard deviation), the correlation coefficient $r$, and the odds ratio. In our blood pressure example, Cohen's $d = 3.2/10 = 0.32$, which is conventionally considered a "small to medium" effect. Reporting effect sizes alongside p-values is increasingly required by journals and professional organizations, because it forces the researcher to address the question that actually matters: not "is the effect nonzero?" but "is the effect large enough to care about?"

Each horizontal bar represents a confidence interval constructed from one particular experiment. The green interval happens to contain the true $\theta$; the yellow interval, centered on a different estimate, also captures it; but the purple interval misses entirely — its sample happened to fall far from the truth.

The critical point is that for a 95% confidence interval procedure, roughly 95 out of every 100 such intervals will cover the true parameter. The 5% that miss are the price of using random data to make inferences about fixed quantities. A frequentist judges the procedure by this long-run coverage rate — not by whether any particular interval succeeded or failed. This is what it means to evaluate methods by their operating characteristics rather than by their performance on a single dataset.

Notice also that the width of each interval reflects the precision of the estimator. Narrower intervals come from more precise estimators (lower variance), which in turn come from larger samples or more informative data. The relationship between interval width, sample size, and Fisher information is one of the central quantitative themes of this chapter: $\text{width} \propto \sigma/\sqrt{n}$ for Gaussian data, and more generally $\text{width} \propto 1/\sqrt{I_n(\theta)}$ where $I_n(\theta)$ is the total Fisher information.

Information-Theoretic Connections

Frequentist inference and information theory are deeply intertwined, even though they developed from different intellectual traditions. The key bridge is the Fisher information, which appears in both the Cramér–Rao bound (a frequentist result) and in the geometry of statistical models (an information-theoretic perspective). Understanding this connection reveals that the limits of statistical estimation are fundamentally about how much "information" the data carry about the parameter — a concept that resonates directly with Shannon's entropy.

To motivate this connection, consider a fundamental question: why are some parameters easier to estimate than others? If you flip a nearly fair coin ($p = 0.5$), it takes many flips to determine whether $p$ is exactly 0.5 or 0.51 — the two hypotheses generate nearly identical data. But if $p = 0.99$, even a few flips quickly distinguish $p = 0.99$ from $p = 0.95$, because the data look very different. The Fisher information quantifies this: it measures how sensitive the distribution of the data is to small changes in the parameter. When the data distribution changes rapidly with $\theta$, the data carry a lot of information about $\theta$, and estimation is easy. When the distribution barely changes, information is scarce, and estimation is hard.

Definition: Score Function and Fisher Information

The score function is the gradient of the log-likelihood with respect to $\theta$:

$$s(\theta; x) = \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log p(x; \theta)$$

The score has zero mean under the true model: $E_\theta[s(\theta; X)] = 0$. The Fisher information is the variance of the score:

$$I(\theta) = \text{Var}_\theta\!\big[s(\theta; X)\big] = E_\theta\!\left[\left(\frac{\partial \log p(X;\theta)}{\partial \theta}\right)^2\right]$$

Under regularity conditions, this equals the expected negative curvature of the log-likelihood:

$$I(\theta) = -E_\theta\!\left[\frac{\partial^2 \log p(X;\theta)}{\partial \theta^2}\right]$$

The curvature interpretation is particularly illuminating. If the log-likelihood $\ell(\theta)$ has high curvature at the true value $\theta_0$, then the likelihood function is sharply peaked — small deviations from $\theta_0$ cause a rapid drop in likelihood. This means the data are very "informative" about the parameter: the likelihood clearly distinguishes $\theta_0$ from nearby alternatives. Conversely, if the curvature is low, the likelihood is flat, and many parameter values are nearly equally consistent with the data — the data carry little information. The Fisher information quantifies exactly this: how sharply the log-likelihood discriminates between nearby parameter values.

The Cramér–Rao bound, $\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}) \geq 1/I(\theta)$, can now be read as an information bound: the precision of any unbiased estimator is limited by the information content of the data. This parallels Shannon's channel capacity theorem, which bounds the rate at which information can be transmitted through a noisy channel. In both cases, there is a fundamental limit set by the "noisiness" of the system, and no clever procedure can exceed it.

The connection to entropy becomes explicit when we consider the relationship between Fisher information and the Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence. The KL divergence between two nearby distributions $p(x; \theta)$ and $p(x; \theta + \delta)$ can be approximated as:

$$D_{\text{KL}}\!\big(p(\cdot;\theta)\;\|\;p(\cdot;\theta+\delta)\big) \approx \frac{1}{2}\,I(\theta)\,\delta^2$$

This shows that Fisher information is the local metric on the space of probability distributions — it measures how "distinguishable" two nearby distributions are. This connects directly to the information-geometric view of statistics, where statistical models are curved manifolds equipped with the Fisher information metric, and estimation is the problem of locating a point on this manifold. The deeper lesson is that the frequentist's Cramér–Rao bound and the information theorist's entropy are two facets of the same underlying structure: the geometry of uncertainty.

For the exponential family — a broad class that includes the Gaussian, Bernoulli, Poisson, and many other distributions — the relationship between Fisher information and entropy is especially clean. The Fisher information of an exponential family distribution equals the second derivative of the log-partition function (which is related to the cumulant generating function), and the entropy is the Legendre dual of that same function. This duality means that estimating parameters (a frequentist task) and measuring uncertainty (an information-theoretic task) are mathematically mirror images of each other. When you do MLE in an exponential family, you are simultaneously maximizing the log-likelihood and matching the sufficient statistics of the data — which is equivalent to minimizing the KL divergence from the empirical distribution to the model.

Worked example: Fisher information for the Poisson distribution. Let $X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, so $p(x;\lambda) = e^{-\lambda}\lambda^x/x!$. The log-likelihood is $\log p(x;\lambda) = -\lambda + x\log\lambda - \log(x!)$. The score is:

$$s(\lambda; x) = \frac{\partial}{\partial\lambda}\log p(x;\lambda) = -1 + \frac{x}{\lambda} = \frac{x - \lambda}{\lambda}$$

The Fisher information is:

$$I(\lambda) = E\!\left[\left(\frac{X-\lambda}{\lambda}\right)^2\right] = \frac{\text{Var}(X)}{\lambda^2} = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda^2} = \frac{1}{\lambda}$$

The Cramér–Rao bound for the Poisson rate is therefore $\text{Var}(\hat{\lambda}) \geq \lambda/n$ (for $n$ observations). The MLE $\hat{\lambda} = \bar{X}$ has $\text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \lambda/n$, so it is efficient. Interestingly, the Fisher information $1/\lambda$ decreases as $\lambda$ grows — which means that larger Poisson rates are easier to estimate precisely (per observation), because high-rate processes generate more "events" and thus more information. This connects beautifully to entropy: the entropy of a Poisson distribution increases with $\lambda$ (more uncertainty in the outcome), yet the Fisher information also increases (making estimation easier). The two are not contradictory — entropy measures unpredictability of individual observations, while Fisher information measures how much those observations reveal about the parameter.

Strengths

Frequentist inference has endured for over a century because it offers a set of guarantees that are difficult to obtain from any other framework. These strengths are not merely theoretical — they have made frequentist methods the default in regulated industries, clinical medicine, and experimental science.

  • Clear operating characteristics: Every frequentist procedure comes with a well-defined error rate. A 95% confidence interval has 95% coverage. A test at level $\alpha = 0.05$ has at most a 5% false positive rate. These guarantees hold regardless of the true parameter value and require no subjective inputs. This makes frequentist methods auditable and reproducible — two scientists applying the same procedure to the same data will reach the same conclusion.
  • No need to specify priors: Unlike Bayesian methods, frequentist inference does not require the analyst to choose a prior distribution for the parameter. This is a major advantage in settings where there is genuine disagreement about prior knowledge, or where the audience may not accept subjective inputs. In regulatory contexts (FDA drug approvals, EPA environmental standards), prior-free methods avoid the controversy of "whose prior?"
  • Strong asymptotic theory: The large-sample theory of frequentist inference — the Central Limit Theorem, the asymptotic normality of the MLE, the chi-squared distribution of likelihood ratio statistics — provides a powerful and general toolkit. For large datasets, these approximations are highly accurate and allow analysts to construct confidence intervals and tests for virtually any parametric model without resorting to simulation.
  • Widely used and well-understood: Frequentist methods are the lingua franca of applied statistics. Nearly every scientific journal, regulatory agency, and introductory textbook uses frequentist language. This widespread adoption means there is an enormous body of worked examples, software implementations, and institutional knowledge to draw on.
  • Optimality results: For many problems, frequentist theory identifies the "best possible" procedure. The Neyman–Pearson lemma gives the most powerful test for simple hypotheses. The Rao–Blackwell theorem and Lehmann–Scheffé theorem provide recipes for constructing minimum-variance unbiased estimators. These optimality results provide a level of theoretical rigor that is hard to match.

Taken together, these strengths make frequentist inference an indispensable part of the statistician's toolkit. Even practitioners who prefer Bayesian methods often use frequentist ideas to calibrate their procedures — checking that Bayesian credible intervals have good frequentist coverage, for example, or using maximum likelihood as a starting point for MCMC. The two paradigms are complementary, not contradictory, and the best practitioners are fluent in both.

Common Misunderstandings

Frequentist inference is frequently misunderstood, even by experienced practitioners. Many of these misunderstandings stem from conflating frequentist statements (which are about procedures and long-run frequencies) with Bayesian statements (which are about degrees of belief). Let us address the most common ones explicitly.

  1. A confidence interval is not a posterior interval. A 95% confidence interval does not mean "there is a 95% probability that $\theta$ is in this interval." It means "this interval was produced by a procedure that captures the true $\theta$ in 95% of repeated experiments." The difference is not pedantic — in some situations (e.g., bounded parameters near a boundary), confidence intervals and Bayesian credible intervals can be dramatically different, leading to different conclusions.
  2. A p-value is not the probability that the null hypothesis is true. The p-value is $P(\text{data this extreme} \mid H_0)$. What most people want is $P(H_0 \mid \text{data})$, which requires Bayes' theorem and a prior on $H_0$. These two quantities can differ enormously. In particular, when the prior probability of $H_0$ is high (e.g., most drug candidates don't work), a p-value of 0.05 can correspond to a posterior probability of $H_0$ being true that is well above 50%.
  3. Statistical significance is not practical significance. With a large enough sample, even a tiny, meaningless effect can achieve $p < 0.001$. A drug that lowers blood pressure by 0.1 mmHg might be statistically significant with $n = 100{,}000$ but clinically useless. Always report effect sizes and confidence intervals alongside p-values. The question "Is the effect nonzero?" is almost always less interesting than "How large is the effect?"
  4. Large samples do not protect you from bad study design. Confounding, selection bias, measurement error, and other design flaws are not fixed by collecting more data. A study that compares a treatment group to a non-randomized control group will produce biased estimates no matter how large $n$ is. Frequentist theory assumes the data were generated by the specified model; if the model is wrong, the guarantees are void. Statistical inference is only as good as the study that produced the data.
  5. Failure to reject $H_0$ does not mean $H_0$ is true. A non-significant result ($p > \alpha$) means the data are compatible with $H_0$, but they may also be compatible with many other hypotheses. The test may simply lack the power to detect a real effect. Saying "we found no effect" when you mean "we failed to reject the null" is a common and consequential error — especially when a study is underpowered. To distinguish "no effect" from "insufficient evidence," you should report the confidence interval and the power of the test against plausible alternative values.
Common Mistake

Beware of "p-hacking" — the practice of trying many analyses and reporting only the one that produces a significant p-value. If you test 20 independent hypotheses at $\alpha = 0.05$, you expect one false positive even when all null hypotheses are true: $P(\text{at least one rejection}) = 1 - (1-0.05)^{20} \approx 0.64$. Multiple testing corrections (Bonferroni, Benjamini–Hochberg) are designed to address this, but they only work if you apply them honestly.

Failure Modes

Every statistical framework has failure modes — situations where its assumptions break down and its guarantees no longer hold. Understanding these is essential for responsible practice. Frequentist inference is no exception, and its failures tend to be subtle because the procedures still produce numbers (p-values, confidence intervals) even when the conditions for their validity are violated.

  • Optional stopping without correction: If you peek at your data as it accumulates and stop the experiment when you first see a significant result, the actual Type I error rate is much higher than the nominal $\alpha$. This is because you are giving yourself multiple chances to reject $H_0$. Sequential analysis methods (e.g., Wald's SPRT, group sequential designs, alpha spending functions) are specifically designed to allow for interim looks while controlling the overall error rate, but standard fixed-sample tests do not have this property.
  • Multiple testing: Testing many hypotheses simultaneously inflates the family-wise error rate. If you test 100 genes for association with a disease at $\alpha = 0.05$, you expect about 5 false positives even if none of the genes are truly associated. Corrections like the Bonferroni method ($\alpha/m$ per test) or the Benjamini–Hochberg procedure (which controls the false discovery rate rather than the family-wise error rate) are necessary but reduce power.
  • Model misspecification: All frequentist guarantees are conditional on the assumed model being correct. If you assume normality but the data have heavy tails, your confidence intervals will be too narrow and your Type I error rate will be inflated. Robust methods (e.g., bootstrap confidence intervals, permutation tests, sandwich standard errors) can mitigate this, but they come with their own limitations and assumptions.
  • Small-sample approximations used too casually: The asymptotic normality of the MLE and the chi-squared distribution of likelihood ratio statistics are large-sample results. For small $n$, they can be poor approximations. The $t$-distribution provides exact inference for the normal mean with unknown variance, but for more complex models, there may be no exact small-sample distribution available, and the analyst must rely on simulation-based methods (bootstrap, permutation tests) or Bayesian approaches.
  • Interpreting confidence statements as posterior beliefs: Perhaps the most pervasive failure mode is psychological rather than technical. Practitioners routinely interpret frequentist outputs as Bayesian — saying "there is a 95% probability that the effect is between 2 and 5" when they should say "the procedure has 95% coverage." This slippage is so common that it corrupts public discourse about statistical evidence, leading to overconfidence in individual results.

The common thread in these failure modes is that frequentist guarantees are conditional on doing the analysis correctly. The machinery only works if the model is approximately right, the testing procedure is pre-specified, the stopping rule is fixed, and the results are interpreted in procedural (not epistemic) terms. When these conditions hold, frequentist inference is remarkably powerful. When they are violated — often silently and inadvertently — the nominal error rates become meaningless, and the analyst may be misled without knowing it. Awareness of these failure modes is what separates a careful statistician from a dangerous one.

The replication crisis and p-value reform. In 2015, the Open Science Collaboration attempted to replicate 100 published psychology experiments and found that only about 36% of the replications produced statistically significant results, compared to 97% of the originals. This "replication crisis" is partly attributable to the failure modes described above — p-hacking, optional stopping, multiple testing, and the conflation of statistical and practical significance. In response, the American Statistical Association issued a landmark statement in 2016 clarifying what p-values can and cannot do, and some journals have moved away from significance thresholds entirely in favor of reporting full results (effect sizes, confidence intervals, and exact p-values). These developments do not invalidate frequentist inference — they highlight the importance of using it correctly.

When Frequentist Inference Is a Good Fit

Frequentist inference is not always the best approach, but there are many settings where its strengths align perfectly with the needs of the problem. Recognizing these settings is a core part of statistical literacy.

  • Controlled experiments with repeated protocols: Clinical trials, A/B tests, and manufacturing quality control involve repeating the same procedure under controlled conditions. This is exactly the setting where "long-run frequency" makes the most sense — the experiment really will be repeated, and you want guarantees that hold across repetitions. The frequentist framework was designed for this scenario.
  • Large datasets with stable estimators: When $n$ is large, asymptotic theory provides excellent approximations. The MLE is consistent, efficient, and asymptotically normal, and confidence intervals based on it have nearly exact coverage. In the era of big data, many problems fall into this regime, making frequentist methods both simple and reliable.
  • Settings where procedural guarantees matter: In regulatory contexts (drug approval, environmental policy, engineering safety standards), the consumer of the analysis needs a guarantee that the procedure controls error rates at pre-specified levels. A Bayesian analysis can be more informative, but its conclusions depend on the choice of prior, which may be contested. Frequentist error-rate guarantees are prior-free and therefore less vulnerable to criticism from stakeholders who disagree about prior beliefs.
  • Exploratory analysis and hypothesis generation: When you are sifting through many variables looking for associations (e.g., genome-wide association studies), frequentist multiple-testing procedures provide principled ways to control false discoveries. The false discovery rate framework of Benjamini and Hochberg is a frequentist tool that has become standard in genomics, neuroscience, and other high-dimensional fields.
  • When prior information is absent or contentious: If there is no reliable prior information about the parameter, or if different experts hold strongly different prior beliefs, a frequentist analysis provides a common ground. The results depend only on the data and the assumed model, not on anyone's subjective beliefs. This objectivity can be especially valuable in adversarial settings (e.g., litigation, policy debates) where the credibility of the analysis depends on its perceived impartiality.

Conversely, when prior information is strong and the sample is small, Bayesian methods often outperform frequentist ones. When the goal is prediction rather than inference, machine learning methods may be more appropriate. And when the assumptions of the parametric model are seriously in doubt, nonparametric or robust methods should be preferred. The wise practitioner matches the method to the problem, using frequentist inference where its strengths are most relevant and switching to alternatives where they are not.

Worked example: A/B testing with frequentist inference. Suppose a tech company wants to test whether a new website design (B) increases click-through rate compared to the current design (A). They randomly assign 5,000 visitors to each design and observe:

  • Design A: 250 clicks out of 5,000 ($\hat{p}_A = 0.050$)
  • Design B: 300 clicks out of 5,000 ($\hat{p}_B = 0.060$)

The estimated difference is $\hat{p}_B - \hat{p}_A = 0.010$ (a 1 percentage point increase). To test $H_0: p_B = p_A$ vs. $H_1: p_B \neq p_A$, compute the pooled proportion $\hat{p} = 550/10000 = 0.055$ and the z-statistic:

$$Z = \frac{\hat{p}_B - \hat{p}_A}{\sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})(1/n_A + 1/n_B)}} = \frac{0.010}{\sqrt{0.055 \times 0.945 \times 0.0004}} = \frac{0.010}{0.00455} \approx 2.20$$

The two-sided p-value is approximately 0.028, significant at $\alpha = 0.05$. A 95% CI for the difference is $0.010 \pm 1.96 \times 0.00455 = [0.001, 0.019]$. The company can conclude that the new design likely increases click-through rate, though the effect could be as small as 0.1 percentage points — which may or may not justify the engineering cost of deployment.

Key Takeaways
  • Frequentist inference treats parameters as fixed unknowns and evaluates procedures by their long-run behavior over repeated samples.
  • An estimator is a function of the data; the sampling distribution describes how it varies across experiments; good estimators are unbiased, consistent, and efficient.
  • The Cramér–Rao bound sets a fundamental floor on estimator variance, determined by the Fisher information — the data's inherent informativeness about the parameter.
  • Maximum likelihood estimation is consistent, asymptotically efficient, and asymptotically normal — making it the default estimation method in most settings.
  • A confidence interval's "95%" refers to the procedure's coverage rate, not the probability that any single interval contains $\theta$.
  • A p-value is the probability of data as extreme as observed under $H_0$ — not the probability that $H_0$ is true, and not a measure of effect size.
  • Fisher information bridges frequentist inference and information theory: it equals the expected curvature of the log-likelihood and locally approximates the KL divergence between neighboring distributions.

Looking Ahead

Frequentist inference provides a rigorous, procedure-based framework for learning from data. Its guarantees — controlled error rates, coverage probabilities, optimality results — are strongest in the settings it was designed for: large samples, well-specified models, pre-registered analyses, and problems where long-run frequency is a natural interpretation of probability.

In the next chapter, we turn to Bayesian inference, which takes a fundamentally different approach. Instead of treating the parameter as a fixed constant and evaluating procedures by their frequency properties, Bayesian inference treats the parameter as a random variable with a prior distribution and updates that distribution in light of the data using Bayes' theorem. The result is a posterior distribution that directly quantifies uncertainty about the parameter — something the frequentist framework deliberately avoids. We will see that the two frameworks often agree in large samples (when the prior is "washed out" by the data) but can diverge dramatically in small samples or when the prior is informative.

The information-theoretic connections we developed in this chapter — Fisher information, the score function, the KL divergence — will appear again in the Bayesian context. Fisher information determines the shape of the posterior in the large-sample limit (via the Bernstein–von Mises theorem), and the KL divergence measures the information gained from prior to posterior. These deep connections reinforce the central theme of this tutorial series: that information, uncertainty, and learning are different facets of the same mathematical structure.

The hard problems push you to think more deeply: proving properties of estimators, exploring the connections between frequentist and Bayesian inference, and analyzing situations where standard methods break down.
