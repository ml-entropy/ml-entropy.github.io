Exercise 51: Measure-Theoretic Conditional Probability (Borel–Kolmogorov Paradox)

Consider a point chosen uniformly at random on the surface of the unit sphere $S^2 = \{(x,y,z) : x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1\}$, parameterized by spherical coordinates $(\theta, \phi)$ where $\theta \in [0, \pi]$ is the polar angle and $\phi \in [0, 2\pi)$ is the azimuthal angle. The uniform (surface area) measure gives density $f(\theta, \phi) = \frac{1}{4\pi} \sin\theta$.

(a) Compute the conditional density of $\theta$ given that the point lies on the great circle $\phi = 0$ by conditioning on the event $\{\phi \in [0, \epsilon)\}$ and taking $\epsilon \to 0$. Show that the resulting density is $f_{\theta \mid \phi=0}(\theta) = \frac{1}{2}\sin\theta$.

(b) Now reparameterize the same sphere using coordinates $(u, v)$ where $u = \cos\theta$ and $v = \phi$. Compute the conditional density of $u$ given $v = 0$ using the same limiting procedure. Show that the resulting density is uniform on $[-1, 1]$: $f_{u \mid v=0}(u) = \frac{1}{2}$.

(c) Explain why these two answers are not contradictory, despite describing "the same" conditional event. What does this reveal about the definition of conditional probability for events of measure zero?

Show Solution

(a) Conditioning in $(\theta, \phi)$ coordinates:

The joint density with respect to the Lebesgue measure $d\theta\, d\phi$ is $f(\theta, \phi) = \frac{1}{4\pi}\sin\theta$. The marginal density of $\phi$ is:

$$f_\phi(\phi) = \int_0^\pi \frac{1}{4\pi}\sin\theta\, d\theta = \frac{1}{4\pi} \cdot 2 = \frac{1}{2\pi}$$

By the standard formula for conditional density:

$$f_{\theta \mid \phi}(\theta \mid \phi = 0) = \frac{f(\theta, 0)}{f_\phi(0)} = \frac{\frac{1}{4\pi}\sin\theta}{\frac{1}{2\pi}} = \frac{\sin\theta}{2}$$

We verify: $\int_0^\pi \frac{1}{2}\sin\theta\, d\theta = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2 = 1$. ✓ This density concentrates mass near $\theta = \pi/2$ (the equator). $\square$

(b) Conditioning in $(u, v)$ coordinates:

With $u = \cos\theta$ we have $du = -\sin\theta\, d\theta$, so the surface area element $\frac{1}{4\pi}\sin\theta\, d\theta\, d\phi$ becomes $\frac{1}{4\pi}\, du\, dv$ (since $\sin\theta\, d\theta = -du$ and $d\phi = dv$, absorbing the sign by reversing limits). The joint density in $(u, v)$ is:

$$g(u, v) = \frac{1}{4\pi}, \quad u \in [-1,1],\; v \in [0, 2\pi)$$

The marginal of $v$ is $g_v(v) = \int_{-1}^{1} \frac{1}{4\pi}\, du = \frac{1}{2\pi}$. Therefore:

$$g_{u \mid v}(u \mid v=0) = \frac{g(u, 0)}{g_v(0)} = \frac{\frac{1}{4\pi}}{\frac{1}{2\pi}} = \frac{1}{2}$$

This is the uniform density on $[-1, 1]$. $\square$

(c) Resolution of the paradox:

The two answers are not contradictory because they condition on different $\sigma$-algebras. In part (a), we condition on the $\sigma$-algebra generated by $\phi$; in part (b), we condition on the $\sigma$-algebra generated by $v = \phi$. Although the event $\{\phi = 0\}$ is the same great circle in both cases, the conditional expectation $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ depends on the entire $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{G}$, not just the specific atom. In the measure-theoretic definition, $P(A \mid \mathcal{G})$ is defined only up to a set of $\mathcal{G}$-measure zero, and different parameterizations yield different regular conditional distributions.

Key insight: The Borel–Kolmogorov paradox demonstrates that "conditioning on an event of probability zero" is not well-defined without specifying the limiting procedure or, equivalently, the sub-$\sigma$-algebra. The elementary definition $P(A \mid B) = P(A \cap B)/P(B)$ breaks down when $P(B) = 0$, and the measure-theoretic remedy (conditional expectation via the Radon–Nikodym theorem) makes the dependence on the $\sigma$-algebra explicit.

Exercise 52: Martingale Stopping Theorem Application

A gambler plays a sequence of fair coin tosses, winning $+1$ for heads and $-1$ for tails. Let $S_n = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n$ be the gambler's fortune after $n$ tosses, where each $X_i \in \{-1, +1\}$ with equal probability, and $S_0 = 0$.

(a) Define $M_n = S_n^2 - n$. Prove that $(M_n)_{n \geq 0}$ is a martingale with respect to the natural filtration $\mathcal{F}_n = \sigma(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$.

(b) Let $a, b > 0$ be positive integers. Define the stopping time $\tau = \min\{n \geq 0 : S_n = -a \text{ or } S_n = b\}$. Using the Optional Stopping Theorem applied to the martingale $(S_n)$, find $P(S_\tau = b)$.

(c) Apply the Optional Stopping Theorem to $(M_n)$ to find $E[\tau]$, the expected duration of the game.

(d) Interpret the result of part (c) in terms of conditional probability: given that the gambler eventually reaches $b$ (rather than $-a$), what is the conditional expected game length?

Show Solution

(a) Proving $(M_n)$ is a martingale:

We need $E[M_{n+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = M_n$. Compute:

$$E[S_{n+1}^2 \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = E[(S_n + X_{n+1})^2 \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = S_n^2 + 2S_n E[X_{n+1}] + E[X_{n+1}^2]$$

Since $X_{n+1}$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}_n$ with $E[X_{n+1}] = 0$ and $E[X_{n+1}^2] = 1$:

$$E[S_{n+1}^2 \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = S_n^2 + 1$$

Therefore $E[M_{n+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = E[S_{n+1}^2 - (n+1) \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = (S_n^2 + 1) - (n+1) = S_n^2 - n = M_n$. $\square$

(b) Finding $P(S_\tau = b)$ via OST on $(S_n)$:

Since $(S_n)$ is a martingale (fair random walk), $\tau$ is a.s. finite (the walk is recurrent), and $|S_{n \wedge \tau}| \leq \max(a, b)$ is bounded. By the Optional Stopping Theorem:

$$E[S_\tau] = E[S_0] = 0$$

At time $\tau$, $S_\tau \in \{-a, b\}$. Let $p = P(S_\tau = b)$. Then:

$$0 = p \cdot b + (1 - p)(-a) = pb - a + pa$$ $$\boxed{p = P(S_\tau = b) = \frac{a}{a + b}}$$

(c) Finding $E[\tau]$ via OST on $(M_n)$:

The stopped process $M_{n \wedge \tau} = S_{n \wedge \tau}^2 - (n \wedge \tau)$ is bounded since $|S_{n \wedge \tau}| \leq \max(a,b)$. We need to verify that $E[\tau] < \infty$ (which follows from the recurrence of the simple random walk hitting any bounded set). By the Optional Stopping Theorem:

$$E[M_\tau] = E[M_0] = 0$$ $$E[S_\tau^2 - \tau] = 0 \implies E[\tau] = E[S_\tau^2]$$

Now compute $E[S_\tau^2]$:

$$E[S_\tau^2] = p \cdot b^2 + (1-p) \cdot a^2 = \frac{a}{a+b} \cdot b^2 + \frac{b}{a+b} \cdot a^2 = \frac{ab(b + a)}{a+b} = ab$$ $$\boxed{E[\tau] = ab}$$

(d) Conditional expected game length:

By the law of total expectation, $E[\tau] = E[\tau \mid S_\tau = b]\, P(S_\tau = b) + E[\tau \mid S_\tau = -a]\, P(S_\tau = -a)$. By symmetry of the random walk, one can show (via optional stopping of $S_n^2 - n$ restricted to each event, or by a direct gambler's ruin analysis on the conditional walk) that:

$$E[\tau \mid S_\tau = b] \cdot \frac{a}{a+b} + E[\tau \mid S_\tau = -a] \cdot \frac{b}{a+b} = ab$$

A separate analysis using the harmonic function approach (solving the boundary value problem $E_x[\tau] = 1 + \frac{1}{2}E_{x-1}[\tau] + \frac{1}{2}E_{x+1}[\tau]$ on $\{-a+1, \ldots, b-1\}$) yields $E_x[\tau] = (a+x)(b-x)$. Setting $x=0$: $E_0[\tau] = ab$ (consistent). Conditioning on hitting $b$:

$$E[\tau \mid S_\tau = b] = \frac{E_0[\tau \cdot \mathbf{1}_{S_\tau = b}]}{P(S_\tau = b)}$$

By computing this via the $h$-transform (Doob's transform with $h(x) = (a+x)/(a+b)$, the harmonic function for hitting $b$), one obtains $E[\tau \mid S_\tau = b] = \frac{b(2a+b)}{3}$ for the conditional duration. This can also be verified: $\frac{b(2a+b)}{3} \cdot \frac{a}{a+b} + \frac{a(a+2b)}{3} \cdot \frac{b}{a+b} = \frac{ab}{3(a+b)}[2a + b + a + 2b] = \frac{ab \cdot 3(a+b)}{3(a+b)} = ab$. ✓ $\square$

Key insight: The Optional Stopping Theorem converts a question about a random stopping time into a simple algebraic equation by exploiting the martingale property. The product $ab$ for $E[\tau]$ is memorable: doubling both boundaries quadruples the expected game length, reflecting the diffusive ($\sqrt{n}$) scaling of random walks.

Exercise 53: Hidden Markov Model Inference

A two-state Hidden Markov Model (HMM) has hidden states $Z_t \in \{0, 1\}$ and observed emissions $Y_t \in \{A, B\}$. The transition matrix is:

$$T = \begin{pmatrix} 0.7 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.6 \end{pmatrix}, \quad T_{ij} = P(Z_{t+1} = j \mid Z_t = i)$$

The emission probabilities are $P(Y_t = A \mid Z_t = 0) = 0.9$, $P(Y_t = A \mid Z_t = 1) = 0.2$. The initial distribution is $P(Z_1 = 0) = 0.5$.

(a) Derive the forward algorithm: define $\alpha_t(i) = P(Y_1, \ldots, Y_t, Z_t = i)$ and show the recursion $\alpha_{t+1}(j) = \left[\sum_i \alpha_t(i)\, T_{ij}\right] B_j(Y_{t+1})$, where $B_j(y) = P(Y_t = y \mid Z_t = j)$.

(b) Given the observation sequence $Y_1 = A$, $Y_2 = B$, $Y_3 = A$, compute $\alpha_t(i)$ for $t = 1, 2, 3$ and both states $i \in \{0, 1\}$. Then compute the filtering probability $P(Z_3 = 0 \mid Y_1 = A, Y_2 = B, Y_3 = A)$.

(c) State and derive the backward algorithm defining $\beta_t(i) = P(Y_{t+1}, \ldots, Y_T \mid Z_t = i)$. Then use the forward–backward algorithm to compute the smoothing probability $P(Z_2 = 0 \mid Y_1 = A, Y_2 = B, Y_3 = A)$.

Show Solution

(a) Deriving the forward recursion:

Define $\alpha_t(i) = P(Y_1, \ldots, Y_t, Z_t = i)$. For the base case:

$$\alpha_1(i) = P(Y_1, Z_1 = i) = P(Z_1 = i)\, P(Y_1 \mid Z_1 = i) = \pi_i \, B_i(Y_1)$$

For the recursion, marginalize over the previous hidden state:

$$\alpha_{t+1}(j) = P(Y_1, \ldots, Y_{t+1}, Z_{t+1} = j) = \sum_i P(Y_1, \ldots, Y_t, Z_t = i)\, P(Z_{t+1} = j \mid Z_t = i)\, P(Y_{t+1} \mid Z_{t+1} = j)$$

The first factor is $\alpha_t(i)$. The second uses the Markov property: $Z_{t+1}$ depends only on $Z_t$, so $P(Z_{t+1} = j \mid Z_t = i, Y_1, \ldots, Y_t) = T_{ij}$. The third uses the emission independence: $P(Y_{t+1} \mid Z_{t+1} = j, Z_t, Y_1, \ldots, Y_t) = B_j(Y_{t+1})$. Therefore:

$$\boxed{\alpha_{t+1}(j) = \left[\sum_i \alpha_t(i)\, T_{ij}\right] B_j(Y_{t+1})} \quad \square$$

(b) Numerical computation:

Emission table: $B_0(A) = 0.9$, $B_0(B) = 0.1$, $B_1(A) = 0.2$, $B_1(B) = 0.8$.

Step $t=1$ ($Y_1 = A$):

$$\alpha_1(0) = 0.5 \times 0.9 = 0.45, \quad \alpha_1(1) = 0.5 \times 0.2 = 0.10$$

Step $t=2$ ($Y_2 = B$):

$$\alpha_2(0) = [\alpha_1(0) \cdot 0.7 + \alpha_1(1) \cdot 0.4] \times B_0(B) = [0.315 + 0.04] \times 0.1 = 0.0355$$ $$\alpha_2(1) = [\alpha_1(0) \cdot 0.3 + \alpha_1(1) \cdot 0.6] \times B_1(B) = [0.135 + 0.06] \times 0.8 = 0.1560$$

Step $t=3$ ($Y_3 = A$):

$$\alpha_3(0) = [\alpha_2(0) \cdot 0.7 + \alpha_2(1) \cdot 0.4] \times 0.9 = [0.02485 + 0.0624] \times 0.9 = 0.078525$$ $$\alpha_3(1) = [\alpha_2(0) \cdot 0.3 + \alpha_2(1) \cdot 0.6] \times 0.2 = [0.01065 + 0.0936] \times 0.2 = 0.020850$$

The filtering probability:

$$P(Z_3 = 0 \mid Y_{1:3}) = \frac{\alpha_3(0)}{\alpha_3(0) + \alpha_3(1)} = \frac{0.078525}{0.078525 + 0.020850} = \frac{0.078525}{0.099375} \approx 0.7902$$

(c) Backward algorithm and smoothing:

Define $\beta_t(i) = P(Y_{t+1}, \ldots, Y_T \mid Z_t = i)$. Base case: $\beta_T(i) = 1$ for all $i$. Recursion: by marginalizing over $Z_{t+1}$:

$$\beta_t(i) = \sum_j T_{ij}\, B_j(Y_{t+1})\, \beta_{t+1}(j)$$

This follows because $P(Y_{t+1}, \ldots, Y_T \mid Z_t = i) = \sum_j P(Z_{t+1} = j \mid Z_t = i)\, P(Y_{t+1} \mid Z_{t+1} = j)\, P(Y_{t+2}, \ldots, Y_T \mid Z_{t+1} = j)$.

Backward computation ($T = 3$):

$$\beta_3(0) = 1, \quad \beta_3(1) = 1$$ $$\beta_2(0) = 0.7 \times 0.9 \times 1 + 0.3 \times 0.2 \times 1 = 0.63 + 0.06 = 0.69$$ $$\beta_2(1) = 0.4 \times 0.9 \times 1 + 0.6 \times 0.2 \times 1 = 0.36 + 0.12 = 0.48$$

Smoothing probability:

$$P(Z_2 = 0 \mid Y_{1:3}) = \frac{\alpha_2(0)\, \beta_2(0)}{\sum_i \alpha_2(i)\, \beta_2(i)} = \frac{0.0355 \times 0.69}{0.0355 \times 0.69 + 0.1560 \times 0.48}$$ $$= \frac{0.024495}{0.024495 + 0.07488} = \frac{0.024495}{0.099375} \approx 0.2465$$

Key insight: The filtering probability $P(Z_3 = 0 \mid Y_{1:3}) \approx 0.79$ is high because the last observation $A$ is strongly associated with state 0. The smoothing probability $P(Z_2 = 0 \mid Y_{1:3}) \approx 0.25$ is low because observation $Y_2 = B$ strongly suggests state 1, and having future information (unlike filtering) allows us to use this evidence more effectively. This illustrates how the forward–backward algorithm combines past and future evidence through conditional probability.

Exercise 54: Random Graph Connectivity and Conditional Probability

In the Erdős–Rényi random graph $G(n, p)$, each of the $\binom{n}{2}$ possible edges is included independently with probability $p$.

(a) Let $X$ be the number of isolated vertices (vertices with degree 0). Compute $E[X]$ and $\text{Var}(X)$. Show that for $p = \frac{\ln n + c}{n}$ with $c$ a fixed constant, $E[X] \to e^{-c}$ as $n \to \infty$.

(b) Using the method of conditional second moments, prove that $X$ converges in distribution to a Poisson random variable with mean $e^{-c}$. Specifically, show that the factorial moments $E[X(X-1)\cdots(X-k+1)] \to (e^{-c})^k$.

(c) Prove that the probability of connectivity satisfies $P(G(n,p) \text{ is connected}) \to e^{-e^{-c}}$ as $n \to \infty$ (for $p = \frac{\ln n + c}{n}$). Explain why the dominant obstruction to connectivity is the existence of isolated vertices.

Show Solution

(a) Mean and variance of $X$:

Let $X_i = \mathbf{1}[\text{vertex } i \text{ is isolated}]$. Then $X = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$. Vertex $i$ is isolated iff all $n-1$ potential edges from $i$ are absent:

$$P(X_i = 1) = (1-p)^{n-1}$$ $$E[X] = n(1-p)^{n-1}$$

Substituting $p = \frac{\ln n + c}{n}$: as $n \to \infty$, $(1-p)^{n-1} = \left(1 - \frac{\ln n + c}{n}\right)^{n-1} \to e^{-(\ln n + c)} = \frac{e^{-c}}{n}$. Therefore $E[X] \to n \cdot \frac{e^{-c}}{n} = e^{-c}$. $\square$

For the variance, we need $E[X^2] = \sum_{i,j} E[X_i X_j]$. When $i = j$: $E[X_i^2] = E[X_i] = (1-p)^{n-1}$. When $i \neq j$: vertices $i$ and $j$ are both isolated iff all edges from $i$ are absent, all edges from $j$ are absent, and the edge $\{i,j\}$ is absent. The edges from $i$ (excluding $\{i,j\}$): $n-2$ edges. Similarly for $j$. Plus the shared edge $\{i,j\}$. Total: $2(n-2) + 1 = 2n-3$ distinct edges, each absent with probability $(1-p)$:

$$E[X_i X_j] = (1-p)^{2n-3} = \frac{(1-p)^{2(n-1)}}{(1-p)^{-1}} \cdot (1-p)^0$$

More precisely: $E[X_i X_j] = (1-p)^{2(n-1)-1}$ (since the edge $\{i,j\}$ is shared). Therefore:

$$E[X^2] = n(1-p)^{n-1} + n(n-1)(1-p)^{2n-3}$$ $$\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = n(1-p)^{n-1} + n(n-1)(1-p)^{2n-3} - n^2(1-p)^{2(n-1)}$$

As $n \to \infty$ with $p = \frac{\ln n + c}{n}$, $\text{Var}(X) \to e^{-c} + (e^{-c})^2 \cdot (1-p)^{-1} - (e^{-c})^2 \to e^{-c} + 0 = e^{-c}$, since $(1-p)^{-1} \to 1$ and the cross terms cancel to leading order. The variance converges to the mean, consistent with Poisson behavior. $\square$

(b) Factorial moments and Poisson convergence:

The $k$-th factorial moment is $E[X(X-1)\cdots(X-k+1)] = E\left[\sum_{i_1 \neq \cdots \neq i_k} X_{i_1} \cdots X_{i_k}\right]$. For $k$ distinct vertices to all be isolated, we need all edges among them and from them to other vertices to be absent. The number of edges involved is $k(n-1) - \binom{k}{2}$ (each of the $k$ vertices has $n-1$ potential edges, but the $\binom{k}{2}$ edges between them are counted twice):

$$E\!\left[\prod_{j=1}^k X_{i_j}\right] = (1-p)^{k(n-1) - \binom{k}{2}}$$

The number of ways to choose $k$ distinct vertices is $n(n-1)\cdots(n-k+1) = n^k(1 + O(1/n))$. So:

$$E[X(X-1)\cdots(X-k+1)] = n^{(k)}(1-p)^{k(n-1) - \binom{k}{2}}$$

As $n \to \infty$: $n^{(k)} \sim n^k$ and $(1-p)^{k(n-1)} \to e^{-k(\ln n + c)} = n^{-k} e^{-kc}$, while $(1-p)^{-\binom{k}{2}} \to 1$. Therefore:

$$E[X(X-1)\cdots(X-k+1)] \to n^k \cdot n^{-k} e^{-kc} = (e^{-c})^k$$

Since all factorial moments converge to those of $\text{Poisson}(e^{-c})$, and the Poisson distribution is determined by its moments, we conclude $X \xrightarrow{d} \text{Poisson}(e^{-c})$ by the method of moments. $\square$

(c) Connectivity threshold:

A graph is connected iff $X_{\text{min-degree}} \geq 1$, where $X_{\text{min-degree}}$ is the minimum degree. It is a fundamental theorem of Erdős and Rényi that at the threshold $p = \frac{\ln n + c}{n}$, the probability that the graph is disconnected is asymptotically equal to the probability that it has an isolated vertex. Heuristically, having a component of size $k$ (for $2 \leq k \leq n/2$) requires all $k(n-k)$ cross-edges to be absent, which has probability $(1-p)^{k(n-k)} \leq e^{-pk(n-k)}$. For $k \geq 2$, $pk(n-k) \geq 2p(n-2) \approx 2\ln n$, so this probability is $O(n^{-2})$, which is negligible compared to the $O(1)$ probability of isolated vertices.

Since isolated vertices are the dominant obstruction and $X \xrightarrow{d} \text{Poisson}(e^{-c})$:

$$P(\text{connected}) = P(X = 0) + o(1) \to e^{-e^{-c}} \quad \square$$

Key insight: The sharp connectivity threshold $p^* = \frac{\ln n}{n}$ arises because this is precisely when isolated vertices disappear: for $c \to +\infty$, $P(\text{connected}) \to 1$; for $c \to -\infty$, $P(\text{connected}) \to 0$. This illustrates a general principle in random structures: phase transitions are often governed by the "simplest" obstructions, which can be analyzed via conditional probability and indicator random variables.

Exercise 55: Concentration Inequality via Conditional Probability (Azuma–Hoeffding)

Let $(M_n)_{n \geq 0}$ be a martingale with respect to a filtration $(\mathcal{F}_n)$, with $M_0 = 0$. Suppose the differences are bounded: $|M_k - M_{k-1}| \leq c_k$ almost surely for deterministic constants $c_k > 0$.

(a) For any $\lambda > 0$, define $W_k = e^{\lambda M_k}$. Show that $E[W_k \mid \mathcal{F}_{k-1}] \leq W_{k-1} \cdot e^{\lambda^2 c_k^2 / 2}$ by using the inequality $E[e^{\lambda X}] \leq e^{\lambda^2 c^2 / 2}$ whenever $E[X] = 0$ and $|X| \leq c$ (Hoeffding's lemma).

(b) Use part (a) to prove the Azuma–Hoeffding inequality: for any $t > 0$,

$$P(M_n \geq t) \leq \exp\!\left(-\frac{t^2}{2\sum_{k=1}^n c_k^2}\right)$$

(c) Application (chromatic number): Let $\chi(G)$ be the chromatic number of the random graph $G(n, 1/2)$. By exposing edges one at a time, show that $\chi(G)$ satisfies the bounded-difference condition with $c_k = 1$. Conclude that $P(|\chi(G) - E[\chi(G)]| \geq t) \leq 2\exp\!\left(-\frac{t^2}{n(n-1)}\right)$.

Show Solution

(a) Bounding the conditional MGF:

Let $D_k = M_k - M_{k-1}$ be the martingale difference. Since $(M_n)$ is a martingale, $E[D_k \mid \mathcal{F}_{k-1}] = 0$, and by assumption $|D_k| \leq c_k$ a.s. We have:

$$E[W_k \mid \mathcal{F}_{k-1}] = E[e^{\lambda M_k} \mid \mathcal{F}_{k-1}] = e^{\lambda M_{k-1}} \cdot E[e^{\lambda D_k} \mid \mathcal{F}_{k-1}]$$

We now apply Hoeffding's lemma: if a random variable $X$ satisfies $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}] = 0$ and $|X| \leq c$ a.s., then $E[e^{\lambda X} \mid \mathcal{G}] \leq e^{\lambda^2 c^2/2}$. (Proof sketch: by convexity of $e^{\lambda x}$, we bound it linearly on $[-c, c]$, then take expectations using $E[X \mid \mathcal{G}] = 0$, and optimize.)

Applying this to $D_k$ conditioned on $\mathcal{F}_{k-1}$:

$$E[e^{\lambda D_k} \mid \mathcal{F}_{k-1}] \leq e^{\lambda^2 c_k^2 / 2}$$ $$\therefore\; E[W_k \mid \mathcal{F}_{k-1}] \leq W_{k-1} \cdot e^{\lambda^2 c_k^2 / 2} \quad \square$$

(b) Proving Azuma–Hoeffding:

By Markov's inequality, for any $\lambda > 0$:

$$P(M_n \geq t) = P(e^{\lambda M_n} \geq e^{\lambda t}) \leq \frac{E[e^{\lambda M_n}]}{e^{\lambda t}}$$

Now iterate the bound from part (a). Define $V_k = e^{\lambda M_k - \frac{\lambda^2}{2}\sum_{j=1}^k c_j^2}$. From part (a):

$$E[V_k \mid \mathcal{F}_{k-1}] = e^{-\frac{\lambda^2}{2}\sum_{j=1}^k c_j^2} E[e^{\lambda M_k} \mid \mathcal{F}_{k-1}] \leq e^{-\frac{\lambda^2}{2}\sum_{j=1}^k c_j^2} \cdot e^{\lambda M_{k-1}} \cdot e^{\frac{\lambda^2 c_k^2}{2}} = V_{k-1}$$

So $(V_k)$ is a supermartingale with $V_0 = 1$. By the supermartingale property, $E[V_n] \leq E[V_0] = 1$, hence:

$$E[e^{\lambda M_n}] \leq e^{\frac{\lambda^2}{2}\sum_{k=1}^n c_k^2}$$

Combining with Markov's inequality:

$$P(M_n \geq t) \leq e^{-\lambda t + \frac{\lambda^2}{2}\sum c_k^2}$$

Optimize over $\lambda$: set $\frac{d}{d\lambda}\left(-\lambda t + \frac{\lambda^2}{2}\sum c_k^2\right) = 0$, giving $\lambda^* = \frac{t}{\sum c_k^2}$. Substituting:

$$P(M_n \geq t) \leq \exp\!\left(-\frac{t^2}{2\sum_{k=1}^n c_k^2}\right) \quad \square$$

(c) Application to chromatic number:

Label the $N = \binom{n}{2}$ potential edges as $e_1, \ldots, e_N$. Reveal them one at a time: let $\mathcal{F}_k = \sigma(Z_1, \ldots, Z_k)$ where $Z_i = \mathbf{1}[e_i \in G]$. Define the Doob martingale $M_k = E[\chi(G) \mid \mathcal{F}_k]$, so $M_0 = E[\chi(G)]$ and $M_N = \chi(G)$.

We claim $|M_k - M_{k-1}| \leq 1$. To see this: $M_k - M_{k-1} = E[\chi(G) \mid \mathcal{F}_k] - E[\chi(G) \mid \mathcal{F}_{k-1}]$. The only difference is the status of edge $e_k$. Adding or removing a single edge can change $\chi(G)$ by at most 1 (if adding an edge creates a conflict in an optimal coloring, at most one vertex needs recoloring with one additional color). Therefore the conditional expectations also shift by at most 1.

Apply Azuma–Hoeffding with $c_k = 1$ for all $k$ and $n' = N = \binom{n}{2} \leq \frac{n(n-1)}{2}$... actually with $c_k = 1$ and the martingale $M_k - M_0$:

$$P(\chi(G) - E[\chi(G)] \geq t) = P(M_N - M_0 \geq t) \leq \exp\!\left(-\frac{t^2}{2N}\right) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{t^2}{n(n-1)}\right)$$

By symmetry (applying to $-M_k$ as well), $P(|\chi(G) - E[\chi(G)]| \geq t) \leq 2\exp\!\left(-\frac{t^2}{n(n-1)}\right)$. $\square$

Key insight: The Azuma–Hoeffding inequality demonstrates the power of conditional probability: by decomposing a complex random variable into a sum of bounded martingale differences (each conditioned on the past), we obtain exponential concentration. The chromatic number example shows that even for combinatorial quantities with complicated global structure, the bounded-difference property—a local, conditional statement—yields strong global concentration.

Exercise 56: Exchangeable Random Partitions (Chinese Restaurant Process)

In the Chinese Restaurant Process (CRP) with parameter $\alpha > 0$, customers $1, 2, 3, \ldots$ arrive sequentially and sit at tables according to the following conditional rule: when customer $n+1$ arrives, given the current seating arrangement with $k$ occupied tables having $n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_k$ customers respectively ($\sum n_i = n$), customer $n+1$ sits at:

  • existing table $i$ with probability $\frac{n_i}{n + \alpha}$, for $i = 1, \ldots, k$
  • a new table with probability $\frac{\alpha}{n + \alpha}$

(a) Verify that these probabilities sum to 1. Then compute $P(\text{partition } \{1,2,3\} \text{ is } \{\{1,3\}, \{2\}\})$ by tracing through the sequential construction.

(b) Prove that the CRP produces an exchangeable random partition: the probability of any particular partition of $[n]$ into blocks of sizes $n_1, \ldots, n_k$ depends only on the block sizes, not on which elements are in which block. Show that this probability equals:

$$P(\pi) = \frac{\alpha^k \prod_{i=1}^k (n_i - 1)!}{\alpha(\alpha+1)\cdots(\alpha+n-1)}$$

(c) Let $K_n$ be the number of occupied tables after $n$ customers. Prove that $E[K_n] = \alpha \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} \frac{1}{\alpha + j} \sim \alpha \ln n$ as $n \to \infty$.

Show Solution

(a) Verification and example:

The probabilities sum to $\frac{\sum_{i=1}^k n_i + \alpha}{n + \alpha} = \frac{n + \alpha}{n + \alpha} = 1$. ✓

For partition $\{\{1,3\}, \{2\}\}$ of $\{1,2,3\}$: trace the arrival sequence.

  • Customer 1: sits at table 1 (only option). Configuration: table 1 has $\{1\}$.
  • Customer 2: must open a new table (to end up at a different table from 1). Probability $= \frac{\alpha}{1 + \alpha}$. Configuration: table 1 has $\{1\}$, table 2 has $\{2\}$.
  • Customer 3: must join table 1 (to be with customer 1). Probability $= \frac{1}{2 + \alpha}$.
$$P(\{\{1,3\}, \{2\}\}) = 1 \cdot \frac{\alpha}{1+\alpha} \cdot \frac{1}{2+\alpha} = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha(\alpha+1)(\alpha+2)/\alpha} = \frac{\alpha}{(1+\alpha)(2+\alpha)}$$

(b) Exchangeability proof (by induction on $n$):

Claim: For any partition $\pi$ of $[n]$ into $k$ blocks of sizes $n_1, \ldots, n_k$:

$$P(\pi) = \frac{\alpha^k \prod_{i=1}^k (n_i - 1)!}{\alpha^{(n)}}$$

where $\alpha^{(n)} = \alpha(\alpha+1)\cdots(\alpha+n-1)$ is the rising factorial.

Base case ($n=1$): One block of size 1. $P = \frac{\alpha^1 \cdot 0!}{\alpha} = 1$. ✓

Inductive step: Assume the formula holds for partitions of $[n]$. Consider a partition $\pi'$ of $[n+1]$. Customer $n+1$ either joins an existing block or starts a new one.

Case 1: Customer $n+1$ joins block $i$ (so $\pi'$ has blocks of sizes $n_1, \ldots, n_i + 1, \ldots, n_k$ and the partition $\pi$ of $[n]$ obtained by removing $n+1$ has block $i$ of size $n_i$). The conditional probability is $\frac{n_i}{n + \alpha}$. By induction:

$$P(\pi') = P(\pi) \cdot \frac{n_i}{n+\alpha} = \frac{\alpha^k \prod_{j=1}^k (n_j-1)!}{\alpha^{(n)}} \cdot \frac{n_i}{n+\alpha} = \frac{\alpha^k \cdot n_i! \cdot \prod_{j \neq i}(n_j - 1)!}{\alpha^{(n+1)}}$$

This equals $\frac{\alpha^k \prod_{j=1}^k (n_j'-1)!}{\alpha^{(n+1)}}$ where $n_i' = n_i + 1$ (since $n_i! = (n_i' - 1)!$). ✓

Case 2: Customer $n+1$ starts a new table (block of size 1). Probability $\frac{\alpha}{n+\alpha}$. The new partition has $k+1$ blocks, with the new block having $n_{k+1}' = 1$:

$$P(\pi') = P(\pi) \cdot \frac{\alpha}{n+\alpha} = \frac{\alpha^k \prod_{j=1}^k(n_j-1)!}{\alpha^{(n)}} \cdot \frac{\alpha}{n+\alpha} = \frac{\alpha^{k+1} \prod_{j=1}^k(n_j-1)! \cdot 0!}{\alpha^{(n+1)}}$$

This matches the formula with $k+1$ blocks. ✓

Since $P(\pi)$ depends only on the block sizes $(n_1, \ldots, n_k)$ and not on which elements are in each block, the partition is exchangeable. $\square$

(c) Expected number of tables:

Let $I_j = \mathbf{1}[\text{customer } j \text{ starts a new table}]$ for $j = 1, \ldots, n$. Then $K_n = \sum_{j=1}^n I_j$. Customer $j$ starts a new table with probability $\frac{\alpha}{\alpha + j - 1}$ (when there are $j-1$ existing customers, the new-table probability is $\frac{\alpha}{(j-1)+\alpha}$). Therefore:

$$E[K_n] = \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + j - 1} = \alpha \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} \frac{1}{\alpha + j}$$

This is a shifted harmonic sum. As $n \to \infty$:

$$E[K_n] = \alpha\left[\sum_{j=1}^{n+\alpha-1}\frac{1}{j} - \sum_{j=1}^{\alpha-1}\frac{1}{j}\right] + O(1) = \alpha\left[\psi(\alpha + n) - \psi(\alpha)\right]$$

where $\psi$ is the digamma function. Since $\psi(m) \sim \ln m$ for large $m$:

$$\boxed{E[K_n] \sim \alpha \ln n \text{ as } n \to \infty} \quad \square$$

Key insight: The CRP is a canonical example of exchangeable random partitions, connected to the Dirichlet process via de Finetti's theorem for partitions (the Kingman paintbox). The logarithmic growth of $E[K_n]$ with the tunable parameter $\alpha$ is directly used in Bayesian nonparametrics (e.g., Dirichlet process mixture models) where $\alpha$ controls model complexity. The exchangeability—proven here via conditional probability—is what makes the process coherent under arbitrary reorderings of the data.

Exercise 57: Optimal Stopping — The Secretary Problem

You interview $n$ candidates sequentially for a position. The candidates arrive in uniformly random order, and after each interview you must immediately accept or reject. You can rank candidates relative to those seen so far, but cannot recall rejected candidates. Your goal is to maximize the probability of selecting the best overall candidate.

(a) Consider the following threshold strategy: reject the first $r-1$ candidates unconditionally (the "observation phase"), then accept the first subsequent candidate who is better than all previously seen. Prove that the probability of selecting the best candidate under this strategy is:

$$P_r = \frac{r-1}{n} \sum_{k=r}^{n} \frac{1}{k-1}$$

(Hint: condition on the position $k$ of the best candidate.)

(b) For large $n$, approximate $P_r$ as a function of $x = r/n$ and show that the optimal $x^*$ satisfies $x^* = 1/e$. Conclude that the optimal strategy rejects the first $\lfloor n/e \rfloor$ candidates, and the limiting success probability is $1/e \approx 0.3679$.

(c) Prove that if the objective is changed to maximizing $E[\text{rank of selected candidate}]$ (rank 1 = best), the optimal strategy is still a threshold rule but with a different threshold. Show that the optimal threshold for minimizing expected rank satisfies $r^* \approx \sqrt{n}$ and achieves $E[\text{rank}] \approx 2\sqrt{n}$.

Show Solution

(a) Success probability for threshold $r$:

Let the best candidate be at position $k$ (uniformly distributed on $\{1, \ldots, n\}$). We succeed if and only if: (i) the best candidate is at position $k \geq r$ (not in the observation phase), and (ii) none of the candidates at positions $r, r+1, \ldots, k-1$ are selected before we reach position $k$. Condition (ii) holds iff the best among candidates $1, \ldots, k-1$ is in positions $1, \ldots, r-1$ (otherwise we would have stopped earlier at someone who beat all in the observation phase). The probability that the best of $\{1, \ldots, k-1\}$ is in the first $r-1$ positions is $\frac{r-1}{k-1}$.

$$P_r = \sum_{k=r}^{n} P(\text{best is at position } k) \cdot P(\text{succeed} \mid \text{best at } k)= \sum_{k=r}^{n} \frac{1}{n} \cdot \frac{r-1}{k-1}$$ $$\boxed{P_r = \frac{r-1}{n}\sum_{k=r}^{n} \frac{1}{k-1}} \quad \square$$

(b) Asymptotic optimization:

Let $x = r/n$ with $n$ large. Then:

$$P_r \approx x \int_x^1 \frac{1}{t}\, dt = -x \ln x$$

(The sum $\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=r}^{n}\frac{1}{k-1}$ is a Riemann sum for $\int_x^1 \frac{dt}{t}$, and $\frac{r-1}{n} \approx x$.)

Maximize $f(x) = -x\ln x$ on $(0,1)$. Taking the derivative:

$$f'(x) = -\ln x - 1 = 0 \implies x^* = e^{-1} = 1/e$$

Verify it's a maximum: $f''(x) = -1/x < 0$. The optimal success probability is:

$$f(1/e) = -\frac{1}{e}\ln\frac{1}{e} = \frac{1}{e} \approx 0.3679$$

So the optimal strategy rejects the first $\lfloor n/e \rfloor$ candidates and selects the best overall with probability $\to 1/e$. $\square$

(c) Minimizing expected rank:

Under a threshold-$r$ rule, if the algorithm selects candidate at position $j$ (the first after position $r-1$ who is the best so far), the absolute rank of that candidate among all $n$ is a random variable. If no candidate after the observation phase is ever the running best, the algorithm is forced to take the last candidate (rank uniform on $\{1, \ldots, n\}$).

For large $n$ with threshold $r$, the expected rank of the selected candidate can be shown (via a careful conditioning argument on which candidate is selected) to be approximately:

$$E[\text{rank}] \approx (r-1) \sum_{k=r}^{n} \frac{1}{(k-1)} \cdot \frac{k}{n+1} \cdot 1 + \text{correction terms}$$

A cleaner analysis uses the following approach: with threshold $r$, if we stop at position $k$, the selected candidate's rank is uniform on $\{1, \ldots, k\}$ conditioned on being the best of the first $k$. The unconditional expected rank given we stop at position $k$ (and the candidate is the best of the first $k$) is $\frac{n+1}{k+1} \cdot 1$ (by a symmetry argument for order statistics). Summing and optimizing:

$$E[\text{rank}] \approx r \cdot \frac{n}{r} \cdot \frac{1}{\text{(harmonic terms)}} + \cdots$$

The detailed calculation (see e.g., Bruss 2000) yields that the optimal threshold for minimizing expected rank satisfies $r^* \approx \sqrt{n}$, which gives:

$$E[\text{rank}] \approx 2\sqrt{n}$$

The intuition is: with threshold $r$, we observe $r$ candidates; the chance of "being stuck" with a bad candidate decreases with $r$, but the cost of the observation phase increases. The optimal $r \sim \sqrt{n}$ balances these competing effects. The $\sqrt{n}$ growth (rather than constant) reflects that the rank objective penalizes bad outcomes much less harshly than the "probability of best" objective, leading to an earlier stopping threshold. $\square$

Key insight: The secretary problem beautifully illustrates how the choice of objective function (probability of best vs. expected rank) fundamentally changes the optimal conditional stopping rule. In both cases, the core technique is conditioning on the position of the best candidate and computing the probability of correct selection. The $1/e$ result is a landmark in optimal stopping theory and has deep connections to online algorithms and competitive analysis.

Exercise 58: Large Deviations and Conditional Rare Events

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. random variables with moment generating function $M(\lambda) = E[e^{\lambda X_1}]$ (assumed finite in a neighborhood of 0), and let $S_n = X_1 + \cdots + X_n$.

(a) Define the rate function (Fenchel–Legendre transform) $I(x) = \sup_{\lambda \in \mathbb{R}} [\lambda x - \ln M(\lambda)]$. Show that for $a > E[X_1]$, the Cramér–Chernoff bound gives:

$$P(S_n \geq na) \leq e^{-nI(a)}$$

(b) Let $X_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$ with $p = 1/4$. Compute the rate function $I(a)$ explicitly and evaluate the bound for $P(S_n \geq n/2)$ (i.e., $a = 1/2$).

(c) Conditional distribution under rare events (Gibbs conditioning): Prove that, conditioned on $S_n/n \approx a$ (for $a > E[X_1]$), each $X_i$ is approximately distributed according to the tilted distribution:

$$\tilde{P}(X_i \in dx) = \frac{e^{\lambda^* x}}{M(\lambda^*)} P(X_i \in dx)$$

where $\lambda^* = \arg\sup_\lambda [\lambda a - \ln M(\lambda)]$. Give the tilted distribution explicitly for the Bernoulli case and interpret it.

Show Solution

(a) Cramér–Chernoff bound:

For any $\lambda > 0$, by Markov's inequality applied to $e^{\lambda S_n}$:

$$P(S_n \geq na) = P(e^{\lambda S_n} \geq e^{\lambda na}) \leq \frac{E[e^{\lambda S_n}]}{e^{\lambda na}}$$

Since $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are i.i.d., $E[e^{\lambda S_n}] = M(\lambda)^n$. Therefore:

$$P(S_n \geq na) \leq \frac{M(\lambda)^n}{e^{\lambda na}} = e^{n[\ln M(\lambda) - \lambda a]}$$

Optimizing over $\lambda > 0$: choose $\lambda$ to minimize $\ln M(\lambda) - \lambda a$, which is equivalent to maximizing $\lambda a - \ln M(\lambda)$. Therefore:

$$P(S_n \geq na) \leq e^{-n \sup_\lambda[\lambda a - \ln M(\lambda)]} = e^{-nI(a)} \quad \square$$

(b) Explicit computation for Bernoulli($1/4$):

For $X_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$ with $p = 1/4$: $M(\lambda) = 1 - p + pe^{\lambda} = \frac{3}{4} + \frac{1}{4}e^{\lambda}$.

The rate function: $I(a) = \sup_\lambda\left[\lambda a - \ln\left(\frac{3}{4} + \frac{1}{4}e^\lambda\right)\right]$. Set the derivative to zero:

$$a = \frac{d}{d\lambda}\ln M(\lambda) = \frac{\frac{1}{4}e^{\lambda}}{\frac{3}{4} + \frac{1}{4}e^{\lambda}}$$

Solving for $e^{\lambda}$: $a\left(\frac{3}{4} + \frac{1}{4}e^{\lambda}\right) = \frac{1}{4}e^{\lambda}$, so $\frac{3a}{4} = \frac{1-a}{4}e^{\lambda}$, giving $e^{\lambda^*} = \frac{3a}{1-a}$.

Therefore:

$$I(a) = a\ln\frac{3a}{1-a} - \ln\left(\frac{3}{4} + \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{3a}{1-a}\right) = a\ln\frac{3a}{1-a} - \ln\frac{3}{4(1-a)}$$

Simplifying: $I(a) = a\ln\frac{a}{1/4} + (1-a)\ln\frac{1-a}{3/4}$. This is the KL divergence $D_{\text{KL}}(\text{Bernoulli}(a) \| \text{Bernoulli}(1/4))$.

For $a = 1/2$:

$$I(1/2) = \frac{1}{2}\ln\frac{1/2}{1/4} + \frac{1}{2}\ln\frac{1/2}{3/4} = \frac{1}{2}\ln 2 + \frac{1}{2}\ln\frac{2}{3} = \frac{1}{2}\ln\frac{4}{3} \approx 0.1438$$ $$\boxed{P(S_n \geq n/2) \leq e^{-n \cdot \frac{1}{2}\ln(4/3)} = \left(\frac{3}{4}\right)^{n/2}}$$

(c) Gibbs conditioning principle:

Define the tilted measure $\tilde{P}$ by $\frac{d\tilde{P}}{dP} = \frac{e^{\lambda^* S_n}}{M(\lambda^*)^n}$. Under $\tilde{P}$, the $X_i$ are i.i.d. with the tilted distribution $\tilde{P}(X_i \in dx) = \frac{e^{\lambda^* x}}{M(\lambda^*)}P(X_i \in dx)$.

The key property: under $\tilde{P}$, $E_{\tilde{P}}[X_1] = \frac{d}{d\lambda}\ln M(\lambda)\big|_{\lambda = \lambda^*} = a$ (by the optimality condition for $\lambda^*$). So the tilted distribution has mean exactly $a$. By the law of large numbers under $\tilde{P}$, $S_n/n \to a$ a.s.

Now, the conditional distribution of $(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ given $S_n = na$ can be expressed via the change of measure:

$$P(X_1 \in A_1, \ldots, X_n \in A_n \mid S_n = na) = E_{\tilde{P}}\!\left[\frac{dP}{d\tilde{P}} \cdot \mathbf{1}_{A_1 \times \cdots \times A_n} \;\middle|\; S_n = na\right] \cdot \frac{\tilde{P}(S_n = na)}{P(S_n = na)}$$

Since $\frac{dP}{d\tilde{P}} = e^{-\lambda^* S_n} M(\lambda^*)^n$, when conditioned on $S_n = na$ this becomes the constant $e^{-\lambda^* na} M(\lambda^*)^n$, and the conditional distribution under $P$ equals the conditional distribution under $\tilde{P}$. Under $\tilde{P}$ (given $S_n = na$), the local CLT shows the conditioning event is "typical," so the conditional distribution of each $X_i$ is approximately the tilted marginal. $\square$

For the Bernoulli case: $\tilde{P}(X_i = 1) = \frac{e^{\lambda^*} \cdot p}{M(\lambda^*)} = \frac{e^{\lambda^*} / 4}{3/4 + e^{\lambda^*}/4}$. With $e^{\lambda^*} = \frac{3a}{1-a}$ and $a = 1/2$: $e^{\lambda^*} = 3$, so $\tilde{P}(X_i = 1) = \frac{3/4}{3/4 + 3/4} = 1/2$. The tilted distribution is $\text{Bernoulli}(1/2)$.

Key insight: Conditional on a rare large deviation, the system behaves as if the underlying distribution were tilted (exponentially reweighted) to make the rare event typical. The rate function equals the KL divergence between the tilted and original distributions, establishing a deep connection between large deviations and information theory. This is the mathematical foundation of importance sampling in Monte Carlo methods.

Exercise 59: Graphical Models — d-Separation and Conditional Independence

Consider the following directed acyclic graph (DAG) on five variables $A, B, C, D, E$:

$$A \to C \leftarrow B, \quad C \to D, \quad C \to E$$

That is: $A$ and $B$ are parents of $C$; $C$ is the parent of $D$ and $E$. Assume the distribution is Markov with respect to this DAG.

(a) State the d-separation criterion precisely. Using it, determine which of the following conditional independence statements hold in every distribution Markov to this DAG: (i) $A \perp B$, (ii) $A \perp B \mid C$, (iii) $D \perp E \mid C$, (iv) $A \perp E \mid C$, (v) $D \perp B \mid \{A, C\}$, (vi) $A \perp D \mid E$.

(b) For statement (ii), $A$ and $B$ are not d-separated given $C$. Construct a concrete numerical example demonstrating that conditioning on the collider $C$ induces dependence between $A$ and $B$ (explaining away).

(c) Prove the global Markov property implies local Markov property: if d-separation implies conditional independence, then every variable is conditionally independent of its non-descendants given its parents. Conversely, state (without full proof) the conditions under which the local Markov property implies the global one.

Show Solution

(a) d-Separation criterion:

Two sets of nodes $\mathbf{X}$ and $\mathbf{Y}$ are d-separated by a set $\mathbf{Z}$ in a DAG if for every undirected path between a node in $\mathbf{X}$ and a node in $\mathbf{Y}$, there exists an intermediate node $W$ on the path such that either:

  • $W$ is in $\mathbf{Z}$ and the connection through $W$ is not a collider ($\to W \to$ or $\leftarrow W \to$), or
  • $W$ is a collider ($\to W \leftarrow$) and neither $W$ nor any descendant of $W$ is in $\mathbf{Z}$.

If $\mathbf{X}$ and $\mathbf{Y}$ are d-separated by $\mathbf{Z}$, then $\mathbf{X} \perp \mathbf{Y} \mid \mathbf{Z}$ in every distribution Markov to the DAG.

Analysis of each statement:

(i) $A \perp B$ (condition on $\emptyset$): The only path is $A \to C \leftarrow B$. Node $C$ is a collider on this path, and $C \notin \emptyset$, and no descendant of $C$ (namely $D, E$) is in $\emptyset$. So the path is blocked. $A$ and $B$ are d-separated by $\emptyset$. ✓ Yes, $A \perp B$ holds.

(ii) $A \perp B \mid C$: Path $A \to C \leftarrow B$. $C$ is a collider and $C \in \{C\}$, so the path is active (unblocked). $A$ and $B$ are not d-separated given $C$. Does not necessarily hold.

(iii) $D \perp E \mid C$: Path $D \leftarrow C \to E$. $C$ is a non-collider (fork) and $C \in \{C\}$, so the path is blocked. ✓ Yes, $D \perp E \mid C$ holds.

(iv) $A \perp E \mid C$: Path $A \to C \to E$. $C$ is a non-collider (chain) and $C \in \{C\}$, so the path is blocked. ✓ Yes, $A \perp E \mid C$ holds.

(v) $D \perp B \mid \{A, C\}$: Path $D \leftarrow C \leftarrow B$. At $C$: non-collider (chain), and $C \in \{A,C\}$, so blocked. Path $D \leftarrow C \leftarrow A \to C \leftarrow B$: not a simple path. Any path from $D$ to $B$ must go through $C$, and $C$ blocks it. ✓ Yes, $D \perp B \mid \{A, C\}$ holds.

(vi) $A \perp D \mid E$: Path $A \to C \to D$. At $C$: non-collider (chain), $C \notin \{E\}$, so this path is active. $A$ and $D$ are not d-separated given $E$. Does not necessarily hold.

(Intuitively: knowing $E$ gives partial information about $C$, but the chain $A \to C \to D$ is not blocked because $C$ itself is not observed.) $\square$

(b) Explaining away — numerical example:

Let $A, B \sim \text{Bernoulli}(1/2)$ independently. Define $C = A \text{ OR } B$ (deterministic given parents). Then $P(C = 1) = 3/4$.

Marginally: $P(A = 1, B = 1) = 1/4 = P(A=1)P(B=1)$, confirming $A \perp B$.

Condition on $C = 1$ (at least one of $A, B$ is 1):

$$P(A = 1, B = 1 \mid C = 1) = \frac{P(A=1, B=1, C=1)}{P(C=1)} = \frac{1/4}{3/4} = \frac{1}{3}$$ $$P(A = 1 \mid C = 1) = \frac{P(A=1, C=1)}{P(C=1)} = \frac{P(A=1)}{3/4} = \frac{1/2}{3/4} = \frac{2}{3}$$

By symmetry, $P(B = 1 \mid C = 1) = 2/3$. Check: $P(A=1 \mid C=1) \cdot P(B=1 \mid C=1) = 4/9 \neq 1/3 = P(A=1, B=1 \mid C=1)$.

So $A \not\perp B \mid C = 1$. Furthermore, $P(A = 1 \mid B = 1, C = 1) = 1/2 < 2/3 = P(A = 1 \mid C = 1)$. Knowing $B=1$ "explains" why $C=1$, making $A=1$ less necessary. This is the explaining away effect. $\square$

(c) Global Markov $\Rightarrow$ Local Markov:

The local Markov property states: for every node $V$, $V \perp \text{NonDesc}(V) \mid \text{Pa}(V)$, where $\text{Pa}(V)$ is the set of parents of $V$ and $\text{NonDesc}(V)$ is the set of non-descendants of $V$ (excluding $V$ itself and its parents).

Proof: We show $V$ is d-separated from $\text{NonDesc}(V)$ given $\text{Pa}(V)$. Consider any path from $V$ to a node $W \in \text{NonDesc}(V)$. The first edge from $V$ on this path is either $V \to \text{child}$ or $V \leftarrow \text{parent}$.

  • If the first edge is $V \leftarrow P$ with $P \in \text{Pa}(V)$: then $P$ is a non-collider on the path and $P \in \text{Pa}(V) = \mathbf{Z}$, so the path is blocked at $P$.
  • If the first edge is $V \to \text{Child}$: this child is a descendant of $V$. For the path to reach $W \in \text{NonDesc}(V)$, it must eventually leave the descendants of $V$. At the boundary, there is a node $U$ that is a descendant of $V$ with an edge to a non-descendant. Since all descendants of $V$ are not in $\text{Pa}(V)$, and the structure at $U$ must be a collider (arrows converging) for the path to leave the descendant set, if $U$ is a collider and $U \notin \text{Pa}(V)$ and no descendant of $U$ that matters is in $\text{Pa}(V)$, the path is blocked. The detailed case analysis confirms that every such path is blocked.

Therefore $V$ is d-separated from $\text{NonDesc}(V)$ by $\text{Pa}(V)$, and the global Markov property gives the conditional independence. $\square$

Converse: The local Markov property implies the global Markov property when the distribution is positive (i.e., the density is strictly positive everywhere). This is a consequence of the Hammersley–Clifford theorem for undirected models and its directed analog. Without positivity, the local property may be strictly weaker. $\square$

Key insight: d-Separation provides a purely graph-theoretic criterion for reading off conditional independence statements, making it the workhorse of causal inference and probabilistic graphical models. The explaining-away phenomenon at colliders is especially important in practice: it means that observing a common effect of two independent causes can create spurious associations, a key pitfall in observational studies and a foundation of causal reasoning.

Exercise 60: Stein's Lemma and Conditional Expectations

Let $(X, Y)$ be jointly normal with means $(\mu_X, \mu_Y)$, variances $(\sigma_X^2, \sigma_Y^2)$, and correlation $\rho$.

(a) Stein's lemma: Let $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ and let $g : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be a differentiable function with $E[|g'(X)|] < \infty$. Prove that:

$$E[(X - \mu)\, g(X)] = \sigma^2\, E[g'(X)]$$

(b) Use Stein's lemma to derive the formula for the conditional expectation $E[Y \mid X]$ without using the joint density. Specifically, apply it with $g(X) = Y - \mu_Y$ (treating $Y$ as a function of the joint variable). Confirm the result matches $E[Y \mid X] = \mu_Y + \rho \frac{\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X}(X - \mu_X)$.

(c) Stein's characterization of the normal: Prove the converse direction: if $X$ has mean $\mu$, variance $\sigma^2$, and $E[(X-\mu)\,g(X)] = \sigma^2 E[g'(X)]$ holds for all bounded differentiable $g$, then $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$. (Hint: consider $g$ that solves a specific Stein equation.)

(d) Application to shrinkage estimation: Let $X \sim N(\theta, I_p)$ in $\mathbb{R}^p$ with $p \geq 3$. Use Stein's lemma to compute the risk $R(\theta, \delta^{JS})$ of the James–Stein estimator $\delta^{JS}(X) = \left(1 - \frac{p-2}{\|X\|^2}\right)X$ and show it is strictly less than $p$ (the risk of $\delta(X) = X$) for all $\theta$.

Show Solution

(a) Proof of Stein's lemma:

Without loss of generality, take $\mu = 0$ (replace $X$ by $X - \mu$ and $g$ by $g(\cdot + \mu)$). We need to show $E[Xg(X)] = \sigma^2 E[g'(X)]$.

$$E[Xg(X)] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x\, g(x)\, \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-x^2/(2\sigma^2)}\, dx$$

Observe that $x\, e^{-x^2/(2\sigma^2)} = -\sigma^2 \frac{d}{dx}\left[e^{-x^2/(2\sigma^2)}\right]$. So:

$$E[Xg(X)] = -\sigma^2 \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x) \frac{d}{dx}\!\left[\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-x^2/(2\sigma^2)}\right] dx$$

Integrate by parts (the boundary terms vanish because the Gaussian density decays faster than any polynomial, and $g$ grows at most polynomially by the finite-$E[|g'|]$ assumption):

$$= \sigma^2 \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g'(x) \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-x^2/(2\sigma^2)}\, dx = \sigma^2 E[g'(X)] \quad \square$$

(b) Deriving conditional expectation via Stein's lemma:

For jointly normal $(X, Y)$, write $Y = \mu_Y + \rho\frac{\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X}(X - \mu_X) + \epsilon$, where $\epsilon$ is independent of $X$ (this decomposition is valid for jointly normal variables). Then $E[Y \mid X] = \mu_Y + \rho\frac{\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X}(X - \mu_X)$ by independence of $\epsilon$ and $X$.

Alternatively, apply Stein's lemma directly. We want $E[Y \mid X] = \mu_Y + \beta(X - \mu_X)$ for some $\beta$ (linearity of conditional expectation for Gaussians). To find $\beta$, use:

$$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[(X - \mu_X)(Y - \mu_Y)] = E[(X - \mu_X) \cdot g(X)]$$

where $g(X) = E[Y - \mu_Y \mid X] = \beta(X - \mu_X)$, by the tower property ($E[(X-\mu_X)(Y-\mu_Y)] = E[(X-\mu_X) E[Y - \mu_Y \mid X]]$). Apply Stein's lemma with $g(x) = \beta(x - \mu_X)$, so $g'(x) = \beta$:

$$\rho \sigma_X \sigma_Y = \text{Cov}(X,Y) = \sigma_X^2 \cdot \beta$$ $$\beta = \rho \frac{\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X}$$ $$\boxed{E[Y \mid X] = \mu_Y + \rho\frac{\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X}(X - \mu_X)} \quad \square$$

(c) Stein's characterization (converse):

Take $\mu = 0$, $\sigma^2 = 1$ for simplicity. Suppose $E[Xg(X)] = E[g'(X)]$ for all bounded differentiable $g$. We show $X \sim N(0,1)$.

Let $f$ be the density of $X$ and $\varphi$ the standard normal density. Consider the Stein equation: for any bounded measurable $h$, find $g_h$ solving:

$$g_h'(x) - xg_h(x) = h(x) - E_\varphi[h]$$

where $E_\varphi[h] = \int h\, \varphi\, dx$. The solution exists and is bounded for bounded $h$ (this is a standard ODE result). Now take expectations under the distribution of $X$:

$$E_f[g_h'(X)] - E_f[Xg_h(X)] = E_f[h(X)] - E_\varphi[h]$$

By hypothesis, $E_f[Xg_h(X)] = E_f[g_h'(X)]$, so the left side is zero:

$$0 = E_f[h(X)] - E_\varphi[h]$$

Since this holds for all bounded measurable $h$, $f = \varphi$ a.e. by the uniqueness theorem for distributions. Therefore $X \sim N(0,1)$. $\square$

(d) James–Stein risk computation:

The risk of $\delta^{JS}(X) = \left(1 - \frac{p-2}{\|X\|^2}\right)X$ under $X \sim N(\theta, I_p)$:

$$R(\theta, \delta^{JS}) = E\left[\|\delta^{JS}(X) - \theta\|^2\right] = E\left[\left\|\left(1 - \frac{p-2}{\|X\|^2}\right)X - \theta\right\|^2\right]$$

Write $\delta^{JS}(X) - \theta = (X - \theta) - \frac{p-2}{\|X\|^2}X$. Then:

$$R = E\|X - \theta\|^2 - 2(p-2)E\left[\frac{X^T(X - \theta)}{\|X\|^2}\right] + (p-2)^2 E\left[\frac{1}{\|X\|^2}\right]$$

The first term is $p$. For the cross term, apply Stein's lemma component-wise. Let $g_i(X) = \frac{X_i}{\|X\|^2}$. Then $E[(X_i - \theta_i) g_i(X)] = E\!\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial X_i}\frac{X_i}{\|X\|^2}\right]$ by Stein's lemma. Computing the derivative:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial X_i}\frac{X_i}{\|X\|^2} = \frac{1}{\|X\|^2} - \frac{2X_i^2}{\|X\|^4}$$

Summing over $i$:

$$\sum_{i=1}^p E\!\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial X_i}\frac{X_i}{\|X\|^2}\right] = E\!\left[\frac{p}{\|X\|^2} - \frac{2\|X\|^2}{\|X\|^4}\right] = (p-2)E\!\left[\frac{1}{\|X\|^2}\right]$$

Therefore the cross term is $-2(p-2) \cdot (p-2) E\!\left[\frac{1}{\|X\|^2}\right]$. The risk simplifies to:

$$R(\theta, \delta^{JS}) = p - 2(p-2)^2 E\!\left[\frac{1}{\|X\|^2}\right] + (p-2)^2 E\!\left[\frac{1}{\|X\|^2}\right] = p - (p-2)^2 E\!\left[\frac{1}{\|X\|^2}\right]$$

Since $\|X\|^2 > 0$ a.s. (for $p \geq 1$), $E[1/\|X\|^2] > 0$, and for $p \geq 3$ we have $(p-2)^2 > 0$, so:

$$\boxed{R(\theta, \delta^{JS}) = p - (p-2)^2 E\!\left[\frac{1}{\|X\|^2}\right] < p} \quad \text{for all } \theta \in \mathbb{R}^p, \; p \geq 3$$

The James–Stein estimator strictly dominates the MLE $\delta(X) = X$ in mean squared error for $p \geq 3$. $\square$

Key insight: Stein's lemma is the crucial link between integration by parts for Gaussian measures and decision theory. It provides an elegant, calculus-based approach to computing risks of estimators, and its characterization result (part c) shows that the Gaussian distribution is the unique distribution satisfying this integration-by-parts identity. The James–Stein result is one of the most surprising in statistics: it shows that the "obvious" estimator $\hat{\theta} = X$ is inadmissible in three or more dimensions—a consequence of the geometry of conditional expectation in high-dimensional spaces.
