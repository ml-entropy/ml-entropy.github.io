Exercise 11: Conditional Probability with Letters

A letter is chosen uniformly at random from the word MISSISSIPPI. Given that the chosen letter is a consonant, what is the probability that it is the letter S?

Show Solution

First, let’s count the letters in MISSISSIPPI (11 letters total):

  • M: 1
  • I: 4 (vowel)
  • S: 4 (consonant)
  • P: 2 (consonant)

Let $A$ = "letter is S" and $B$ = "letter is a consonant." The consonants are M, S, S, S, S, P, P – that is 7 consonants in total.

  • $P(B) = 7/11$ (7 consonants out of 11 letters).
  • $P(A \cap B) = 4/11$ (the 4 S’s are all consonants, so $A \cap B = A$).
$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} = \frac{4/11}{7/11} = \frac{4}{7}$$

Answer: $P(\text{S} \mid \text{consonant}) = 4/7 \approx 0.5714$.

Exercise 12: Medical Test – Basic Sensitivity

A medical test for a disease has a sensitivity (true positive rate) of 95% and a specificity (true negative rate) of 90%. In a population, 2% of people have the disease. If a person tests positive, what is the probability they actually have the disease?

Show Solution

Let $D$ = "has disease" and $T^+$ = "tests positive." We are given:

  • $P(T^+ \mid D) = 0.95$ (sensitivity)
  • $P(T^- \mid D^c) = 0.90$, so $P(T^+ \mid D^c) = 0.10$ (false positive rate)
  • $P(D) = 0.02$, so $P(D^c) = 0.98$

First, find $P(T^+)$ using the law of total probability:

$$P(T^+) = P(T^+ \mid D) P(D) + P(T^+ \mid D^c) P(D^c)$$ $$= (0.95)(0.02) + (0.10)(0.98) = 0.019 + 0.098 = 0.117$$

Now apply Bayes’ theorem:

$$P(D \mid T^+) = \frac{P(T^+ \mid D) P(D)}{P(T^+)} = \frac{(0.95)(0.02)}{0.117} = \frac{0.019}{0.117} \approx 0.1624$$

Even with a fairly accurate test, the low disease prevalence means most positive results are false positives.

Answer: $P(\text{disease} \mid \text{positive}) \approx 0.1624 \approx 16.2\%$.

Exercise 13: Weather Conditional Probability

Based on historical data for a city: the probability it rains on any given day is 0.30, the probability it is cloudy is 0.50, and the probability it is both cloudy and rainy is 0.25. Given that it is cloudy today, what is the probability it will rain?

Show Solution

Let $R$ = "rain" and $C$ = "cloudy." We are given:

  • $P(R) = 0.30$
  • $P(C) = 0.50$
  • $P(R \cap C) = 0.25$

We want $P(R \mid C)$. By the definition of conditional probability:

$$P(R \mid C) = \frac{P(R \cap C)}{P(C)} = \frac{0.25}{0.50} = 0.50$$

So on a cloudy day, there is a 50% chance of rain – higher than the unconditional 30%. This makes intuitive sense: clouds are a precondition for rain, so observing clouds increases the rain probability.

Note that $P(R \mid C) = 0.50 \neq 0.30 = P(R)$, so rain and cloudiness are not independent.

Answer: $P(\text{rain} \mid \text{cloudy}) = 0.50 = 50\%$.

Exercise 14: Sampling With vs. Without Replacement

A bowl contains 6 green and 4 yellow candies. Two candies are drawn one after the other. Find the probability that both are green (a) with replacement, and (b) without replacement. Compare the results.

Show Solution

Let $G_1$ = "first candy is green" and $G_2$ = "second candy is green."

(a) With replacement: After drawing the first candy, it is put back. The composition stays the same, so the draws are independent.

$$P(G_1 \cap G_2) = P(G_1) \cdot P(G_2) = \frac{6}{10} \cdot \frac{6}{10} = \frac{36}{100} = \frac{9}{25}$$

(b) Without replacement: After drawing the first green candy, only 5 green remain out of 9 total. We use the multiplication rule:

$$P(G_1 \cap G_2) = P(G_1) \cdot P(G_2 \mid G_1) = \frac{6}{10} \cdot \frac{5}{9} = \frac{30}{90} = \frac{1}{3}$$

Comparison: $9/25 = 0.36$ (with replacement) vs. $1/3 \approx 0.3333$ (without replacement). Without replacement gives a slightly lower probability because removing a green candy reduces the proportion of green candies remaining.

Answer: (a) $P = 9/25 = 0.36$; (b) $P = 1/3 \approx 0.3333$.

Exercise 15: Playing Cards – Suits and Number Cards

A card is drawn from a standard 52-card deck. Given that the card is a number card (2 through 10), what is the probability that it is a diamond?

Show Solution

Let $D$ = "card is a diamond" and $N$ = "card is a number card (2–10)."

Each suit has 9 number cards (2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10), and there are 4 suits, so there are $9 \times 4 = 36$ number cards total.

  • $P(N) = 36/52 = 9/13$
  • $P(D \cap N) = 9/52$ (the 9 number cards in diamonds)
$$P(D \mid N) = \frac{P(D \cap N)}{P(N)} = \frac{9/52}{36/52} = \frac{9}{36} = \frac{1}{4}$$

This equals $P(D) = 13/52 = 1/4$, so the events "diamond" and "number card" are independent. The suit of a card does not depend on whether it is a number card or face card.

Answer: $P(\text{diamond} \mid \text{number card}) = 1/4 = 0.25$.

Exercise 16: Birthday-Type Conditional Probability

In a group of 3 people, assume each person’s birth month is chosen uniformly at random from the 12 months (a simplified model). Given that all three people were born in different months, what is the probability that their birth months also fall in three different calendar quarters (Q1: Jan–Mar, Q2: Apr–Jun, Q3: Jul–Sep, Q4: Oct–Dec)?

Show Solution

We model each person’s birth month as chosen uniformly from 12 months. Each quarter contains exactly 3 months.

Let $A$ = "all three birthdays in different quarters" and $B$ = "all three birthdays in different months."

Step 1: Find $P(B)$. The first person can have any month. The second must avoid that month (11/12 choices), and the third must avoid both (10/12 choices):

$$P(B) = 1 \cdot \frac{11}{12} \cdot \frac{10}{12} = \frac{110}{144} = \frac{55}{72}$$

Step 2: Find $P(A \cap B)$. We need three people in three different quarters and three different months. Count ordered month selections: the first person picks any of 12 months. The second person must be in a different quarter (3 remaining quarters $\times$ 3 months each = 9 month choices). The third person must be in a quarter different from the first two (2 remaining quarters $\times$ 3 months each = 6 month choices). Since different quarters guarantee different months, the different-months condition is automatically satisfied:

$$P(A \cap B) = 1 \cdot \frac{9}{12} \cdot \frac{6}{12} = \frac{54}{144} = \frac{3}{8}$$

Step 3: Apply conditional probability:

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} = \frac{3/8}{55/72} = \frac{3}{8} \cdot \frac{72}{55} = \frac{216}{440} = \frac{27}{55}$$

Answer: $P(\text{different quarters} \mid \text{different months}) = 27/55 \approx 0.4909$.

Exercise 17: Independence with Divisibility

A number is chosen uniformly at random from $\{1, 2, 3, \ldots, 12\}$. Let $A$ = "the number is divisible by 3" and $B$ = "the number is divisible by 4." Are $A$ and $B$ independent?

Show Solution

We check the independence condition $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$.

  • $A = \{3, 6, 9, 12\}$, so $P(A) = 4/12 = 1/3$.
  • $B = \{4, 8, 12\}$, so $P(B) = 3/12 = 1/4$.
  • $A \cap B$ = numbers divisible by both 3 and 4, i.e., divisible by $\text{lcm}(3,4) = 12$. So $A \cap B = \{12\}$ and $P(A \cap B) = 1/12$.

Now compare:

$$P(A) \cdot P(B) = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{12}$$

Since $P(A \cap B) = 1/12 = P(A) \cdot P(B)$, the events are independent.

We can verify: $P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} = \frac{1/12}{1/4} = \frac{1}{3} = P(A)$. Learning divisibility by 4 does not change the probability of divisibility by 3.

Answer: $A$ and $B$ are independent.

Exercise 18: Conditional Probability in Sports

A basketball player makes 80% of her free throws overall. When the game is tied, she makes 70% of her free throws. In 25% of games, the game is tied at the time of a free throw. If she makes a free throw, what is the probability the game was tied?

Show Solution

Let $M$ = "makes the free throw" and $T$ = "game is tied." We are given:

  • $P(M) = 0.80$
  • $P(M \mid T) = 0.70$
  • $P(T) = 0.25$

We want $P(T \mid M)$. By Bayes’ theorem:

$$P(T \mid M) = \frac{P(M \mid T) \cdot P(T)}{P(M)} = \frac{(0.70)(0.25)}{0.80} = \frac{0.175}{0.80} = 0.21875$$

Notice that $P(T \mid M) \approx 0.219 < 0.25 = P(T)$. This makes sense: she shoots slightly worse when tied, so making a shot is mild evidence the game was not tied.

Answer: $P(\text{tied} \mid \text{makes free throw}) = 7/32 = 0.21875 \approx 21.9\%$.

Exercise 19: Basic Bayes’ Theorem Application

A taxi company has 85 blue taxis and 15 green taxis. A witness to a hit-and-run accident says the taxi was green. Testing shows the witness correctly identifies the colour of a taxi 80% of the time. What is the probability the taxi was actually green?

Show Solution

Let $G$ = "taxi is green" and $W$ = "witness says green." We have:

  • $P(G) = 15/100 = 0.15$ and $P(G^c) = 0.85$ (taxi is blue)
  • $P(W \mid G) = 0.80$ (witness is correct when it is green)
  • $P(W \mid G^c) = 0.20$ (witness is wrong when it is blue)

First find $P(W)$ using the law of total probability:

$$P(W) = P(W \mid G) P(G) + P(W \mid G^c) P(G^c) = (0.80)(0.15) + (0.20)(0.85)$$ $$= 0.12 + 0.17 = 0.29$$

Now apply Bayes’ theorem:

$$P(G \mid W) = \frac{P(W \mid G) P(G)}{P(W)} = \frac{0.12}{0.29} = \frac{12}{29} \approx 0.4138$$

Despite the witness saying "green," there is less than a 50% chance the taxi was actually green. The large proportion of blue taxis (the prior) pulls the posterior probability down – a classic illustration of Bayesian reasoning.

Answer: $P(\text{green} \mid \text{witness says green}) = 12/29 \approx 41.4\%$.

Exercise 20: Complement Rule with Conditional Probability

A software system has two independent servers, A and B. The probability that server A is running is 0.95 and the probability that server B is running is 0.90. Given that at least one server is running, what is the probability that both are running?

Show Solution

Let $A$ = "server A is running" and $B$ = "server B is running." We are given:

  • $P(A) = 0.95$, $P(B) = 0.90$
  • $A$ and $B$ are independent

We want $P(A \cap B \mid A \cup B)$. First, since $A$ and $B$ are independent:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) = (0.95)(0.90) = 0.855$$

Next, find $P(A \cup B)$ using the complement. The complement of "at least one running" is "neither is running":

$$P(A^c \cap B^c) = P(A^c) \cdot P(B^c) = (0.05)(0.10) = 0.005$$ $$P(A \cup B) = 1 - P(A^c \cap B^c) = 1 - 0.005 = 0.995$$

Now apply the definition of conditional probability:

$$P(A \cap B \mid A \cup B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A \cup B)} = \frac{0.855}{0.995} = \frac{171}{199} \approx 0.8593$$

Given that at least one server is up, there is about an 85.9% chance both are up. The condition barely changes the probability because both-down is extremely unlikely (only 0.5%).

Answer: $P(\text{both running} \mid \text{at least one running}) = 171/199 \approx 0.8593 \approx 85.9\%$.
